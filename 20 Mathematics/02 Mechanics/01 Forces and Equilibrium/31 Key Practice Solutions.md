# Forces and Equilibrium Key Practice Solutions

This note gives worked solutions for [[20 Mathematics/02 Mechanics/01 Forces and Equilibrium/30 Key Practice Problems|Forces and Equilibrium Key Practice Problems]]. Use it to check the force diagram, equations, signs, units, and physical interpretation. Take $g=9.8\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}$ where a mass is given.

## A. Particle Equilibrium and Components

### Problem 1

Let $T$ be the tension in the sloping string and $H$ the tension in the
horizontal string. Vertical equilibrium gives

$$
T\sin50^\circ=36.
$$

So

$$
T=\frac{36}{\sin50^\circ}=47.0\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

Horizontal equilibrium gives

$$
H=T\cos50^\circ=30.2\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

Check: the sloping string has enough upward component to balance the load, and
the horizontal string balances the sideways component.

### Problem 2

The resultant of the two given forces has components

$$
(28,45).
$$

Its magnitude is

$$
\sqrt{28^2+45^2}=53.0\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

The angle of this resultant above east is

$$
\tan^{-1}\frac{45}{28}=58.1^\circ.
$$

The third force must be equal and opposite to the resultant. Therefore it is

$$
53.0\ \mathrm{N}
$$

acting $58.1^\circ$ south of west.

### Problem 3

Let $T_1$ be the tension in the $30^\circ$ string and $T_2$ the tension in the
$60^\circ$ string. Horizontal equilibrium gives

$$
T_1\cos30^\circ=T_2\cos60^\circ.
$$

Vertical equilibrium gives

$$
T_1\sin30^\circ+T_2\sin60^\circ=100.
$$

From the horizontal equation,

$$
T_1=\frac{T_2\cos60^\circ}{\cos30^\circ}.
$$

Substitute this into the vertical equation:

$$
\frac{T_2\cos60^\circ}{\cos30^\circ}\sin30^\circ
+T_2\sin60^\circ=100.
$$

This gives

$$
T_2=86.6\ \mathrm{N},\qquad T_1=50.0\ \mathrm{N}
$$

to 3 s.f. Check: the horizontal components are both
$43.3\ \mathrm{N}$ in opposite directions.

### Problem 4

Resolve parallel to the smooth plane:

$$
T=6g\sin20^\circ=20.1\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

Resolve perpendicular to the plane:

$$
R=6g\cos20^\circ=55.3\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

The plane is smooth, so there is no friction term.

### Problem 5

The block is in vertical equilibrium, so the normal contact force on the block
is

$$
R=8g=78.4\ \mathrm{N}
$$

upwards. By Newton's third law, the force exerted by the block on the table is

$$
78.4\ \mathrm{N}
$$

downwards. This action-reaction pair acts on two different bodies.

## B. Friction and Limiting Equilibrium

### Problem 6

The normal contact force is

$$
R=10g=98\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

At limiting equilibrium,

$$
F=\mu R=0.25(98)=24.5\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

The least horizontal force needed to start motion is therefore

$$
24.5\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

### Problem 7

Vertical equilibrium gives

$$
R+P\sin25^\circ=14g,
$$

so

$$
R=14g-P\sin25^\circ.
$$

At the limiting point,

$$
P\cos25^\circ=0.30R.
$$

Substitute for $R$:

$$
P\cos25^\circ=0.30(14g-P\sin25^\circ).
$$

Hence

$$
P(\cos25^\circ+0.30\sin25^\circ)=0.30(14g).
$$

Therefore

$$
P=39.8\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

Check: the pull has an upward component, so the answer is less than
$0.30(14g)$.

### Problem 8

At the least coefficient, the particle is just about to slide down the plane.
Thus friction acts up the plane and is limiting.

Parallel to the plane:

$$
F=mg\sin18^\circ.
$$

Perpendicular to the plane:

$$
R=mg\cos18^\circ.
$$

Since $F=\mu R$,

$$
\mu=\tan18^\circ=0.325\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

### Problem 9

For the hanging mass, equilibrium gives

$$
T=2g=19.6\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

For the block on the plane, the component of its weight down the plane is

$$
3g\sin25^\circ=12.4\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

The tension is larger than this component, so without friction the block would
tend to move up the plane. Friction therefore acts down the plane.

Equilibrium along the plane gives

$$
F=T-3g\sin25^\circ.
$$

Hence

$$
F=19.6-12.4=7.18\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

The normal contact force is

$$
R=3g\cos25^\circ=26.6\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

The least coefficient of friction is

$$
\mu=\frac{F}{R}=\frac{7.18}{26.6}=0.269\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

### Problem 10

The particle is just prevented from sliding down the plane, so friction and
the force $P$ both act up the plane. The normal contact force is

$$
R=4g\cos30^\circ.
$$

At limiting equilibrium,

$$
P+\mu R=4g\sin30^\circ.
$$

Therefore

$$
P=4g\sin30^\circ-0.20(4g\cos30^\circ).
$$

So

$$
P=12.8\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

## C. Moments and Rigid Bodies

### Problem 11

Let the support reaction at the far end be $R_B$ and the hinge reaction be
$R_A$. Take moments about the hinge:

$$
5R_B=80(2.5)+120(4).
$$

Thus

$$
R_B=136\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

Vertical force equilibrium gives

$$
R_A+R_B=80+120.
$$

Therefore

$$
R_A=200-136=64\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

### Problem 12

Let $T$ be the string tension. Take moments about the hinge:

$$
3T\sin40^\circ=60(1.5).
$$

So

$$
T=\frac{30}{\sin40^\circ}=46.7\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

The string pulls left and upward on the rod. Its horizontal component is

$$
T\cos40^\circ=35.8\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

So the horizontal hinge reaction is $35.8\ \mathrm{N}$ to the right.

The upward component of the tension is

$$
T\sin40^\circ=30\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

Vertical equilibrium gives

$$
V+30=60,
$$

so the vertical hinge reaction is

$$
30\ \mathrm{N}
$$

upwards.

### Problem 13

Let $H$ be the normal reaction at the smooth wall. The wall is smooth, so this
force is horizontal. Let $N$ be the normal reaction at the floor and $F$ the
friction at the floor.

Vertical equilibrium gives

$$
N=200.
$$

Horizontal equilibrium gives

$$
F=H.
$$

Take moments about the foot of the ladder. The perpendicular distance from the
foot to the wall reaction is $5\sin60^\circ$. The horizontal distance from the
foot to the centre of mass is $2.5\cos60^\circ$. Hence

$$
H(5\sin60^\circ)=200(2.5\cos60^\circ).
$$

So

$$
H=57.7\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

Therefore

$$
F=57.7\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

The least coefficient of friction is

$$
\mu=\frac{F}{N}=\frac{57.7}{200}=0.289\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

### Problem 14

Let the left and right support reactions be $R_A$ and $R_B$. Vertical
equilibrium gives

$$
R_A+R_B=90+150+120=360.
$$

Take moments about the left end:

$$
6R_B=90(3)+150(2)+120(5).
$$

Thus

$$
6R_B=1170,
$$

so

$$
R_B=195\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

Therefore

$$
R_A=360-195=165\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

### Problem 15

The sliding threshold is

$$
P=\mu R=0.25(300)=75\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

The toppling threshold is found by taking moments about the lower edge. The
weight acts through the centre, $0.40\ \mathrm{m}$ from the edge. Hence

$$
P(1.20)=300(0.40).
$$

So

$$
P=100\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

Since

$$
75<100,
$$

the box slides before it topples.

## D. Centre of Mass and Mixed Review

### Problem 16

The three square plates have equal mass, so average the coordinates of their
centres:

$$
\bar x=\frac{0.5+1.5+0.5}{3}=\frac56,
$$

and

$$
\bar y=\frac{0.5+0.5+1.5}{3}=\frac56.
$$

The centre of mass is

$$
\left(\frac56,\frac56\right)\ \mathrm{m}.
$$

### Problem 17

The rod's own centre of mass is $2\ \mathrm{m}$ from $A$. Treat the rod and
the particle as two masses on a line:

$$
\bar x=\frac{3(2)+2(4)}{3+2}.
$$

Thus

$$
\bar x=\frac{14}{5}=2.8\ \mathrm{m}
$$

from $A$.

### Problem 18

For a uniform triangular lamina, the centre of mass is the centroid. Average
the vertex coordinates:

$$
\bar x=\frac{0+6+0}{3}=2,
$$

and

$$
\bar y=\frac{0+0+3}{3}=1.
$$

The centre of mass is

$$
(2,1).
$$

### Problem 19

Let $T$ be the tension. Take moments about the hinge at $A$:

$$
5T\sin30^\circ=80(2.5)+100(3).
$$

Therefore

$$
5T\sin30^\circ=500,
$$

so

$$
T=200\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

The string pulls left and upward. Its horizontal component is

$$
T\cos30^\circ=173\ \mathrm{N}\quad \text{to 3 s.f.}
$$

so the horizontal hinge reaction is $173\ \mathrm{N}$ to the right.

The vertical component of the tension is

$$
T\sin30^\circ=100\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

Vertical equilibrium gives

$$
V+100=80+100.
$$

Hence the vertical hinge reaction is

$$
80\ \mathrm{N}
$$

upwards.

### Problem 20

The sliding threshold is

$$
P=\mu R=0.60(160)=96\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

For toppling, take moments about the lower edge in the direction of toppling.
The horizontal distance from the edge to the line of action of the weight is
$0.25\ \mathrm{m}$. At the toppling threshold,

$$
P(0.80)=160(0.25).
$$

Thus

$$
P=50.0\ \mathrm{N}.
$$

Since

$$
50.0<96,
$$

the block topples before it slides. The first loss of equilibrium occurs at

$$
P=50.0\ \mathrm{N}.
$$
