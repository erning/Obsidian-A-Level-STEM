# Trigonometry and Circular Measure Key Practice Solutions

Use these solutions to check method choice and algebra for [[20 Mathematics/01 Pure Mathematics/03 Trigonometry and Circular Measure/30 Key Practice Problems|Key Practice Problems]].

## A. Circular Measure

1. Convert degrees to radians by multiplying by $\frac{\pi}{180}$:

   $$
   150^\circ=\frac{150\pi}{180}=\frac{5\pi}{6}.
   $$

   Convert radians to degrees by multiplying by $\frac{180}{\pi}$:

   $$
   \frac{7\pi}{6}\times\frac{180}{\pi}=210^\circ.
   $$

2. The arc length is

   $$
   s=r\theta=6\cdot\frac{2\pi}{3}=4\pi\text{ cm}.
   $$

   The sector area is

   $$
   A=\frac{1}{2}r^2\theta
   =\frac{1}{2}(36)\cdot\frac{2\pi}{3}
   =12\pi\text{ cm}^2.
   $$

3. From $s=r\theta$,

   $$
   \theta=\frac{s}{r}=\frac{10}{8}=\frac{5}{4}.
   $$

   The sector area is

   $$
   A=\frac{1}{2}r^2\theta
   =\frac{1}{2}(64)\cdot\frac{5}{4}
   =40\text{ cm}^2.
   $$

4. The sector area is

   $$
   \frac{1}{2}(5^2)(1.2)=15.
   $$

   The triangle area is

   $$
   \frac{1}{2}(5^2)\sin1.2=12.5\sin1.2.
   $$

   Hence the minor segment area is

   $$
   15-12.5\sin1.2\approx3.35\text{ cm}^2.
   $$

5. First convert the angle:

   $$
   40^\circ=\frac{2\pi}{9}.
   $$

   The sector area is

   $$
   \frac{1}{2}(10^2)\cdot\frac{2\pi}{9}
   =\frac{100\pi}{9}\text{ cm}^2.
   $$

   The triangle area is

   $$
   \frac{1}{2}(10)(10)\sin40^\circ
   =50\sin40^\circ\text{ cm}^2.
   $$

6. Use

   $$
   24=\frac{1}{2}(4^2)\theta=8\theta.
   $$

   So

   $$
   \theta=3.
   $$

   The arc length is

   $$
   s=r\theta=4(3)=12\text{ cm}.
   $$

## B. Exact Values, Inverses, and Graphs

7. Use reference angles and quadrant signs:

   $$
   \sin\frac{7\pi}{6}=-\frac{1}{2},
   $$

   $$
   \cos\frac{5\pi}{6}=-\frac{\sqrt3}{2},
   $$

   and

   $$
   \tan\frac{3\pi}{4}=-1.
   $$

8. Using the principal ranges of the inverse trigonometric functions:

   $$
   \sin^{-1}\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)=-\frac{\pi}{6},
   $$

   $$
   \cos^{-1}\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)=\frac{2\pi}{3},
   $$

   and

   $$
   \tan^{-1}(-1)=-\frac{\pi}{4}.
   $$

9. The period of $2\sin x-1$ is $2\pi$. Since

   $$
   -1\le\sin x\le1,
   $$

   the range is

   $$
   -3\le y\le1.
   $$

10. The period is

    $$
    \frac{2\pi}{2}=\pi.
    $$

    Since $-1\le\cos2x\le1$,

    $$
    0\le1-\cos2x\le2.
    $$

    The range is $0\le y\le2$. Minima occur when

    $$
    \cos2x=1.
    $$

    Hence, for $0\le x\le2\pi$,

    $$
    x=0,\pi,2\pi.
    $$

11. The period is $\pi$. Vertical asymptotes occur when

    $$
    x-\frac{\pi}{4}=\frac{\pi}{2}+k\pi.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    x=\frac{3\pi}{4}+k\pi,\qquad k\in\mathbb Z.
    $$

## C. Trigonometric Equations and Identities

12. The reference angle is $\frac{\pi}{3}$. Sine is positive in quadrants I
    and II, so

    $$
    x=\frac{\pi}{3},\frac{2\pi}{3}.
    $$

13. The reference angle is $\frac{\pi}{3}$. Cosine is negative in quadrants II
    and III, so

    $$
    x=\frac{2\pi}{3},\frac{4\pi}{3}.
    $$

14. The tangent period is $\pi$. In the interval $-\pi<x\le\pi$,

    $$
    x=-\frac{\pi}{4},\frac{3\pi}{4}.
    $$

15. Factor the equation:

    $$
    \sin x(2\sin x-1)=0.
    $$

    Hence $\sin x=0$ or $\sin x=\frac{1}{2}$. Therefore

    $$
    x=0,\frac{\pi}{6},\frac{5\pi}{6},\pi.
    $$

16. Let $u=\cos x$. Then

    $$
    2u^2-3u+1=0
    $$

    so

    $$
    (2u-1)(u-1)=0.
    $$

    Thus $\cos x=\frac{1}{2}$ or $\cos x=1$. In $0\le x<2\pi$,

    $$
    x=0,\frac{\pi}{3},\frac{5\pi}{3}.
    $$

17. Use the Pythagorean identity:

    $$
    \frac{1-\cos^2x}{1-\sin^2x}
    =\frac{\sin^2x}{\cos^2x}
    =\tan^2x.
    $$

    The original denominator is $\cos^2x$, so the restriction is
    $\cos x\ne0$.

18. Since

    $$
    \sec^2x-1=\tan^2x,
    $$

    the left-hand side is

    $$
    \frac{\tan^2x}{\tan x}=\tan x.
    $$

    This proves the identity where $\cos x\ne0$ and $\tan x\ne0$.

## D. Compound Angles, Reciprocal Functions, and Auxiliary Angles

19. Use $75^\circ=45^\circ+30^\circ$:

    $$
    \sin75^\circ
    =\sin45^\circ\cos30^\circ+\cos45^\circ\sin30^\circ.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    \sin75^\circ
    =\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\cdot\frac{\sqrt3}{2}
    +\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}
    =\frac{\sqrt6+\sqrt2}{4}.
    $$

20. Since $A$ is in quadrant II,

    $$
    \cos A=-\frac{4}{5}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    \sin2A=2\sin A\cos A
    =2\cdot\frac{3}{5}\cdot\left(-\frac{4}{5}\right)
    =-\frac{24}{25},
    $$

    and

    $$
    \cos2A=\cos^2A-\sin^2A
    =\frac{16}{25}-\frac{9}{25}
    =\frac{7}{25}.
    $$

21. Use $\sin2x=2\sin x\cos x$:

    $$
    2\sin x\cos x=\cos x.
    $$

    Factor without dividing by $\cos x$:

    $$
    \cos x(2\sin x-1)=0.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    x=\frac{\pi}{2},\frac{3\pi}{2}
    $$

    or

    $$
    x=\frac{\pi}{6},\frac{5\pi}{6}.
    $$

    The full set is

    $$
    x=\frac{\pi}{6},\frac{\pi}{2},\frac{5\pi}{6},\frac{3\pi}{2}.
    $$

22. Let $t=\tan\theta$. Then

    $$
    t+\frac{1}{t}=4.
    $$

    So

    $$
    t^2-4t+1=0,
    $$

    which gives

    $$
    t=2\pm\sqrt3.
    $$

    Both values are positive, so in $0<\theta<\pi$ the solutions are in
    quadrant I:

    $$
    \theta=\frac{\pi}{12},\frac{5\pi}{12}.
    $$

23. Use

    $$
    \sec^2x-\tan^2x=1
    $$

    and

    $$
    \operatorname{cosec}^2x-\cot^2x=1.
    $$

    Therefore the expression simplifies to

    $$
    2
    $$

    where the original expression is defined.

24. Expand

    $$
    R\sin(\theta+\alpha)
    =R\sin\theta\cos\alpha+R\cos\theta\sin\alpha.
    $$

    Match coefficients:

    $$
    R\cos\alpha=3,\qquad R\sin\alpha=4.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    R=5,\qquad \alpha=\tan^{-1}\frac{4}{3}.
    $$

    So

    $$
    3\sin\theta+4\cos\theta
    =5\sin(\theta+\alpha).
    $$

    The maximum value is $5$ and the minimum value is $-5$.

25. From question 24,

    $$
    3\sin\theta+4\cos\theta=5\sin(\theta+\alpha),
    \qquad \alpha=\tan^{-1}\frac{4}{3}.
    $$

    So

    $$
    \sin(\theta+\alpha)=0.
    $$

    In $0\le\theta<2\pi$,

    $$
    \theta=\pi-\alpha,\ 2\pi-\alpha.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    \theta=\pi-\tan^{-1}\frac{4}{3},\
    2\pi-\tan^{-1}\frac{4}{3}.
    $$

26. Since $\tan u=\sqrt3$ has general solution

    $$
    u=\frac{\pi}{3}+k\pi,\qquad k\in\mathbb Z,
    $$

    we have

    $$
    \theta-\frac{\pi}{6}=\frac{\pi}{3}+k\pi.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    \theta=\frac{\pi}{2}+k\pi,\qquad k\in\mathbb Z.
    $$
