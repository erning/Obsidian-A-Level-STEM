# Learning Map

This map follows CAIE Chemistry 9701 as a route through atomic structure, chemical bonding, physical chemistry, inorganic chemistry, organic chemistry, analysis, and practical skills. The syllabus gives the official topic order; the learning goal is fluency: explain structure, predict reactivity, calculate quantities, interpret data, and connect laboratory observations to chemical models.

## Route

1. Foundations of chemical language: topics 1-4 build atomic structure, amount of substance, formulae, equations, bonding, structure, and states of matter. These topics supply the vocabulary and quantitative tools used everywhere else.
2. AS Level physical chemistry: topics 5-8 introduce enthalpy changes, redox, electrochemistry, equilibria, acids and bases, and reaction kinetics. Learn each topic through definitions, equations, graphs, assumptions, and experimental evidence.
3. AS Level inorganic chemistry: topics 9-12 use periodic trends to explain chemical periodicity, Group 2, Group 17, nitrogen, and sulfur. Treat trends as consequences of structure, bonding, energetics, and redox behaviour.
4. AS Level organic chemistry: topics 13-22 introduce functional groups, nomenclature, isomerism, mechanisms, reactions, polymerisation, synthesis, and analytical techniques. The central skill is moving between structure, mechanism, reagent, condition, observation, and product.
5. A Level physical chemistry: topics 23-26 extend energetics, electrochemistry, equilibria, and kinetics using lattice energy, entropy, Gibbs free energy, electrode potentials, pH, buffers, solubility product, partition coefficients, rate equations, and catalysis.
6. A Level inorganic chemistry: topics 27-28 extend Group 2 and introduce transition elements, complex ions, ligand exchange, colour, redox, stereoisomerism, and stability constants.
7. A Level organic chemistry and analysis: topics 29-37 extend organic language into aromatic chemistry, phenol, acyl chlorides, amines, amides, amino acids, condensation polymers, synthesis, chromatography, and NMR spectroscopy.
8. Practical skills run through the whole course: observation, measurement, safety, qualitative analysis, planning, data processing, graph work, uncertainty, and evaluation should be connected back to topic notes rather than isolated at the end.

## Learning Questions

Use these questions for every topic:

- What particles, structures, bonds, or functional groups are doing the chemical work?
- What quantities, equations, or constants describe the system?
- What observations, spectra, graphs, or numerical data would support the explanation?
- What conditions, assumptions, and limitations control the method?
- What reaction type, mechanism, or trend explains the change?
- How would the idea appear in a practical task or data-analysis problem?
- Which earlier topic supplies the model, and which later topic extends it?

## Cross-Topic Connections

- Atomic structure connects to periodicity, ionisation energy, redox, transition elements, and spectroscopy.
- Bonding and structure connect to states of matter, physical properties, organic shape, intermolecular forces, and lattice energy.
- Amount of substance connects to titration, gas calculations, electrolysis, equilibrium composition, rate data, and practical yield.
- Energetics connects to Hess cycles, bond energies, lattice energy, entropy, Gibbs free energy, and feasibility.
- Equilibria connects to acids and bases, buffers, solubility product, partition coefficients, ligand exchange, and industrial conditions.
- Kinetics connects to collision theory, Boltzmann distributions, rate equations, mechanisms, catalysts, and practical rate measurements.
- Redox connects to Group 17, nitrogen and sulfur chemistry, electrolysis, electrode potentials, transition elements, and organic oxidation and reduction.
- Organic mechanisms connect functional-group chemistry to synthesis, polymerisation, analytical evidence, and stereochemistry.

## Three Passes

First pass: build the map. Read the syllabus topic route, write the core idea for each topic, and record the main definitions, formulae, functional groups, and practical observations.

Second pass: build usable methods. Work through calculations, mechanisms, equations, synthetic routes, qualitative tests, and data interpretation until each representation can be translated into another.

Third pass: build connections. Link repeated branches across AS and A Level, especially energetics, electrochemistry, equilibria, kinetics, Group 2, organic synthesis, and analytical techniques.

## Expected Outcome

By the end, you should be able to move between particle-level explanation, symbolic equation, mechanism, calculation, graph, laboratory observation, and analytical evidence. Chemistry becomes reliable when those translations agree.
