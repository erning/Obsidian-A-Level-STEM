# Experimental Design and Safety - Lecture Notes

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 Syllabus 2025-2027, section 5 *Practical assessment*. Experimental design and safety appear in both examined practical papers: Paper 3 *Advanced Practical Skills* (laboratory-based) and Paper 5 *Planning, Analysis and Evaluation* (written planning). Relevant syllabus pages are 58-64 (Paper 3) and 71-74 (Paper 5, defining the problem and method).

The core skill is turning a chemical question into a safe, reliable procedure in which one variable is changed, one is measured, and everything else is held constant.

## Variables

Every quantitative investigation is built on three kinds of variable.

- The **independent variable** is the quantity you deliberately change.
- The **dependent variable** is the quantity you measure to see the effect.
- **Control variables** are all the other factors that could affect the dependent variable and that you keep constant.

A common error is naming a control variable without saying how it is kept constant. "Keep temperature constant" is incomplete; "use a water-bath at $30\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ for all runs" is a method.

The range of the independent variable matters. To show a pattern, take readings that span a sensible range with an even distribution, not five values clustered together. Paper 5 planning rewards a range wide enough to test the prediction and enough readings to reveal the shape of any graph.

## Prediction and Hypothesis

A prediction states how the dependent variable is expected to respond to the independent variable. It can be expressed in words or as a sketch of the expected graph.

A good prediction is testable. "More concentrated acid reacts faster" is weak. "The time for the cross to disappear is inversely proportional to the concentration of sodium thiosulfate" predicts a specific shape and can be checked against a graph of time against concentration.

## Choosing Apparatus

Apparatus is chosen for range, precision and practicality.

| Quantity | Typical apparatus | Precision to record |
| --- | --- | --- |
| volume delivered | burette | nearest $0.05\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ |
| volume transferred | pipette ($10$ or $25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$) | fixed volume |
| approximate volume | measuring cylinder | nearest $0.5\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ |
| mass | balance (reads to $0.01\ \mathrm{g}$) | nearest $0.01\ \mathrm{g}$ |
| temperature | thermometer, calibrated every $1\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ | nearest $0.5\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ |
| time | stop-clock | nearest $1\ \mathrm{s}$ |

Explain why the apparatus suits the measurement. A burette is chosen for a titration because it delivers variable volumes to high precision; a pipette is chosen to transfer one exact volume repeatedly.

## Control Experiments

A control experiment checks that the effect seen is really caused by the independent variable and not by something else. If you are testing whether a catalyst increases rate, a control run without the catalyst (all else identical) shows the background rate.

In a written plan, state what the control is, what is removed or held at zero, and what result would confirm your interpretation.

## Standard Laboratory Practice

Several techniques make a quantitative result reliable. They recur in planning marks.

- **Making a standard solution**: dissolve a accurately weighed mass and make up to the mark in a volumetric flask, ensuring all solid is transferred and the solution is mixed.
- **Weighing by difference**: weigh the container, tip out the sample, weigh the container again; the difference is the true mass used. This avoids error from residue left behind.
- **Concordant titres**: repeat the titration until two titres agree within $0.10\ \mathrm{cm^3}$, then average only the concordant values.
- **Heating to constant mass**: in gravimetric work, heat, cool, weigh, and repeat until two consecutive masses agree, confirming the reaction is complete.
- **Extra readings near a key region**: take more readings around the steepest part of a curve or the expected end point, where one extra point changes the conclusion most.

## Safety: Hazard and Risk

A **hazard** is something with the potential to cause harm. A **risk** is the chance that it actually does, combined with how serious the harm would be. Good safety work identifies the hazard and then reduces the risk.

The syllabus lists precautions that match specific risks:

- use a **fume hood** for hazardous or poisonous gases;
- avoid **naked flames** near flammable materials;
- wear **eye protection** and **chemically resistant gloves** for irritant or corrosive substances;
- use a **test-tube holder** when heating and point the open end away from people;
- use **small quantities** of reagents to reduce both risk and waste.

A weak answer names a hazard without the matching precaution ("acid is corrosive"). A strong answer links them ("dilute sulfuric acid is an irritant, so wear gloves and eye protection and use a pipette filler, not mouth pipetting").

## Risk Assessment Routine

For each step of a planned method:

1. Identify the chemical or physical hazard (corrosive, toxic, flammable, hot, evolved gas).
2. State who or what is at risk.
3. State the precaution that reduces that risk.
4. State what to do if the precaution fails (e.g. rinse skin with water).

## Worked-Thinking Routine

For a planning or design question:

1. Write the aim as a prediction linking independent and dependent variables.
2. List the independent variable and its range.
3. List the dependent variable and how it is measured, with apparatus and precision.
4. List each control variable and how it is held constant.
5. Describe the apparatus arrangement and the sequence of steps.
6. Add a results table with headings and units.
7. Add safety precautions matched to real hazards.
8. Add one control experiment or reliability check.

## Common Mistakes

- Naming a control variable without saying how it is kept constant.
- Listing apparatus without explaining why its precision suits the measurement.
- Stating a safety precaution that does not match the actual hazard.
- Changing two variables at once, so the cause of any change is unclear.
- Saying "repeat for accuracy" without saying what is averaged or checked.
