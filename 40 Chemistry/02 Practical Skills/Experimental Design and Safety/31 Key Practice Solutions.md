# Experimental Design and Safety - Key Practice Solutions

## Solutions

1. Independent: concentration of sodium thiosulfate. Dependent: time for the cross to disappear. Controls (any three, with method): total volume kept at $25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ by diluting with water; volume and concentration of acid fixed and measured with the same pipette; temperature kept at room temperature and recorded; same flask and same printed cross.

2. Use a plastic (polystyrene) cup, which is a poor conductor, so less heat escapes through the walls. Add a lid, which stops warm air and vapour leaving, reducing heat loss to the surroundings. Both keep more of the reaction heat inside the measured solution.

3. Weighing by difference (weigh container, tip out sample, weigh container again) gives the mass actually used, because any residue stays in the container and is accounted for by the second weighing. Weighing directly on a watch glass loses any solid left behind, so the recorded mass overestimates what reacted.

4. A control experiment is a run in which the independent variable is removed or set to zero, with everything else identical, used to check that the independent variable really causes the effect. Example: run the reaction without the catalyst, keeping concentration, volume and temperature the same, to measure the background rate.

5. Transfer the acid with a $25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ pipette (a fixed volume, no reading needed beyond the mark). Deliver the alkali from a burette, read to the nearest $0.05\ \mathrm{cm^3}$.

6. $2.0\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ sodium hydroxide is an irritant. Precautions: wear eye protection and chemically resistant gloves; use a pipette filler, never mouth pipetting; rinse any spill with plenty of water.

7. Repeating detects random errors and gives a mean that is more reliable than a single titre. Concordant titres are two titres within $0.10\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ of each other; only concordant titres are averaged.

8. Small quantities reduce the risk from any hazardous product (less gas, less heat, less toxic fume) and reduce chemical waste, which is both safer and cheaper to dispose of.

9. Three controls: mass of marble chips (weighed to $0.01\ \mathrm{g}$ for each run); volume and concentration of acid (measured with a pipette or measuring cylinder); surface area or chip size of the marble (use chips from the same batch, similarly sized).

10. "Keep everything else the same" does not say which variables matter or how each is held constant, so the method cannot be followed or checked. Proper rewrite for one control: "keep the volume of acid constant at $25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$, measured with the same pipette for every run".

11. Place each reactant solution in its own tube in the water bath, leave them until a thermometer in each shows the set temperature, then mix them quickly and start timing. This ensures the reaction actually starts at the intended temperature.

12. Any two: rinse the burette and pipette with the solutions they will hold; swirl the flask the same way each time; read the burette at eye level to avoid parallax; perform a rough titration first, then titrate dropwise near the end point; repeat to obtain concordant titres.
