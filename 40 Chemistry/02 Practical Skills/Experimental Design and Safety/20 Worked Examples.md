# Experimental Design and Safety - Worked Examples

These examples model how to turn a question into a plan with clear variables, apparatus, controls and matched safety precautions. They follow the routine in [the lecture notes](10%20Lecture%20Notes.md).

## Example 1: Rate of the thiosulfate-acid reaction

**Question.** Plan an experiment to investigate how the concentration of sodium thiosulfate affects the rate of its reaction with dilute hydrochloric acid, using the disappearing-cross method.

**Prediction.** The higher the concentration of sodium thiosulfate, the shorter the time for the printed cross to disappear, because the frequency of effective collisions increases. The expected graph of time against concentration is a falling curve.

**Variables.**

- Independent: concentration of $\ce{Na2S2O3(aq)}$, varied from $0.05$ to $0.40\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ by diluting with water.
- Dependent: time $t$ for the cross to disappear, measured with a stop-clock reading to $1\ \mathrm{s}$.
- Controlled: volume of thiosulfate solution ($25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ measured with a pipette), volume and concentration of $\ce{HCl}$ ($5\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ of $1.0\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$), temperature (all runs at room temperature, recorded once), same flask and same printed cross.

**Method.**

1. Pipette $25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ of $0.40\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ sodium thiosulfate into a conical flask placed over the cross.
2. Add $5\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ of $1.0\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ hydrochloric acid, start the clock, and swirl once.
3. Look down through the flask; stop the clock when the cross just disappears.
4. Repeat with $0.32$, $0.24$, $0.16$ and $0.08\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ thiosulfate, made by diluting the $0.40\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ stock with the appropriate volume of water so the total volume stays $25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$.
5. Repeat each concentration once and average concordant times.

**Results table headings.**

| $[\ce{Na2S2O3}]$ / mol dm⁻³ | volume of water / cm³ | time $t$ / s | $1/t$ / s⁻¹ |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |

**Why $1/t$.** Because rate is proportional to $1/t$, plotting $1/t$ against concentration tests the prediction that rate rises with concentration and should give a straight line through the origin for a first-order-style check.

**Safety.** Sulfur dioxide is evolved, which is an irritant. Use a fume hood or a well-ventilated room, keep volumes small, and avoid leaning over the flask. Wear eye protection. The acid is an irritant, so use a pipette filler.

**Check.** The total volume is constant in every run, so concentration is the only variable changing. Temperature is recorded, not assumed.

## Example 2: Thermometric enthalpy of neutralisation

**Question.** Plan how to measure the temperature change when $25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ of $2.0\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ hydrochloric acid is neutralised by excess $2.0\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ sodium hydroxide.

**Prediction.** The temperature rises as the reaction is exothermic, then slowly falls as heat is lost.

**Variables.**

- Independent: volume of $\ce{NaOH}$ added.
- Dependent: temperature of the mixture.
- Controlled: concentration of both solutions, starting temperature, the same insulated cup, the same stirring.

**Method.**

1. Place $25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ of $2.0\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ $\ce{HCl}$ in a plastic cup inside a beaker for support. Record the initial temperature every minute for three minutes to establish a baseline.
2. Add $25\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ of $2.0\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ $\ce{NaOH}$ (an excess relative to the acid), stir, and record the maximum temperature reached.
3. Continue recording each minute for several minutes to show the cooling trend.
4. Plot temperature against time and extrapolate the cooling line back to the moment of mixing to correct for heat lost during the reaction.

**Why insulation matters.** A plastic cup reduces heat loss to the surroundings; extrapolation corrects for the heat that still escapes. Both improve the accuracy of $\Delta T$.

**Safety.** $2.0\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ acid and alkali are irritants. Wear eye protection and gloves, use a pipette filler, and rinse any spill with water.

**Reliability check.** A control run replacing $\ce{NaOH}$ with an equal volume of water would show the temperature drift caused by mixing and stirring alone, helping separate the reaction heat from apparatus effects.

## Example 3: Choosing apparatus and risk for gas collection

**Question.** A carbonate of unknown formula is to be identified from the volume of carbon dioxide released with excess dilute hydrochloric acid. Outline the apparatus choice, the measurements, and the safety.

**Apparatus reasoning.** The gas volume is the dependent variable, so a gas syringe or an inverted measuring cylinder over water is suitable. A gas syringe is preferred because it reads directly and avoids solubility loss of $\ce{CO2}$ in water. The carbonate mass is measured by difference on a balance reading to $0.01\ \mathrm{g}$, and the acid volume with a measuring cylinder since it is in excess and only approximate precision is needed.

**Measurements.** Record the mass of carbonate used and the final gas volume. Repeat to check that two runs agree closely.

**Safety.** Dilute hydrochloric acid is an irritant; wear eye protection and use a pipette or measuring cylinder, not mouth pipetting. There are no flammable materials, so a naked flame is not a specific hazard, but the apparatus should be clamped securely to avoid spills.

**Control of variables.** Use the same acid concentration and an excess in every run, so that the gas volume depends only on the amount of carbonate and its formula.

## Quick Checks for Any Plan

- Is exactly one variable changed?
- Is the dependent variable measured with stated apparatus and precision?
- Is each control variable held constant by a stated method?
- Does the safety precaution match a real hazard in the method?
- Is there a results table with headings and units?
- Is there a control experiment or a reliability check?
