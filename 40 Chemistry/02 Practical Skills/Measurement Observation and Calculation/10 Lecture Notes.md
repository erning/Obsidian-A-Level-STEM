# Measurement, Observation and Calculation - Lecture Notes

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 Syllabus 2025-2027, section 5 *Practical assessment*, Paper 3 skill areas: *manipulation, measurement and observation*; *presentation of data and observations*; and the calculation parts of *analysis, conclusions and evaluation* (syllabus pages 59-62). The mathematical requirements on page 75 also apply.

The thread running through this topic is: record what the apparatus can actually tell you, present it clearly, and do not invent precision.

## Recording to the Precision of the Apparatus

Each instrument has a precision. A reading should reflect it, and every reading of the same quantity in one set of data should be recorded to the same precision.

- A burette is read to the nearest $0.05\ \mathrm{cm^3}$, so titres such as $23.45\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ or $23.50\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ are expected, with the final digit being $0$ or $5$.
- A thermometer calibrated every $1\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ is read to the nearest $0.5\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$.
- A balance reading to $0.01\ \mathrm{g}$ gives masses such as $2.46\ \mathrm{g}$.
- A measuring cylinder calibrated every $1.0\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ is read to the nearest $0.5\ \mathrm{cm^3}$.

If one raw mass is recorded as $0.06\ \mathrm{g}$, then every mass in that table is given to the nearest $0.01\ \mathrm{g}$. Mixing $0.06\ \mathrm{g}$ with $0.1\ \mathrm{g}$ in the same column loses credit.

## Observations

Observations describe what you can detect directly: colour, precipitate, gas, solubility, temperature change, smell. They are not conclusions.

- Record colour in simple words first: "blue", "yellow".
- Add detail only when it discriminates: "pale blue", "dark brown".
- Make comparisons when they matter: "darker brown than at three minutes".
- Record "no change" or "remains colourless" when that is what happens. A negative observation rules out possibilities.

A precipitate is written as, for example, "white ppt.". Conventional symbols such as $\Delta H$ or "ppt." may be used without explanation.

## Tables

A results table is drawn before readings are taken, so data are recorded once.

- Each column needs a heading and units. Accepted forms are `volume / cm³`, `volume (cm³)` or `volume in cm³`. Use one form consistently.
- Raw and processed data can share one table, with each column labelled.
- Every entry in a column is recorded to the same precision.
- Leave a column for calculated quantities if they help analysis.

Example heading style:

| volume of $\ce{Na2S2O3}$ / cm³ | volume of water / cm³ | time for cross to disappear / s | $1/t$ / s⁻¹ |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |

## Graphs

A graph reveals the pattern and lets you read off unknowns or gradients.

- Plot the independent variable on the $x$-axis and the dependent variable on the $y$-axis, unless a calculation demands otherwise.
- Label both axes with quantity and units, using the same convention as the table headings.
- Choose scales that are easy to read: $1$, $2$ or $5$ units per $20\ \mathrm{mm}$ square. Avoid scales of $3$, $4$, $6$ or $7$.
- The data points should occupy at least half of the grid in both directions.
- Plot points as a cross $\times$ or a circled dot $\odot$.
- Draw a straight line or a smooth curve of best fit. Do not join every point. An even scatter of points on either side along the whole line is the aim.
- Identify anomalous points rather than hiding them.

## Gradient of a Straight-Line Graph

Use two points on the line of best fit that are more than half the length of the plotted line apart. For points $(x_1,y_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2)$:

$$
m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}
$$

A large triangle gives a more reliable gradient than a small one, because the same reading uncertainty is a smaller fraction of a larger difference. The intercept on the $y$-axis is $c$ in $y = mx + c$.

## Significant Figures in Calculations

A calculated quantity should be given to the same number of significant figures as the least precise value used, or one more. Never more.

If a titre is $23.45\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ (four significant figures) and the alkali concentration is $0.100\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ (three significant figures), the calculated acid concentration may be written as $0.131\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ (three) or $0.1305\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ (four). Writing $0.13048\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ is not acceptable.

Keep extra digits through the working and round only at the end, so that intermediate rounding does not distort the answer.

## Uncertainty and Percentage Error

The maximum uncertainty in a single reading is taken as half the smallest division of the instrument. A thermometer calibrated every $1\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ has a maximum uncertainty of $\pm 0.5\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$.

For a measured change, the uncertainties at both ends add, so the uncertainty in a temperature change measured from two readings is $\pm(0.5 + 0.5) = \pm 1.0\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$.

The percentage error in a measured quantity is:

$$
\text{percentage error} = \frac{\text{uncertainty}}{\text{measured value}} \times 100
$$

For a temperature change of $14.0\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ measured with a $1\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ thermometer:

$$
\text{percentage error} = \frac{2 \times 0.5}{14.0} \times 100 = 7.14\%
$$

The factor of $2$ appears because the change is the difference of two readings. A larger measured change gives a smaller percentage error, which is one reason for using enough reagent to give a clear signal.

## Systematic and Random Errors

- A **systematic error** shifts every reading the same way, for example a balance with a zero error or a thermometer reading $1\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ high throughout. It is reduced by using the same instrument for all readings and by calibration.
- A **random error** varies from reading to reading, for example fluctuating room temperature. It is reduced by repeats and averaging.

"Human error" is not an acceptable description. Be specific: name the instrument, the limitation, and whether it is systematic or random.

## Worked-Thinking Routine

For a quantitative question:

1. Identify the measured quantities and their precision.
2. Lay out a table with headings and units before calculating.
3. Carry units through every line of working.
4. Decide the significant figures from the least precise input.
5. For a graph question, check axes, scales and the size of the gradient triangle.
6. End with a check that the answer has a plausible size and sign.

## Common Mistakes

- Recording one reading as $23.4\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ and another as $23.50\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ in the same titre column.
- Quoting a calculated concentration to six significant figures.
- Joining every point on a graph instead of drawing a line of best fit.
- Using a tiny triangle for a gradient.
- Writing "human error" instead of naming a specific systematic or random error.
- Forgetting that a temperature change has uncertainty from both readings.
