# Planning, Analysis and Evaluation - Lecture Notes

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 Syllabus 2025-2027, section 5, *Paper 5 Planning, Analysis and Evaluation* (syllabus pages 71-74). Paper 5 is a written paper; it does not need laboratory facilities in the exam, but every skill depends on real laboratory experience. The four skill areas are planning, analysis, drawing conclusions and evaluation.

Paper 5 has two or more questions totalling $30$ marks. Questions may be set in chemistry outside the syllabus content; any information you are not expected to know is provided.

## What Paper 5 Tests

| Skill area | What you do | Minimum marks |

| Planning: method | Apparatus, steps, measurements, table, safety | (part of 12) |

The remaining $6$ marks are spread across the grid and vary by paper.

## Planning: Defining the Problem

Begin by making the question precise.

- State the **aim** as a prediction linking the independent and dependent variables.
- Identify the **independent variable** and how it will be changed over a sensible range.
- Identify the **dependent variable** and exactly how it will be measured.
- List the **control variables** and how each is held constant.
- Express the prediction in words or as a sketch of the expected graph.
- Identify the main **risks** and the precautions that match them.

A prediction as a graph is powerful: sketch the axes, label them, and draw the line you expect. If you predict direct proportionality, the sketch is a straight line through the origin; if you predict an inverse relationship, it is a curve.

## Planning: Method

The method is a sequence another chemist could follow.

- Describe the **apparatus arrangement**, in words or with a labelled diagram.
- State each **step** in order, including how the independent variable is changed and how the dependent variable is measured.
- Choose **measuring instruments** that give suitable precision, and say why.
- Suggest appropriate **volumes and concentrations** of reagents.
- Describe **safety precautions** matched to real hazards: fume hood for hazardous gases, no naked flames for flammables, gloves and eye protection for irritants.
- Describe any **control experiment** that checks the independent variable really causes the effect.
- Draw the **results table** with headings and units before collecting data.
- Refer back to the **prediction**: state what result would support it.

Standard laboratory practice earns marks here: making standard solutions, weighing by difference, concordant titres, heating to constant mass, and taking extra readings near the steepest part of a curve.

## Analysis: Dealing with Data

Given data, decide what processing reveals the conclusion.

- Identify the **calculations** needed: mean, percentage, percentage gain or loss, rate, concentration, amount of substance, molar mass, gas volume.
- Use **tables and graphs** to expose the pattern, including the spread of the data.
- Plot a graph of $y$ against $x$ and use it to find $m$ and $c$ in $y = mx + c$.
- When data are non-linear, choose quantities to plot that **linearise** the relationship. For example, if rate is expected to be proportional to concentration, plot rate against concentration for a straight line; if time is inversely related to concentration, plot $1/\text{time}$ against concentration.
- Calculate the **percentage error** of key measurements.
- Give calculated quantities to the **correct number of significant figures**.

Choosing axes from a range of values is a recurring skill. Read the question to see which relationship is expected, then pick variables that produce a straight line through the origin or a clear intercept.

## Drawing Conclusions

A conclusion does more than repeat the numbers.

- Describe the **main features** of the data or graph: the trend, the shape, any intercept.
- State whether the data **support the prediction**.
- Give a **scientific explanation** that links the pattern to chemistry, not only arithmetic agreement.
- Make **further predictions** or suggest improvements.
- Judge whether a **measurement error** could account for any gap between expected and experimental values.

For example, if an enthalpy change is less exothermic than expected, a conclusion should link the gap to heat loss, not simply note that the numbers differ.

## Evaluation

Evaluation judges how much trust the conclusion deserves.

- Identify **anomalous values**, suggest causes, and say how to handle them (ignore and repeat, or re-measure).
- Judge whether readings have been **adequately replicated** and whether the **range** is sufficient.
- Assess whether the **control variables** were effectively held constant.
- Identify and explain **weaknesses** in the procedure.
- Suggest the effect of changing **concentrations or conditions**, or of **incorrect apparatus use**.
- Comment on **validity**: do the data actually test the prediction, or could a hidden variable explain the result?
- Comment on **reliability**: do repeats agree closely enough that the result can be trusted?

A line of best fit with points evenly scattered and no anomalies is reliable. A cluster of points off the line signals a problem to explain.

## Worked-Thinking Routine

For a Paper 5 question:

1. Read the background and underline the relationship being tested.
2. In planning, write variables, then method, then table, then safety.
3. In analysis, decide the calculation and the graph that linearises the data.
4. In conclusion, describe the trend and link it to chemistry.
5. In evaluation, name the single largest source of error and one realistic improvement.

## Common Mistakes

- Listing apparatus without describing the sequence of steps.
- Stating a prediction without identifying the control variables.
- Plotting the wrong quantities and losing the straight-line relationship.
- Describing an improvement that does not address the main error.
- Saying data "prove" a prediction rather than "support" it.
- Quoting a conclusion to more significant figures than the data justify.
