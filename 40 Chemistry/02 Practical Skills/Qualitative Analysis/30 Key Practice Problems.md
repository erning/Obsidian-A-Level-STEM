# Qualitative Analysis - Key Practice Problems

## Problems

1. Solution $\ce{X}$ gives a white precipitate with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ that dissolves in excess. With $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ it gives a white precipitate insoluble in excess. Identify the cation and explain how the ammonia test rules out the alternative.

2. Solid $\ce{Y}$ effervesces with dilute nitric acid, giving a gas that turns limewater milky. A solution of $\ce{Y}$ gives a white precipitate with $\ce{Ba^{2+}(aq)}$ that does not dissolve in dilute acid. What can you conclude about $\ce{Y}$?

3. A pale blue solution gives no precipitate with $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ but turns deep blue. Explain what has happened and which ion is present.

4. Compound $\ce{W}$ gives an orange precipitate with 2,4-DNPH, no reaction with Fehling's reagent, and a yellow precipitate with alkaline aqueous iodine. Deduce the functional groups present.

5. For the halide ions $\ce{Cl-}$, $\ce{Br-}$ and $\ce{I-}$, state the colour of the silver halide precipitate and its solubility in $\ce{NH3(aq)}$.

6. Explain why dilute nitric acid is added to a solution before testing it with silver nitrate.

7. State the test and result for each gas: ammonia, carbon dioxide, hydrogen, oxygen.

8. A solution gives a white precipitate with both $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ and $\ce{NH3(aq)}$, insoluble in excess in both. Suggest the cation.

9. Describe how to distinguish a sulfate from a sulfite using barium chloride and acidified potassium manganate(VII).

10. Explain why the observation "no change" must be recorded in qualitative analysis, giving one example of what it rules out.

11. Describe the correct order of tests to identify an unknown carbonyl compound using 2,4-DNPH, Fehling's reagent and alkaline aqueous iodine.

12. Solid $\ce{Z}$ gives no precipitate with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$, but on warming a gas is produced that turns damp red litmus paper blue. Identify the cation and write the ionic equation.
