# Qualitative Analysis

Qualitative analysis is about using observations to identify substances. The useful skill is disciplined recording: observe first, infer second.

## Syllabus Route

Paper 3 may include an observational problem involving unknown substances. The exam paper provides qualitative analysis notes for the relevant tests. Candidates are expected to record observations, analyse them, and draw appropriate conclusions.

## Observation Routine

- Use an appropriate quantity of the unknown.
- Add only the specified amount of reagent.
- Record all observations, including "no change" and "remains colourless".
- Describe colour, precipitate formation, gas evolution, solubility in excess reagent, and any change on heating.
- Use excess $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ or $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ where a precipitate forms and solubility in excess is part of the test.
- Confirm a gas when effervescence is observed.

## From Observation to Conclusion

| Record | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Observation | What was seen, smelled, measured, or tested. |
| Test result | The observation matched to a known reagent test. |
| Conclusion | The ion, gas, or substance suggested by the evidence. |

Examples of observation wording:

- "White precipitate forms; insoluble in excess $\ce{NH3(aq)}$."
- "Effervescence; gas turns damp red litmus paper blue."
- "No visible change on addition of dilute acid."

## Caution Points

- Do not skip negative observations; they often rule out alternatives.
- Do not name an ion before recording the evidence.
- Use precise colour words only when the difference matters.
- If an unknown may contain ions outside the notes, rely on the information provided in the question.
