# Qualitative Analysis - Lecture Notes

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 Syllabus 2025-2027, section 5 *Practical assessment*: the qualitative analysis method on page 64, the organic analysis tests on the same page, and the *Qualitative analysis notes* (cations, anions, gases and elements) reproduced on pages 69-70. In Paper 3 one question is an observational problem, and the Qualitative analysis notes are provided as part of the exam paper.

The skill is disciplined observation followed by cautious inference: see first, conclude second.

## Good Qualitative Analysis Habits

- Treat all unknown materials with caution.
- Use an **appropriate quantity** of the material under test; do not waste it or overload the test.
- Add **only the specified amount** of reagent, then more if a test calls for excess.
- Work safely: use a test-tube holder when heating, and heat solids in a hard-glass test-tube.
- Record **all** observations, including "no change" or "remains a colourless solution".
- Use **excess** $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ or $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ when a precipitate forms, to test its solubility in excess.
- Identify a **gas** whenever effervescence is observed.

A negative observation is evidence. "No precipitate with aqueous ammonia" rules out several cations and must be written down.

## Recording and Concluding

Keep observation and conclusion separate.

| Observation | What it is | Conclusion (example) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| white precipitate, soluble in excess $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ | a recorded event | $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ or $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ possible |
| gas turns damp red litmus blue | a test result | ammonia |
| no change on adding dilute acid | a negative result | carbonate unlikely |

Write observations in the order the tests were done. Only draw a conclusion when the observations support it, and keep alternative explanations if the evidence does not yet distinguish them.

## Cation Tests with NaOH(aq) and NH3(aq)

Adding aqueous sodium hydroxide and aqueous ammonia, including in excess, is the main way to identify cations. The behaviour in excess distinguishes ions with the same initial precipitate.

| Cation | With $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ | With $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ | white ppt., soluble in excess | white ppt., insoluble in excess |
| $\ce{Ca^{2+}}$ | white ppt. (unless very dilute) | no ppt. |
| $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ | white ppt., insoluble in excess | white ppt., insoluble in excess |
| $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ | white ppt., soluble in excess | white ppt., soluble in excess |
| $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ | green ppt., turning brown in air | green ppt., turning brown in air |
| $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ | red-brown ppt., insoluble in excess | red-brown ppt., insoluble in excess |
| $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ | pale blue ppt., insoluble in excess | pale blue ppt., soluble in excess giving dark blue solution |
| $\ce{Cr^{3+}}$ | grey-green ppt., soluble in excess giving dark green solution | grey-green ppt., insoluble in excess |
| $\ce{NH4+}$ | no ppt.; ammonia given off on warming |: |

The solubility in excess is often the deciding clue. $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ and $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ both give a white precipitate soluble in excess $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$, but $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ is insoluble in excess $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ while $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ dissolves. $\ce{Ca^{2+}}$ gives no precipitate with $\ce{NH3(aq)}$, separating it from $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$.

Ammonium ions give no precipitate; warming with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ releases ammonia, detected by damp red litmus turning blue.

## Anion Tests

| Anion | Test and result |
| --- | --- |
| carbonate, $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$ | liberates $\ce{CO2}$ with dilute acid |
| sulfate, $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$ | white ppt. with $\ce{Ba^{2+}(aq)}$, insoluble in dilute acid |
| sulfite, $\ce{SO3^{2-}}$ | white ppt. with $\ce{Ba^{2+}(aq)}$ soluble in dilute acid; decolourises acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ |
| chloride, $\ce{Cl-}$ | white ppt. with $\ce{Ag+(aq)}$, soluble in $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ |
| bromide, $\ce{Br-}$ | cream/off-white ppt. with $\ce{Ag+(aq)}$, partially soluble in $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ |
| iodide, $\ce{I-}$ | pale yellow ppt. with $\ce{Ag+(aq)}$, insoluble in $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ |
| nitrate, $\ce{NO3-}$ | ammonia liberated with $\ce{OH-}$ and $\ce{Al}$ foil on heating |
| nitrite, $\ce{NO2-}$ | as nitrate; also decolourises acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ |
| thiosulfate, $\ce{S2O3^{2-}}$ | off-white/pale yellow ppt. slowly with $\ce{H+}$ |

Acidifying before the silver nitrate test removes interfering carbonate or hydroxide ions. To distinguish a sulfate from a sulfite, both give a white barium precipitate, but only the sulfite's precipitate dissolves in dilute acid and only the sulfite decolourises acidified potassium manganate(VII).

## Organic Analysis Tests

These tests, with their positive results, are in the syllabus for practical analysis.

| Test | Positive result | Inference |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fehling's reagent | orange/red precipitate | aldehyde group |
| Tollens' reagent | silver mirror or black precipitate | aldehyde group |
| alkaline aqueous iodine | yellow precipitate of tri-iodomethane | $\ce{CH3CO-}$ or $\ce{CH3CH(OH)-}$ group |
| acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ | purple to colourless | a group that can be oxidised |

A full carbonyl analysis often uses 2,4-DNPH first to confirm a carbonyl, then Fehling's or Tollens' reagent to decide aldehyde or ketone. The tri-iodomethane test then checks for the $\ce{CH3CO-}$ group.

## Working with Unfamiliar Ions

The unknown may contain ions not in the provided notes. In that case, record the observations carefully and draw conclusions of a general nature (for example, "a carbonate is present because effervescence with acid gives a gas that turns limewater milky"), rather than guessing a specific ion.

## Worked-Thinking Routine

For an observational problem:

1. Read the list of tests and do them in order.
2. Record each observation immediately, including "no change".
3. Add excess reagent where a precipitate forms, and record solubility in excess.
4. Build a shortlist of ions consistent with each observation.
5. Cross out ions contradicted by any observation.
6. State the conclusion only when one ion fits every observation.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing a conclusion before recording the observation.
- Forgetting to test solubility in excess, so $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ and $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ cannot be separated.
- Omitting "no change" observations that rule out alternatives.
- Naming a gas without the matching test result.
- Applying organic tests out of order, for example using Fehling's reagent before confirming a carbonyl exists.
