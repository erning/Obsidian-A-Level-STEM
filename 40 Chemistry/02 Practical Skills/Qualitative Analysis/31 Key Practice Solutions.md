# Qualitative Analysis - Key Practice Solutions

## Solutions

1. The cation is $\ce{Al^{3+}}$. A white precipitate soluble in excess $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ fits both $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ and $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$, but with $\ce{NH3(aq)}$, aluminium hydroxide is insoluble in excess while zinc hydroxide dissolves. The observed "insoluble in excess" with ammonia rules out $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$.

2. The gas turning limewater milky is carbon dioxide, showing a carbonate. The white barium precipitate insoluble in dilute acid shows a sulfate. So $\ce{Y}$ contains both carbonate and sulfate ions.

3. With excess ammonia, $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ does not form the pale blue hydroxide precipitate but instead the deep blue complex $\ce{[Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]^{2+}}$. The colour change to deep blue is the evidence for $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$.

4. 2,4-DNPH confirms a carbonyl group (aldehyde or ketone). No reaction with Fehling's reagent rules out an aldehyde, so $\ce{W}$ is a ketone. The yellow precipitate with alkaline iodine shows the $\ce{CH3CO-}$ group. So $\ce{W}$ is a methyl ketone.

5. $\ce{Cl-}$: white precipitate ($\ce{AgCl}$), soluble in dilute $\ce{NH3(aq)}$. $\ce{Br-}$: cream/off-white precipitate ($\ce{AgBr}$), partially soluble (dissolves in concentrated $\ce{NH3(aq)}$). $\ce{I-}$: pale yellow precipitate ($\ce{AgI}$), insoluble in $\ce{NH3(aq)}$.

6. Dilute nitric acid removes carbonate and hydroxide ions, which would otherwise form white precipitates with silver nitrate and mask the halide test. Nitric acid is used (not hydrochloric acid) because it does not add chloride ions.

7. Ammonia: turns damp red litmus paper blue. Carbon dioxide: turns limewater milky. Hydrogen: "pops" with a lighted splint. Oxygen: relights a glowing splint.

8. A white precipitate insoluble in excess with both $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ and $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ suggests $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$.

9. Both sulfate and sulfite give a white precipitate with barium chloride. Add dilute acid: barium sulfate does not dissolve, but barium sulfite does. Add acidified potassium manganate(VII): a sulfite decolourises it (purple to colourless), while a sulfate does not.

10. A negative observation is evidence that rules out possibilities. For example, "no precipitate with $\ce{NH3(aq)}$" rules out most metal cations and points towards ammonium, so it must be recorded rather than left blank.

11. First confirm a carbonyl with 2,4-DNPH (orange precipitate). Then distinguish aldehyde from ketone with Fehling's reagent (orange/red precipitate for an aldehyde). Then test for the $\ce{CH3CO-}$ group with alkaline aqueous iodine (yellow precipitate of tri-iodomethane).

12. No precipitate with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ rules out most metal cations. A gas that turns damp red litmus blue is ammonia, released when an ammonium salt is warmed with alkali:

   $$\ce{NH4+ + OH- -> NH3 ^ + H2O}$$

   The cation is $\ce{NH4+}$.
