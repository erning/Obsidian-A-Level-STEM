# Qualitative Analysis - Worked Examples

These examples show how to move from observations to conclusions using the Qualitative analysis notes. They follow [[40 Chemistry/02 Practical Skills/Qualitative Analysis/10 Lecture Notes|the lecture notes]]. The full cation, anion, gas and element tables are provided in the exam, so these examples focus on reasoning, not memorising.

## Example 1: Identifying a cation

**Observations.** Solution $\ce{X}$ gives a white precipitate with aqueous sodium hydroxide, soluble in excess $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$. With aqueous ammonia, $\ce{X}$ gives a white precipitate insoluble in excess.

**Step 1: shortlist.** A white precipitate with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ suggests $\ce{Al^{3+}}$, $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$, $\ce{Ca^{2+}}$ or $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$.

**Step 2: use solubility in excess $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$.** Solubility in excess removes $\ce{Ca^{2+}}$ and $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ (their hydroxides are insoluble in excess). The remaining candidates are $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ and $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$.

**Step 3: use the $\ce{NH3(aq)}$ result.** With ammonia, $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ gives a white precipitate insoluble in excess, while $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ gives a white precipitate soluble in excess. The observation "insoluble in excess" matches $\ce{Al^{3+}}$.

**Conclusion.** $\ce{X}$ contains aluminium ions, $\ce{Al^{3+}(aq)}$.

**Check.** Every observation is consistent with $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ and rules out the alternatives. No step is skipped.

## Example 2: Identifying an anion

**Observations.** Solid $\ce{Y}$ effervesces with dilute nitric acid to give a gas that turns limewater milky. A separate solution of $\ce{Y}$ gives a white precipitate with barium chloride that does not dissolve in dilute acid.

**Step 1: the gas.** A gas turning limewater milky is carbon dioxide. Effervescence with acid and $\ce{CO2}$ suggests a carbonate.

**Step 2: the barium test.** A white precipitate with $\ce{Ba^{2+}}$ that is insoluble in dilute acid is the test for a sulfate.

**Step 3: reconcile.** The two pieces of evidence point to two different anions. The sample is therefore a mixture or a compound containing both, most plausibly a solid containing both carbonate and sulfate ions. Record both conclusions, because both observations are real and neither should be ignored.

**Note.** If the barium precipitate had dissolved in dilute acid, a sulfite would be likely, because barium sulfite is soluble in acid while barium sulfate is not.

## Example 3: Identifying an ammonium salt

**Observations.** Solid $\ce{Z}$ gives no precipitate with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$, but on warming a gas is produced that turns damp red litmus paper blue.

**Reasoning.** No precipitate rules out most metal cations. A gas that turns damp red litmus blue is ammonia. Ammonia is released when an ammonium salt is warmed with alkali:

$$
\ce{NH4+ + OH- -> NH3 ^ + H2O}
$$

**Conclusion.** $\ce{Z}$ is an ammonium salt. To name it fully, the anion would need a separate test on a fresh sample.

## Example 4: Halide ions in mixture

**Observations.** Acidified silver nitrate added to a solution gives a cream precipitate. The precipitate is only partially soluble in concentrated aqueous ammonia.

**Reasoning.** A white precipitate soluble in ammonia indicates chloride; a pale yellow precipitate insoluble in ammonia indicates iodide; a cream precipitate partially soluble indicates bromide.

**Conclusion.** The solution contains bromide ions, $\ce{Br-(aq)}$.

**Check.** If the sample were a mixture of chloride and iodide, the colour would be a misleading blend. Acidifying with dilute nitric acid before the test removes carbonate and hydroxide, which would otherwise interfere.

## Example 5: Organic functional group from tests

**Observations.** Compound $\ce{W}$ forms an orange precipitate with 2,4-DNPH. With Fehling's reagent there is no red precipitate. With alkaline aqueous iodine a yellow precipitate forms.

**Step 1: the 2,4-DNPH test.** An orange precipitate with 2,4-DNPH confirms a carbonyl group, so $\ce{W}$ is an aldehyde or a ketone.

**Step 2: Fehling's reagent.** No red precipitate means $\ce{W}$ is not an aldehyde. It is therefore a ketone.

**Step 3: the tri-iodomethane test.** A yellow precipitate with alkaline aqueous iodine shows the $\ce{CH3CO-}$ group. So $\ce{W}$ is a methyl ketone, that is, a ketone containing the $\ce{CH3CO-}$ group such as propanone or butanone.

**Conclusion.** $\ce{W}$ is a methyl ketone. The three tests are used in order: confirm a carbonyl, then distinguish aldehyde from ketone, then test for the methyl group next to the carbonyl.

## Example 6: Recording a negative observation

**Observations.** A pale blue solution gives no precipitate with aqueous ammonia; instead the solution darkens to deep blue.

**Reasoning.** The expected pale blue precipitate of copper(II) hydroxide does not form because excess ammonia converts the copper(II) ions directly to the deep blue complex $\ce{[Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]^{2+}}$. The colour change to deep blue is itself the evidence for $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$.

**Conclusion.** The solution contains copper(II) ions. Recording "no precipitate, solution turns deep blue" is essential; writing only "no precipitate" would wrongly rule out copper.

## Quick Checks for an Observational Problem

- Is every observation written down, including "no change"?
- Is solubility in excess tested and recorded whenever a precipitate forms?
- Does each conclusion follow from observations rather than preceding them?
- Are gases confirmed by their specific test, not just by effervescence?
- When two tests seem to conflict, are both recorded and both conclusions kept?
