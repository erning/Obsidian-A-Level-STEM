# 32 Hydroxy Compounds - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Ethyl Ethanoate from an Acyl Chloride

Write an equation for forming ethyl ethanoate from ethanol and an acyl chloride.

Use ethanoyl chloride and ethanol:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + CH3CH2OH -> CH3COOCH2CH3 + HCl}
$$

Check: acyl chloride reactions form $\ce{HCl}$ as a by-product, not water.

## Example 2: Preparing Phenol from Phenylamine

State a two-stage route from phenylamine to phenol.

First, react phenylamine with nitrous acid below $10\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ to form a diazonium salt. Nitrous acid can be generated from $\ce{NaNO2}$ and dilute acid.

$$
\ce{C6H5NH2 ->[HNO2,\ below\ 10 ^\circ C] C6H5N2+Cl-}
$$

Then warm the diazonium salt with water:

$$
\ce{C6H5N2+Cl- + H2O ->[warm] C6H5OH + N2 + HCl}
$$

Check: low temperature is needed for the diazonium salt stage.

## Example 3: Explaining Phenol Acidity

Explain why phenol is more acidic than ethanol.

Phenol loses $\ce{H+}$ to form the phenoxide ion, $\ce{C6H5O-}$. The negative charge is stabilised by delocalisation into the aromatic ring.

Ethanol forms ethoxide, $\ce{CH3CH2O-}$. This ion has no aromatic delocalisation, and the ethyl group donates electron density, making the negative ion less stable.

Therefore phenol is more acidic than ethanol.

Check: stronger acid means the conjugate base is more stable.

## Example 4: Bromination of Phenol

Predict the product when phenol reacts with bromine water at room temperature.

The $\ce{-OH}$ group activates the ring and directs to the 2-, 4- and 6-positions. Phenol reacts readily with bromine water to form 2,4,6-tribromophenol.

$$
\ce{C6H5OH + 3Br2 -> C6H2Br3OH + 3HBr}
$$

Check: no $\ce{AlBr3}$ catalyst is needed, unlike benzene bromination.

## Example 5: Nitration Conditions

Compare nitration of benzene and phenol.

Benzene requires concentrated $\ce{HNO3}$ and concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$ at $25$ to $60\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Phenol reacts with dilute $\ce{HNO3(aq)}$ at room temperature to give a mixture of 2-nitrophenol and 4-nitrophenol.

Reason: the $\ce{-OH}$ group donates electron density into the aromatic ring, making phenol more reactive towards electrophilic substitution and directing substitution to 2- and 4-positions.

Check: different conditions are part of the syllabus point, not a minor detail.

## Example 6: Azo Coupling

Identify the key functional group formed when benzenediazonium chloride couples with phenol in alkaline solution.

The product is an azo compound. The key group is the azo group:

$$
\ce{-N=N-}
$$

Check: azo compounds are often used as dyes, and this route connects phenol chemistry to diazonium salt chemistry.
