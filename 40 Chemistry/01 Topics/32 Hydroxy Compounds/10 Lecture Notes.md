# 32 Hydroxy Compounds - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 A Level subject content for Topic 32: 32.1 Alcohols and 32.2 Phenol. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## AS Link

AS hydroxy compounds introduced alcohols: combustion, substitution, dehydration, esterification and oxidation. A Level keeps alcohol chemistry but adds faster ester formation with acyl chlorides. It also introduces phenol, where the $\ce{-OH}$ group is attached directly to an aromatic ring. Phenol is not just "an alcohol with a benzene ring"; the aromatic system changes acidity and ring reactivity.

## Alcohols and Acyl Chlorides

Alcohols react with acyl chlorides at room temperature to form esters and hydrogen chloride. This is faster and more vigorous than acid-catalysed esterification with carboxylic acids.

For ethanol and ethanoyl chloride:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + CH3CH2OH -> CH3COOCH2CH3 + HCl}
$$

The product is ethyl ethanoate. The mechanism is addition-elimination at the acyl chloride carbonyl carbon, linking this topic to Topic 33.

## Producing Phenol from Phenylamine

Phenol can be produced from phenylamine through a diazonium salt.

First, phenylamine reacts with nitrous acid below $10\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Nitrous acid is commonly generated from $\ce{NaNO2}$ and dilute acid.

$$
\ce{C6H5NH2 ->[HNO2,\ below\ 10 ^\circ C] C6H5N2+Cl-}
$$

Then warming the diazonium salt with water gives phenol:

$$
\ce{C6H5N2+Cl- + H2O ->[warm] C6H5OH + N2 + HCl}
$$

The low temperature is important for forming and keeping the diazonium salt long enough to use it.

## Phenol as an Acid

Phenol is a weak acid. It reacts with aqueous sodium hydroxide to form sodium phenoxide:

$$
\ce{C6H5OH + NaOH -> C6H5O-Na+ + H2O}
$$

It also reacts with sodium metal to form sodium phenoxide and hydrogen:

$$
\ce{2C6H5OH + 2Na -> 2C6H5O-Na+ + H2}
$$

Phenol is more acidic than ethanol because the phenoxide ion, $\ce{C6H5O-}$, is stabilised by delocalisation of the negative charge into the aromatic ring. Ethoxide, $\ce{CH3CH2O-}$, lacks this stabilisation and is destabilised by the electron-donating ethyl group.

The relative acidity is:

phenol > water > ethanol

## Phenol and Electrophilic Substitution

The $\ce{-OH}$ group donates electron density into the aromatic ring and directs electrophilic substitution to the 2-, 4- and 6-positions. This makes phenol much more reactive than benzene towards nitration and bromination.

Phenol reacts with dilute aqueous nitric acid at room temperature to give a mixture of 2-nitrophenol and 4-nitrophenol:

$$
\ce{C6H5OH + HNO3 -> 2-HOC6H4NO2 + 4-HOC6H4NO2 + H2O}
$$

Phenol reacts with bromine water at room temperature to form 2,4,6-tribromophenol:

$$
\ce{C6H5OH + 3Br2 -> C6H2Br3OH + 3HBr}
$$

This reaction decolourises bromine water and gives a white precipitate of 2,4,6-tribromophenol.

## Phenol and Azo Coupling

In alkaline solution, phenol reacts with diazonium salts to form azo compounds. The important structural feature in an azo compound is the azo group, $\ce{-N=N-}$.

For example, benzenediazonium chloride couples with phenol in $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ to give an azo dye. This connects Topic 32 to Topic 34 Nitrogen Compounds.

## Applying Phenol Chemistry to Other Phenolic Compounds

Other phenolic compounds, such as naphthol, contain $\ce{-OH}$ directly attached to an aromatic system. They can show similar acidity and similar activating and directing effects in electrophilic substitution.

The exact product positions depend on the aromatic system, but the reasoning starts from the same idea: the phenolic $\ce{-OH}$ group interacts with the aromatic $\pi$ system.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating phenol as a normal alcohol in every reaction.
- Saying phenol is acidic without explaining phenoxide ion stabilisation.
- Using concentrated acid nitration conditions for phenol when dilute $\ce{HNO3(aq)}$ at room temperature is in the syllabus.
- Forgetting that phenol reacts with bromine water without the Lewis acid catalyst needed for benzene.
- Missing the 2-, 4- and 6-directing effect of the phenolic $\ce{-OH}$ group.
