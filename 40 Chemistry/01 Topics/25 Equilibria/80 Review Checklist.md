# 25 Equilibria - Review Checklist

## Recall

- Define conjugate acid and conjugate base.
- Define pH, $K_a$, p$K_a$, $K_w$, $K_{sp}$, and $K_{pc}$.
- State that $K_b$ and $K_w = K_a \times K_b$ are not tested in this syllabus.
- Define a buffer solution and describe how one can be made.
- State what the common ion effect means.

## Perform

- Identify conjugate acid-base pairs in equations.
- Calculate pH for strong acids and strong alkalis.
- Calculate pH for weak acids using $K_a$.
- Calculate buffer pH from weak acid and conjugate base amounts.
- Explain buffer action with equations for added acid and added alkali.
- Write $K_{sp}$ expressions from balanced solubility equilibria.
- Calculate molar solubility from $K_{sp}$ and vice versa.
- Calculate ion concentration in the presence of a common ion.
- Use a partition coefficient to find the distribution of solute between two solvents.

## Explain

- Strong means fully dissociated, not necessarily concentrated.
- Weak acids establish equilibria, so $K_a$ is used.
- Buffers resist pH change because both weak acid and conjugate base are present.
- Common ions reduce solubility by shifting the dissolving equilibrium left.
- Partition coefficients depend on the polarities of the solute and solvents.

## Avoid

- Do not use initial buffer concentrations before accounting for neutralisation.
- Do not include solids in $K_{sp}$ expressions.
- Do not forget powers in $K_{sp}$, such as $[\ce{OH-}]^2$ for $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$.
- Do not reverse the numerator and denominator of $K_{pc}$.
- Do not treat a partition coefficient as valid if the solute changes physical state or reacts in one solvent.

## Ready To Move On

You are ready when you can choose the right equilibrium model from the wording of the question, write the expression without help, and explain each numerical answer in terms of acid-base behaviour, solubility, common ions, or solvent polarity.
