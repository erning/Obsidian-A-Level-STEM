# 19 Nitrogen Compounds - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow the CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level subject content for topic 19, **Nitrogen compounds**:

- 19.1 Primary amines
- 19.2 Nitriles and hydroxynitriles

The syllabus states that classification of amines will not be tested at AS Level. The required focus is production of primary amines, production of nitriles and hydroxynitriles, and hydrolysis of nitriles to carboxylic acids.

## Learning Scope

By the end of this topic you should be able to:

- recall production of primary amines from halogenoalkanes and ammonia in ethanol heated under pressure;
- recall production of nitriles from halogenoalkanes and potassium cyanide in ethanol with heat;
- recall production of hydroxynitriles from aldehydes and ketones using hydrogen cyanide with potassium cyanide as catalyst and heat;
- describe hydrolysis of nitriles using dilute acid, or dilute alkali followed by acidification, to form carboxylic acids;
- use nitrogen-containing intermediates in short AS synthesis routes.

## The Three AS Nitrogen Families

| Family | Functional group | Typical AS route |
|---|---|---|
| primary amine | $\ce{-NH2}$ | halogenoalkane + $\ce{NH3}$ |
| nitrile | $\ce{-C#N}$ | halogenoalkane + $\ce{KCN}$ |
| hydroxynitrile | $\ce{-OH}$ and $\ce{-C#N}$ on the addition product | aldehyde or ketone + $\ce{HCN}$ |

In structural formulae, the nitrile carbon is part of the carbon chain. For example, $\ce{CH3CH2CN}$ has three carbon atoms and is propanenitrile.

## Primary Amines from Halogenoalkanes

A halogenoalkane reacts with ammonia in ethanol, heated under pressure, to form a primary amine.

For bromoethane:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br + 2NH3 -> CH3CH2NH2 + NH4Br}
$$

This is a nucleophilic substitution reaction. The ammonia molecule attacks the carbon bonded to the halogen, and the carbon-halogen bond is broken.

Why pressure is named: ammonia is volatile, so pressure helps maintain ammonia in the reacting mixture when heated.

The product is a primary amine because the nitrogen is bonded to one carbon group. You do not need to classify amines further at AS Level, but you should recognise $\ce{-NH2}$ in the product.

## Nitriles from Halogenoalkanes

A halogenoalkane reacts with potassium cyanide in ethanol and heat to form a nitrile.

For 1-bromopropane:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2CH2Br + KCN -> CH3CH2CH2CN + KBr}
$$

This is also nucleophilic substitution. The cyanide ion, $\ce{CN-}$, provides a carbon nucleophile. Because the carbon of $\ce{CN-}$ becomes part of the organic molecule, this reaction increases the carbon chain length by one.

This is one of the most useful AS synthesis moves:

$$
\ce{R-X ->[KCN,\ ethanol,\ heat] R-CN ->[hydrolysis] R-COOH}
$$

## Hydroxynitriles from Aldehydes and Ketones

Aldehydes and ketones react with hydrogen cyanide, $\ce{HCN}$, using $\ce{KCN}$ as catalyst and heat, to form hydroxynitriles.

For ethanal:

$$
\ce{CH3CHO + HCN -> CH3CH(OH)CN}
$$

For propanone:

$$
\ce{CH3COCH3 + HCN -> (CH3)2C(OH)CN}
$$

This reaction is nucleophilic addition to a carbonyl compound. The cyanide ion attacks the electron-deficient carbonyl carbon, and proton transfer gives the alcohol group. The product contains both a hydroxy group and a nitrile group.

Hydroxynitrile formation also increases the carbon count by one, because the $\ce{CN}$ carbon is added to the carbonyl compound.

## Hydrolysis of Nitriles

Nitriles hydrolyse to carboxylic acids. The syllabus gives two possible sets of conditions:

- dilute acid;
- dilute alkali followed by acidification.

For propanenitrile:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2CN + 2H2O + H+ -> CH3CH2COOH + NH4+}
$$

Under alkaline conditions, the immediate organic product is a carboxylate ion or salt. Acidification is needed to produce the carboxylic acid:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2COO- + H+ -> CH3CH2COOH}
$$

## Synthesis Patterns

### Making a Primary Amine

To replace a halogen atom with $\ce{-NH2}$:

$$
\ce{R-X ->[NH3,\ ethanol,\ heat,\ pressure] R-NH2}
$$

### Making a Longer Carboxylic Acid

To increase the chain length by one and end at a carboxylic acid:

$$
\ce{R-X ->[KCN,\ ethanol,\ heat] R-CN ->[hydrolysis] R-COOH}
$$

For example:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br ->[KCN,\ ethanol,\ heat] CH3CH2CN ->[dilute\ acid,\ heat] CH3CH2COOH}
$$

### Making a Hydroxynitrile

To add $\ce{HCN}$ across a carbonyl group:

$$
\ce{RCHO ->[HCN,\ KCN,\ heat] RCH(OH)CN}
$$

$$
\ce{R2CO ->[HCN,\ KCN,\ heat] R2C(OH)CN}
$$

## Analysis Links

Nitrogen compounds can be recognised using structure and spectroscopy evidence:

| Evidence | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| $\ce{C#N}$ absorption around $2200-2250\ \mathrm{cm^{-1}}$ | nitrile group present |
| $\ce{N-H}$ absorption around $3300-3500\ \mathrm{cm^{-1}}$ | amine or amide group possible; in this AS topic, primary amines are relevant |
| $\ce{O-H}$ absorption around $3200-3600\ \mathrm{cm^{-1}}$ plus nitrile evidence | hydroxynitrile possible |
| Product has one more carbon atom after reaction with $\ce{KCN}$ or $\ce{HCN}$ | cyanide has introduced a carbon atom |

Spectral evidence is strongest when it fits the reaction route and the molecular formula. A $\ce{C#N}$ absorption alone suggests a nitrile group, but the structure still has to match carbon count and other data.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting that $\ce{KCN}$ substitution increases the carbon chain length by one.
- Counting the nitrile carbon incorrectly when naming nitriles.
- Writing aqueous ammonia instead of ammonia in ethanol heated under pressure for primary amine formation.
- Forgetting acidification after alkaline nitrile hydrolysis.
- Confusing nitriles, $\ce{R-C#N}$, with amines, $\ce{R-NH2}$.
- Treating amine classification as an AS requirement.
