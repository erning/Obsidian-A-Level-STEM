# 30 Hydrocarbons - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 A Level subject content for Topic 30.1 Arenes. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## AS Link

AS hydrocarbons focused on alkanes and alkenes. Alkanes react by free-radical substitution under ultraviolet light, while alkenes react by electrophilic addition because the $\ce{C=C}$ bond has a localised electron-rich $\pi$ bond. A Level hydrocarbons focuses on arenes, especially benzene and methylbenzene. Arenes contain a delocalised aromatic $\pi$ system, so they usually react by electrophilic substitution rather than addition.

## Why Arenes Prefer Substitution

Benzene is planar, with each carbon atom $sp^2$ hybridised. The six p orbitals overlap to form a delocalised $\pi$ system above and below the ring. This delocalisation gives aromatic stabilisation.

If benzene underwent addition like an alkene, the delocalised system would be disrupted. In electrophilic substitution, the ring first attacks an electrophile, then loses $\ce{H+}$ so aromaticity is restored. This preserves the stabilised aromatic system.

## Halogenation of the Ring

Benzene and methylbenzene react with chlorine or bromine in the presence of a Lewis acid catalyst, such as $\ce{AlCl3}$ or $\ce{AlBr3}$, to form halogenoarenes.

For bromination of benzene:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + Br2 ->[AlBr3] C6H5Br + HBr}
$$

The catalyst polarises the halogen molecule and helps generate an electrophile. The mechanism is electrophilic substitution:

1. The aromatic $\pi$ system attacks the electrophile, forming a positively charged intermediate.
2. A proton is removed.
3. The aromatic $\pi$ system is restored.

Methylbenzene gives mainly substitution at the 2- and 4-positions because the methyl group directs incoming electrophiles to those positions.

## Nitration

Benzene and methylbenzene are nitrated using a mixture of concentrated $\ce{HNO3}$ and concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$ at $25$ to $60\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$.

For benzene:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + HNO3 ->[conc. H2SO4][25-60 ^\circ C] C6H5NO2 + H2O}
$$

The electrophile is the nitronium ion, $\ce{NO2+}$, generated by the acid mixture:

$$
\ce{HNO3 + 2H2SO4 -> NO2+ + H3O+ + 2HSO4-}
$$

The benzene ring undergoes electrophilic substitution to form nitrobenzene.

## Friedel-Crafts Reactions

Friedel-Crafts reactions introduce carbon-containing groups onto an aromatic ring.

**Alkylation** uses $\ce{CH3Cl}$, $\ce{AlCl3}$ and heat:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + CH3Cl ->[AlCl3][heat] C6H5CH3 + HCl}
$$

**Acylation** uses $\ce{CH3COCl}$, $\ce{AlCl3}$ and heat:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + CH3COCl ->[AlCl3][heat] C6H5COCH3 + HCl}
$$

Both are electrophilic substitution reactions. Alkylation introduces an alkyl group; acylation introduces an acyl group, $\ce{-COR}$.

## Side-Chain Oxidation

An alkylbenzene side-chain can be completely oxidised to a carboxyl group using hot alkaline $\ce{KMnO4}$ followed by dilute acid. Methylbenzene gives benzoic acid:

$$
\ce{C6H5CH3 ->[hot alkaline KMnO4][then dilute acid] C6H5COOH}
$$

The carbon attached to the ring becomes the carbon of the $\ce{-COOH}$ group. This reaction is important because it connects arenes to carboxylic acids.

## Hydrogenation of the Ring

The benzene ring can be hydrogenated with $\ce{H2}$ and a Pt or Ni catalyst with heat to form a cyclohexane ring:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + 3H2 ->[Pt/Ni][heat] C6H12}
$$

This is not the usual mild reaction of an alkene. The harsher conditions reflect the stability of the aromatic ring.

## Ring or Side-Chain Halogenation

Reaction conditions decide whether halogenation occurs in the ring or in the side-chain.

| Conditions | Main process | Example outcome |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{Cl2}$ or $\ce{Br2}$ with $\ce{AlCl3}$ or $\ce{AlBr3}$ | electrophilic substitution in the aromatic ring | methylbenzene gives mainly 2- and 4-halogenated methylbenzene |
| $\ce{Cl2}$ or $\ce{Br2}$ with ultraviolet light | free-radical substitution in the side-chain | methylbenzene gives $\ce{C6H5CH2Cl}$ with chlorine |

This links directly back to AS alkane chemistry: side-chain halogenation behaves like free-radical substitution of an alkyl group.

## Directing Effects

In electrophilic substitution, a substituent already on the ring influences where the next substituent enters. CAIE limits this to:

- 2- and 4-directing groups: $\ce{-NH2}$, $\ce{-OH}$ and $\ce{-R}$
- 3-directing groups: $\ce{-NO2}$, $\ce{-COOH}$ and $\ce{-COR}$

For example, methylbenzene tends to form 2- and 4-nitromethylbenzene on nitration. Benzoic acid tends to form 3-nitrobenzoic acid.

## Working Routine

- Decide from the conditions whether the reaction happens in the aromatic ring or in the side-chain.
- For electrophilic substitution, draw the electrophile attacking the ring and a proton then leaving to restore aromaticity.
- Use the directing effect of the group already on the ring to predict the position of substitution.
## Common Mistakes

- Using alkene electrophilic addition mechanisms for benzene.
- Forgetting the Lewis acid catalyst for ring halogenation.
- Confusing ring halogenation with side-chain halogenation.
- Omitting the temperature range for nitration.
- Predicting a directing effect without identifying the group already on the ring.
