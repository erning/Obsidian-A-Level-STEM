# 30 Hydrocarbons

## Core Idea

A Level hydrocarbons focuses on arenes. The topic explains why benzene and methylbenzene favour electrophilic substitution, how reaction conditions control products, and how substituents direct further reactions on the ring.

## Source Alignment

- Primary syllabus source: CAIE Chemistry 9701, A Level subject content.
- 30 Hydrocarbons
- 30.1 Arenes

## What to Learn

- Describe reactions of benzene and methylbenzene, including halogenation, nitration, Friedel-Crafts reactions, side-chain oxidation, and hydrogenation.
- Explain electrophilic substitution in arenes using delocalisation and aromatic stabilisation.
- Predict whether halogenation occurs in the side-chain or aromatic ring from reaction conditions.
- Use directing effects of common substituents in electrophilic substitution.

## How to Study

- Start from benzene bonding: delocalisation explains the preference for substitution over addition.
- Learn reagents and conditions together; arene chemistry is condition-sensitive.
- Separate reactions of the ring from reactions of a side-chain.
- Use directing effects to predict positions before writing products.

## Practice Directions

- Draw mechanisms for nitration and bromination of benzene.
- Predict products for benzene and methylbenzene under named conditions.
- Choose reagents for introducing alkyl, acyl, nitro, or halogen substituents onto an aromatic ring.
- Explain why an arene reaction differs from an alkene addition reaction.

## Common Traps

- Expecting benzene to undergo addition as readily as alkenes.
- Mixing up ring halogenation and side-chain halogenation conditions.
- Forgetting the Lewis acid catalyst in arene halogenation or Friedel-Crafts reactions.
- Applying directing effects without first identifying the substituent already on the ring.
