# Atomic Structure - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Counting Particles in an Ion

Deduce the number of protons, neutrons and electrons in $^{56}_{26}\ce{Fe^{3+}}$.

**Working**

The proton number is $26$, so there are $26$ protons.

The mass number is $56$, so:

$$
\text{neutrons} = 56 - 26 = 30
$$

A $3+$ ion has lost three electrons compared with the neutral atom:

$$
\text{electrons} = 26 - 3 = 23
$$

**Answer:** $26$ protons, $30$ neutrons and $23$ electrons.

**Check:** The ion has three more protons than electrons, so the charge is $3+$.

## Example 2: Deflection in an Electric Field

Three beams, each travelling at the same velocity, contain protons, neutrons and electrons. Predict their behaviour in an electric field.

**Working**

Protons are positively charged, so they are attracted to the negative plate. Electrons are negatively charged, so they are attracted to the positive plate. Neutrons have no charge, so they are not deflected.

The electron beam is deflected more strongly than the proton beam because the electron has a much smaller mass but the same magnitude of charge.

**Answer:** proton beam towards the negative plate; electron beam towards the positive plate with greater deflection; neutron beam undeflected.

## Example 3: Explaining Isotope Properties

$^{35}_{17}\ce{Cl}$ and $^{37}_{17}\ce{Cl}$ are isotopes. Explain why they have the same chemical properties but different masses.

**Working**

Both atoms have $17$ protons, so they are atoms of chlorine. A neutral atom of each isotope has $17$ electrons with the same electronic arrangement. Chemical properties depend on electron arrangement, especially outer-shell electrons, so the chemical properties are the same.

Their neutron numbers differ:

$$
^{35}\ce{Cl}: 35 - 17 = 18
$$

$$
^{37}\ce{Cl}: 37 - 17 = 20
$$

The isotope with more neutrons has a greater mass.

**Answer:** same chemical properties because the electron arrangement is the same; different masses because the numbers of neutrons are different.

## Example 4: Writing Electronic Configurations

Write the full electronic configuration of sulfur and the configuration of $\ce{S^{2-}}$.

**Working**

Sulfur has proton number $16$, so a neutral sulfur atom has $16$ electrons.

Fill the sub-shells:

$$
\ce{S}: 1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^4
$$

The sulfide ion has gained two electrons, so it has $18$ electrons:

$$
\ce{S^{2-}}: 1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^6
$$

**Answer:** $\ce{S}$ is $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^4$; $\ce{S^{2-}}$ is $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^6$.

**Check:** $\ce{S^{2-}}$ has the same configuration as $\ce{Ar}$.

## Example 5: Transition Metal Ion Configuration

Write the shorthand electronic configuration of $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$.

**Working**

Neutral iron has $26$ electrons:

$$
\ce{Fe}: [\ce{Ar}] 3d^6 4s^2
$$

When iron forms ions, electrons are removed from $4s$ before $3d$. Removing two electrons gives:

$$
\ce{Fe^{2+}}: [\ce{Ar}] 3d^6
$$

**Answer:** $[\ce{Ar}] 3d^6$.

**Check:** The ion has $24$ electrons: $18$ from $[\ce{Ar}]$ plus $6$ in $3d$.

## Example 6: Explaining an Atomic Radius Trend

Explain why atomic radius decreases from sodium to chlorine across Period 3.

**Working**

From sodium to chlorine, proton number increases. Electrons are added to the same main shell, so shielding increases only slightly. The stronger nuclear charge attracts the outer electrons more strongly.

**Answer:** atomic radius decreases because increasing nuclear charge pulls the outer shell closer while shielding does not increase enough to cancel this effect.

## Example 7: Deducing Group from Successive Ionisation Energies

An element has the following successive ionisation energies in arbitrary units:

| IE | 1st | 2nd | 3rd | 4th | 5th |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| value | 590 | 1150 | 4940 | 6480 | 8120 |

Deduce the group of the element.

**Working**

The very large jump is between the second and third ionisation energies:

$$
\frac{4940}{1150} \text{ is much larger than the earlier change}
$$

This means two electrons are removed relatively easily, then the third electron is from an inner shell.

**Answer:** the element has two outer-shell electrons and is in Group 2.

**Check:** This pattern is consistent with an atom such as magnesium.

## Example 8: Explaining an Ionisation Energy Dip

Explain why the first ionisation energy of sulfur is lower than that of phosphorus.

**Working**

Phosphorus has a $3p^3$ outer sub-shell, with three singly occupied $p$ orbitals. Sulfur has $3p^4$, so one $3p$ orbital contains a pair of electrons. The paired electrons repel each other.

This spin-pair repulsion makes it easier to remove one electron from sulfur.

**Answer:** sulfur has a paired $3p$ electron, so spin-pair repulsion lowers the energy needed to remove one electron compared with phosphorus.
