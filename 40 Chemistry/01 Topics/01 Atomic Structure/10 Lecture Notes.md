# Atomic Structure

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 Syllabus 2025-2027, AS Level subject content, Physical chemistry, Topic 1 Atomic structure:

- 1.1 Particles in the atom and atomic radius
- 1.2 Isotopes
- 1.3 Electrons, energy levels and atomic orbitals
- 1.4 Ionisation energy

For electronic configuration and ionisation energy, the syllabus limits assessed atoms and ions to ground-state species from hydrogen to krypton.

## Atoms, Particles and Charge

An atom is mostly empty space. Almost all its mass is concentrated in a very small, dense nucleus containing protons and neutrons. Electrons occupy shells in the space around the nucleus.

| Particle | Relative charge | Relative mass | Position |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| proton | $+1$ | $1$ | nucleus |
| neutron | $0$ | $1$ | nucleus |
| electron | $-1$ | about $\frac{1}{1836}$ | shells around nucleus |

The mass of an atom comes mainly from protons and neutrons. The charge of an atom or ion depends on the balance between protons and electrons.

- **Atomic number** or **proton number**, $Z$, is the number of protons.
- **Mass number** or **nucleon number**, $A$, is the number of protons plus neutrons.
- Number of neutrons $= A - Z$.
- In a neutral atom, number of electrons $= Z$.
- In a cation, electrons have been lost.
- In an anion, electrons have been gained.

For example, $^{24}_{12}\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ has $12$ protons, $12$ neutrons and $10$ electrons.

## Beams in an Electric Field

When beams of protons, neutrons and electrons move at the same velocity through an electric field:

- protons are deflected towards the negative plate because they are positively charged
- electrons are deflected towards the positive plate because they are negatively charged
- neutrons are not deflected because they are neutral
- electrons are deflected much more than protons because an electron has a much smaller mass

This is a useful way to link charge and mass. Direction shows the sign of charge; amount of deflection depends on charge-to-mass ratio.

## Atomic Radius and Ionic Radius

Atomic radius is a measure of atomic size. It is not the distance to a hard edge, because electron clouds do not have sharp boundaries.

Across a period, atomic radius generally decreases. Proton number increases, so nuclear charge increases. Electrons are added to the same main shell, so shielding does not increase enough to cancel the stronger attraction. The outer electrons are pulled closer to the nucleus.

Down a group, atomic radius increases. Each step down adds another occupied shell. The outer electrons are further from the nucleus and are more shielded by inner shells.

Ionic radius follows the same ideas, but charge matters strongly:

- cations are smaller than their parent atoms because electrons have been removed and the remaining electrons are attracted more strongly
- anions are larger than their parent atoms because extra electrons increase electron-electron repulsion
- in an isoelectronic series, the ion with more protons has the smaller radius

For example, $\ce{Na+}$, $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ and $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ each have $10$ electrons, but $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ has the greatest nuclear charge and the smallest radius.

## Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Isotopic notation shows mass number at the top left and proton number at the bottom left, for example $^{35}_{17}\ce{Cl}$ and $^{37}_{17}\ce{Cl}$.

Isotopes of the same element have the same chemical properties because they have the same number and arrangement of electrons. Chemical reactions involve electrons, especially outer-shell electrons, rather than the number of neutrons in the nucleus.

Isotopes of the same element can have different physical properties, limited here to mass and density. A heavier isotope has a greater mass per atom, so samples enriched in that isotope can differ in density.

## Shells, Sub-Shells and Orbitals

Electrons in atoms occupy energy levels.

- A **shell** is a main energy level. Its principal quantum number is $n$.
- A **sub-shell** is a region within a shell, such as $s$, $p$ or $d$.
- An **orbital** is a region of space that can hold up to two electrons with opposite spins.
- The **ground state** is the lowest-energy electronic configuration of an atom or ion.

| Sub-shell | Number of orbitals | Maximum electrons |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| $s$ | 1 | 2 |
| $p$ | 3 | 6 |
| $d$ | 5 | 10 |

For the first three shells and the $4s$ and $4p$ sub-shells, the filling order used at this level is:

$$
1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p
$$

This order reflects energy. Electrons fill lower-energy orbitals first. Within orbitals of equal energy, electrons occupy separate orbitals singly before pairing, because this reduces inter-electron repulsion.

## Electronic Configurations

An electronic configuration shows how electrons are arranged. The full configuration for iron is:

$$
\ce{Fe}: 1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^6 3d^6 4s^2
$$

The shorthand configuration uses the previous noble gas:

$$
\ce{Fe}: [\ce{Ar}] 3d^6 4s^2
$$

When transition metal ions form, electrons are removed from $4s$ before $3d$. For example:

$$
\ce{Fe^{2+}}: [\ce{Ar}] 3d^6
$$

Electrons-in-boxes notation shows orbitals as boxes and electrons as arrows. For oxygen:

$$
\ce{O}: 1s^2 2s^2 2p^4
$$

The $2p$ electrons are arranged as two singly occupied $p$ orbitals and one paired $p$ orbital. This follows the idea that electrons occupy separate equal-energy orbitals before pairing.

A **free radical** is a species with one or more unpaired electrons. For example, $\ce{Cl.}$ represents a chlorine atom with an unpaired electron.

## Shapes of Atomic Orbitals

An $s$ orbital is spherical. There is one $s$ orbital in each shell.

A $p$ orbital is dumb-bell shaped. There are three $p$ orbitals in a $p$ sub-shell, arranged at right angles to one another. They are often labelled $p_x$, $p_y$ and $p_z$.

At this level, the important point is not the exact boundary of the orbital but the shape and orientation used later to explain bonding.

## First and Successive Ionisation Energies

First ionisation energy is the energy required to remove one electron from each atom in one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous $1+$ ions.

For magnesium:

$$
\ce{Mg(g) -> Mg+(g) + e-}
$$

Second ionisation energy removes one electron from each $1+$ ion:

$$
\ce{Mg+(g) -> Mg^{2+}(g) + e-}
$$

Always include gaseous state symbols when writing ionisation energy equations.

Ionisation energy is caused by attraction between the positive nucleus and the electron being removed. The main factors are:

- **nuclear charge**: more protons usually increase attraction
- **atomic or ionic radius**: greater distance weakens attraction
- **shielding by inner shells and sub-shells**: inner electrons reduce the attraction felt by outer electrons
- **spin-pair repulsion**: paired electrons in the same orbital repel each other, making one of them easier to remove

## Trends in Ionisation Energy

Down a group, first ionisation energy decreases. The outer electron is in a shell further from the nucleus and is more shielded, so it is less strongly attracted.

Across a period, first ionisation energy generally increases because nuclear charge increases while the outer electron is added to the same shell. However, there are important dips:

- from Group 2 to Group 13, the electron removed is from a higher-energy $p$ sub-shell, so it is easier to remove than an $s$ electron
- from Group 15 to Group 16, the electron removed is paired in a $p$ orbital, so spin-pair repulsion makes removal easier

Successive ionisation energies always increase for the same element because each electron is removed from an increasingly positive ion. A very large jump shows that the next electron is being removed from an inner shell. This jump can be used to deduce the number of outer-shell electrons and therefore the group of the element.

For example, if the large jump is between the second and third ionisation energies, the atom has two outer-shell electrons and is in Group 2.

## Study Routine

For particle-count questions, write $Z$, $A$, charge, then calculate protons, neutrons and electrons in that order.

For electronic configurations, count electrons first, fill in the order $1s$, $2s$, $2p$, $3s$, $3p$, $4s$, $3d$, $4p$, then adjust carefully for ionic charge.

For ionisation energy explanations, name the trend and then identify the factor that changes. Avoid saying only "it is harder to remove"; explain why the attraction is stronger or weaker.
