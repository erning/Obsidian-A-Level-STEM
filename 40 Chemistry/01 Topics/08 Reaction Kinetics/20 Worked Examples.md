# Reaction Kinetics - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Average Rate From Gas Volume

**Question.** Calcium carbonate reacts with hydrochloric acid:

$$
\ce{CaCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> CaCl2(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

The volume of $\ce{CO2}$ collected increases from $12.0\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ at $20\ \mathrm{s}$ to $36.0\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ at $80\ \mathrm{s}$. Calculate the average rate of $\ce{CO2}$ production over this interval.

**Working.**

Change in volume:

$$
\Delta V = 36.0 - 12.0 = 24.0\ \mathrm{cm^3}
$$

Change in time:

$$
\Delta t = 80 - 20 = 60\ \mathrm{s}
$$

Average rate:

$$
\text{rate} = \frac{24.0}{60} = 0.400\ \mathrm{cm^3\,s^{-1}}
$$

**Check.** The unit matches gas volume divided by time.

## Example 2: Concentration Effect

**Question.** Explain why increasing the concentration of hydrochloric acid increases the rate of reaction with magnesium:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

**Working.**

Increasing the acid concentration increases the number of $\ce{H+}$ ions per unit volume. Magnesium atoms and $\ce{H+}$ ions collide more frequently at the metal surface. Therefore, the frequency of effective collisions increases, so the reaction rate increases.

**Check.** The explanation links concentration to particles per unit volume, collision frequency and effective collisions.

## Example 3: Temperature And Boltzmann Distribution

**Question.** Explain why a small increase in temperature can cause a large increase in reaction rate.

**Working.**

At higher temperature, particles have greater kinetic energy on average. They move faster, so collision frequency increases.

More importantly, the Boltzmann distribution changes. The curve becomes lower and broader, and a much larger fraction of particles has energy greater than or equal to the activation energy, $E_A$. This greatly increases the frequency of effective collisions.

**Check.** A strong answer does not stop at "more collisions"; it explains the increased fraction of particles above $E_A$.

## Example 4: Activation Energy From A Pathway Diagram

**Question.** A reaction pathway diagram shows reactants at $50\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$, products at $20\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$, and the uncatalysed peak at $125\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$. With a catalyst, the peak is $85\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$. Calculate $\Delta H$, uncatalysed $E_A$ and catalysed $E_A$.

**Working.**

Enthalpy change:

$$
\Delta H = 20 - 50 = -30\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

Uncatalysed activation energy:

$$
E_A = 125 - 50 = 75\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

Catalysed activation energy:

$$
E_A = 85 - 50 = 35\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

**Check.** The catalyst lowers $E_A$ but $\Delta H$ remains $-30\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$.

## Example 5: Time To A Fixed Observation

**Question.** In a sodium thiosulfate and hydrochloric acid experiment, the time for a cross to disappear is $48\ \mathrm{s}$ in experiment A and $24\ \mathrm{s}$ in experiment B. Compare the relative rates using $1/t$.

The reaction forms sulfur:

$$
\ce{S2O3^{2-}(aq) + 2H+(aq) -> S(s) + SO2(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

**Working.**

Relative rate for A:

$$
\frac{1}{48} = 0.0208\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}
$$

Relative rate for B:

$$
\frac{1}{24} = 0.0417\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}
$$

Experiment B is twice as fast as experiment A.

**Check.** Shorter time to the same observation means faster rate, not slower rate.

## Example 6: Homogeneous Or Heterogeneous Catalyst

**Question.** Classify each catalyst as homogeneous or heterogeneous.

1. A solid iron catalyst used with gaseous nitrogen and hydrogen in the Haber process.
2. An aqueous acid catalyst used in an aqueous ester hydrolysis reaction.

**Working.**

In the Haber process, the catalyst is solid and the reactants are gases. The catalyst is in a different physical state, so it is heterogeneous.

In aqueous ester hydrolysis, the acid catalyst and reaction mixture are in the same aqueous phase. The catalyst is homogeneous.

**Check.** The classification depends on physical state relative to the reactants, not on whether the catalyst is industrial or laboratory-based.
