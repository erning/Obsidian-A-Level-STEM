# Reaction Kinetics - Review Checklist

## Recall

- Rate of reaction is change per unit time.
- Effective collisions lead to reaction; non-effective collisions do not.
- Higher concentration increases particles per unit volume and collision frequency.
- Higher gas pressure increases collision frequency for gaseous reactants.
- Activation energy, $E_A$, is the minimum energy required for a collision to be effective.
- Higher temperature increases the fraction of particles with energy at least $E_A$.
- A catalyst provides a different mechanism with lower activation energy.
- Homogeneous catalysts are in the same physical state as reactants; heterogeneous catalysts are in a different physical state.

## Skills

- Calculate average rates from gas volume, mass or concentration data.
- Interpret graph gradients as rates.
- Use $1/t$ as a relative rate for fixed-observation experiments.
- Explain concentration and pressure effects using effective collisions.
- Sketch and explain Boltzmann distributions with $E_A$ marked.
- Compare catalysed and uncatalysed reaction pathway diagrams.
- Classify catalysts as homogeneous or heterogeneous.

## Common Errors To Avoid

- Explaining temperature only by saying particles collide more often.
- Forgetting that the area under a Boltzmann distribution represents total particles.
- Drawing a catalyst as changing the reactant or product enthalpy level.
- Saying a catalyst changes $\Delta H$ or $K$.
- Forgetting units in rate calculations.
- Interpreting a shorter time to a fixed endpoint as a slower rate.

## Ready To Move On When

- You can calculate rate from a table and explain what the unit means.
- You can explain rate changes using collision frequency and effective collisions.
- You can use the Boltzmann distribution to explain temperature and catalyst effects.
- You can label $E_A$ and $\Delta H$ separately on a reaction pathway diagram.
- You can connect catalyst effects here to equilibrium ideas in [Equilibria](../07%20Equilibria/00%20Overview.md).
