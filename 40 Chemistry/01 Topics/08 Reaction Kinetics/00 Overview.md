# 08 Reaction Kinetics

## Core Idea

Reaction kinetics explains how fast reactions happen and why rate changes with concentration, pressure, temperature and catalysts. AS work uses collision theory, activation energy, Boltzmann distributions, reaction pathway diagrams and experimental rate evidence.

## What to Learn

- Explain and use rate of reaction, frequency of collisions, effective collisions and non-effective collisions.
- Explain concentration and pressure effects in terms of frequency of effective collisions.
- Use experimental data to calculate reaction rate.
- Define activation energy, $E_A$, as the minimum energy required for a collision to be effective.
- Sketch and use the Boltzmann distribution to explain activation energy and temperature effects.
- Explain catalysts and catalysis, including homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysts.
- Explain that a catalyst provides a different mechanism with lower activation energy.
- Construct and interpret reaction pathway diagrams with and without an effective catalyst.

## How to Study

Every rate explanation should connect to collision frequency, the fraction of collisions that are effective, or both. Higher concentration and higher gas pressure usually increase collision frequency. Higher temperature increases collision frequency slightly but, more importantly, increases the fraction of particles with energy at least $E_A$.

For data questions, decide what is being measured: concentration change, gas volume change, mass loss, colour intensity or time to a fixed observation. Rate is a change divided by time, and graph gradients give rates.

## Practice Directions

- Calculate average rates from tables of concentration, mass or gas volume.
- Estimate instantaneous rates from gradients of tangents.
- Explain concentration, pressure and temperature effects using collision theory.
- Sketch Boltzmann distributions and mark $E_A$.
- Compare catalysed and uncatalysed reaction pathway diagrams.
- Classify catalysts as homogeneous or heterogeneous from their physical state.

## Common Traps

- Saying higher temperature only means more collisions, without explaining the increased fraction of effective collisions.
- Confusing activation energy with enthalpy change.
- Assuming a catalyst changes $\Delta H$, equilibrium constant or final yield.
- Treating a shorter time to a fixed observation as a smaller rate, when it means a faster reaction.
- Forgetting units when calculating rates from data.
