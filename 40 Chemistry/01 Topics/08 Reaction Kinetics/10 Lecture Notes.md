# Reaction Kinetics - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level Physical chemistry, Topic 8, Reaction kinetics: 8.1 Rate of reaction, 8.2 Effect of temperature on reaction rates and the concept of activation energy, and 8.3 Homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysts. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## What Rate Means

Rate of reaction measures how quickly reactants are used up or products are formed. It is a change divided by time:

$$
\text{rate} = \frac{\text{change in amount, concentration, mass or volume}}{\text{time taken}}
$$

The units depend on what is measured. For concentration, rate may be $\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}\,s^{-1}}$. For gas volume, rate may be $\mathrm{cm^3\,s^{-1}}$. For mass loss, rate may be $\mathrm{g\,s^{-1}}$.

For a reaction such as:

$$
\ce{CaCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> CaCl2(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

rate can be followed by measuring the volume of $\ce{CO2}$ produced per unit time or by measuring the decrease in mass as $\ce{CO2}$ escapes.

## Collision Theory

For particles to react, they must collide. Not every collision causes reaction.

The frequency of collisions is how often particles collide in a given time. An effective collision is a collision that leads to reaction. A non-effective collision is a collision that does not lead to reaction.

For a collision to be effective, particles must have enough energy and a suitable orientation. At AS level, the energy requirement is the main focus. Particles must have energy at least equal to the activation energy, $E_A$.

## Concentration And Pressure

Increasing the concentration of a solution usually increases reaction rate. There are more reacting particles per unit volume, so collisions happen more frequently. If a greater number of collisions occur per second, the frequency of effective collisions also increases.

For gases, increasing pressure has a similar effect. Higher pressure means gas particles are closer together in a smaller volume, so they collide more frequently. This increases the frequency of effective collisions and increases rate.

When explaining concentration or pressure effects, use collision language:

- more particles per unit volume;
- more frequent collisions;
- more frequent effective collisions;
- faster reaction.

Do not say only "there are more collisions" if the question asks for rate. The collisions that matter are effective collisions.

## Measuring Rate From Data

Average rate over a time interval is:

$$
\text{average rate} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta t}
$$

where $y$ is the measured quantity, such as concentration or volume.

For a product volume graph, the gradient is positive because product volume increases. For a reactant concentration graph, the gradient is negative because reactant concentration decreases. Rate is often quoted as a positive value, so use the magnitude of the gradient if needed.

Instantaneous rate at a particular time is found from the gradient of the tangent to the curve at that time. Initial rate is the rate at the start of the reaction, usually found from the tangent at $t = 0$.

For experiments where the time to a fixed observation is measured, such as a precipitate hiding a cross, a shorter time means a faster reaction. A common relative rate measure is:

$$
\text{relative rate} = \frac{1}{t}
$$

This is not the same as a full concentration-time rate, but it is useful for comparing experiments.

## Activation Energy

Activation energy, $E_A$, is the minimum energy required for a collision to be effective.

Reaction pathway diagrams show $E_A$ as the vertical energy gap from reactants to the top of the energy barrier. Enthalpy change, $\Delta H$, is the vertical energy difference between products and reactants. These are different quantities.

For:

$$
\ce{H2(g) + I2(g) -> 2HI(g)}
$$

reacting particles must collide with enough energy to weaken old bonds and begin forming new bonds. The top of the pathway represents the high-energy transition state.

## Boltzmann Distribution

A Boltzmann distribution shows the distribution of molecular energies in a gas or reaction mixture at a given temperature.

Important features:

- the curve starts at the origin because no particles have zero energy;
- the area under the curve represents the total number of particles;
- the curve has a peak because many particles have intermediate energies;
- only particles with energy greater than or equal to $E_A$ can react when they collide effectively.

On a Boltzmann diagram, $E_A$ is shown as a vertical line. The area to the right of $E_A$ represents particles with enough energy to react.

## Temperature And Rate

Increasing temperature increases reaction rate for two reasons.

First, particles move faster, so collision frequency increases.

Second, and more importantly, the distribution of energies changes so that a much larger fraction of particles has energy at least equal to $E_A$. This causes a large increase in the frequency of effective collisions.

When temperature increases, the Boltzmann curve becomes lower and broader, with its peak shifted to higher energy. The total area under the curve stays the same if the number of particles is unchanged. The area to the right of $E_A$ increases.

A strong AS explanation of temperature effect should mention both the Boltzmann distribution and effective collisions.

## Catalysts And Catalysis

A catalyst increases the rate of reaction and is chemically unchanged at the end of the reaction. Catalysis is the increase in rate caused by a catalyst.

In the presence of a catalyst, a reaction has a different mechanism with lower activation energy. On a Boltzmann distribution, lowering $E_A$ moves the activation-energy line to the left, so a larger fraction of particles has enough energy to react at the same temperature.

A catalyst does not change the enthalpy change of reaction because the reactants and products are unchanged. It also does not change the equilibrium constant or final equilibrium position. It increases the rate of reaching equilibrium by increasing both forward and reverse rates.

## Homogeneous And Heterogeneous Catalysts

A homogeneous catalyst is in the same physical state as the reactants. For example, an aqueous catalyst in an aqueous reaction mixture is homogeneous.

A heterogeneous catalyst is in a different physical state from the reactants. A solid catalyst used with gaseous reactants is heterogeneous. Many industrial catalysts are heterogeneous because reactants can adsorb onto the solid surface, react, and desorb as products.

The syllabus requires use of the terms homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysts, not a detailed surface-chemistry mechanism.

## Reaction Pathway Diagrams With Catalysts

A catalysed reaction pathway has a lower peak than the uncatalysed pathway. This shows lower activation energy. The reactants and products are at the same enthalpy levels in both pathways, so $\Delta H$ is unchanged.

For an exothermic reaction:

- products are lower than reactants;
- the catalysed pathway has a lower peak;
- $\Delta H$ is the same for catalysed and uncatalysed routes.

For an endothermic reaction:

- products are higher than reactants;
- the catalysed pathway still has a lower peak;
- $\Delta H$ remains positive and unchanged.

## Practical Evidence For Rate

Common rate measurements include:

- gas volume produced over time, using a gas syringe;
- mass loss over time when a gas escapes;
- colour intensity or absorbance over time;
- time for a precipitate to obscure a mark;
- sampling and titration at intervals.

Good experimental design changes one variable at a time. For example, to test concentration, keep temperature, total volume, particle size and catalyst amount constant where possible.

Repeats help identify anomalous times. Graphs help show whether rate changes during a reaction. Rates often decrease over time because reactant concentrations fall, causing fewer effective collisions per second.

## Working Routine

For a rate explanation, ask: did collision frequency change, did the fraction of particles above $E_A$ change, or did the mechanism change?

For a data question, identify the measured quantity, calculate change divided by time, and attach units.

For a catalyst question, say "different mechanism with lower activation energy" and then link this to the Boltzmann distribution or pathway diagram.

## Common Mistakes

- Explaining a rate increase by more collisions only; the key is more frequent effective collisions.
- Saying a catalyst is used up; it is regenerated.
- Claiming a catalyst changes the equilibrium yield; it changes rate only.
- Stopping the temperature explanation at particles moving faster; the shift in the fraction above the activation energy matters more.
- Drawing the Boltzmann curve not starting at the origin.

- Can you show why a catalyst changes $E_A$ but not $\Delta H$?
- Can you distinguish homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysts?
