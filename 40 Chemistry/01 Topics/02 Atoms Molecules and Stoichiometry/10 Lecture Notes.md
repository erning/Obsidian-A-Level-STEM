# Atoms, Molecules and Stoichiometry

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 Syllabus 2025-2027, AS Level subject content, Physical chemistry, Topic 2 Atoms, molecules and stoichiometry:

- 2.1 Relative masses of atoms and molecules
- 2.2 The mole and the Avogadro constant

This topic supplies the quantitative language used throughout AS Chemistry: formulae, balanced equations, amount of substance, concentration, gas volume, percentage yield, limiting reagent and significant figures.

## Relative Masses

The **unified atomic mass unit** is one twelfth of the mass of one carbon-12 atom.

Relative masses compare particles with this standard, so they have no units.

| Quantity | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| relative isotopic mass | mass of an atom of an isotope compared with $\frac{1}{12}$ of the mass of a carbon-12 atom |
| relative atomic mass, $A_r$ | weighted mean mass of atoms of an element compared with $\frac{1}{12}$ of the mass of a carbon-12 atom |
| relative molecular mass, $M_r$ | sum of relative atomic masses in a molecule |
| relative formula mass | sum of relative atomic masses in a formula unit, often used for ionic compounds |

For example:

$$
M_r(\ce{CO2}) = 12.0 + 2(16.0) = 44.0
$$

For an ionic compound such as $\ce{MgCl2}$, the relative formula mass is:

$$
24.3 + 2(35.5) = 95.3
$$

## The Mole and the Avogadro Constant

One mole is the amount of substance containing the Avogadro constant number of specified particles.

$$
N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23}\ \mathrm{mol}^{-1}
$$

The central relationships are:

$$
n = \frac{N}{N_A}
$$

$$
n = \frac{m}{M}
$$

where $n$ is amount of substance in mol, $N$ is number of particles, $m$ is mass in grams and $M$ is molar mass in $\mathrm{g\ mol^{-1}}$.

Always state what particles are being counted: atoms, molecules, ions, electrons or formula units.

## Formulae of Ionic Compounds

Ionic formulae are built from charge balance. The total positive charge must equal the total negative charge.

Examples:

$$
\ce{Mg^{2+} + 2Cl- -> MgCl2}
$$

$$
\ce{Al^{3+} + PO4^{3-} -> AlPO4}
$$

The syllabus requires recall of these ions:

| Ion | Formula |
| --- | --- |
| nitrate | $\ce{NO3-}$ |
| carbonate | $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$ |
| sulfate | $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$ |
| hydroxide | $\ce{OH-}$ |
| ammonium | $\ce{NH4+}$ |
| zinc | $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ |
| silver | $\ce{Ag+}$ |
| hydrogencarbonate | $\ce{HCO3-}$ |
| phosphate | $\ce{PO4^{3-}}$ |

Oxidation numbers in names are shown by Roman numerals. For example, iron(III) sulfate contains $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ and $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$, giving $\ce{Fe2(SO4)3}$.

## Balanced Equations and Ionic Equations

A balanced equation conserves atoms and charge. State symbols are:

- $\ce{(s)}$ solid
- $\ce{(l)}$ liquid
- $\ce{(g)}$ gas
- $\ce{(aq)}$ aqueous solution

Example:

$$
\ce{CaCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> CaCl2(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

An ionic equation includes only the reacting ions and species. Spectator ions are omitted.

For acid reacting with carbonate:

$$
\ce{CO3^{2-}(aq) + 2H+(aq) -> CO2(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

Net ionic equations are especially useful in precipitation, acid-base and redox reactions.

## Empirical and Molecular Formulae

The **empirical formula** is the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound.

The **molecular formula** gives the actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule.

To find an empirical formula from composition data:

1. Convert each mass or percentage to moles.
2. Divide all mole amounts by the smallest.
3. Multiply if needed to reach whole numbers.

To find a molecular formula:

$$
\text{whole-number multiplier} = \frac{M_r\ \text{of molecular formula}}{M_r\ \text{of empirical formula}}
$$

## Hydrated and Anhydrous Compounds

A **hydrated** compound contains water of crystallisation in its crystal structure. An **anhydrous** compound has no water of crystallisation.

Example:

$$
\ce{CuSO4*5H2O}
$$

This formula means that each formula unit of $\ce{CuSO4}$ is associated with five water molecules in the crystal.

When a hydrate is heated, water can be lost:

$$
\ce{CuSO4*5H2O(s) -> CuSO4(s) + 5H2O(g)}
$$

Hydrate calculations compare moles of anhydrous salt with moles of water lost.

## Reacting Masses

Stoichiometry begins with a balanced equation. The coefficients give mole ratios, not mass ratios.

General routine:

1. Write the balanced equation.
2. Convert the known quantity to moles.
3. Use the equation ratio to find moles of the required substance.
4. Convert moles to the requested quantity.

For mass:

$$
m = nM
$$

For percentage yield:

$$
\text{percentage yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100
$$

## Solutions

Concentration is amount per unit volume:

$$
c = \frac{n}{V}
$$

For AS calculations, concentration is usually in $\mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ and volume must be in $\mathrm{dm^3}$.

$$
1\ \mathrm{dm^3} = 1000\ \mathrm{cm^3}
$$

So:

$$
25.0\ \mathrm{cm^3} = 0.0250\ \mathrm{dm^3}
$$

## Gas Volumes

Gas volume calculations use mole ratios in the same way as mass calculations. If a question gives a molar gas volume, use it consistently.

For gases at the same temperature and pressure, volume ratios are the same as mole ratios.

Example:

$$
\ce{CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)}
$$

At the same temperature and pressure, $1$ volume of methane reacts with $2$ volumes of oxygen.

The ideal gas equation belongs mainly to [States of Matter](../04%20States%20of%20Matter/10%20Lecture%20Notes.md), but the mole concept is the same.

## Limiting and Excess Reagents

The limiting reagent is the reactant that is completely used up first. It determines the maximum amount of product.

To identify it:

1. Calculate moles of each reactant.
2. Divide each mole amount by its equation coefficient.
3. The smaller value identifies the limiting reagent.

The other reagent is in excess. Excess reagent remains after the reaction stops.

## Significant Figures

CAIE expects answers to reflect the significant figures given or requested. Keep extra digits during working, then round at the end.

Do not lose significant figures unnecessarily in intermediate steps. Do not report more significant figures than the data justifies.

## Working Routine

- Convert every quantity to moles before using the equation ratio.
- Find the limiting reagent by dividing each mole amount by its coefficient; the smallest value is limiting.
- At constant temperature and pressure, use mole ratios directly as gas volume ratios.
- Carry units through every line and round only at the end to the correct number of significant figures.
## Common Mistakes

- Using mass ratios instead of mole ratios.
- Forgetting to convert cm3 to dm3 (or m3) before dividing.
- Giving an answer to more significant figures than the data justify.
- Calculating from the excess reagent instead of the limiting reagent.
- Leaving spectator ions in an ionic equation.
