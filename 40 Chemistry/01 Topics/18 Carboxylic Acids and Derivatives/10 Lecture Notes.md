# 18 Carboxylic Acids and Derivatives - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow the CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level subject content for topic 18, **Carboxylic acids and derivatives**:

- 18.1 Carboxylic acids
- 18.2 Esters

The syllabus focus is not a full survey of all carboxylic acid derivatives. At AS Level the named derivative family is esters, and the required chemistry is production, reactions, hydrolysis, and synthetic use.

## Learning Scope

By the end of this topic you should be able to:

- recall how carboxylic acids are produced by oxidation, nitrile hydrolysis, and ester hydrolysis;
- describe reactions of carboxylic acids with reactive metals, alkalis, carbonates, alcohols, and lithium aluminium hydride;
- recall ester production from an alcohol and a carboxylic acid using concentrated sulfuric acid as catalyst;
- describe ester hydrolysis by dilute acid and by dilute alkali with heat;
- use carboxylic acids and esters as links in AS organic synthesis.

## Core Functional Group

A carboxylic acid contains the carboxyl group, $\ce{-COOH}$. It behaves as a weak acid because the $\ce{O-H}$ bond in this group can donate $\ce{H+}$ to bases.

General formulae used in this topic:

| Family | General structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| carboxylic acid | $\ce{RCOOH}$ | ethanoic acid, $\ce{CH3COOH}$ |
| carboxylate salt | $\ce{RCOO- M+}$ | sodium ethanoate, $\ce{CH3COONa}$ |
| ester | $\ce{RCOOR'}$ | ethyl ethanoate, $\ce{CH3COOCH2CH3}$ |

In an ester, the part $\ce{RCOO-}$ comes from the carboxylic acid and the part $\ce{-OR'}$ comes from the alcohol.

## Making Carboxylic Acids

### Oxidation of Primary Alcohols and Aldehydes

Primary alcohols and aldehydes are oxidised to carboxylic acids by refluxing with acidified potassium dichromate(VI), $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$, or acidified potassium manganate(VII), $\ce{KMnO4}$.

For ethanol:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2OH + 2[O] -> CH3COOH + H2O}
$$

For ethanal:

$$
\ce{CH3CHO + [O] -> CH3COOH}
$$

The word **refluxing** matters. If a primary alcohol is distilled as it is oxidised, the aldehyde can be collected before further oxidation. For carboxylic acid formation, the mixture is heated under reflux so the aldehyde remains in the reaction mixture and is oxidised further.

### Hydrolysis of Nitriles

Nitriles hydrolyse to carboxylic acids using dilute acid or dilute alkali followed by acidification.

For ethanenitrile:

$$
\ce{CH3CN + 2H2O + H+ -> CH3COOH + NH4+}
$$

Under alkaline conditions the first organic product is a carboxylate ion. Acidification converts it to the carboxylic acid:

$$
\ce{CH3COO- + H+ -> CH3COOH}
$$

This route is important in synthesis because making a nitrile from a halogenoalkane increases the carbon chain length by one carbon atom before hydrolysis gives the acid.

### Hydrolysis of Esters

Esters can be hydrolysed back to carboxylic acids. The syllabus gives two sets of conditions:

- dilute acid and heat;
- dilute alkali and heat followed by acidification.

Acid hydrolysis is reversible:

$$
\ce{CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O <=> CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH}
$$

Alkaline hydrolysis first forms a carboxylate salt:

$$
\ce{CH3COOCH2CH3 + NaOH -> CH3COONa + CH3CH2OH}
$$

Acidification then gives the carboxylic acid:

$$
\ce{CH3COONa + HCl -> CH3COOH + NaCl}
$$

## Reactions of Carboxylic Acids

### With Reactive Metals

Carboxylic acids react with reactive metals to form a salt and hydrogen gas.

$$
\ce{2CH3COOH + Mg -> (CH3COO)2Mg + H2}
$$

Observation: effervescence, and the gas gives a squeaky pop with a lighted splint.

### With Alkalis

Carboxylic acids neutralise alkalis to form a salt and water.

$$
\ce{CH3COOH + NaOH -> CH3COONa + H2O}
$$

This is an acid-base reaction. There is usually no dramatic visible change unless an indicator is present.

### With Carbonates

Carboxylic acids react with carbonates to form a salt, water, and carbon dioxide.

$$
\ce{2CH3COOH + Na2CO3 -> 2CH3COONa + H2O + CO2}
$$

Observation: effervescence. The carbon dioxide gives a white precipitate with limewater.

### With Alcohols: Esterification

A carboxylic acid reacts with an alcohol to form an ester and water. Concentrated sulfuric acid is used as a catalyst.

$$
\ce{CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH <=> CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O}
$$

This is a condensation reaction because two molecules join and a small molecule, water, is eliminated.

### Reduction to Primary Alcohols

Carboxylic acids are reduced by lithium aluminium hydride, $\ce{LiAlH4}$, to primary alcohols.

$$
\ce{CH3COOH + 4[H] -> CH3CH2OH + H2O}
$$

Do not use sodium borohydride, $\ce{NaBH4}$, for this AS transformation. $\ce{NaBH4}$ reduces aldehydes and ketones, but the syllabus names $\ce{LiAlH4}$ for carboxylic acids.

## Esters as Products and Evidence

Esters are formed from alcohols and carboxylic acids:

$$
\ce{RCOOH + R'OH <=> RCOOR' + H2O}
$$

To name an ester:

1. Name the alkyl group from the alcohol first.
2. Name the carboxylate part from the acid second.

For example, methanol plus propanoic acid gives methyl propanoate:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2COOH + CH3OH <=> CH3CH2COOCH3 + H2O}
$$

Hydrolysis is the reverse direction. In acid hydrolysis the acid is regenerated directly. In alkaline hydrolysis the salt is formed first, so acidification is required if the final product must be the carboxylic acid.

## Analysis Links

Carboxylic acids and esters are often identified by a combination of reaction evidence and spectral evidence.

Useful AS clues:

| Evidence | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Effervescence with carbonate; gas gives white precipitate with limewater | carboxylic acid group present |
| Broad infrared $\ce{O-H}$ absorption around $2500-3000\ \mathrm{cm^{-1}}$ plus $\ce{C=O}$ absorption | carboxylic acid likely |
| Infrared $\ce{C=O}$ absorption in the ester range and $\ce{C-O}$ absorption | ester likely |
| Hydrolysis gives an alcohol and an acid or salt | ester present |

Do not rely on one clue alone when a question gives several pieces of evidence. A carbonyl absorption proves a $\ce{C=O}$ bond, but not by itself whether the compound is an acid, aldehyde, ketone, or ester.

## Synthesis Routes

Carboxylic acids sit near the end of many AS organic routes:

$$
\ce{RCH2OH ->[oxidation,\ reflux] RCHO ->[oxidation] RCOOH}
$$

$$
\ce{R-X ->[KCN,\ ethanol,\ heat] R-CN ->[hydrolysis] R-COOH}
$$

$$
\ce{RCOOH + R'OH <=>[conc.\ H2SO4] RCOOR' + H2O}
$$

When planning a route, write each step with:

- the organic starting material and product;
- the reagent and condition;
- the reaction type;
- any required final acidification.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing ester hydrolysis products without considering whether the conditions are acidic or alkaline.
- Forgetting acidification after alkaline hydrolysis of a nitrile or ester when a carboxylic acid is required.
- Using distillation instead of reflux for complete oxidation of a primary alcohol to a carboxylic acid.
- Assigning the wrong half of an ester to the alcohol.
- Treating $\ce{NaBH4}$ as a general reducing agent for all carbonyl-containing compounds.
