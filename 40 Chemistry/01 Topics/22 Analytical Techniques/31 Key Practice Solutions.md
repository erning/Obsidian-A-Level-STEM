# 22 Analytical Techniques - Key Practice Solutions

## Solutions

1. A broad carboxyl $\ce{O-H}$ absorption at $2500-3000\ \mathrm{cm^{-1}}$ together with a $\ce{C=O}$ absorption, typically in the carbonyl/carboxyl range, supports a carboxylic acid group.

2. The absorption at $2220\ \mathrm{cm^{-1}}$ suggests a nitrile group, $\ce{-C#N}$.

3. The relative molecular mass is 74.

4. Use a weighted mean:

   $$
   A_r = \frac{(10 \times 20.0) + (11 \times 80.0)}{100}
   $$

   $$
   A_r = \frac{200 + 880}{100} = 10.8
   $$

5. Use the $[M+1]^+$ formula:

   $$
   n = \frac{100 \times 5.5}{1.1 \times 100} = 5
   $$

   The compound contains five carbon atoms.

6. A $3:1$ $M:M+2$ pattern suggests one chlorine atom.

7. A $1:1$ $M:M+2$ pattern suggests one bromine atom.

8. One possible fragment ion is $\ce{C2H5+}$, since $2(12) + 5(1) = 29$.

9. 2,4-DNPH shows a carbonyl compound. Tollens' reagent gives a silver mirror with aldehydes, so the functional group is an aldehyde.

10. A yellow precipitate with alkaline aqueous iodine supports the presence of a $\ce{CH3CO}$ group in an aldehyde or ketone, or a $\ce{CH3CH(OH)}$ group in an alcohol.

11. The gas is ammonia, $\ce{NH3}$.

12. Calculate the ratio of spot distance to solvent-front distance:

   $$
   R_f = \frac{2.4}{6.0} = 0.40
   $$

13. Hydrolysis to ethanol and ethanoic acid suggests ethyl ethanoate:

   $$
   \ce{CH3COOCH2CH3}
   $$

   Its relative molecular mass is $4(12) + 8(1) + 2(16) = 88$, matching the molecular ion peak.

14. "No change" is evidence. It can rule out ions or functional groups that would have given a visible reaction, and it prevents a conclusion being based only on positive observations.
