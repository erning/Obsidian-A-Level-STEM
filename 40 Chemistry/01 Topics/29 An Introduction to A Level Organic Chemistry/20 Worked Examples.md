# 29 An Introduction to A Level Organic Chemistry - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Identifying Functional Groups

A molecule has the structural formula $\ce{C6H5COCl}$. Identify the main functional group and explain why it is not a halogenoarene.

The group $\ce{-COCl}$ is an acyl chloride group. The chlorine is bonded to the carbonyl carbon, not directly to the benzene ring.

So $\ce{C6H5COCl}$ is benzoyl chloride, an aromatic acyl chloride. It is not a halogenoarene because a halogenoarene has a halogen atom directly attached to an aromatic ring, as in chlorobenzene, $\ce{C6H5Cl}$.

Check: the bonding environment of chlorine determines the class, not just the presence of chlorine.

## Example 2: Naming an Aromatic Compound

Name the compound with a benzene ring bearing $\ce{-OH}$ at position 1 and bromine atoms at positions 2, 4 and 6.

The $\ce{-OH}$ group directly attached to the benzene ring makes the parent compound phenol. Number the carbon attached to $\ce{-OH}$ as position 1. The bromine atoms are then at positions 2, 4 and 6.

Name: 2,4,6-tribromophenol.

Check: this is within the A Level aromatic naming scope: one benzene ring with simple substituents.

## Example 3: Classifying a Mechanism Term

Classify this reaction using the A Level mechanism terms in Topic 29:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + NH3 -> CH3CONH2 + HCl}
$$

This is an acyl chloride reacting with ammonia. Ammonia attacks the electron-deficient carbonyl carbon, a tetrahedral intermediate forms, chloride leaves, and the carbonyl reforms.

The mechanism term is addition-elimination.

Check: the product is an amide, but the mechanism name comes from the two-stage movement: addition followed by elimination.

## Example 4: Explaining Benzene Shape

Explain why benzene is planar and why all six carbon-carbon bonds are equivalent.

Each carbon in benzene is $sp^2$ hybridised and forms three $\sigma$ bonds in a trigonal planar arrangement. The six carbon atoms therefore lie in one plane. Each carbon also has an unhybridised p orbital. These p orbitals overlap sideways to form a delocalised $\pi$ system above and below the ring.

Because the $\pi$ electrons are delocalised around the whole ring, benzene does not contain three localised double bonds. All six carbon-carbon bonds have the same character and length.

Check: aromatic stabilisation explains why benzene usually undergoes substitution rather than addition.

## Example 5: Optical Isomerism

Butan-2-ol has structural formula $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH2CH3}$. Explain whether it can show optical isomerism.

The second carbon is attached to four different groups:

- $\ce{H}$
- $\ce{OH}$
- $\ce{CH3}$
- $\ce{CH2CH3}$

This carbon is a chiral centre, so butan-2-ol can exist as two enantiomers. The enantiomers rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions. An equal mixture of them would be a racemic mixture and would be optically inactive overall.

Check: the argument identifies the chiral centre and the four different groups.

## Example 6: Drug Chirality

A drug is made as a racemic mixture, but only one enantiomer has the desired biological activity. Explain two possible approaches to obtain the useful enantiomer.

One approach is to separate the racemic mixture into the two pure enantiomers. This gives the desired enantiomer directly, although separation can be difficult.

Another approach is to use a chiral catalyst in the synthesis so that mainly one optical isomer is produced.

Check: the reason this matters is that biological targets are chiral, so the two enantiomers can have different biological activity.
