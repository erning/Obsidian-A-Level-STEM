# 29 An Introduction to A Level Organic Chemistry

## Core Idea

This topic refreshes organic notation and naming for A Level, then introduces the extra language needed for aromatic chemistry, acyl derivatives, amines, amides, amino acids, optical isomerism, and A Level mechanisms.

## Source Alignment

- Primary syllabus source: CAIE Chemistry 9701, A Level subject content.
- 29 An introduction to A Level organic chemistry
- 29.1 Formulas, functional groups and the naming of organic compounds
- 29.2 Characteristic organic reactions
- 29.3 Shapes of aromatic organic molecules; sigma and pi bonds
- 29.4 Isomerism: optical

## What to Learn

- Interpret general, structural, displayed, and skeletal formulae for A Level functional groups.
- Use systematic nomenclature for simple aliphatic and aromatic molecules within the syllabus scope.
- Recognise electrophilic substitution and addition-elimination as A Level mechanism terms.
- Explain the shape and bonding of benzene and other aromatic molecules using sigma bonds and a delocalised pi system.
- Use optical isomerism, enantiomer, optically active, racemic mixture, and chirality in context.

## How to Study

- Build a functional-group map before learning individual reaction families.
- Redraw structures in more than one representation so skeletal formulae become automatic.
- Connect aromatic shape and delocalisation to later arene reactivity.
- For optical isomerism, find the chiral centre first and then discuss the pair of enantiomers.

## Practice Directions

- Name simple aliphatic, cyclic, and aromatic compounds from structures.
- Convert between displayed, structural, and skeletal formulae.
- Identify functional groups in molecules containing several groups.
- Decide whether a molecule can show optical isomerism and explain why.

## Common Traps

- Treating benzene as if it were simply cyclohexa-1,3,5-triene.
- Confusing a functional group with the whole homologous series name.
- Calling a molecule chiral without identifying a chiral centre or another source of chirality.
- Forgetting that a racemic mixture is optically inactive overall.
