# 29 An Introduction to A Level Organic Chemistry - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 A Level subject content for Topic 29: formulas, functional groups and naming, characteristic organic reaction terms, shapes of aromatic organic molecules, and optical isomerism. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## AS Link

AS organic chemistry introduced homologous series, functional groups, displayed and structural formulae, structural isomerism, stereoisomerism in alkenes, and mechanisms such as free-radical substitution, electrophilic addition and nucleophilic substitution. Topic 29 keeps that toolkit but widens the range. A Level organic chemistry adds aromatic compounds, acyl derivatives, amines, amides, amino acids, optical isomerism and new mechanism language.

The practical habit is unchanged: identify the functional group first, then choose the reactions and conditions that the syllabus allows.

## Functional Groups Added at A Level

CAIE expects you to interpret and use general, structural, displayed and skeletal formulae for the A Level classes in the syllabus table. The key new or extended families are:

| Class | Functional group idea | Simple example |
|---|---|---|
| arene | benzene ring as part of the structure | benzene |
| halogenoarene | halogen directly bonded to an aromatic ring | chlorobenzene |
| phenol | $\ce{-OH}$ directly bonded to an aromatic ring | phenol |
| acyl chloride | $\ce{RCOCl}$ | propanoyl chloride |
| secondary and tertiary amines | nitrogen bonded to two or three carbon groups | secondary amine: $\ce{R2NH}$ |
| amides | $\ce{RCONH2}$, $\ce{RCONHR}$ or $\ce{RCONR2}$ | propanamide |
| amino acids | amine and carboxyl groups in the same molecule | 2-aminoethanoic acid |

The functional group controls much of the physical and chemical behaviour. A phenol and an alcohol both contain $\ce{-OH}$, but phenol is not treated as a simple alcohol because the $\ce{-OH}$ group is attached directly to an aromatic ring. An acyl chloride and a halogenoalkane both contain chlorine, but the chlorine is in a different bonding environment and reacts by a different route.

## Formulae and Representations

The same molecule can be represented in several ways.

- A **general formula** shows the pattern for a class, such as $\ce{RCOCl}$ for an acyl chloride.
- A **structural formula** shows the order of atoms in a compact line, such as $\ce{CH3CH2COCl}$.
- A **displayed formula** shows all bonds explicitly. Where a benzene ring is part of the molecule, CAIE does not expect a fully displayed benzene ring to be drawn.
- A **skeletal formula** omits most carbon and hydrogen labels. Each line end or vertex is a carbon atom unless another atom is written.

At A Level, skeletal formulae become especially important for aromatic molecules and larger molecules with several functional groups. Do not read a line-angle formula as decoration: every vertex, ring position and heteroatom matters.

## Naming Scope

You need systematic nomenclature for simple aliphatic organic molecules, including cyclic compounds with one ring of up to six carbon atoms, using the functional groups in the syllabus table. For esters and amides, the syllabus scope is up to six plus six carbon atoms; for esters and nitriles, straight chains only are expected. Naming of secondary and tertiary amines is not required.

You also need simple aromatic names with one benzene ring and one or more simple substituents, such as 3-nitrobenzoic acid and 2,4,6-tribromophenol. Number the ring to give the lowest sensible set of locants, and remember that a named parent such as phenol or benzoic acid fixes position 1.

## A Level Mechanism Terms

Topic 29 introduces two mechanism labels that recur through later A Level organic chemistry.

**Electrophilic substitution** is the characteristic mechanism of many arene reactions. An electrophile attacks the electron-rich aromatic $\pi$ system, but the final product restores aromaticity by losing a proton. Overall, one atom or group on the ring is substituted, not added across a double bond.

Example outcome:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + Br2 ->[AlBr3] C6H5Br + HBr}
$$

**Addition-elimination** is the mechanism used by acyl chlorides with nucleophiles such as water, alcohols, ammonia and amines. A nucleophile first adds to the electron-deficient carbonyl carbon. Then chloride leaves and the carbonyl group reforms.

Example outcome:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + CH3CH2OH -> CH3COOCH2CH3 + HCl}
$$

The names are useful because they describe the movement of the reaction, not just the product.

## Shape and Bonding in Aromatic Molecules

Benzene is planar. Each carbon atom is $sp^2$ hybridised and forms three $\sigma$ bonds: two to neighbouring carbon atoms and one to hydrogen or a substituent. The remaining p orbital on each carbon overlaps sideways with the p orbitals on the other ring carbons to form a delocalised $\pi$ system above and below the plane of the ring.

This delocalised $\pi$ system is not the same as three isolated carbon-carbon double bonds. All six carbon-carbon bonds in benzene are equivalent. Delocalisation gives aromatic stabilisation, so benzene tends to react by substitution, which preserves the aromatic ring, rather than by addition, which would destroy the delocalised system.

Other aromatic molecules keep the same core idea: a planar system with $\sigma$ bonds forming the framework and delocalised $\pi$ electrons spread across the aromatic part of the molecule.

## Optical Isomerism

Optical isomerism occurs when molecules are non-superimposable mirror images. Such a pair is called a pair of **enantiomers**. In many A Level examples, a chiral centre is a carbon atom attached to four different groups.

Enantiomers have identical physical and chemical properties in ordinary achiral conditions, but they differ in two important ways.

- They rotate plane-polarised light by equal amounts in opposite directions.
- They may have different biological activity because biological systems are chiral.

A substance is **optically active** if it rotates plane-polarised light. A **racemic mixture** contains equal amounts of two enantiomers and is optically inactive overall because the rotations cancel.

## Chirality and Drug Molecules

Chirality matters in drug synthesis because the two enantiomers of a drug molecule can interact differently with chiral biological targets such as enzymes or receptors. One enantiomer may have the desired therapeutic effect while the other is less active or produces unwanted effects.

There are two A Level consequences.

- A racemic mixture may need to be separated into two pure enantiomers.
- A chiral catalyst may be used to make mainly one optical isomer.

The syllabus notes that molecules can contain more than one chiral centre, but meso compounds and terms such as diastereoisomers are not required.

## Working Routine

When faced with a new A Level organic molecule:

1. Identify every functional group.
2. Decide whether the carbon skeleton is aliphatic, cyclic or aromatic.
3. Convert between displayed, structural and skeletal formulae if needed.
4. Name the compound within the syllabus scope.
5. Ask which mechanism term fits any stated reaction.
6. Check for a chiral centre by looking for a carbon attached to four different groups.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating benzene as cyclohexa-1,3,5-triene with three ordinary double bonds.
- Calling any molecule with a carbon attached to four bonds chiral without checking that the four attached groups are different.
- Using addition language for arene reactions that actually proceed by electrophilic substitution.
- Forgetting that amides and amines have different functional groups and very different basicity.
- Translating old AS rules directly into A Level cases without checking the new bonding environment.
