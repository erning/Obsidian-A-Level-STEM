# 36 Organic Synthesis - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 A Level subject content for Topic 36.1 Organic synthesis. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## AS Link

AS organic synthesis used a smaller reaction map: alkanes, alkenes, halogenoalkanes, alcohols, carbonyl compounds, carboxylic acids, esters, nitriles and amines. A Level synthesis keeps all AS reactions and adds arenes, phenols, acyl chlorides, amides, amino acids, diazonium salts, condensation polymers and advanced analysis. The aim is not to memorise isolated equations, but to choose reliable syllabus reactions in a sequence.

## Starting with Functional Groups

For a molecule containing several functional groups, first identify each one. Then ask:

- Which functional group is likely to react with the stated reagent?
- Which groups may be unchanged under those conditions?
- Does the reaction change the carbon skeleton, or only the functional group?
- Are there possible by-products or competing reactions?

For example, $\ce{C6H5COCl}$ contains an aromatic ring and an acyl chloride group. With ethanol at room temperature, the acyl chloride reacts to form an ester; the benzene ring is not the reacting site.

## A Practical Reaction Map

Useful A Level links include:

| Transformation | Reagents and conditions |
|---|---|
| benzene to nitrobenzene | concentrated $\ce{HNO3}$ and concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$, $25$ to $60\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ |
| nitrobenzene to phenylamine | hot $\ce{Sn}$ and concentrated $\ce{HCl}$, then $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ |
| phenylamine to diazonium salt | $\ce{HNO2}$, or $\ce{NaNO2}$ and dilute acid, below $10\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ |
| diazonium salt to phenol | warm with water |
| benzene to halogenoarene | $\ce{Cl2}$ or $\ce{Br2}$ with $\ce{AlCl3}$ or $\ce{AlBr3}$ |
| benzene to alkylbenzene | $\ce{CH3Cl}$, $\ce{AlCl3}$, heat |
| benzene to acylbenzene | $\ce{CH3COCl}$, $\ce{AlCl3}$, heat |
| alkylbenzene to benzoic acid | hot alkaline $\ce{KMnO4}$, then dilute acid |
| carboxylic acid to acyl chloride | $\ce{PCl3}$ and heat, $\ce{PCl5}$, or $\ce{SOCl2}$ |
| acyl chloride to ester | alcohol or phenol, room temperature |
| acyl chloride to amide | ammonia or primary/secondary amine, room temperature |
| halogenoalkane to amine | ethanolic $\ce{NH3}$, heat under pressure |
| nitrile to amine | $\ce{LiAlH4}$ or $\ce{H2/Ni}$ |

Use this as a map, not as a list to recite. The correct route depends on the target molecule.

## Working Backwards

Route planning is often easier backwards.

1. Identify the target functional group.
2. Ask which immediate precursor can make it.
3. Repeat until you reach the starting compound.
4. Check that every step is a syllabus reaction.
5. Add reagents, conditions and likely by-products.

For example, to make phenyl benzoate, the final step can be reaction of benzoyl chloride with phenol. That suggests making benzoyl chloride from benzoic acid, and benzoic acid from methylbenzene by side-chain oxidation if methylbenzene is available.

## Carbon Skeleton Checks

Some reactions change the carbon skeleton; many do not.

- Nitrile formation from a halogenoalkane using cyanide adds one carbon.
- Oxidation of methylbenzene converts the side-chain carbon into $\ce{-COOH}$ but does not add a new carbon.
- Friedel-Crafts acylation introduces an acyl group.
- Acyl chloride reactions replace the chlorine on $\ce{-COCl}$ with a nucleophile but keep the acyl carbon.

Always check carbon count before accepting a route.

## Analysing a Given Synthetic Route

For each step in a route, identify:

- the type of reaction, such as electrophilic substitution, nucleophilic substitution, oxidation, reduction, condensation or addition-elimination;
- the reagent and condition;
- the organic product;
- inorganic or small-molecule by-products, such as $\ce{HCl}$, $\ce{H2O}$ or $\ce{N2}$;
- any functional groups that remain unchanged.

This habit prevents incomplete answers. In A Level synthesis, a route without conditions is not really a route.

## Competing Functional Groups

Molecules with several functional groups require selectivity. Acyl chlorides react rapidly with water, alcohols, phenol, ammonia and amines, so they may need to be used before hydrolysis can occur. Phenol and phenylamine are activated aromatic rings, so their ring substitution conditions are milder than benzene. Amino acids can exist as zwitterions, so their acid-base state depends on pH.

When a molecule contains more than one reactive site, explain why the chosen reagent reacts where you say it does.

## Working Routine

- Count carbons at the start and target, and note where the skeleton must change.
- Build aromatic targets via nitration, reduction, diazotisation or Friedel-Crafts steps as needed.
- For each step, write the reagents, conditions and the small-molecule by-product.
## Common Mistakes

- Giving a product without reagents and conditions.
- Using reactions outside the syllabus when a syllabus route exists.
- Forgetting that a nitrile route adds one carbon.
- Ignoring by-products such as $\ce{HCl}$ in acyl chloride reactions.
- Planning forwards only and getting stuck after the first familiar reaction.
