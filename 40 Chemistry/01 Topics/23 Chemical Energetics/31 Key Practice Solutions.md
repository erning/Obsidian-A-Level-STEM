# 23 Chemical Energetics - Key Practice Solutions

## 1. Lattice Energy From A Born-Haber Cycle

For:

$$
\ce{Li(s) + 1/2F2(g) -> LiF(s)}
$$

the Born-Haber expression is:

$$
\Delta H^\circ_{\text{f}}
= \Delta H^\circ_{\text{at}}(\ce{Li})
+ \Delta H^\circ_{\text{at}}(\ce{F})
+ \text{IE}_1(\ce{Li})
+ \text{EA}_1(\ce{F})
+ \Delta H^\circ_{\text{latt}}
$$

Substitute:

$$
-617 = 161 + 79 + 520 - 328 + \Delta H^\circ_{\text{latt}}
$$

The non-lattice sum is $432\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}$, so:

$$
\Delta H^\circ_{\text{latt}} = -617 - 432 = -1049\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}
$$

## 2. Born-Haber Cycle With A 2+ Cation

The cycle for $\ce{MgCl2(s)}$ needs:

- atomisation of $\ce{Mg(s)}$ to $\ce{Mg(g)}$
- atomisation of $\ce{Cl2(g)}$ to $2\ce{Cl(g)}$
- first and second ionisation energies of magnesium to form $\ce{Mg^{2+}(g)}$
- two first electron affinities of chlorine to form $2\ce{Cl-(g)}$
- lattice energy for $\ce{Mg^{2+}(g) + 2Cl-(g) -> MgCl2(s)}$

Both $\text{IE}_1$ and $\text{IE}_2$ are needed because magnesium must lose two electrons to form $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$.

## 3. Enthalpy Change Of Solution

Use:

$$
\Delta H^\circ_{\text{sol}}
= -\Delta H^\circ_{\text{latt}}
+ \Delta H^\circ_{\text{hyd}}(\ce{Na+})
+ \Delta H^\circ_{\text{hyd}}(\ce{Br-})
$$

$$
\Delta H^\circ_{\text{sol}} = +742 - 406 - 336 = 0\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}
$$

With these rounded data, dissolving is approximately thermoneutral by enthalpy.

## 4. Interpreting Hydration Enthalpy

$\ce{Al^{3+}}$ has a higher charge and a smaller radius than $\ce{Na+}$, so it has much greater charge density. Water molecules are polar, so the negative end of each water molecule is attracted more strongly to $\ce{Al^{3+}}$. Stronger ion-dipole attractions release more energy during hydration, so the hydration enthalpy of $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ is more exothermic.

## 5. Predicting Entropy Signs

(a) Positive. A liquid becomes a gas, so the particles have many more possible arrangements.

(b) Negative. Three moles of gaseous reactants form two moles of gaseous products, reducing the number of gas particles.

(c) Usually positive. A solid ionic lattice forms separated aqueous ions, so particles become more dispersed in solution.

## 6. Calculating Entropy Change

$$
\Delta S^\circ = 2S^\circ(\ce{CO2}) - [2S^\circ(\ce{CO}) + S^\circ(\ce{O2})]
$$

$$
\Delta S^\circ = 2(214) - [2(198) + 205]
$$

$$
\Delta S^\circ = 428 - 601 = -173\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}
$$

The sign is reasonable because three moles of gas form two moles of gas.

## 7. Gibbs Free Energy At A Given Temperature

Convert entropy:

$$
\Delta S^\circ = -0.0807\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}
$$

Then:

$$
\Delta G^\circ = -74.8 - 298(-0.0807)
$$

$$
\Delta G^\circ = -74.8 + 24.0 = -50.8\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}
$$

The reaction is feasible under standard conditions because $\Delta G^\circ < 0$.

## 8. Temperature And Feasibility

(a) At the temperature where $\Delta G^\circ = 0$:

$$
T = \frac{\Delta H^\circ}{\Delta S^\circ}
$$

Convert entropy:

$$
\Delta S^\circ = 0.250\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}
$$

$$
T = \frac{112}{0.250} = 448\ \text{K}
$$

(b) Because both $\Delta H^\circ$ and $\Delta S^\circ$ are positive, the process is more likely to be feasible above $448\ \text{K}$.
