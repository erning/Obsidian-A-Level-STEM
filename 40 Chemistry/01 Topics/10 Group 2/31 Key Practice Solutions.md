# Group 2 - Key Practice Solutions

## Solutions

1. The AS Group 2 metals are $\ce{Mg}$, $\ce{Ca}$, $\ce{Sr}$ and $\ce{Ba}$. They commonly form $\ce{M^{2+}}$ ions.

2. Reactivity increases down the group. Atomic radius increases and shielding increases, so the outer electrons are less strongly attracted to the nucleus. The two outer electrons are lost more easily, so the metal forms $\ce{M^{2+}}$ ions more readily.

3. The general equation is:

$$
\ce{2M(s) + O2(g) -> 2MO(s)}
$$

For magnesium:

$$
\ce{2Mg(s) + O2(g) -> 2MgO(s)}
$$

4. Magnesium with steam:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + H2O(g) -> MgO(s) + H2(g)}
$$

Calcium with cold water:

$$
\ce{Ca(s) + 2H2O(l) -> Ca(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

An observation is effervescence as hydrogen forms. The mixture becomes alkaline and may turn cloudy because calcium hydroxide is only moderately soluble.

5. With dilute hydrochloric acid:

$$
\ce{M(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MCl2(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

With dilute sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{M(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> MSO4 + H2(g)}
$$

6. Calcium oxide with water:

$$
\ce{CaO(s) + H2O(l) -> Ca(OH)2(aq)}
$$

Calcium oxide with dilute hydrochloric acid:

$$
\ce{CaO(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> CaCl2(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

7. Barium hydroxide with dilute hydrochloric acid:

$$
\ce{Ba(OH)2(aq) + 2HCl(aq) -> BaCl2(aq) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

Barium hydroxide with dilute sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{Ba(OH)2(aq) + H2SO4(aq) -> BaSO4(s) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

8. General carbonate equation:

$$
\ce{MCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MCl2(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

The gas is $\ce{CO2}$. It turns limewater milky:

$$
\ce{CO2(g) + Ca(OH)2(aq) -> CaCO3(s) + H2O(l)}
$$

9. Group 2 carbonate:

$$
\ce{MCO3(s) -> MO(s) + CO2(g)}
$$

Group 2 nitrate:

$$
\ce{2M(NO3)2(s) -> 2MO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)}
$$

10. Thermal stability increases down the group for both carbonates and nitrates:

$$
\ce{MgCO3 < CaCO3 < SrCO3 < BaCO3}
$$

$$
\ce{Mg(NO3)2 < Ca(NO3)2 < Sr(NO3)2 < Ba(NO3)2}
$$

Barium compounds are the most thermally stable in these series.

11. Group 2 hydroxides become more soluble down the group:

$$
\ce{Mg(OH)2 < Ca(OH)2 < Sr(OH)2 < Ba(OH)2}
$$

Group 2 sulfates become less soluble down the group:

$$
\ce{MgSO4 > CaSO4 > SrSO4 > BaSO4}
$$

So $\ce{Ba(OH)2}$ is more soluble than $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$, but $\ce{BaSO4}$ is much less soluble than $\ce{MgSO4}$.

12. Brown fumes and a gas that relights a glowing splint indicate a Group 2 nitrate. Brown fumes are $\ce{NO2}$ and the splint test shows $\ce{O2}$:

$$
\ce{2M(NO3)2(s) -> 2MO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)}
$$
