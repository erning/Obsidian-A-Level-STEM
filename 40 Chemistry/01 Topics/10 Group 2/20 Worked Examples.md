# Group 2 - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Predict Reactivity with Water

**Question.** Calcium reacts with cold water, but magnesium reacts only very slowly. Explain the difference and write the equation for calcium with water.

**Working.**

Down Group 2, atomic radius increases and the outer electrons are more shielded. It is easier to form $\ce{M^{2+}}$ ions, so reactivity increases. Calcium is below magnesium, so calcium reacts more readily with cold water.

The equation is:

$$
\ce{Ca(s) + 2H2O(l) -> Ca(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

The solution becomes alkaline because calcium hydroxide is formed.

**Check.** The gas is hydrogen, not oxygen.

## Example 2: Write Acid Equations

**Question.** Write equations for magnesium reacting with dilute hydrochloric acid and barium reacting with dilute sulfuric acid.

**Working.**

With hydrochloric acid, the salt is a chloride:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

With sulfuric acid, the salt is a sulfate:

$$
\ce{Ba(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> BaSO4(s) + H2(g)}
$$

The barium sulfate is insoluble, so it can form a white coating or precipitate.

**Check.** Group 2 metals form $\ce{2+}$ ions, so the chloride formula is $\ce{MCl2}$.

## Example 3: Oxide and Hydroxide Reactions

**Question.** Strontium oxide is added to water and then neutralised with hydrochloric acid. Write equations for both steps.

**Working.**

The oxide reacts with water to form the hydroxide:

$$
\ce{SrO(s) + H2O(l) -> Sr(OH)2(aq)}
$$

The hydroxide reacts with hydrochloric acid to form a chloride and water:

$$
\ce{Sr(OH)2(aq) + 2HCl(aq) -> SrCl2(aq) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

**Check.** The hydroxide has two $\ce{OH-}$ groups, so it needs 2 moles of $\ce{HCl}$ per mole.

## Example 4: Carbonate with Acid

**Question.** A white Group 2 carbonate reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid to give a colourless gas that turns limewater milky. Write a general equation and identify the gas.

**Working.**

Group 2 carbonates react with acids to produce carbon dioxide:

$$
\ce{MCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MCl2(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

The gas is $\ce{CO2}$. It turns limewater milky because calcium carbonate forms:

$$
\ce{CO2(g) + Ca(OH)2(aq) -> CaCO3(s) + H2O(l)}
$$

**Check.** Effervescence with acid suggests carbonate, but the limewater test confirms $\ce{CO2}$.

## Example 5: Thermal Decomposition

**Question.** Predict the products when calcium nitrate is heated strongly. State two observations.

**Working.**

Group 2 nitrates decompose to the metal oxide, nitrogen dioxide and oxygen:

$$
\ce{2Ca(NO3)2(s) -> 2CaO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)}
$$

Observations:

- Brown fumes of $\ce{NO2}$.
- Oxygen relights a glowing splint.

**Check.** Do not write calcium nitrite as the product. Group 2 nitrates form the oxide at this level.

## Example 6: Compare Solubility Trends

**Question.** Predict which is more soluble in water: $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$ or $\ce{Ba(OH)2}$; $\ce{MgSO4}$ or $\ce{BaSO4}$. Explain the trend.

**Working.**

Group 2 hydroxides become more soluble down the group, so $\ce{Ba(OH)2}$ is more soluble than $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$.

Group 2 sulfates become less soluble down the group, so $\ce{MgSO4}$ is more soluble than $\ce{BaSO4}$.

**Check.** Hydroxides and sulfates have opposite solubility trends. This is one of the most common mistakes in the topic.
