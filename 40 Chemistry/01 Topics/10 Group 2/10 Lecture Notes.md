# Group 2 - Lecture Notes

## Learning Scope

This note follows CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level section 10. It covers magnesium to barium, their reactions with oxygen, water and dilute acids, reactions of their oxides, hydroxides and carbonates, thermal decomposition of nitrates and carbonates, and solubility trends for hydroxides and sulfates. No Chemistry coursebook alignment is claimed.

The Group 2 metals in this AS topic are:

$$
\ce{Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba}
$$

They commonly form $\ce{M^{2+}}$ ions. In general equations, $\ce{M}$ means a Group 2 metal.

## Main Trend Down Group 2

Down the group, atomic radius increases because each element has one more occupied electron shell. The outer electrons are further from the nucleus and more shielded. It becomes easier to remove the two outer electrons, so the metals become more reactive down the group.

This trend appears most clearly in reactions with water and dilute acids. Magnesium reacts slowly with cold water, calcium reacts steadily, and strontium and barium react more vigorously.

## Reactions of Group 2 Metals with Oxygen

Group 2 metals burn in oxygen to form metal oxides:

$$
\ce{2M(s) + O2(g) -> 2MO(s)}
$$

Examples:

$$
\ce{2Mg(s) + O2(g) -> 2MgO(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{2Ca(s) + O2(g) -> 2CaO(s)}
$$

Magnesium burns with a bright white flame to form white magnesium oxide. Calcium, strontium and barium also form white or pale solid oxides in the simple AS pattern.

## Reactions of Group 2 Metals with Water

The general reaction with cold water is:

$$
\ce{M(s) + 2H2O(l) -> M(OH)2 + H2(g)}
$$

Magnesium reacts very slowly with cold water because $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$ is sparingly soluble and can coat the metal surface:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + 2H2O(l) -> Mg(OH)2(s) + H2(g)}
$$

Calcium reacts more readily:

$$
\ce{Ca(s) + 2H2O(l) -> Ca(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

Down the group, reactions become more vigorous. The solution becomes alkaline because hydroxide ions are produced. Barium hydroxide is more soluble than calcium hydroxide, so the alkaline solution can be stronger.

Magnesium reacts more clearly with steam:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + H2O(g) -> MgO(s) + H2(g)}
$$

## Reactions of Group 2 Metals with Dilute Acids

Group 2 metals react with dilute hydrochloric acid to form a chloride and hydrogen:

$$
\ce{M(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MCl2(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

Example:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

With dilute sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{M(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> MSO4 + H2(g)}
$$

Example:

$$
\ce{Ca(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> CaSO4(s) + H2(g)}
$$

Hydrogen gas is observed as effervescence. A lighted splint gives a squeaky pop. Reactions with sulfuric acid can slow if an insoluble sulfate forms a coating on the metal.

## Reactions of Group 2 Oxides

Group 2 oxides are basic oxides. With water they form hydroxides:

$$
\ce{MO(s) + H2O(l) -> M(OH)2}
$$

Example:

$$
\ce{CaO(s) + H2O(l) -> Ca(OH)2(aq)}
$$

This reaction produces an alkaline solution or suspension.

With dilute hydrochloric acid:

$$
\ce{MO(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MCl2(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

With dilute sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{MO(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> MSO4 + H2O(l)}
$$

These are acid-base reactions.

## Reactions of Group 2 Hydroxides

Group 2 hydroxides are bases. Their solubility increases down the group:

$$
\ce{Mg(OH)2 < Ca(OH)2 < Sr(OH)2 < Ba(OH)2}
$$

This means $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$ is sparingly soluble, while $\ce{Ba(OH)2}$ is much more soluble.

With dilute hydrochloric acid:

$$
\ce{M(OH)2 + 2HCl(aq) -> MCl2(aq) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

With dilute sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{M(OH)2 + H2SO4(aq) -> MSO4 + 2H2O(l)}
$$

Because hydroxides become more soluble down the group, equal amounts of solid hydroxide generally produce more alkaline mixtures down the group.

## Reactions of Group 2 Carbonates

Group 2 carbonates are insoluble or only very slightly soluble in water, so they do not form strongly alkaline solutions by dissolving in water.

They react with dilute acids to form a salt, carbon dioxide and water:

$$
\ce{MCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MCl2(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{MCO3(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> MSO4 + CO2(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

The observation is effervescence. The gas turns limewater milky:

$$
\ce{CO2(g) + Ca(OH)2(aq) -> CaCO3(s) + H2O(l)}
$$

## Thermal Decomposition of Group 2 Carbonates

Group 2 carbonates decompose on heating to give the metal oxide and carbon dioxide:

$$
\ce{MCO3(s) -> MO(s) + CO2(g)}
$$

Example:

$$
\ce{MgCO3(s) -> MgO(s) + CO2(g)}
$$

Thermal stability increases down the group:

$$
\ce{MgCO3 < CaCO3 < SrCO3 < BaCO3}
$$

This means magnesium carbonate decomposes most easily and barium carbonate is most difficult to decompose. At AS level, you should be able to state the trend and use it for predictions. A useful qualitative idea is that larger cations down the group have lower charge density and disturb the carbonate ion less, so the carbonate is more stable.

## Thermal Decomposition of Group 2 Nitrates

Group 2 nitrates decompose on heating to form the metal oxide, nitrogen dioxide and oxygen:

$$
\ce{2M(NO3)2(s) -> 2MO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)}
$$

Example:

$$
\ce{2Mg(NO3)2(s) -> 2MgO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)}
$$

Observations:

- Brown fumes of $\ce{NO2}$ are produced.
- Oxygen is produced and can relight a glowing splint.
- A metal oxide solid remains.

Thermal stability increases down the group:

$$
\ce{Mg(NO3)2 < Ca(NO3)2 < Sr(NO3)2 < Ba(NO3)2}
$$

## Solubility of Hydroxides and Sulfates

The two solubility trends are opposite:

| Compound type | Trend down Group 2 | Practical consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Hydroxides, $\ce{M(OH)2}$ | Solubility increases | Solutions become more alkaline down the group |
| Sulfates, $\ce{MSO4}$ | Solubility decreases | $\ce{BaSO4}$ is very insoluble |

Typical order for hydroxides:

$$
\ce{Mg(OH)2 < Ca(OH)2 < Sr(OH)2 < Ba(OH)2}
$$

Typical order for sulfates:

$$
\ce{MgSO4 > CaSO4 > SrSO4 > BaSO4}
$$

The decreasing sulfate solubility is why barium ions are used in qualitative sulfate tests. When aqueous barium ions are added to a sulfate solution, a white precipitate of barium sulfate forms:

$$
\ce{Ba^{2+}(aq) + SO4^{2-}(aq) -> BaSO4(s)}
$$

## Working Routine

When a Group 2 question asks for a prediction, identify the compound type first:

1. Metal reaction? Reactivity usually increases down the group.
2. Hydroxide solubility? Increases down the group.
3. Sulfate solubility? Decreases down the group.
4. Carbonate or nitrate thermal stability? Increases down the group.
5. Acid with carbonate? Expect salt, $\ce{CO2}$ and water.
6. Acid with metal? Expect salt and $\ce{H2}$.

## Common Mistakes

- Reversing the two solubility trends: hydroxides become more soluble down the group, sulfates less soluble.
- Writing barium sulfate as soluble; it is very insoluble, which is the basis of the sulfate test.
- Forgetting that the thermal stability of carbonates and nitrates increases down the group.
- Giving the wrong decomposition products for nitrates.
- Saying reactivity decreases down the group; it increases.
## Quick Self-Checks

- Can you write the general formulae $\ce{MO}$, $\ce{M(OH)2}$, $\ce{MCO3}$, $\ce{M(NO3)2}$ and $\ce{MSO4}$?
- Can you write the general equations for metals with water and acids?
- Can you keep the hydroxide and sulfate solubility trends separate?
- Can you state which carbonate or nitrate is most thermally stable?
- Can you use gas tests for $\ce{H2}$, $\ce{CO2}$ and $\ce{O2}$ in these reactions?
