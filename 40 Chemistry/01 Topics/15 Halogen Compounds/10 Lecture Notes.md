# 15 Halogen Compounds - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level subject content for Topic 15.1, halogenoalkanes. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## What a Halogenoalkane Is

A halogenoalkane contains a halogen atom bonded to an alkyl group, written generally as $\ce{R-X}$, where $\ce{X}$ is $\ce{F}$, $\ce{Cl}$, $\ce{Br}$ or $\ce{I}$. At AS, halogenoalkanes are important because the polar carbon-halogen bond can be attacked by nucleophiles or can be involved in elimination.

The carbon-halogen bond is polar because halogens are more electronegative than carbon:

$$
\ce{C^{\delta+}-X^{\delta-}}
$$

The carbon attached to the halogen is electron-deficient, so it is a common reaction centre.

## Primary, Secondary and Tertiary Halogenoalkanes

Classification depends on how many carbon atoms are attached to the carbon bearing the halogen.

| Class | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | halogen-bearing carbon attached to one carbon, or none in halomethane | $\ce{CH3CH2Br}$ |
| Secondary | halogen-bearing carbon attached to two carbons | $\ce{CH3CHBrCH3}$ |
| Tertiary | halogen-bearing carbon attached to three carbons | $\ce{(CH3)3CBr}$ |

This classification matters because it affects whether nucleophilic substitution tends to proceed by $S_N2$ or $S_N1$.

## Producing Halogenoalkanes

CAIE AS requires these routes.

1. Free-radical substitution of alkanes by $\ce{Cl2}$ or $\ce{Br2}$ in ultraviolet light:

$$
\ce{CH3CH3 + Br2 ->[uv] CH3CH2Br + HBr}
$$

2. Electrophilic addition of an alkene with a halogen, $\ce{X2}$, or hydrogen halide, $\ce{HX(g)}$, at room temperature:

$$
\ce{CH2=CH2 + Br2 -> CH2BrCH2Br}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3CH=CH2 + HBr -> CH3CHBrCH3}
$$

3. Substitution of an alcohol. Possible reagents include $\ce{HX(g)}$, $\ce{KCl}$ with concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$ or concentrated $\ce{H3PO4}$, $\ce{PCl3}$ and heat, $\ce{PCl5}$, or $\ce{SOCl2}$:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2OH + HBr -> CH3CH2Br + H2O}
$$

## Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions

In nucleophilic substitution, a nucleophile attacks the electron-deficient carbon and the halide ion leaves.

### With Aqueous Sodium Hydroxide

Halogenoalkanes react with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ and heat to produce alcohols:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br + OH- -> CH3CH2OH + Br-}
$$

### With Potassium Cyanide

Halogenoalkanes react with $\ce{KCN}$ in ethanol and heat to produce nitriles:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br + CN- -> CH3CH2CN + Br-}
$$

This extends the carbon chain by one carbon because the carbon of $\ce{CN-}$ becomes part of the organic product.

### With Ammonia

Halogenoalkanes react with $\ce{NH3}$ in ethanol, heated under pressure, to produce amines:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br + 2NH3 -> CH3CH2NH2 + NH4Br}
$$

The extra ammonia neutralises the hydrogen halide formed.

### With Aqueous Silver Nitrate in Ethanol

Aqueous silver nitrate in ethanol is used to identify the halogen present. The halogenoalkane is warmed with ethanolic silver nitrate. Hydrolysis releases halide ions, which form precipitates:

$$
\ce{Ag+ + Cl- -> AgCl(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{Ag+ + Br- -> AgBr(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{Ag+ + I- -> AgI(s)}
$$

Observations:

- $\ce{AgCl}$: white precipitate.
- $\ce{AgBr}$: cream precipitate.
- $\ce{AgI}$: yellow precipitate.

The speed of precipitate formation shows relative reactivity: iodoalkanes react fastest, then bromoalkanes, then chloroalkanes. This follows the decreasing carbon-halogen bond strength from $\ce{C-Cl}$ to $\ce{C-Br}$ to $\ce{C-I}$.

## Elimination

With $\ce{NaOH}$ in ethanol and heat, halogenoalkanes undergo elimination to form alkenes. For bromoethane:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br + OH- -> CH2=CH2 + H2O + Br-}
$$

The hydroxide ion acts as a base, removing a hydrogen atom from a carbon next to the carbon bearing the halogen. A carbon-carbon double bond forms as the halide leaves.

The solvent matters. Aqueous hydroxide favours substitution to form an alcohol. Ethanolic hydroxide favours elimination to form an alkene.

## $S_N2$ Mechanism

Primary halogenoalkanes tend to react by $S_N2$. The mechanism is one step. The nucleophile attacks the carbon from the side opposite the leaving group while the carbon-halogen bond breaks.

For bromoethane with hydroxide:

- Curly arrow from a lone pair on $\ce{OH-}$ to the electron-deficient carbon.
- Curly arrow from the $\ce{C-Br}$ bond to $\ce{Br}$.
- Product: ethanol and bromide ion.

Overall:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br + OH- -> CH3CH2OH + Br-}
$$

$S_N2$ is favoured in primary halogenoalkanes because the carbon is less sterically hindered.

## $S_N1$ Mechanism

Tertiary halogenoalkanes tend to react by $S_N1$. The mechanism has two main steps.

Step 1: heterolytic fission of the carbon-halogen bond forms a carbocation:

$$
\ce{(CH3)3CBr -> (CH3)3C+ + Br-}
$$

Step 2: the nucleophile attacks the carbocation:

$$
\ce{(CH3)3C+ + OH- -> (CH3)3COH}
$$

$S_N1$ is favoured for tertiary halogenoalkanes because alkyl groups stabilise the carbocation by inductive effects. Secondary halogenoalkanes can react by a mixture of $S_N1$ and $S_N2$, depending on structure and conditions.

## Study Routine

For any halogenoalkane reaction, decide first whether the reagent is acting as a nucleophile or as a base. Then choose substitution or elimination, check the solvent, and identify the likely mechanism from the halogenoalkane class.
