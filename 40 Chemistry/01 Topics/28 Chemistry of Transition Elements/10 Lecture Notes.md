# 28 Chemistry of Transition Elements - Lecture Notes

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 sections 28.1-28.5, "Chemistry of transition elements". The local source checked is the 2025-2027 syllabus, pages 44-46. No local Chemistry coursebook has been verified, so the wording and scope are based on the syllabus only.

The syllabus focuses on the first row of transition elements from $\ce{Ti}$ to $\ce{Cu}$. The topic links electronic structure to variable oxidation states, catalysis, complex ions, colour, stereoisomerism, redox calculations, ligand exchange, and stability constants.

## What Counts as a Transition Element

CAIE defines a transition element as:

> a d-block element which forms one or more stable ions with incomplete d orbitals

The definition is about stable ions, not just the atom being in the d-block. For example, copper is included because $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ has an incomplete d subshell. Zinc is not normally treated as a transition element under this definition because $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ has a full $3d^{10}$ subshell.

When transition metal atoms form ions, the $4s$ electrons are removed before the $3d$ electrons. The energies of the $3d$ and $4s$ subshells are similar, and this is central to variable oxidation states.

## 3d Orbital Shapes

You should be able to sketch the shapes of a $3d_{xy}$ orbital and a $3d_{z^2}$ orbital.

- A $3d_{xy}$ orbital has four lobes in the $xy$ plane, between the $x$ and $y$ axes.
- A $3d_{z^2}$ orbital has two main lobes along the $z$ axis and a ring of electron density around the middle.

The sketches are used later to understand that d orbitals can point in different directions relative to ligands. The syllabus does not require detailed mathematical orbital theory here.

## Characteristic Properties

Transition elements have four characteristic properties in this topic:

- they have variable oxidation states
- they behave as catalysts
- they form complex ions
- they form coloured compounds

These properties come from accessible $3d$ and $4s$ energy levels and from vacant d orbitals that can accept electron pairs from ligands.

## Variable Oxidation States

Transition elements can form ions with different oxidation states because the $3d$ and $4s$ subshells are close in energy. Different numbers of electrons can be removed or used in bonding without an enormous jump in energy.

Examples include:

$$
\ce{Fe^{2+}} \text{ and } \ce{Fe^{3+}}
$$

$$
\ce{Cu+} \text{ and } \ce{Cu^{2+}}
$$

$$
\ce{Mn^{2+}} \text{ and } \ce{MnO4-}
$$

Variable oxidation states make transition elements useful in redox chemistry. An ion can accept or donate electrons and move between stable oxidation states.

## Catalytic Behaviour

Transition elements and their compounds often act as catalysts for two related reasons.

First, they can change oxidation state during a reaction and then be regenerated. This gives an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.

Second, they have vacant d orbitals that are energetically accessible and can form dative covalent bonds with ligands. Reactant molecules may bind to the metal centre, which can bring them close together, weaken bonds, or orient them for reaction.

A good catalytic explanation should not just say "transition metals are catalysts". It should mention more than one stable oxidation state or accessible vacant d orbitals, depending on the example.

## Ligands and Dative Covalent Bonds

A ligand is a species that contains a lone pair of electrons that forms a dative covalent bond to a central metal atom or ion.

The ligand donates both electrons in the bond. The metal centre accepts the electron pair into an available orbital.

Common ligand types in the syllabus are:

| Ligand type | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Monodentate | Donates one lone pair to one metal centre. | $\ce{H2O}$, $\ce{NH3}$, $\ce{Cl-}$, $\ce{CN-}$ |
| Bidentate | Donates two lone pairs to the same metal centre. | 1,2-diaminoethane, $\ce{H2NCH2CH2NH2}$, often called en; ethanedioate, $\ce{C2O4^2-}$ |
| Polydentate | Donates several lone pairs to the same metal centre. | $\ce{EDTA^4-}$ |

A complex is a molecule or ion formed by a central metal atom or ion surrounded by one or more ligands.

## Coordination Number, Formula, and Charge

The coordination number is the number of dative covalent bonds formed between ligands and the central metal atom or ion.

For monodentate ligands, the coordination number is the number of ligands attached. For bidentate ligands, each ligand makes two dative bonds, so each bidentate ligand contributes 2 to the coordination number.

Examples:

| Complex ion | Metal oxidation state | Ligands | Coordination number |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| $\ce{[Cu(H2O)6]^2+}$ | +2 | six $\ce{H2O}$ ligands | 6 |
| $\ce{[Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]^2+}$ | +2 | four $\ce{NH3}$ and two $\ce{H2O}$ ligands | 6 |
| $\ce{[CoCl4]^2-}$ | +2 | four $\ce{Cl-}$ ligands | 4 |
| $\ce{[Fe(CN)6]^3-}$ | +3 | six $\ce{CN-}$ ligands | 6 |
| $\ce{[Ni(en)3]^2+}$ | +2 | three bidentate en ligands | 6 |

To find the charge on a complex ion, add the metal ion charge and all ligand charges. Neutral ligands such as $\ce{H2O}$ and $\ce{NH3}$ do not change the charge. Negative ligands such as $\ce{Cl-}$, $\ce{CN-}$, $\ce{C2O4^2-}$, and $\ce{EDTA^4-}$ do.

## Shapes of Complexes

The syllabus requires linear, square planar, tetrahedral, and octahedral complexes.

| Coordination number | Common geometry | Typical bond angle |
|---:|---|---:|
| 2 | linear | $180^\circ$ |
| 4 | tetrahedral | about $109.5^\circ$ |
| 4 | square planar | $90^\circ$ |
| 6 | octahedral | $90^\circ$ |

Coordination number alone does not always fix the shape. Coordination number 4 may be tetrahedral or square planar, depending on the metal ion and ligands.

## Ligand Exchange

Ligand exchange occurs when one ligand in a complex is replaced by another ligand. The metal centre remains, but the coordination environment changes.

For copper(II), the starting aqueous ion is usually written as:

$$
\ce{[Cu(H2O)6]^2+}
$$

It is pale blue. With excess ammonia, ligand exchange forms a deep blue complex:

$$
\ce{[Cu(H2O)6]^2+(aq) + 4NH3(aq) <=> [Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]^2+(aq) + 4H2O(l)}
$$

With concentrated chloride ions, ligand exchange forms a chloride complex:

$$
\ce{[Cu(H2O)6]^2+(aq) + 4Cl-(aq) <=> [CuCl4]^2-(aq) + 6H2O(l)}
$$

The colour changes from blue to green or yellow-green as the ligand and geometry change.

For cobalt(II), the aqueous ion is:

$$
\ce{[Co(H2O)6]^2+}
$$

It is pink. With concentrated chloride ions:

$$
\ce{[Co(H2O)6]^2+(aq) + 4Cl-(aq) <=> [CoCl4]^2-(aq) + 6H2O(l)}
$$

The colour changes from pink to blue. Adding water reverses the equilibrium because water becomes the ligand in excess.

Hydroxide ions often form precipitates with copper(II) and cobalt(II) ions:

$$
\ce{[M(H2O)6]^2+(aq) + 2OH-(aq) -> [M(H2O)4(OH)2](s) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

where $\ce{M}$ may be $\ce{Cu}$ or $\ce{Co}$ in this context. A ligand exchange answer should include both the observation and the species responsible for it where possible.

## Redox Reactions and Standard Electrode Potentials

Transition elements often appear in redox systems because they have variable oxidation states. To predict feasibility using standard electrode potentials, combine the half-cell that is reduced with the half-cell that is oxidised.

The reaction is feasible under standard conditions if:

$$
E^\circ_\mathrm{cell} > 0
$$

The syllabus specifically names these systems in acid solution:

$$
\ce{MnO4- / C2O4^2-}
$$

$$
\ce{MnO4- / Fe^{2+}}
$$

and the reaction:

$$
\ce{Cu^{2+} / I-}
$$

Key equations include:

$$
\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}
$$

$$
\ce{C2O4^2- -> 2CO2 + 2e-}
$$

$$
\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5Fe^{2+} -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O + 5Fe^{3+}}
$$

$$
\ce{2MnO4- + 16H+ + 5C2O4^2- -> 2Mn^{2+} + 8H2O + 10CO2}
$$

$$
\ce{2Cu^{2+} + 4I- -> 2CuI(s) + I2}
$$

In calculations, use the mole ratio from the balanced equation. In feasibility questions, use the electrode potentials supplied in the question.

## Colour of Complexes

Free gaseous transition metal ions have degenerate d orbitals: the d orbitals are at the same energy. In a complex, ligands approach the metal ion and interact differently with different d orbitals. The d orbitals split into two non-degenerate sets.

In an octahedral complex:

- two d orbitals are higher in energy
- three d orbitals are lower in energy

In a tetrahedral complex:

- three d orbitals are higher in energy
- two d orbitals are lower in energy

The energy gap is called $\Delta E$. If an electron absorbs a photon with the right energy, it is promoted from a lower d orbital to a higher d orbital:

$$
\Delta E = h\nu
$$

The frequency of light absorbed is linked to $\Delta E$. The colour observed is the complementary colour to the colour absorbed.

Different ligands change $\Delta E$, so they change the frequency of light absorbed and therefore the colour observed. This is why ligand exchange in copper(II) and cobalt(II) complexes produces visible colour changes.

## Why Some Transition Metal Species Are Not Coloured

Colour from d-d transitions requires an electron that can be promoted between split d orbitals. Species with $d^0$ or $d^{10}$ configurations often do not show this kind of colour because there is no suitable d-d promotion.

This is a useful check, but the syllabus focus is on explaining colours in terms of non-degenerate d orbitals, $\Delta E$, frequency absorbed, and complementary colour observed.

## Stereoisomerism in Complexes

Stereoisomers have the same formula and the same bonding pattern, but a different arrangement in space.

### Geometrical Isomerism

Geometrical isomerism occurs when ligands can be arranged differently around the metal centre and cannot freely rotate into one another.

Square planar complexes can show cis/trans isomerism. For example:

$$
\ce{[Pt(NH3)2Cl2]}
$$

In the cis isomer, the two $\ce{Cl-}$ ligands are next to each other. In the trans isomer, they are opposite each other.

Octahedral complexes can also show cis/trans isomerism, for example:

$$
\ce{[Co(NH3)4(H2O)2]^2+}
$$

and complexes with bidentate ligands such as:

$$
\ce{[Ni(en)2(H2O)2]^2+}
$$

### Optical Isomerism

Optical isomers are non-superimposable mirror images. They are often formed by octahedral complexes with bidentate ligands because the chelate rings can arrange into left-handed and right-handed forms.

Examples include:

$$
\ce{[Ni(en)3]^2+}
$$

and:

$$
\ce{[Ni(en)2(H2O)2]^2+}
$$

where en means 1,2-diaminoethane, $\ce{H2NCH2CH2NH2}$.

### Overall Polarity

To deduce the overall polarity of a complex, consider the shape and whether bond dipoles cancel. A trans square planar or trans octahedral complex may be non-polar if equal dipoles oppose and cancel. A cis complex is often polar because the dipoles do not cancel symmetrically.

This is a geometry question: draw or imagine the arrangement before deciding polarity.

## Stability Constants

The stability constant, $K_\mathrm{stab}$, is the equilibrium constant for the formation of a complex ion in a solvent from its constituent ions or molecules.

For a general complex:

$$
\ce{M^{n+}(aq) + xL(aq) <=> [ML_x]^{n+}(aq)}
$$

where $\ce{L}$ is a neutral ligand, the expression is:

$$
K_\mathrm{stab} =
\frac{[\ce{[ML_x]^{n+}}]}{[\ce{M^{n+}}][\ce{L}]^x}
$$

If charged ligands are involved, include their concentrations in the expression with the correct powers. Water is not included in $K_\mathrm{stab}$ expressions.

For example:

$$
\ce{M^{2+}(aq) + EDTA^4-(aq) <=> [M(EDTA)]^2-(aq)}
$$

$$
K_\mathrm{stab} =
\frac{[\ce{[M(EDTA)]^2-}]}{[\ce{M^{2+}}][\ce{EDTA^4-}]}
$$

A large $K_\mathrm{stab}$ means the equilibrium lies far to the right and the complex ion is stable. Ligand exchange can be explained by comparing stability constants: the more stable complex is favoured at equilibrium.

## Answer Routines

For a complex ion formula:

1. Identify the metal ion charge.
2. Count ligand charges.
3. Use coordination number to count dative bonds.
4. Add charges to get the complex ion charge.

For a colour explanation:

1. State that ligands split degenerate d orbitals into non-degenerate sets.
2. Link $\Delta E$ to the frequency of light absorbed using $\Delta E = h\nu$.
3. State that the observed colour is complementary to the absorbed colour.
4. If ligand exchange occurs, say that a different ligand changes $\Delta E$.

For a stability constant:

1. Write the equilibrium equation.
2. Put the complex concentration in the numerator.
3. Put metal ion and ligand concentrations in the denominator with powers from coefficients.
4. Do not include water.

## Common Mistakes

- Defining a transition element as any d-block element without mentioning stable ions with incomplete d orbitals.
- Forgetting that ligands donate lone pairs to form dative covalent bonds.
- Losing charge when writing complex ion formulae.
- Treating coordination number as the number of ligands instead of the number of dative bonds.
- Saying the observed colour is the same as the absorbed colour.
- Including water in a $K_\mathrm{stab}$ expression.
- Ignoring acid conditions in $\ce{MnO4-}$ redox equations.
