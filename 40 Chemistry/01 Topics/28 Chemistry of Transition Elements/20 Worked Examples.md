# 28 Chemistry of Transition Elements - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Applying the Definition

**Question.** Explain why copper is treated as a transition element but zinc is not, using the CAIE definition.

**Working.**

A transition element is a d-block element that forms one or more stable ions with incomplete d orbitals.

Copper forms $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$, which has an incomplete d subshell. Zinc forms $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$, which has a full $3d^{10}$ subshell. Therefore zinc does not meet the definition, even though it is in the d-block.

**Answer.** Copper is a transition element because it forms stable $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ ions with incomplete d orbitals. Zinc is not, because $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ has a full $3d^{10}$ subshell.

**Check.** The answer refers to stable ions, not only atom positions in the periodic table.

## Example 2: Formula, Charge, and Coordination Number

**Question.** A complex contains $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ and six cyanide ligands, $\ce{CN-}$. Write the formula of the complex ion and state its coordination number and shape.

**Working.**

Each $\ce{CN-}$ ligand has charge $-1$. Six ligands give total ligand charge $-6$. The metal ion is $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$, so the overall charge is:

$$
+3 + (-6) = -3
$$

The complex ion is:

$$
\ce{[Fe(CN)6]^3-}
$$

Each $\ce{CN-}$ is monodentate, so six ligands form six dative covalent bonds. The coordination number is 6. Coordination number 6 complexes are usually octahedral.

**Answer.** $\ce{[Fe(CN)6]^3-}$, coordination number 6, octahedral.

**Check.** Neutral and charged ligands must be handled differently when finding the charge.

## Example 3: Ligand Exchange and Colour

**Question.** Concentrated hydrochloric acid is added to a pink solution containing $\ce{[Co(H2O)6]^2+}$. The solution turns blue. Explain the colour change.

**Working.**

The chloride ion acts as a ligand and replaces water ligands:

$$
\ce{[Co(H2O)6]^2+(aq) + 4Cl-(aq) <=> [CoCl4]^2-(aq) + 6H2O(l)}
$$

The ligand changes from $\ce{H2O}$ to $\ce{Cl-}$, and the complex changes from octahedral $\ce{[Co(H2O)6]^2+}$ to tetrahedral $\ce{[CoCl4]^2-}$. Different ligands and geometry change the splitting of d orbitals, $\Delta E$. A different frequency of light is absorbed, so a different complementary colour is observed.

**Answer.** The solution turns blue because ligand exchange forms $\ce{[CoCl4]^2-}$, changing $\Delta E$ and therefore the colour observed.

**Check.** The colour explanation links ligand exchange to d-orbital splitting, not just to a memorised colour.

## Example 4: Redox Titration with Acidified Manganate(VII)

**Question.** $25.00\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ of $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ solution is titrated with $0.0200\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$ acidified $\ce{MnO4-}$. The mean titre is $18.60\ \mathrm{cm^3}$. Calculate the concentration of $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$.

**Working.**

The balanced equation is:

$$
\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5Fe^{2+} -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O + 5Fe^{3+}}
$$

Moles of $\ce{MnO4-}$:

$$
n = 0.0200 \times \frac{18.60}{1000} = 3.72 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{mol}
$$

The mole ratio is:

$$
\ce{MnO4- : Fe^{2+}} = 1 : 5
$$

Moles of $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$:

$$
5 \times 3.72 \times 10^{-4} = 1.86 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{mol}
$$

Concentration of $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$:

$$
c = \frac{1.86 \times 10^{-3}}{25.00/1000}
= 0.0744\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}
$$

**Answer.** The concentration of $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ is $0.0744\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}$.

**Check.** Acidified $\ce{MnO4-}$ reacts with $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ in a $1:5$ mole ratio.

## Example 5: Using Electrode Potentials

**Question.** Use the following standard electrode potentials to decide whether acidified $\ce{MnO4-}$ can oxidise $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ to $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$.

$$
\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}
\quad E^\circ = +1.52\ \mathrm{V}
$$

$$
\ce{Fe^{3+} + e- -> Fe^{2+}}
\quad E^\circ = +0.77\ \mathrm{V}
$$

**Working.**

Manganate(VII) is reduced, so its half-cell stays as written. Iron(II) is oxidised, so the iron half-equation runs in reverse.

$$
E^\circ_\mathrm{cell} = E^\circ_\mathrm{reduction} - E^\circ_\mathrm{oxidation\ pair}
$$

$$
E^\circ_\mathrm{cell} = +1.52 - (+0.77) = +0.75\ \mathrm{V}
$$

Since $E^\circ_\mathrm{cell}$ is positive, the reaction is feasible under standard conditions.

**Answer.** Yes. Acidified $\ce{MnO4-}$ can oxidise $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ because $E^\circ_\mathrm{cell}=+0.75\ \mathrm{V}$.

**Check.** A positive cell potential supports feasibility under standard conditions.

## Example 6: Colour and Complementary Colour

**Question.** A complex absorbs orange light. What colour is likely to be observed, and why does ligand exchange change this colour?

**Working.**

The colour observed is complementary to the colour absorbed. The complement of orange is blue, so a blue colour is likely to be observed.

Ligand exchange changes the ligands around the metal ion. This changes the splitting of the d orbitals and therefore changes $\Delta E$. Since $\Delta E = h\nu$, a different frequency of light is absorbed. The complementary colour observed changes.

**Answer.** The complex is likely to appear blue. Ligand exchange changes $\Delta E$, so the absorbed frequency and observed complementary colour change.

**Check.** The observed colour is not the absorbed colour.

## Example 7: Geometrical Isomerism and Polarity

**Question.** $\ce{[Pt(NH3)2Cl2]}$ is square planar. Explain why it has cis/trans isomers and identify which isomer is more likely to be polar.

**Working.**

In a square planar complex, ligands occupy positions in one plane at $90^\circ$ intervals. The two $\ce{Cl-}$ ligands can be adjacent or opposite.

- In the cis isomer, the two $\ce{Cl-}$ ligands are adjacent.
- In the trans isomer, the two $\ce{Cl-}$ ligands are opposite.

The trans isomer is more symmetrical, so equal bond dipoles can cancel. The cis isomer is less symmetrical, so the dipoles are less likely to cancel.

**Answer.** $\ce{[Pt(NH3)2Cl2]}$ has cis/trans isomers because the two $\ce{Cl-}$ ligands can be adjacent or opposite in a square planar arrangement. The cis isomer is more likely to be polar.

**Check.** Polarity is decided from spatial arrangement, not just formula.

## Example 8: Stability Constant Calculation

**Question.** For the equilibrium:

$$
\ce{Ni^{2+}(aq) + 6NH3(aq) <=> [Ni(NH3)6]^2+(aq)}
$$

the equilibrium concentrations are:

$$
[\ce{[Ni(NH3)6]^2+}] = 0.120\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}
$$

$$
[\ce{Ni^{2+}}] = 2.00 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}
$$

$$
[\ce{NH3}] = 0.300\ \mathrm{mol\ dm^{-3}}
$$

Calculate $K_\mathrm{stab}$.

**Working.**

The expression is:

$$
K_\mathrm{stab} =
\frac{[\ce{[Ni(NH3)6]^2+}]}{[\ce{Ni^{2+}}][\ce{NH3}]^6}
$$

Substitute:

$$
K_\mathrm{stab} =
\frac{0.120}{(2.00 \times 10^{-5})(0.300)^6}
$$

$$
(0.300)^6 = 7.29 \times 10^{-4}
$$

$$
K_\mathrm{stab} =
\frac{0.120}{1.458 \times 10^{-8}}
= 8.23 \times 10^6
$$

**Answer.** $K_\mathrm{stab}=8.23 \times 10^6$.

**Check.** The ligand concentration is raised to the power 6, and water is not included.
