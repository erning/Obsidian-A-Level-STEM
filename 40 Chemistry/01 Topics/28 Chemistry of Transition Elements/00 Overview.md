# 28 Chemistry of Transition Elements

## Core Idea

Transition element chemistry links electronic structure to variable oxidation states, catalysis, complex ions, colour, stereoisomerism, and stability constants. It is a bridge between inorganic observations and equilibrium, redox, and bonding models.

## What to Learn

- Define a transition element using stable ions with incomplete d orbitals.
- Explain variable oxidation states, catalytic behaviour, and complex-ion formation.
- Use ligand, complex, coordination number, geometry, and ligand exchange accurately.
- Connect d-orbital splitting with the colours of complexes.
- Interpret stereoisomerism and stability constants for transition element complexes.

## How to Study

- Practise drawing or describing complex ions with charge, coordination number, and geometry together.
- Use oxidation state bookkeeping before writing transition metal redox equations.
- Treat colour as evidence linked to ligand field changes, not as a memorised label alone.
- Compare ligand exchange reactions using stability constants and equilibrium reasoning.

## Practice Directions

- Predict formulas and charges of complex ions from metal ions and ligands.
- Describe ligand exchange and colour changes for common copper(II) and cobalt(II) complexes.
- Use electrode potentials in transition metal redox calculations.
- Identify geometrical and optical isomerism in suitable complex ions.

## Common Traps

- Calling every d-block element a transition element without checking for an incomplete d orbital in a stable ion.
- Forgetting that ligands donate lone pairs to form dative covalent bonds.
- Losing charge when building a complex ion formula.
- Confusing the colour absorbed with the complementary colour observed.
