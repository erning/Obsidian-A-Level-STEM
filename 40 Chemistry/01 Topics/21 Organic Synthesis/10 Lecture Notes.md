# 21 Organic Synthesis - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow the CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level subject content for topic 21, **Organic synthesis**:

- 21.1 Organic synthesis

This topic does not introduce a separate list of new reactions. It asks you to use the reactions already in the AS syllabus to identify functional groups, predict properties and reactions, devise multi-step routes, and analyse routes in terms of reaction type, reagents, and possible by-products.

## Learning Scope

By the end of this topic you should be able to:

- identify organic functional groups using reactions in the syllabus;
- predict properties and reactions of molecules containing several functional groups;
- devise multi-step synthetic routes using AS reactions;
- analyse a given synthetic route by naming reaction types, reagents, conditions, and possible by-products.

## The Synthesis Mindset

Organic synthesis is controlled navigation through functional groups. You are usually asked to move from a starting molecule to a target molecule, using only known AS reactions.

For every step, ask four questions:

1. What functional group is changing?
2. What reagent and condition make that change?
3. What reaction type is it?
4. What else could form as a by-product or competing product?

A complete route is not just a list of compounds. It must include reagents and conditions.

## AS Functional Group Map

Use this map as a working toolkit. It is deliberately written as transformations, because synthesis questions ask how one compound becomes another.

| Transformation | Reagents and conditions | Reaction type |
|---|---|---|
| alkene $\to$ alkane | $\ce{H2(g)}$, Pt/Ni catalyst, heat | electrophilic addition / hydrogenation |
| alkene $\to$ alcohol | steam, $\ce{H2O(g)}$, $\ce{H3PO4}$ catalyst | electrophilic addition |
| alkene $\to$ halogenoalkane | $\ce{HX(g)}$, room temperature | electrophilic addition |
| alkene $\to$ diol | cold dilute acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ | oxidation |
| halogenoalkane $\to$ alcohol | $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$, heat | nucleophilic substitution |
| halogenoalkane $\to$ nitrile | $\ce{KCN}$ in ethanol, heat | nucleophilic substitution |
| halogenoalkane $\to$ primary amine | $\ce{NH3}$ in ethanol, heat under pressure | nucleophilic substitution |
| halogenoalkane $\to$ alkene | $\ce{NaOH}$ in ethanol, heat | elimination |
| alcohol $\to$ halogenoalkane | suitable halogenating reagent such as $\ce{HX}$, $\ce{PCl5}$, $\ce{PCl3}$ with heat, or $\ce{SOCl2}$ | substitution |
| alcohol $\to$ alkene | heated catalyst such as $\ce{Al2O3}$, or concentrated acid | elimination / dehydration |
| primary alcohol $\to$ aldehyde | acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or $\ce{KMnO4}$, distillation | oxidation |
| primary alcohol $\to$ carboxylic acid | acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or $\ce{KMnO4}$, reflux | oxidation |
| secondary alcohol $\to$ ketone | acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or $\ce{KMnO4}$, distillation | oxidation |
| aldehyde or ketone $\to$ alcohol | $\ce{NaBH4}$ or $\ce{LiAlH4}$ | reduction |
| aldehyde or ketone $\to$ hydroxynitrile | $\ce{HCN}$, $\ce{KCN}$ catalyst, heat | nucleophilic addition |
| aldehyde $\to$ carboxylic acid | acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or $\ce{KMnO4}$, reflux | oxidation |
| carboxylic acid + alcohol $\to$ ester | concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$ catalyst | condensation / esterification |
| ester $\to$ alcohol + acid or salt | dilute acid, or dilute alkali and heat | hydrolysis |
| nitrile $\to$ carboxylic acid | dilute acid, or dilute alkali followed by acidification | hydrolysis |
| carboxylic acid $\to$ primary alcohol | $\ce{LiAlH4}$ | reduction |
| alkene $\to$ addition polymer | suitable polymerisation conditions | addition polymerisation |

You do not need to use every route in every question. Choose the shortest valid AS route that changes the required functional group without damaging another part of the molecule.

## Functional Group Identification

Synthesis and analysis overlap. The syllabus expects you to identify functional groups using reactions in the syllabus.

| Test or evidence | Positive result | Functional group suggested |
|---|---|---|
| aqueous bromine | decolourised | $\ce{C=C}$ |
| 2,4-DNPH reagent | orange precipitate | carbonyl compound, aldehyde or ketone |
| Fehling's or Tollens' reagent | orange/red precipitate, or silver mirror/black precipitate | aldehyde |
| alkaline aqueous iodine | yellow precipitate of tri-iodomethane | $\ce{CH3CO-}$ in aldehyde/ketone or $\ce{CH3CH(OH)-}$ in alcohol |
| acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ changes from purple to colourless | oxidisable compound | often alkene, primary alcohol, secondary alcohol, aldehyde, or other oxidisable compound in context |
| carbonate gives effervescence; gas gives white precipitate with limewater | $\ce{CO2}$ formed | carboxylic acid |
| aqueous silver nitrate in ethanol after halogenoalkane reaction | silver halide precipitate | halogen in halogenoalkane |

Do not use tests mechanically. The same observation may fit more than one functional group unless the question context narrows it.

## Predicting Reactions in Molecules with Several Functional Groups

When a molecule contains several functional groups, each group may react independently. Good synthesis answers choose conditions that target the required group.

Example: $\ce{HOCH2CH2Br}$ contains both an alcohol group and a halogenoalkane group.

- $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ and heat can substitute $\ce{Br}$ to give a diol.
- Ethanolic $\ce{NaOH}$ and heat can eliminate $\ce{HBr}$ to give an alkene alcohol.
- A halogenating reagent can convert the alcohol group to another halogenoalkane.

You must look at the whole molecule, not only the first functional group you notice.

## Working Backwards

A reliable route-design method is retrosynthesis: start at the target and ask what precursor could make it using one AS step.

If the target is a carboxylic acid:

- it may come from oxidation of a primary alcohol or aldehyde;
- it may come from hydrolysis of a nitrile;
- it may come from hydrolysis of an ester followed by acidification.

If the target is an alcohol:

- it may come from hydration of an alkene;
- it may come from substitution of a halogenoalkane with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$;
- it may come from reduction of an aldehyde, ketone, or carboxylic acid;
- it may come from hydrolysis of an ester.

If the target has one more carbon atom than the starting halogenoalkane, consider the nitrile route.

## Possible By-Products and Route Analysis

The syllabus asks you to analyse possible by-products. Common AS examples include:

- free-radical substitution of alkanes can give a mixture of substituted products;
- addition of $\ce{HX}$ to an unsymmetrical alkene may form more than one positional product, with the major product explained by carbocation stability and Markovnikov addition;
- substitution and elimination can compete for halogenoalkanes depending on aqueous or ethanolic hydroxide conditions;
- esterification produces water as the condensation by-product;
- alkaline hydrolysis of esters produces a carboxylate salt rather than the free acid until acidified;
- incomplete combustion can produce carbon monoxide and unburnt hydrocarbons.

When analysing a given route, comment on these only when they are chemically relevant. Do not invent by-products unrelated to the reaction conditions.

## Worked-Thinking Routine for Route Questions

1. Count carbon atoms in the starting material and target.
2. Identify all functional groups in both.
3. Mark the bonds or groups that must change.
4. Search the AS map for one-step transformations.
5. If no one-step route exists, insert an intermediate.
6. Check that reagents and conditions do not change a functional group you need to preserve.
7. Write the route with reagents, conditions, and reaction types.

## Common Mistakes

- Giving only reagents, without conditions.
- Using reactions outside the AS syllabus when an AS route exists.
- Forgetting that $\ce{KCN}$ increases the carbon chain by one.
- Oxidising a primary alcohol under distillation when the target is a carboxylic acid.
- Reducing carboxylic acids with $\ce{NaBH4}$ instead of $\ce{LiAlH4}$.
- Ignoring competing substitution and elimination reactions.
- Naming a test result without connecting it to the functional group.

## Quick Self-Checks

1. How can you convert a halogenoalkane into an alcohol, nitrile, amine, or alkene?
2. Which routes make carboxylic acids?
3. Which AS reactions increase the carbon count by one?
4. How can you tell whether an alcohol oxidation should use distillation or reflux?
5. What should every step of a synthesis route include?
