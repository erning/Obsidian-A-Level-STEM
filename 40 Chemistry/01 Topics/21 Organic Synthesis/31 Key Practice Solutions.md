# 21 Organic Synthesis - Key Practice Solutions

## Solutions

1. Use $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ and heat. The reaction type is nucleophilic substitution.

   $$
   \ce{CH3CH2Cl ->[NaOH(aq),\ heat] CH3CH2OH}
   $$

2. Use $\ce{NaOH}$ in ethanol and heat. The reaction type is elimination.

   $$
   \ce{CH3CH2Cl ->[NaOH,\ ethanol,\ heat] CH2=CH2}
   $$

3. First use $\ce{KCN}$ in ethanol and heat, nucleophilic substitution, to form propanenitrile:

   $$
   \ce{CH3CH2Br ->[KCN,\ ethanol,\ heat] CH3CH2CN}
   $$

   Then hydrolyse the nitrile using dilute acid and heat, or dilute alkali and heat followed by acidification:

   $$
   \ce{CH3CH2CN ->[dilute\ acid,\ heat] CH3CH2COOH}
   $$

4. Hydrate ethene to ethanol:

   $$
   \ce{CH2=CH2 ->[steam,\ H3PO4] CH3CH2OH}
   $$

   Oxidise some ethanol to ethanoic acid using acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or $\ce{KMnO4}$ under reflux:

   $$
   \ce{CH3CH2OH + 2[O] -> CH3COOH + H2O}
   $$

   Esterify ethanoic acid with ethanol using concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$ as catalyst:

   $$
   \ce{CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH <=> CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O}
   $$

5. 2,4-DNPH shows a carbonyl compound. No silver mirror with Tollens' reagent suggests it is not an aldehyde, so the functional group is a ketone.

6. The functional group is $\ce{C=C}$ in an alkene.

7. To make propanal, use acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ and distillation:

   $$
   \ce{CH3CH2CH2OH + [O] -> CH3CH2CHO + H2O}
   $$

   Distillation removes the aldehyde before further oxidation. To make propanoic acid, heat under reflux so oxidation continues to the acid.

8. The reaction type is nucleophilic substitution. A balanced equation is:

   $$
   \ce{CH3CH2Br + 2NH3 -> CH3CH2NH2 + NH4Br}
   $$

   The inorganic by-product is ammonium bromide, $\ce{NH4Br}$.

9. The starting bromoethane has two carbons. The cyanide ion introduces a carbon that becomes part of the nitrile group, so propanenitrile has three carbons. Hydrolysis changes $\ce{-C#N}$ to $\ce{-COOH}$ without changing the carbon count.

10. Aldehyde to primary alcohol: $\ce{NaBH4}$ or $\ce{LiAlH4}$, reduction.

   Ketone to secondary alcohol: $\ce{NaBH4}$ or $\ce{LiAlH4}$, reduction.

   Carboxylic acid to primary alcohol: $\ce{LiAlH4}$, reduction.

11. $\ce{NaBH4}$ is suitable for reducing aldehydes and ketones, but the AS reagent for reducing carboxylic acids is $\ce{LiAlH4}$. Use $\ce{LiAlH4}$ to convert ethanoic acid to ethanol.

12. The alkene group can be identified by decolourising aqueous bromine. The carboxylic acid group can be identified by reaction with a carbonate to produce effervescence; the carbon dioxide gives a white precipitate with limewater.
