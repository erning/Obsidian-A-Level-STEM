# States of Matter - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Explaining Gas Pressure

Explain why the pressure of a gas in a sealed container increases when temperature increases.

**Working**

Gas pressure comes from collisions between gas molecules and the container walls. When temperature increases, molecules have greater average kinetic energy. They move faster, collide with the walls more often and collide with greater force.

**Answer:** pressure increases because faster molecules produce more frequent and more forceful collisions with the container walls.

## Example 2: Calculating Moles from $pV = nRT$

A gas occupies $500\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ at $101\ \mathrm{kPa}$ and $298\ \mathrm{K}$. Calculate the amount of gas. Use $R = 8.31\ \mathrm{J\ K^{-1}\ mol^{-1}}$.

**Working**

Convert units:

$$
p = 101\ \mathrm{kPa} = 101000\ \mathrm{Pa}
$$

$$
V = 500\ \mathrm{cm^3} = 500 \times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m^3} = 5.00 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{m^3}
$$

Rearrange:

$$
n = \frac{pV}{RT}
$$

Substitute:

$$
n = \frac{101000 \times 5.00 \times 10^{-4}}{8.31 \times 298}
$$

$$
n = 0.0204\ \mathrm{mol}
$$

**Answer:** $2.04 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{mol}$.

## Example 3: Determining Relative Molecular Mass

$0.580\ \mathrm{g}$ of a gas occupies $250\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ at $100\ \mathrm{kPa}$ and $300\ \mathrm{K}$. Calculate its $M_r$. Use $R = 8.31\ \mathrm{J\ K^{-1}\ mol^{-1}}$.

**Working**

Convert units:

$$
p = 100000\ \mathrm{Pa}
$$

$$
V = 250 \times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m^3} = 2.50 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{m^3}
$$

Use $pV = nRT$:

$$
n = \frac{pV}{RT} = \frac{100000 \times 2.50 \times 10^{-4}}{8.31 \times 300}
$$

$$
n = 0.0100\ \mathrm{mol}
$$

Molar mass:

$$
M = \frac{m}{n} = \frac{0.580}{0.0100} = 57.9\ \mathrm{g\ mol^{-1}}
$$

**Answer:** $M_r = 57.9$.

## Example 4: Ideal and Real Gas Assumptions

State the assumptions about particle volume and intermolecular forces in an ideal gas, and explain why a real gas may deviate from ideal behaviour at high pressure.

**Working**

An ideal gas has zero particle volume and no intermolecular forces of attraction. At high pressure, gas particles are close together. Their own volume becomes more significant compared with the volume of the container, and intermolecular attractions can become more important.

**Answer:** ideal gases have zero particle volume and no intermolecular attractions; real gases deviate at high pressure because particles are close enough for particle volume and attractions to matter.

## Example 5: Deducing Structure from Properties

A solid has a high melting point. It does not conduct electricity when solid, but it conducts when molten. Deduce its likely structure.

**Working**

High melting point suggests strong attractions in a giant structure. Not conducting as a solid means there are no mobile charged particles in the solid state. Conducting when molten suggests ions become mobile after melting.

**Answer:** giant ionic structure.

**Check:** This pattern matches substances such as $\ce{NaCl}$.

## Example 6: Comparing Diamond, Graphite and $\ce{C60}$

Compare the electrical conductivity of diamond, graphite and buckminsterfullerene, $\ce{C60}$.

**Working**

Diamond is a giant molecular structure. Each carbon forms four covalent bonds, so there are no delocalised electrons. It does not conduct electricity.

Graphite is a giant molecular structure in layers. Each carbon forms three covalent bonds, leaving one electron delocalised. These electrons can move along the layers, so graphite conducts.

$\ce{C60}$ is a simple molecular substance. Electrons are not free to move between molecules, so it does not conduct electricity.

**Answer:** graphite conducts electricity; diamond and $\ce{C60}$ do not.

## Example 7: Explaining Solubility and Conductivity of Sodium Chloride

Explain why solid $\ce{NaCl}$ does not conduct electricity but aqueous $\ce{NaCl}$ does.

**Working**

Solid $\ce{NaCl}$ contains ions, but they are fixed in a giant ionic lattice. They cannot move through the solid, so there are no mobile charge carriers.

In aqueous solution, the lattice has separated into hydrated $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ ions. These ions are mobile and carry charge.

**Answer:** solid $\ce{NaCl}$ does not conduct because its ions are fixed; aqueous $\ce{NaCl}$ conducts because its ions are mobile.
