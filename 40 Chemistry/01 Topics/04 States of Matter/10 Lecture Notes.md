# States of Matter

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 Syllabus 2025-2027, AS Level subject content, Physical chemistry, Topic 4 States of matter:

- 4.1 The gaseous state: ideal and real gases and $pV = nRT$
- 4.2 Bonding and structure

The topic has two linked aims: use the particle model and ideal gas equation for gases, then use structure and bonding to explain physical properties of solids.

## Gas Pressure

Gas molecules move randomly. When they collide with the walls of a container, they exert a force on the walls. Pressure is force per unit area, so gas pressure comes from many molecular collisions with the container walls.

If temperature increases, molecules have greater average kinetic energy. They move faster, collide more often and collide harder, so pressure increases if volume and amount are fixed.

If volume decreases at constant temperature and amount, molecules collide with the walls more often per unit area, so pressure increases.

## Ideal Gases

An ideal gas is a model gas. The syllabus defines ideal gases as having:

- zero particle volume
- no intermolecular forces of attraction

Real gas particles do have volume and can attract one another. Real gases behave most like ideal gases when pressure is low and temperature is high, because particles are far apart and attractions are less significant.

## The Ideal Gas Equation

The ideal gas equation is:

$$
pV = nRT
$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Common SI unit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| $p$ | pressure | $\mathrm{Pa}$ |
| $V$ | volume | $\mathrm{m^3}$ |
| $n$ | amount of substance | $\mathrm{mol}$ |
| $R$ | gas constant | $\mathrm{J\ K^{-1}\ mol^{-1}}$ |
| $T$ | temperature | $\mathrm{K}$ |

Use the value of $R$ given in the question or data booklet. With SI units, $R$ is commonly $8.31\ \mathrm{J\ K^{-1}\ mol^{-1}}$.

Unit conversions are often the main source of errors:

$$
1\ \mathrm{kPa} = 1000\ \mathrm{Pa}
$$

$$
1\ \mathrm{cm^3} = 1 \times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m^3}
$$

$$
T/\mathrm{K} = T/^\circ\mathrm{C} + 273
$$

To find relative molecular mass from gas data:

1. Use $pV = nRT$ to calculate moles of gas.
2. Use $M = \frac{m}{n}$.
3. For a molecular gas, the molar mass in $\mathrm{g\ mol^{-1}}$ has the same numerical value as $M_r$.

## Crystalline Solids and Lattices

A crystalline solid has a regular arrangement of particles. The particles and attractions depend on the type of structure.

The syllabus requires simple descriptions of these structures:

| Structure type | Particles | Attractions | Required examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| giant ionic | positive and negative ions | ionic bonds throughout lattice | $\ce{NaCl}$, $\ce{MgO}$ |
| simple molecular | molecules | intermolecular forces between molecules | iodine, $\ce{C60}$, ice |
| giant molecular | atoms in a covalent network | covalent bonds throughout structure | $\ce{SiO2}$, graphite, diamond |
| giant metallic | positive metal ions and delocalised electrons | metallic bonding | copper |

The term **giant molecular** is the CAIE syllabus wording here. This note keeps that wording and uses giant covalent only as a helpful synonym.

## Giant Ionic Structures

Giant ionic structures contain oppositely charged ions in a regular lattice. Strong electrostatic attractions act in many directions.

Properties:

- high melting and boiling points because many strong ionic bonds must be overcome
- do not conduct electricity when solid because ions are fixed in place
- conduct electricity when molten or aqueous because ions are mobile
- often soluble in water when attractions between ions and water molecules can overcome lattice attractions

Examples: $\ce{NaCl}$ and $\ce{MgO}$.

## Simple Molecular Structures

Simple molecular substances contain molecules. Atoms within each molecule are held by covalent bonds, but separate molecules are held by intermolecular forces.

Properties:

- low melting and boiling points compared with giant structures because intermolecular forces are usually weaker than ionic, covalent or metallic bonds
- do not usually conduct electricity because there are no mobile charged particles
- solubility depends on interactions with the solvent

Examples:

- iodine, $\ce{I2}$, has simple molecules with van der Waals' forces between molecules
- buckminsterfullerene, $\ce{C60}$, has covalent bonds within each molecule and intermolecular forces between molecules
- ice is a simple molecular solid with hydrogen bonds between water molecules

## Giant Molecular Structures

Giant molecular structures contain atoms joined by covalent bonds in a large network.

Diamond contains carbon atoms covalently bonded in a three-dimensional tetrahedral network. It has a very high melting point and does not conduct electricity because all outer electrons are used in covalent bonds.

Graphite contains carbon atoms covalently bonded in layers. Each carbon forms three covalent bonds, leaving one electron delocalised. Graphite conducts electricity along the layers because these delocalised electrons are mobile. Layers can slide over one another because forces between layers are weaker than covalent bonds within layers.

Silicon(IV) oxide, $\ce{SiO2}$, has a giant covalent network. Each silicon is bonded to oxygen atoms in a large structure, giving a high melting point.

## Giant Metallic Structures

Giant metallic structures contain positive metal ions in a lattice with delocalised electrons.

Copper is a required example. It conducts electricity because delocalised electrons are mobile. It has a high melting point because metallic bonding is strong in the giant lattice.

## Deducing Structure from Properties

When given physical property data, ask:

- Is the melting point high or low?
- Does the solid conduct electricity?
- Does the molten substance or aqueous solution conduct electricity?
- Is it soluble in water or in a non-polar solvent?
- Are there mobile ions or electrons?

Typical patterns:

| Evidence | Likely structure |
| --- | --- |
| high melting point, conducts when molten but not solid | giant ionic |
| low melting point, non-conductor | simple molecular |
| very high melting point, non-conductor | giant molecular such as diamond or $\ce{SiO2}$ |
| high melting point, conducts as solid | giant metallic or graphite |

Always explain the property using particles, structure and attraction. For example, "high melting point" is not enough; say which attractions require much energy to overcome.

## Working Routine

- For ideal-gas calculations, convert to Pa, m^3 and K before using pV = nRT.
- To deduce structure from properties, ask the four questions: melting point, solid conductivity, molten or aqueous conductivity, and solubility.
- To find Mr from gas data, first obtain n from pV = nRT, then use M = m/n.
## Common Mistakes

- Using kPa or cm3 directly in pV = nRT without converting to Pa and m3.
- Forgetting to convert degrees Celsius to kelvin.
- Saying simple molecular substances have low melting points because covalent bonds are weak; the covalent bonds are strong, the intermolecular forces are weak.
- Calling C60 a giant structure; it is simple molecular.
- Explaining graphite's conductivity without linking it to delocalised electrons in the layers.
