# States of Matter - Key Practice Problems

## Problems

1. Explain the origin of pressure in a gas in terms of molecular collisions.

2. State the two assumptions about particle volume and intermolecular forces in an ideal gas.

3. A gas occupies $2.40\ \mathrm{dm^3}$ at $100\ \mathrm{kPa}$ and $298\ \mathrm{K}$. Calculate the amount of gas using $R = 8.31\ \mathrm{J\ K^{-1}\ mol^{-1}}$.

4. $0.960\ \mathrm{g}$ of a gas occupies $400\ \mathrm{cm^3}$ at $120\ \mathrm{kPa}$ and $300\ \mathrm{K}$. Calculate its $M_r$ using $R = 8.31\ \mathrm{J\ K^{-1}\ mol^{-1}}$.

5. Explain why a real gas is more likely to behave ideally at low pressure and high temperature.

6. Describe the lattice structure of $\ce{NaCl}$ and explain why it has a high melting point.

7. Explain why solid $\ce{MgO}$ does not conduct electricity but molten $\ce{MgO}$ does.

8. Iodine has a much lower melting point than sodium chloride. Explain this difference in terms of structure and bonding.

9. Compare the structures and electrical conductivities of diamond and graphite.

10. Explain why copper conducts electricity as a solid.

11. A substance has a low melting point, does not conduct electricity and is insoluble in water. Suggest its likely structure and explain your reasoning.

12. A solid has a very high melting point and does not conduct electricity in any state. It is insoluble in water. Suggest a possible structure and give one example from the syllabus.
