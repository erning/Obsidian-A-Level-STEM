# Equilibria - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level Physical chemistry, Topic 7, Equilibria: 7.1 Chemical equilibria, reversible reactions and dynamic equilibrium, and 7.2 Brønsted-Lowry theory of acids and bases. The main emphasis is dynamic equilibrium, equilibrium constants and Le Chatelier's principle, while the acid-base outcomes are included because they are part of the verified Topic 7 scope. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## Reversible Reactions

A reversible reaction can occur in both directions. The forward reaction changes reactants into products, while the reverse reaction changes products back into reactants. Reversible reactions are written with an equilibrium sign:

$$
\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}
$$

The sign does not mean the reaction has stopped. It means both directions are possible.

## Dynamic Equilibrium

Dynamic equilibrium is established when:

- the forward and reverse reactions are both continuing;
- the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction;
- the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant;
- the system is closed, so substances cannot escape or enter.

The word dynamic matters. At equilibrium, particles are still reacting. The concentrations are constant because the two rates are equal, not because nothing is happening.

A closed system is needed. For a gas equilibrium, if a product gas escapes, the reverse reaction cannot maintain equilibrium. For a liquid system, if a reactant is continuously removed or added, the concentrations will not settle at equilibrium values.

## Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's principle states that if a change is made to a system at dynamic equilibrium, the position of equilibrium moves to minimise this change.

This principle predicts the direction of shift after a disturbance. It does not say that the disturbance is completely removed. The system responds partly, then settles at a new equilibrium position.

### Concentration

If the concentration of a reactant is increased, the equilibrium shifts to use up some of that reactant. If the concentration of a product is increased, the equilibrium shifts to use up some of that product.

For:

$$
\ce{CH3COOH(aq) + C2H5OH(aq) <=> CH3COOC2H5(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

adding more ethanol shifts the position to the right, producing more ester and water.

### Pressure

Pressure changes mainly affect equilibria involving gases. Increasing pressure shifts the equilibrium towards the side with fewer gas molecules. Decreasing pressure shifts it towards the side with more gas molecules.

For:

$$
\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}
$$

the left side has 4 moles of gas and the right side has 2 moles of gas. Increasing pressure shifts the position to the right.

If both sides have the same number of gas molecules, pressure has no effect on equilibrium position.

### Temperature

Temperature changes affect both equilibrium position and the value of the equilibrium constant.

For an exothermic forward reaction, heat can be treated as a product. Increasing temperature shifts equilibrium in the endothermic direction, so it shifts left. Decreasing temperature shifts it right.

For an endothermic forward reaction, heat can be treated as a reactant. Increasing temperature shifts equilibrium right. Decreasing temperature shifts it left.

### Catalyst

A catalyst provides a different pathway with lower activation energy. It increases the rates of both forward and reverse reactions. It does not change the position of equilibrium and does not change the value of the equilibrium constant. It only helps the system reach equilibrium faster.

## Equilibrium Constants In Concentration: $K_c$

For a general homogeneous equilibrium:

$$
\ce{aA + bB <=> cC + dD}
$$

the equilibrium constant in terms of concentration is:

$$
K_c = \frac{[\ce{C}]^c[\ce{D}]^d}{[\ce{A}]^a[\ce{B}]^b}
$$

Only species whose concentrations vary in the equilibrium expression are included. Pure solids and pure liquids are usually omitted because their concentrations are effectively constant.

For:

$$
\ce{H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)}
$$

the expression is:

$$
K_c = \frac{[\ce{HI}]^2}{[\ce{H2}][\ce{I2}]}
$$

The powers come from the coefficients in the balanced equation.

## Equilibrium Constants In Partial Pressure: $K_p$

For gas equilibria, equilibrium constants may be written in terms of partial pressures. The partial pressure of a gas is the pressure it would exert if it alone occupied the container.

Mole fraction is:

$$
\text{mole fraction of gas A} = \frac{\text{amount of gas A}}{\text{total amount of gas}}
$$

Partial pressure is:

$$
p_A = \text{mole fraction of A} \times \text{total pressure}
$$

For:

$$
\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}
$$

the $K_p$ expression is:

$$
K_p = \frac{p_{\ce{NH3}}^2}{p_{\ce{N2}}p_{\ce{H2}}^3}
$$

The syllabus does not require use of the relationship between $K_p$ and $K_c$.

## Calculating Equilibrium Quantities

Equilibrium calculations are usually easiest if you organise the amount of each species before writing the expression.

For concentration calculations:

1. Use the balanced equation.
2. List initial amounts or concentrations.
3. Use the stoichiometric ratio to find equilibrium amounts.
4. Convert amounts to concentrations if needed.
5. Substitute into $K_c$.

For pressure calculations:

1. Find equilibrium amounts of each gas.
2. Find total amount of gas.
3. Calculate mole fractions.
4. Calculate partial pressures.
5. Substitute into $K_p$.

AS calculations are not expected to require solving quadratic equations.

## What Changes The Equilibrium Constant?

The value of $K_c$ or $K_p$ for a reaction changes only when temperature changes.

Changing concentration or pressure changes the equilibrium position, but at the same temperature the system settles so that the equilibrium expression still has the same value.

A catalyst does not change the equilibrium constant. It changes the rate of approach to equilibrium.

For an exothermic forward reaction, increasing temperature decreases $K$ because the equilibrium shifts towards reactants. For an endothermic forward reaction, increasing temperature increases $K$ because the equilibrium shifts towards products.

## Industrial Examples

### Haber Process

The Haber process makes ammonia:

$$
\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}
$$

The forward reaction is exothermic and produces fewer moles of gas. Low temperature and high pressure would favour ammonia yield. In industry, a moderate temperature is used to give a reasonable rate, high pressure is used to improve yield without excessive cost, and an iron catalyst is used to increase rate without changing equilibrium position.

Typical conditions are about $450\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$, high pressure of about $200\ \mathrm{atm}$, and an iron catalyst. These are compromise conditions: lower temperature would give a higher equilibrium yield but a slower rate, while much higher pressure would improve yield but increase cost and safety demands.

### Contact Process

The Contact process includes the equilibrium:

$$
\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)}
$$

The forward reaction is exothermic and produces fewer moles of gas. Lower temperature and higher pressure favour $\ce{SO3}$, but industrial conditions balance yield, rate and cost. A $\ce{V2O5}$ catalyst increases the rate of reaching equilibrium.

Typical conditions are about $450\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$, a pressure close to atmospheric pressure, and a $\ce{V2O5}$ catalyst. A very high pressure is not usually needed because the equilibrium yield is already high under these conditions, so extra pressure would add cost for little gain.

## Brønsted-Lowry Acids And Bases

A Brønsted-Lowry acid is a proton donor. A Brønsted-Lowry base is a proton acceptor.

For example:

$$
\ce{HCl(aq) + H2O(l) -> H3O+(aq) + Cl-(aq)}
$$

$\ce{HCl}$ donates a proton, so it acts as an acid. Water accepts a proton, so it acts as a base.

The common acids named in the syllabus are hydrochloric acid, $\ce{HCl}$; sulfuric acid, $\ce{H2SO4}$; nitric acid, $\ce{HNO3}$; and ethanoic acid, $\ce{CH3COOH}$.

The common alkalis named in the syllabus are sodium hydroxide, $\ce{NaOH}$; potassium hydroxide, $\ce{KOH}$; and ammonia, $\ce{NH3}$.

## Strong And Weak Acids And Bases

Strong acids and strong bases are fully dissociated in aqueous solution. Weak acids and weak bases are partially dissociated in aqueous solution.

This is about extent of dissociation, not concentration. A dilute strong acid can have a low concentration but still be fully dissociated. A concentrated weak acid can have a high concentration but still be only partially dissociated.

Water has pH 7. Acid solutions have pH below 7. Alkaline solutions have pH above 7.

Strong and weak acids behave differently:

- at the same concentration, a strong acid has lower pH than a weak acid;
- a strong acid usually reacts faster with a reactive metal because it has a higher concentration of $\ce{H+}$ ions;
- a strong acid has higher conductivity than a weak acid at the same concentration because it contains more ions.

## Neutralisation, Salts And Titration Curves

Neutralisation occurs when $\ce{H+(aq)}$ and $\ce{OH-(aq)}$ form water:

$$
\ce{H+(aq) + OH-(aq) -> H2O(l)}
$$

Salts are formed in neutralisation reactions. For example:

$$
\ce{HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) -> NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

AS work includes sketching pH titration curves for combinations of strong and weak acids with strong and weak alkalis. The steep vertical section is important because an indicator should change colour within the sharp pH change near the end point. Suitable indicators are chosen from given transition-range data; $pK_a$ values are not required.

Typical curve patterns:

- strong acid with strong alkali: large vertical section, equivalence point near pH 7;
- strong acid with weak alkali: equivalence point below pH 7;
- weak acid with strong alkali: equivalence point above pH 7;
- weak acid with weak alkali: smaller pH change near equivalence, so indicator choice is more difficult.

## Working Routine

For a Le Chatelier question, state the disturbance, identify which direction minimises it, then describe the new position. Mention rate only if a catalyst or temperature-rate point is being asked.

For a $K_c$ or $K_p$ question, write the expression first. Then calculate concentrations or partial pressures and substitute carefully.

For an acid-base question, decide whether the question is about proton transfer, extent of dissociation, pH trend, neutralisation or indicator choice. Do not mix up strong with concentrated.

## Common Mistakes

- Saying equilibrium means the reaction has stopped; both rates continue but are equal.
- Forgetting that only temperature changes the value of K.
- Mixing up a shift in position with a change in K.
- Writing solids or pure liquids into a Kc expression.
- Confusing a strong acid with a concentrated acid.
