# Equilibria - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Applying Le Chatelier's Principle

**Question.** For the equilibrium:

$$
\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}
$$

the forward reaction is exothermic. Predict the effect of increasing pressure, increasing temperature and adding an iron catalyst.

**Working.**

Increasing pressure favours the side with fewer gas molecules. The left side has 4 moles of gas; the right side has 2 moles. The equilibrium shifts right, increasing the equilibrium yield of $\ce{NH3}$.

Increasing temperature favours the endothermic direction. Since the forward reaction is exothermic, the reverse reaction is endothermic. The equilibrium shifts left, decreasing the equilibrium yield of $\ce{NH3}$. The value of $K$ decreases.

Adding an iron catalyst increases the rates of the forward and reverse reactions. It does not change the equilibrium position or the value of $K$.

**Check.** Pressure and temperature can change equilibrium position; catalyst changes only rate of reaching equilibrium.

## Example 2: Writing A $K_c$ Expression

**Question.** Write the $K_c$ expression for:

$$
\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)}
$$

**Working.**

Products go on top, reactants on the bottom. Coefficients become powers.

$$
K_c = \frac{[\ce{SO3}]^2}{[\ce{SO2}]^2[\ce{O2}]}
$$

**Check.** The coefficient 2 in front of $\ce{SO2}$ and $\ce{SO3}$ appears as a square in the expression.

## Example 3: Calculating $K_c$

**Question.** In a $2.00\ \mathrm{dm^3}$ flask at equilibrium, there are $0.200\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{H2}$, $0.200\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{I2}$ and $1.20\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{HI}$ for:

$$
\ce{H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)}
$$

Calculate $K_c$.

**Working.**

Equilibrium concentrations:

$$
[\ce{H2}] = \frac{0.200}{2.00} = 0.100\ \mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}
$$

$$
[\ce{I2}] = \frac{0.200}{2.00} = 0.100\ \mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}
$$

$$
[\ce{HI}] = \frac{1.20}{2.00} = 0.600\ \mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}
$$

Expression:

$$
K_c = \frac{[\ce{HI}]^2}{[\ce{H2}][\ce{I2}]}
$$

Substitute:

$$
K_c = \frac{(0.600)^2}{(0.100)(0.100)} = 36.0
$$

**Check.** The large value means products are favoured at this temperature.

## Example 4: Partial Pressures And $K_p$

**Question.** At equilibrium for:

$$
\ce{N2O4(g) <=> 2NO2(g)}
$$

the mixture contains $0.30\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{N2O4}$ and $0.70\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{NO2}$. The total pressure is $200\ \mathrm{kPa}$. Calculate the partial pressures and write $K_p$ in terms of these values.

**Working.**

Total amount of gas:

$$
0.30 + 0.70 = 1.00\ \mathrm{mol}
$$

Mole fractions:

$$
x_{\ce{N2O4}} = 0.30
$$

$$
x_{\ce{NO2}} = 0.70
$$

Partial pressures:

$$
p_{\ce{N2O4}} = 0.30 \times 200 = 60\ \mathrm{kPa}
$$

$$
p_{\ce{NO2}} = 0.70 \times 200 = 140\ \mathrm{kPa}
$$

The expression is:

$$
K_p = \frac{p_{\ce{NO2}}^2}{p_{\ce{N2O4}}}
$$

Substitute if required:

$$
K_p = \frac{(140)^2}{60} = 327\ \mathrm{kPa}
$$

**Check.** Partial pressures add to the total pressure: $60 + 140 = 200\ \mathrm{kPa}$.

## Example 5: Equilibrium Amounts From $K_c$

**Question.** For:

$$
\ce{A(g) + B(g) <=> C(g)}
$$

a $1.00\ \mathrm{dm^3}$ flask initially contains $1.00\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{A}$ and $1.00\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{B}$. At equilibrium, $0.40\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{C}$ is present. Calculate $K_c$.

**Working.**

If $0.40\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{C}$ forms, $0.40\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{A}$ and $0.40\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\ce{B}$ are used.

Equilibrium amounts:

$$
n(\ce{A}) = 1.00 - 0.40 = 0.60\ \mathrm{mol}
$$

$$
n(\ce{B}) = 1.00 - 0.40 = 0.60\ \mathrm{mol}
$$

$$
n(\ce{C}) = 0.40\ \mathrm{mol}
$$

Because volume is $1.00\ \mathrm{dm^3}$, these numbers equal the concentrations in $\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}$.

$$
K_c = \frac{[\ce{C}]}{[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]}
$$

$$
K_c = \frac{0.40}{(0.60)(0.60)} = 1.11
$$

**Check.** This AS calculation does not require solving a quadratic equation because the equilibrium amount of product was given.

## Example 6: Strong And Weak Acid Evidence

**Question.** Two acids have the same concentration. Acid X has pH 1 and reacts rapidly with magnesium. Acid Y has pH 3 and reacts more slowly with magnesium. Explain which acid is stronger.

**Working.**

At the same concentration, the stronger acid is more fully dissociated and produces a higher concentration of $\ce{H+}$ ions.

Acid X has lower pH, so it has a higher concentration of $\ce{H+}$ ions. It also reacts faster with magnesium, which is consistent with more frequent effective collisions involving $\ce{H+}$.

**Answer.** Acid X is stronger. Acid Y is weaker because it is only partially dissociated.

**Check.** Strong does not mean concentrated. The question states equal concentration, so pH and reaction behaviour compare extent of dissociation.
