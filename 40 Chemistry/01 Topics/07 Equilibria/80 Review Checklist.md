# Equilibria - Review Checklist

## Recall

- A reversible reaction can proceed in both directions.
- Dynamic equilibrium requires a closed system, equal forward and reverse rates, and constant concentrations.
- Le Chatelier's principle predicts how equilibrium position moves to minimise a change.
- Increasing pressure favours fewer moles of gas.
- Increasing temperature favours the endothermic direction.
- A catalyst does not change equilibrium position or $K$.
- Only temperature changes the value of $K_c$ or $K_p$.
- Brønsted-Lowry acids donate protons; Brønsted-Lowry bases accept protons.

## Skills

- Write $K_c$ expressions with correct powers.
- Calculate equilibrium concentrations from amounts and volumes.
- Use mole fraction to calculate partial pressure.
- Write $K_p$ expressions for gas equilibria.
- Predict qualitative shifts from concentration, pressure and temperature changes.
- Explain Haber process and Contact process conditions using equilibrium and rate.
- Distinguish strong and weak acids from concentrated and dilute acids.
- Sketch qualitative titration curves and choose indicators from transition ranges.

## Common Errors To Avoid

- Saying equilibrium means equal amounts of reactants and products.
- Forgetting the closed system condition.
- Saying a catalyst increases yield.
- Using total pressure where partial pressure is required.
- Forgetting coefficients become powers in $K_c$ and $K_p$.
- Claiming concentration changes alter $K$ at constant temperature.
- Treating strong acid and concentrated acid as the same idea.

## Ready To Move On When

- You can define dynamic equilibrium precisely.
- You can apply Le Chatelier's principle without mixing up rate and equilibrium position.
- You can write and use $K_c$ and $K_p$ expressions from balanced equations.
- You can calculate partial pressures from mole fractions.
- You can explain why industrial conditions are compromises.
- You can compare strong and weak acids using dissociation, pH, conductivity and reaction with metals.
