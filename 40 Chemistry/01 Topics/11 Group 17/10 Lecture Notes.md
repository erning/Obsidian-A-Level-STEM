# Group 17 - Lecture Notes

## Learning Scope

This note follows CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level section 11. It covers physical properties of chlorine, bromine and iodine; halogen reactivity; hydrogen halides; halide ion tests with silver ions, aqueous ammonia and concentrated sulfuric acid; and chlorine reactions with sodium hydroxide and water. No Chemistry coursebook alignment is claimed.

The halogens in this AS topic are:

$$
\ce{Cl2, Br2, I2}
$$

Their halide ions are:

$$
\ce{Cl-, Br-, I-}
$$

## Colours and Physical States

| Halogen | Colour and state at room temperature | Vapour colour |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{Cl2}$ | Pale green gas | Pale green |
| $\ce{Br2}$ | Red-brown liquid | Orange-brown |
| $\ce{I2}$ | Grey-black solid | Purple vapour |

Volatility decreases down the group. Chlorine is a gas, bromine is a volatile liquid, and iodine is a solid that sublimes when warmed.

## Volatility and Intermolecular Forces

Halogen molecules are non-polar diatomic molecules. The main intermolecular forces are instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces.

Down the group:

- Molecules contain more electrons.
- Electron clouds are larger and more easily distorted.
- Instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces become stronger.
- More energy is needed to separate molecules.

Therefore boiling point increases and volatility decreases from chlorine to iodine.

## Halogen-Halogen Bond Strength

The bond strength of the halogen molecules decreases down the group:

$$
\ce{Cl-Cl > Br-Br > I-I}
$$

As atoms become larger, the bonding pair is further from the nuclei and orbital overlap is less effective. The covalent bond is weaker. This helps explain why the hydrogen halides become less thermally stable down the group.

## Halogens as Oxidising Agents

Halogens gain electrons to form halide ions:

$$
\ce{X2 + 2e- -> 2X-}
$$

The oxidising strength decreases down the group:

$$
\ce{Cl2 > Br2 > I2}
$$

Chlorine is the strongest oxidising agent of these three. It can oxidise bromide and iodide ions:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + 2Br-(aq) -> 2Cl-(aq) + Br2(aq)}
$$

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + 2I-(aq) -> 2Cl-(aq) + I2(aq)}
$$

Bromine can oxidise iodide ions:

$$
\ce{Br2(aq) + 2I-(aq) -> 2Br-(aq) + I2(aq)}
$$

Iodine cannot oxidise chloride or bromide ions under these conditions.

## Halide Ions as Reducing Agents

Halide ions lose electrons to form halogen molecules:

$$
\ce{2X- -> X2 + 2e-}
$$

Reducing strength increases down the group:

$$
\ce{I- > Br- > Cl-}
$$

Iodide ions are the strongest reducing agents because the outer electrons are furthest from the nucleus and most shielded. They are most easily lost.

This trend is the opposite of the halogen oxidising strength trend.

## Reactions of Halogens with Hydrogen

Halogens react with hydrogen to form hydrogen halides:

$$
\ce{H2(g) + Cl2(g) -> 2HCl(g)}
$$

$$
\ce{H2(g) + Br2(g) -> 2HBr(g)}
$$

$$
\ce{H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)}
$$

The reactivity decreases down the group. Chlorine reacts readily, especially in light. Bromine reacts less vigorously. Iodine reacts least readily and the reaction is reversible.

## Thermal Stability of Hydrogen Halides

Thermal stability decreases down the group:

$$
\ce{HCl > HBr > HI}
$$

The $\ce{H-X}$ bond becomes longer and weaker down the group as the halogen atom gets larger. A weaker bond breaks more easily when heated, so hydrogen iodide is the least thermally stable.

## Halide Tests with Silver Ions and Aqueous Ammonia

Halide ions form silver halide precipitates with aqueous silver ions:

$$
\ce{Ag+(aq) + Cl-(aq) -> AgCl(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{Ag+(aq) + Br-(aq) -> AgBr(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{Ag+(aq) + I-(aq) -> AgI(s)}
$$

| Halide ion | Silver halide precipitate | Effect of aqueous ammonia |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{Cl-}$ | White $\ce{AgCl}$ | Dissolves in dilute aqueous ammonia |
| $\ce{Br-}$ | Cream $\ce{AgBr}$ | Dissolves in concentrated aqueous ammonia |
| $\ce{I-}$ | Yellow $\ce{AgI}$ | Insoluble in concentrated aqueous ammonia |

The syllabus does not require the formula of the silver-ammonia complex. In practical descriptions, acidifying with dilute nitric acid before adding silver nitrate removes interfering carbonate or hydroxide ions.

## Halide Ions with Concentrated Sulfuric Acid

Concentrated sulfuric acid reacts differently with chloride, bromide and iodide ions because reducing strength increases down the group.

### Chloride Ions

Chloride ions are not strong enough reducing agents to reduce concentrated sulfuric acid. The main reaction is acid-base:

$$
\ce{NaCl(s) + H2SO4(l) -> NaHSO4(s) + HCl(g)}
$$

Observation: steamy fumes of $\ce{HCl}$.

### Bromide Ions

Bromide ions reduce concentrated sulfuric acid to sulfur dioxide. Hydrogen bromide first forms, then is oxidised:

$$
\ce{NaBr(s) + H2SO4(l) -> NaHSO4(s) + HBr(g)}
$$

$$
\ce{2HBr(g) + H2SO4(l) -> Br2(g) + SO2(g) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

Observations include steamy fumes, orange-brown bromine and choking $\ce{SO2}$.

### Iodide Ions

Iodide ions are stronger reducing agents. They reduce concentrated sulfuric acid further, giving iodine and reduction products such as $\ce{SO2}$, sulfur or $\ce{H2S}$:

$$
\ce{NaI(s) + H2SO4(l) -> NaHSO4(s) + HI(g)}
$$

$$
\ce{2HI(g) + H2SO4(l) -> I2(s) + SO2(g) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{6HI(g) + H2SO4(l) -> 3I2(s) + S(s) + 4H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{8HI(g) + H2SO4(l) -> 4I2(s) + H2S(g) + 4H2O(l)}
$$

Observations can include purple iodine vapour or black iodine solid, yellow sulfur and the smell of $\ce{H2S}$.

## Chlorine with Cold Aqueous Sodium Hydroxide

Chlorine disproportionates in cold dilute aqueous sodium hydroxide:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) -> NaCl(aq) + NaClO(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

Oxidation numbers:

- In $\ce{Cl2}$, chlorine is 0.
- In $\ce{NaCl}$, chlorine is -1.
- In $\ce{NaClO}$, chlorine is +1.

The same element is both reduced and oxidised, so this is disproportionation.

## Chlorine with Hot Aqueous Sodium Hydroxide

With hot concentrated aqueous sodium hydroxide, chlorine disproportionates further:

$$
\ce{3Cl2(aq) + 6NaOH(aq) -> 5NaCl(aq) + NaClO3(aq) + 3H2O(l)}
$$

Oxidation numbers:

- Chlorine in $\ce{Cl2}$ is 0.
- Chlorine in $\ce{NaCl}$ is -1.
- Chlorine in $\ce{NaClO3}$ is +5.

Again, chlorine is both reduced and oxidised.

## Chlorine in Water Purification

Chlorine is used in water purification because it forms species that kill bacteria. In water:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + H2O(l) <=> HCl(aq) + HOCl(aq)}
$$

Hypochlorous acid can form the chlorate(I) ion:

$$
\ce{HOCl(aq) <=> H+(aq) + ClO-(aq)}
$$

The active species $\ce{HOCl}$ and $\ce{ClO-}$ kill bacteria. A useful ionic equation in alkaline solution is:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + 2OH-(aq) -> Cl-(aq) + ClO-(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

## Working Routine

- For oxidising and reducing strength, use the trends Cl2 > Br2 > I2 (oxidising) and I- > Br- > Cl- (reducing).
- To identify a halide, acidify with dilute nitric acid, add silver nitrate, note the precipitate colour, then test its solubility in ammonia.
- For chlorine disproportionation, assign oxidation numbers to every chlorine-containing species.
## Common Mistakes

- Reversing the oxidising-strength trend; chlorine is the strongest oxidising agent, not iodine.
- Adding silver nitrate without first acidifying, so carbonate or hydroxide ions interfere.
- Forgetting that iodide can reduce concentrated sulfuric acid as far as hydrogen sulfide or sulfur.
- Identifying a halogen from its colour alone without confirmatory tests.
- Failing to recognise the reaction of chlorine with sodium hydroxide as disproportionation.
