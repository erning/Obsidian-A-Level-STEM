# Group 17 - Key Practice Solutions

## Solutions

1. Chlorine, $\ce{Cl2}$, is a pale green gas. Bromine, $\ce{Br2}$, is a red-brown liquid with orange-brown vapour. Iodine, $\ce{I2}$, is a grey-black solid with purple vapour when warmed.

2. Volatility decreases down the group because instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces become stronger. Down the group, halogen molecules have more electrons and larger electron clouds. These are more easily distorted, so stronger intermolecular forces require more energy to overcome.

3. $\ce{X-X}$ bond strength decreases down the group:

$$
\ce{Cl-Cl > Br-Br > I-I}
$$

The atoms become larger, so the bonding pair is further from the nuclei and orbital overlap is less effective.

4. Oxidising strength decreases down the group:

$$
\ce{Cl2 > Br2 > I2}
$$

Chlorine oxidises iodide ions:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + 2I-(aq) -> 2Cl-(aq) + I2(aq)}
$$

Iodine forms, seen as a brown solution or purple colour in an organic solvent if used.

5. Reducing strength of halide ions increases down the group:

$$
\ce{I- > Br- > Cl-}
$$

Iodide ions are largest and their outer electrons are most shielded from the nucleus. They lose electrons most easily, so they are the strongest reducing agents.

6. The equations are:

$$
\ce{H2(g) + Cl2(g) -> 2HCl(g)}
$$

$$
\ce{H2(g) + Br2(g) -> 2HBr(g)}
$$

$$
\ce{H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)}
$$

Reactivity decreases from chlorine to iodine. Chlorine reacts readily, bromine reacts less vigorously, and iodine reacts least readily with a reversible reaction.

7. Thermal stability decreases:

$$
\ce{HCl > HBr > HI}
$$

The $\ce{H-X}$ bond becomes longer and weaker down the group as the halogen atom becomes larger. The weaker $\ce{H-I}$ bond breaks most easily on heating.

8. The halide ion is iodide, $\ce{I-}$. Yellow $\ce{AgI}$ is insoluble in concentrated aqueous ammonia:

$$
\ce{Ag+(aq) + I-(aq) -> AgI(s)}
$$

9. Sodium chloride gives mainly an acid-base reaction:

$$
\ce{NaCl(s) + H2SO4(l) -> NaHSO4(s) + HCl(g)}
$$

The observation is steamy fumes of $\ce{HCl}$. Chloride ions are not strong enough reducing agents to reduce concentrated sulfuric acid.

10. First hydrogen iodide forms:

$$
\ce{NaI(s) + H2SO4(l) -> NaHSO4(s) + HI(g)}
$$

Iodide then reduces sulfuric acid to hydrogen sulfide:

$$
\ce{8HI(g) + H2SO4(l) -> 4I2(s) + H2S(g) + 4H2O(l)}
$$

Iodide is oxidised from -1 to 0 in iodine. Sulfur is reduced from +6 in sulfuric acid to -2 in hydrogen sulfide.

11. Cold aqueous sodium hydroxide:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) -> NaCl(aq) + NaClO(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

Hot aqueous sodium hydroxide:

$$
\ce{3Cl2(aq) + 6NaOH(aq) -> 5NaCl(aq) + NaClO3(aq) + 3H2O(l)}
$$

In both reactions, chlorine starts at oxidation number 0. It becomes -1 in $\ce{NaCl}$. It becomes +1 in $\ce{NaClO}$ or +5 in $\ce{NaClO3}$. The same element is both reduced and oxidised, so the reactions are disproportionation reactions.

12. Chlorine reacts with water to form hypochlorous acid:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + H2O(l) <=> HCl(aq) + HOCl(aq)}
$$

Hypochlorous acid can form chlorate(I) ions:

$$
\ce{HOCl(aq) <=> H+(aq) + ClO-(aq)}
$$

The active species $\ce{HOCl}$ and $\ce{ClO-}$ kill bacteria. In alkaline solution:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + 2OH-(aq) -> Cl-(aq) + ClO-(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$
