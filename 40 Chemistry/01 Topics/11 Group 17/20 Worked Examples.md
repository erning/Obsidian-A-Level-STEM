# Group 17 - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Explain Volatility

**Question.** Chlorine is a gas, bromine is a liquid and iodine is a solid at room temperature. Explain this trend.

**Working.**

The halogens are non-polar diatomic molecules. The main intermolecular forces are instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces. Down the group, molecules have more electrons and larger electron clouds. These are more easily distorted, so the intermolecular forces become stronger.

More energy is needed to separate the molecules, so boiling point increases and volatility decreases from chlorine to iodine.

**Check.** This explanation is about intermolecular forces, not the covalent $\ce{X-X}$ bond inside each molecule.

## Example 2: Predict Displacement Reactions

**Question.** Chlorine water is added to aqueous potassium bromide, and bromine water is added to aqueous potassium chloride. Predict what happens and write any ionic equation.

**Working.**

Chlorine is a stronger oxidising agent than bromine, so chlorine oxidises bromide ions:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + 2Br-(aq) -> 2Cl-(aq) + Br2(aq)}
$$

An orange-brown colour from bromine appears.

Bromine is not a strong enough oxidising agent to oxidise chloride ions, so there is no reaction with aqueous potassium chloride.

**Check.** Halogens displace halide ions below them in Group 17.

## Example 3: Hydrogen Halide Stability

**Question.** Explain why hydrogen iodide is less thermally stable than hydrogen chloride.

**Working.**

Iodine atoms are larger than chlorine atoms. The $\ce{H-I}$ bond is longer and weaker than the $\ce{H-Cl}$ bond because orbital overlap is less effective. A weaker bond breaks more easily when heated, so $\ce{HI}$ is less thermally stable than $\ce{HCl}$.

**Check.** Thermal stability of hydrogen halides follows bond strength, not boiling point.

## Example 4: Identify Halide Ions with Silver Nitrate and Ammonia

**Question.** A solution gives a cream precipitate with aqueous silver nitrate. The precipitate dissolves in concentrated aqueous ammonia but not in dilute aqueous ammonia. Identify the halide ion.

**Working.**

Silver halide precipitates:

- $\ce{AgCl}$ is white and dissolves in dilute aqueous ammonia.
- $\ce{AgBr}$ is cream and dissolves in concentrated aqueous ammonia.
- $\ce{AgI}$ is yellow and insoluble in concentrated aqueous ammonia.

The halide ion is bromide, $\ce{Br-}$.

The precipitation equation is:

$$
\ce{Ag+(aq) + Br-(aq) -> AgBr(s)}
$$

**Check.** Use both colour and ammonia solubility. Colour alone can be difficult in practice.

## Example 5: Concentrated Sulfuric Acid with Bromide

**Question.** Sodium bromide reacts with concentrated sulfuric acid. Write equations and explain the redox change.

**Working.**

First, hydrogen bromide forms:

$$
\ce{NaBr(s) + H2SO4(l) -> NaHSO4(s) + HBr(g)}
$$

Then bromide in $\ce{HBr}$ reduces sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{2HBr(g) + H2SO4(l) -> Br2(g) + SO2(g) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

Bromine is formed because bromide ions are oxidised from -1 to 0. Sulfur in sulfuric acid is reduced from +6 to +4 in $\ce{SO2}$.

**Check.** Chloride gives mainly an acid-base reaction, while bromide and iodide give redox reactions.

## Example 6: Chlorine Disproportionation

**Question.** Chlorine reacts with cold aqueous sodium hydroxide. Write the equation and show why it is disproportionation.

**Working.**

The equation is:

$$
\ce{Cl2(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) -> NaCl(aq) + NaClO(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

Oxidation numbers:

- Chlorine in $\ce{Cl2}$ is 0.
- Chlorine in $\ce{NaCl}$ is -1, so chlorine is reduced.
- Chlorine in $\ce{NaClO}$ is +1, so chlorine is oxidised.

The same element is both oxidised and reduced, so the reaction is disproportionation.

**Check.** In hot concentrated sodium hydroxide, the product is $\ce{NaClO3}$ instead of $\ce{NaClO}$.
