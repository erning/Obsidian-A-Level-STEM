# 34 Nitrogen Compounds - Key Practice Solutions

1. Use ammonia in ethanol, heated under pressure.

$$
\ce{RBr + NH3 -> RNH2 + HBr}
$$

2. React the halogenoalkane with a primary amine in ethanol, heated in a sealed tube or under pressure.

$$
\ce{RBr + R'NH2 -> R'NH R + HBr}
$$

3. Use $\ce{LiAlH4}$ or $\ce{H2/Ni}$.

4. General equation:

$$
\ce{RCONH2 ->[LiAlH4] RCH2NH2}
$$

5. Ethylamine is most basic because the ethyl group donates electron density to nitrogen. Ammonia has no alkyl group. Phenylamine is less basic because the nitrogen lone pair is delocalised into the benzene ring.

6. In amides, the nitrogen lone pair is delocalised into the carbonyl group, so it is much less available to accept a proton.

7. Nitrate benzene using concentrated $\ce{HNO3}$ and concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$ at $25$ to $60\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ to form nitrobenzene. Then reduce with hot $\ce{Sn}$ and concentrated $\ce{HCl}$, followed by $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$, to form phenylamine.

8. The product is 2,4,6-tribromophenylamine.

$$
\ce{C6H5NH2 + 3Br2 -> C6H2Br3NH2 + 3HBr}
$$

9. Phenylamine reacts with $\ce{HNO2}$, or $\ce{NaNO2}$ and dilute acid, below $10\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ to form benzenediazonium chloride.

10. Warm the diazonium salt with water to form phenol, nitrogen and hydrogen chloride.

$$
\ce{C6H5N2+Cl- + H2O ->[warm] C6H5OH + N2 + HCl}
$$

11. The azo group is $\ce{-N=N-}$. Benzenediazonium chloride couples with phenol in $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ to form an azo compound.

12. Equations:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + NH3 -> CH3CONH2 + HCl}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + CH3NH2 -> CH3CONHCH3 + HCl}
$$

13. Amides hydrolyse with aqueous acid or aqueous alkali. Amides are reduced to amines by $\ce{LiAlH4}$.

14. A zwitterion has both positive and negative charges but no overall charge. For 2-aminoethanoic acid:

$$
\ce{H2NCH2COOH <=> +H3NCH2COO-}
$$

15. Below the isoelectric point, the amino acid is overall positive and moves towards the cathode. At the isoelectric point, it has no overall charge and does not move overall. Above the isoelectric point, it is overall negative and moves towards the anode.
