# 34 Nitrogen Compounds - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Route to Ethylamine from Bromoethane

State reagents and conditions for converting bromoethane into ethylamine.

Use ammonia in ethanol, heated under pressure.

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br + NH3 -> CH3CH2NH2 + HBr}
$$

Check: excess ammonia helps favour the primary amine over further substitution.

## Example 2: Increasing the Carbon Chain with a Nitrile

Suggest a route from bromoethane to propylamine.

First substitute bromine with cyanide to form propanenitrile:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br ->[KCN,\ ethanol,\ heat] CH3CH2CN}
$$

Then reduce the nitrile:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2CN ->[LiAlH4] CH3CH2CH2NH2}
$$

Check: the nitrile route adds one carbon to the chain before reduction.

## Example 3: Basicity Order

Arrange ethylamine, ammonia and phenylamine in order of decreasing basicity and explain.

Order:

ethylamine > ammonia > phenylamine

Ethylamine is most basic because the ethyl group donates electron density to nitrogen, making the lone pair more available. Ammonia has no alkyl group. Phenylamine is least basic because the nitrogen lone pair is delocalised into the benzene ring.

Check: compare lone-pair availability, not just molecular size.

## Example 4: Phenylamine from Benzene

Give a route from benzene to phenylamine.

First nitrate benzene:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + HNO3 ->[conc. H2SO4][25-60 ^\circ C] C6H5NO2 + H2O}
$$

Then reduce nitrobenzene with hot $\ce{Sn}$ and concentrated $\ce{HCl}$, followed by $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$:

$$
\ce{C6H5NO2 ->[Sn/concentrated HCl][then NaOH(aq)] C6H5NH2}
$$

Check: the alkali step is needed to release phenylamine.

## Example 5: Diazonium Salt to Phenol

State how phenylamine can be converted into phenol.

React phenylamine with nitrous acid below $10\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ to form a diazonium salt. Then warm with water.

$$
\ce{C6H5NH2 ->[HNO2,\ below\ 10 ^\circ C] C6H5N2+Cl-}
$$

$$
\ce{C6H5N2+Cl- + H2O ->[warm] C6H5OH + N2 + HCl}
$$

Check: the temperature condition belongs to diazonium salt formation.

## Example 6: Amide Formation

Predict the product when propanoyl chloride reacts with methylamine.

An acyl chloride reacts with a primary amine to form a substituted amide.

$$
\ce{CH3CH2COCl + CH3NH2 -> CH3CH2CONHCH3 + HCl}
$$

The organic product is N-methylpropanamide, though detailed naming of substituted amides is less important here than recognising amide formation.

Check: $\ce{HCl}$ is eliminated.

## Example 7: Amide Basicity

Explain why ethanamide is much less basic than ethylamine.

In ethylamine, the nitrogen lone pair is localised and available to accept a proton. In ethanamide, the nitrogen lone pair is delocalised into the carbonyl group. This reduces its availability for bonding to $\ce{H+}$.

Therefore ethanamide is much less basic.

Check: amide basicity is controlled by the carbonyl group.

## Example 8: Electrophoresis

An amino acid has an isoelectric point of pH 6.0. Predict its movement during electrophoresis at pH 3.0 and pH 10.0.

At pH 3.0, the pH is below the isoelectric point, so the amino acid is more protonated and has an overall positive charge. It moves towards the cathode.

At pH 10.0, the pH is above the isoelectric point, so the amino acid is more deprotonated and has an overall negative charge. It moves towards the anode.

Check: at pH 6.0 it would have no overall movement.
