# 34 Nitrogen Compounds - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 A Level subject content for Topic 34: primary and secondary amines, phenylamine and azo compounds, amides and amino acids. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## AS Link

AS nitrogen chemistry introduced nitriles, amines and some routes involving halogenoalkanes. A Level widens the chemistry by adding secondary amines, reduction of amides, phenylamine, diazonium salts, azo dyes, amide hydrolysis and reduction, and amino acid behaviour. The link to AS remains clear: nitrogen lone pairs make many nitrogen compounds bases or nucleophiles, but the availability of the lone pair depends strongly on the bonding environment.

## Making Primary and Secondary Amines

Primary amines can be made by reacting halogenoalkanes with ammonia in ethanol, heated under pressure:

$$
\ce{RBr + NH3 -> RNH2 + HBr}
$$

Further reaction can occur, so conditions and excess ammonia are used to favour the primary amine.

Secondary amines can be made by reacting halogenoalkanes with primary amines in ethanol, heated in a sealed tube or under pressure:

$$
\ce{RBr + R'NH2 -> R'NH R + HBr}
$$

Primary amines can also be made by reducing nitriles:

$$
\ce{RCN ->[LiAlH4] RCH2NH2}
$$

or using $\ce{H2/Ni}$:

$$
\ce{RCN + 2H2 ->[Ni] RCH2NH2}
$$

Amides can be reduced with $\ce{LiAlH4}$ to amines:

$$
\ce{RCONH2 ->[LiAlH4] RCH2NH2}
$$

The carbonyl oxygen is removed and the carbonyl carbon becomes a $\ce{-CH2-}$ group.

## Amines as Bases

Amines act as bases because the nitrogen lone pair can accept a proton.

$$
\ce{RNH2 + H2O <=> RNH3+ + OH-}
$$

Aliphatic amines are usually stronger bases than ammonia because alkyl groups donate electron density to nitrogen, making the lone pair more available. Ethylamine is therefore more basic than ammonia.

Phenylamine is less basic than ammonia because the nitrogen lone pair is delocalised into the benzene ring and is less available to accept $\ce{H+}$.

Amides are much weaker bases than amines because the nitrogen lone pair is delocalised into the carbonyl group. This makes the lone pair much less available for protonation.

Useful comparison:

ethylamine > ammonia > phenylamine > amide

## Amides from Acyl Chlorides

Ammonia and amines react with acyl chlorides at room temperature to form amides. This is a condensation reaction because a small molecule, $\ce{HCl}$, is eliminated.

With ammonia:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + NH3 -> CH3CONH2 + HCl}
$$

With a primary amine:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + CH3NH2 -> CH3CONHCH3 + HCl}
$$

This reaction connects Topic 34 directly to Topic 33.

## Phenylamine Preparation

Phenylamine is prepared from benzene in two stages.

First nitrate benzene to nitrobenzene:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + HNO3 ->[conc. H2SO4][25-60 ^\circ C] C6H5NO2 + H2O}
$$

Then reduce nitrobenzene using hot tin and concentrated hydrochloric acid, followed by $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$:

$$
\ce{C6H5NO2 ->[Sn/concentrated HCl][then NaOH(aq)] C6H5NH2}
$$

The alkali is added to release free phenylamine from its salt.

## Phenylamine Reactions

Phenylamine reacts with bromine water at room temperature. The $\ce{-NH2}$ group strongly activates the aromatic ring and directs substitution to the 2-, 4- and 6-positions:

$$
\ce{C6H5NH2 + 3Br2 -> C6H2Br3NH2 + 3HBr}
$$

The product is 2,4,6-tribromophenylamine.

Phenylamine also reacts with nitrous acid below $10\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ to form a diazonium salt:

$$
\ce{C6H5NH2 ->[HNO2,\ below\ 10 ^\circ C] C6H5N2+Cl-}
$$

Nitrous acid may be generated from $\ce{NaNO2}$ and dilute acid. Warming the diazonium salt with water gives phenol:

$$
\ce{C6H5N2+Cl- + H2O ->[warm] C6H5OH + N2 + HCl}
$$

## Azo Compounds

Benzenediazonium chloride couples with phenol in $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ to form an azo compound. The important group is the azo group:

$$
\ce{-N=N-}
$$

Azo compounds are often used as dyes because the extended delocalised system absorbs visible light. Other azo dyes can be formed by similar diazonium coupling routes.

## Amide Reactions

Amides are produced from acyl chlorides and ammonia or primary amines at room temperature.

Amides can be hydrolysed by aqueous acid or aqueous alkali. Hydrolysis breaks the amide bond. Under acidic conditions, a carboxylic acid and an ammonium salt can form. Under alkaline conditions, a carboxylate salt and ammonia or an amine can form.

Amides can also be reduced with $\ce{LiAlH4}$ to amines:

$$
\ce{RCONH2 ->[LiAlH4] RCH2NH2}
$$

## Amino Acids and Zwitterions

Amino acids contain both an amine group and a carboxylic acid group. They can behave as acids and bases. In the solid state and often in solution, they exist as zwitterions with both a positive and a negative charge:

$$
\ce{H2NCH2COOH <=> +H3NCH2COO-}
$$

The **isoelectric point** is the pH at which the amino acid has no overall charge and does not move in an electric field.

At low pH, amino acids are more protonated and tend to carry a positive charge. At high pH, they are more deprotonated and tend to carry a negative charge.

## Peptide Bonds and Electrophoresis

Amino acids join by condensation to form amide, or peptide, bonds:

$$
\ce{H2NCH(R)COOH + H2NCH(R')COOH -> H2NCH(R)CONHCH(R')COOH + H2O}
$$

Dipeptides and tripeptides contain two and three amino acid residues respectively.

Electrophoresis separates amino acids and peptides according to charge at a given pH. A cation moves towards the cathode, an anion moves towards the anode, and a species with no overall charge remains near the baseline.

To predict movement:

1. Compare the pH with the species' isoelectric point.
2. Below the isoelectric point, the species is overall positive.
3. Above the isoelectric point, the species is overall negative.
4. At the isoelectric point, there is no overall movement.

## Working Routine

- Rank basicity by the availability of the nitrogen lone pair: ethylamine above ammonia above phenylamine above amides.
- Use diazonium chemistry below 10 degrees Celsius, then couple with phenol to make an azo dye.
- For amino acids, decide the charge at the stated pH before predicting movement in electrophoresis.
## Common Mistakes

- Assuming amides are as basic as amines.
- Forgetting that phenylamine is less basic than ammonia.
- Missing the below $10\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ condition for diazonium salt formation.
- Omitting the $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ step after reducing nitrobenzene with tin and hydrochloric acid.
- Predicting amino acid movement in electrophoresis without first deciding the charge at the stated pH.
