# 33 Carboxylic Acids and Derivatives - Key Practice Solutions

1. Use hot alkaline $\ce{KMnO4}$ followed by dilute acid.

$$
\ce{C6H5CH3 ->[hot alkaline KMnO4][then dilute acid] C6H5COOH}
$$

2. $\ce{PCl3}$ with heat, $\ce{PCl5}$, or $\ce{SOCl2}$.

3. Equation:

$$
\ce{CH3COOH + SOCl2 -> CH3COCl + SO2 + HCl}
$$

4. Methanoic acid can be oxidised by Fehling's reagent, Tollens' reagent, acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ or acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$. The products are carbon dioxide and water.

5. Ethanedioic acid is oxidised by warm acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ to carbon dioxide.

6. Carboxylic acids are stronger because carboxylate ions are stabilised by delocalisation over two oxygen atoms. Phenols are weaker than carboxylic acids but stronger than alcohols because phenoxide ions are stabilised by delocalisation into an aromatic ring. Alcohols form alkoxide ions with no comparable delocalisation.

7. Three chlorine atoms withdraw electron density more strongly than one chlorine atom. This stabilises the conjugate base more, so trichloroethanoic acid is stronger.

8. Equations:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + H2O -> CH3COOH + HCl}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + CH3CH2OH -> CH3COOCH2CH3 + HCl}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + NH3 -> CH3CONH2 + HCl}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + CH3NH2 -> CH3CONHCH3 + HCl}
$$

9. A nucleophile attacks the electron-deficient carbonyl carbon. The carbonyl $\pi$ bond opens to form a tetrahedral intermediate. The carbonyl reforms and chloride leaves. Proton transfer gives the final product.

10. Ethanoyl chloride hydrolyses fastest because the carbonyl carbon is very electrophilic and chloride leaves readily. Chloroethane hydrolyses more slowly by nucleophilic substitution. Chlorobenzene is least reactive because the aryl $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond has partial double-bond character from interaction with the aromatic ring.
