# 33 Carboxylic Acids and Derivatives - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 A Level subject content for Topic 33: 33.1 Carboxylic acids, 33.2 Esters and 33.3 Acyl chlorides. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## AS Link

AS carboxylic acid chemistry included acidity, esterification, reduction and hydrolysis of esters. A Level extends the family in three directions: aromatic carboxylic acids such as benzoic acid, highly reactive acyl chlorides, and acidity trends explained by substituent effects. Acyl chlorides become a synthesis hub because they react at room temperature with water, alcohols, phenol, ammonia and amines.

## Benzoic Acid from Alkylbenzenes

An alkylbenzene side-chain can be completely oxidised to a carboxyl group using hot alkaline $\ce{KMnO4}$ followed by dilute acid.

For methylbenzene:

$$
\ce{C6H5CH3 ->[hot alkaline KMnO4][then dilute acid] C6H5COOH}
$$

The aromatic ring remains, and the side-chain carbon directly attached to the ring becomes the carboxyl carbon. This reaction links Topic 30 arenes with carboxylic acids.

## Making Acyl Chlorides

Carboxylic acids are converted into acyl chlorides using one of these reagents:

- $\ce{PCl3}$ and heat
- $\ce{PCl5}$
- $\ce{SOCl2}$

General equation:

$$
\ce{RCOOH -> RCOCl}
$$

With thionyl chloride:

$$
\ce{RCOOH + SOCl2 -> RCOCl + SO2 + HCl}
$$

Acyl chlorides are much more reactive than carboxylic acids because the carbonyl carbon is strongly electron-deficient and chloride is a good leaving group.

## Carboxylic Acids That Can Be Further Oxidised

Most simple carboxylic acids resist further oxidation under ordinary syllabus conditions, but CAIE highlights two exceptions.

Methanoic acid, $\ce{HCOOH}$, is oxidised to carbon dioxide and water by Fehling's reagent, Tollens' reagent, acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ or acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$:

$$
\ce{HCOOH + [O] -> CO2 + H2O}
$$

Ethanedioic acid, $\ce{HOOCCOOH}$, is oxidised by warm acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ to carbon dioxide:

$$
\ce{HOOCCOOH + [O] -> 2CO2 + H2O}
$$

## Relative Acidities

Carboxylic acids are stronger acids than phenols, and phenols are stronger acids than alcohols. The key is conjugate-base stability.

- Carboxylate ions, $\ce{RCOO-}$, are stabilised by delocalisation over two oxygen atoms.
- Phenoxide ions are stabilised by delocalisation into the aromatic ring.
- Alkoxide ions have no comparable delocalisation and are often destabilised by electron-donating alkyl groups.

The broad order is:

carboxylic acids > phenols > water > alcohols

Chlorine-substituted carboxylic acids are stronger than unsubstituted carboxylic acids because chlorine withdraws electron density by the inductive effect. This stabilises the carboxylate ion. The effect is stronger when more chlorine atoms are present and when chlorine is closer to the $\ce{-COOH}$ group.

Example trend:

$$
\ce{CCl3COOH > CHCl2COOH > CH2ClCOOH > CH3COOH}
$$

## Esters from Acyl Chlorides

Acyl chlorides react with alcohols at room temperature to form esters and hydrogen chloride.

Ethyl ethanoate from ethanoyl chloride and ethanol:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + CH3CH2OH -> CH3COOCH2CH3 + HCl}
$$

Phenyl benzoate from benzoyl chloride and phenol:

$$
\ce{C6H5COCl + C6H5OH -> C6H5COOC6H5 + HCl}
$$

This route is faster and not an equilibrium esterification like the AS reaction of a carboxylic acid with an alcohol.

## Reactions of Acyl Chlorides

All of these reactions occur at room temperature.

| Reagent | Product | By-product |
|---|---|---|
| water | carboxylic acid | $\ce{HCl}$ |
| alcohol | ester | $\ce{HCl}$ |
| phenol | ester | $\ce{HCl}$ |
| ammonia | amide | $\ce{HCl}$ |
| primary or secondary amine | substituted amide | $\ce{HCl}$ |

Examples:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + H2O -> CH3COOH + HCl}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + NH3 -> CH3CONH2 + HCl}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + CH3NH2 -> CH3CONHCH3 + HCl}
$$

## Addition-Elimination Mechanism

Acyl chloride reactions proceed by addition-elimination.

1. A nucleophile attacks the electron-deficient carbonyl carbon.
2. The $\pi$ bond of the carbonyl opens, giving a tetrahedral intermediate.
3. The carbonyl reforms and $\ce{Cl-}$ leaves.
4. A proton transfer gives the final product and $\ce{HCl}$ or its salt in the reaction mixture.

This is different from nucleophilic substitution in halogenoalkanes. In acyl chlorides, the carbonyl group controls the mechanism.

## Ease of Hydrolysis

Acyl chlorides hydrolyse rapidly at room temperature because the carbonyl carbon is very electrophilic and chloride is a good leaving group.

Alkyl chlorides hydrolyse more slowly under suitable nucleophilic substitution conditions because only the polar $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond is being attacked.

Halogenoarenes are much less reactive because the aryl $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond has partial double-bond character from interaction with the aromatic $\pi$ system.

Relative ease:

acyl chlorides > alkyl chlorides > halogenoarenes

## Working Routine

- Place acyl chlorides at the centre: they react at room temperature with water, alcohols, phenol, ammonia and amines.
- Explain acidity trends through the stability of the conjugate base, including delocalisation and inductive effects.
- Rank ease of hydrolysis as acyl chlorides above alkyl chlorides above halogenoarenes.
## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting $\ce{HCl}$ as a by-product in acyl chloride reactions.
- Treating acyl chloride reactions as AS esterification equilibria.
- Writing halogenoalkane substitution mechanisms for acyl chlorides.
- Explaining acidity trends without discussing conjugate-base stability.
- Forgetting that methanoic acid and ethanedioic acid can be further oxidised.
