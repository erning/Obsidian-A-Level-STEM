# 33 Carboxylic Acids and Derivatives - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Methylbenzene to Benzoic Acid

Give reagents and conditions for converting methylbenzene into benzoic acid.

Use hot alkaline $\ce{KMnO4}$, then dilute acid.

$$
\ce{C6H5CH3 ->[hot alkaline KMnO4][then dilute acid] C6H5COOH}
$$

Check: the side-chain is oxidised; the aromatic ring remains.

## Example 2: Making an Acyl Chloride

State three possible reagents for converting propanoic acid into propanoyl chloride.

Possible reagents are $\ce{PCl3}$ with heat, $\ce{PCl5}$, or $\ce{SOCl2}$.

With thionyl chloride:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2COOH + SOCl2 -> CH3CH2COCl + SO2 + HCl}
$$

Check: the $\ce{-OH}$ of the carboxylic acid is replaced by chlorine.

## Example 3: Acyl Chloride and Ammonia

Predict the product when ethanoyl chloride reacts with ammonia at room temperature.

Ammonia reacts with an acyl chloride to form an amide.

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + NH3 -> CH3CONH2 + HCl}
$$

The organic product is ethanamide.

Check: in practice, excess ammonia may also neutralise $\ce{HCl}$, but the organic product remains ethanamide.

## Example 4: Phenyl Benzoate

Write an equation for forming phenyl benzoate from an acyl chloride and a phenol.

Use benzoyl chloride and phenol:

$$
\ce{C6H5COCl + C6H5OH -> C6H5COOC6H5 + HCl}
$$

Check: the acyl part comes from benzoyl chloride; the phenoxy part comes from phenol.

## Example 5: Explaining Acid Strength

Explain why chloroethanoic acid is stronger than ethanoic acid.

Chlorine is more electronegative and withdraws electron density through the $\sigma$ bond framework. This inductive effect stabilises the chloroethanoate ion formed after loss of $\ce{H+}$.

Ethanoate has no chlorine substituent, so its conjugate base is less stabilised.

Therefore chloroethanoic acid is stronger.

Check: stronger acid corresponds to a more stable conjugate base.

## Example 6: Addition-Elimination

Outline the mechanism type for hydrolysis of ethanoyl chloride.

Water attacks the electron-deficient carbonyl carbon. The carbonyl $\pi$ bond opens to give a tetrahedral intermediate. The carbonyl reforms, chloride leaves, and proton transfer gives ethanoic acid and hydrogen chloride.

Overall:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl + H2O -> CH3COOH + HCl}
$$

The mechanism is addition-elimination.

Check: this is not direct displacement at an alkyl carbon.

## Example 7: Hydrolysis Comparison

Arrange ethanoyl chloride, chloroethane and chlorobenzene in order of ease of hydrolysis.

Ethanoyl chloride hydrolyses most easily because the carbonyl carbon is strongly electrophilic and $\ce{Cl-}$ is a good leaving group. Chloroethane hydrolyses more slowly by nucleophilic substitution. Chlorobenzene is least reactive because the aryl $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond has partial double-bond character.

Order:

$$
\ce{CH3COCl > CH3CH2Cl > C6H5Cl}
$$
