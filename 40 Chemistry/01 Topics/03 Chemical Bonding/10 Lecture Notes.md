# Chemical Bonding

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 Syllabus 2025-2027, AS Level subject content, Physical chemistry, Topic 3 Chemical bonding:

- 3.1 Electronegativity and bonding
- 3.2 Ionic bonding
- 3.3 Metallic bonding
- 3.4 Covalent bonding and coordinate (dative covalent) bonding
- 3.5 Shapes of molecules
- 3.6 Intermolecular forces, electronegativity and bond properties
- 3.7 Dot-and-cross diagrams

The syllabus states that Pauling electronegativity values will be given where necessary. The presence of covalent character in some ionic compounds is not assessed in this section.

## Electronegativity

Electronegativity is the power of an atom to attract electrons to itself.

The main factors are:

- **nuclear charge**: a greater positive charge attracts bonding electrons more strongly
- **atomic radius**: a smaller atom attracts bonding electrons more strongly because the nucleus is closer
- **shielding by inner shells and sub-shells**: more shielding weakens attraction from the nucleus

Across a period, electronegativity generally increases because nuclear charge increases and atomic radius decreases. Down a group, electronegativity generally decreases because atomic radius and shielding increase.

Differences in Pauling electronegativity values help predict bonding:

- a large difference favours ionic bonding
- a small difference favours covalent bonding
- a difference between two non-metal atoms can produce a polar covalent bond

## Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonding is the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions: positively charged cations and negatively charged anions.

In sodium chloride, sodium transfers one electron to chlorine:

$$
\ce{Na -> Na+ + e-}
$$

$$
\ce{Cl + e- -> Cl-}
$$

The ions $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ attract each other in a giant ionic lattice. The attraction is not just between one pair of ions; each ion is attracted to oppositely charged ions around it.

Examples required by the syllabus include:

- sodium chloride, $\ce{NaCl}$
- magnesium oxide, $\ce{MgO}$
- calcium fluoride, $\ce{CaF2}$

## Metallic Bonding

Metallic bonding is the electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions and delocalised electrons.

In a metal, outer-shell electrons are delocalised. They can move through the lattice of positive metal ions. This explains why metals conduct electricity: the delocalised electrons are mobile charged particles.

## Covalent Bonding

Covalent bonding is electrostatic attraction between the nuclei of two atoms and a shared pair of electrons.

The shared pair is attracted to both nuclei. This is why a covalent bond is directional: it is associated with overlap between orbitals on the bonded atoms.

Molecules named in the syllabus include:

| Molecule | Key bonding point |
| --- | --- |
| $\ce{H2}$ | one single covalent bond |
| $\ce{O2}$ | one double bond |
| $\ce{N2}$ | one triple bond |
| $\ce{Cl2}$ | one single covalent bond |
| $\ce{HCl}$ | polar covalent bond |
| $\ce{CO2}$ | two double bonds |
| $\ce{NH3}$ | three bonds and one lone pair on nitrogen |
| $\ce{CH4}$ | four single bonds |
| $\ce{C2H6}$ | single bonds only |
| $\ce{C2H4}$ | carbon-carbon double bond |

Period 3 elements can expand their octet. Required examples include:

- sulfur dioxide, $\ce{SO2}$
- phosphorus pentachloride, $\ce{PCl5}$
- sulfur hexafluoride, $\ce{SF6}$

## Coordinate Bonding

A coordinate bond, also called a dative covalent bond, is a covalent bond in which both electrons in the shared pair are donated by the same atom.

The product is still held by electrostatic attraction between nuclei and a shared pair of electrons. After formation, a coordinate bond behaves like a covalent bond.

Example: ammonia reacts with hydrogen chloride to form ammonium chloride. Nitrogen in $\ce{NH3}$ donates a lone pair to $\ce{H+}$:

$$
\ce{NH3 + H+ -> NH4+}
$$

The syllabus also includes coordinate bonding in $\ce{Al2Cl6}$, where chlorine atoms can donate lone pairs to electron-deficient aluminium atoms.

## Sigma and Pi Bonds

Covalent bonds can be described in terms of orbital overlap.

A $\sigma$ bond is formed by direct overlap of orbitals between the bonding atoms. Electron density lies along the line between the nuclei.

A $\pi$ bond is formed by sideways overlap of adjacent $p$ orbitals above and below the $\sigma$ bond.

Bond patterns:

- a single bond is one $\sigma$ bond
- a double bond is one $\sigma$ bond and one $\pi$ bond
- a triple bond is one $\sigma$ bond and two $\pi$ bonds

Examples:

- $\ce{H2}$ has one $\sigma$ bond
- $\ce{C2H6}$ has $\sigma$ bonds only
- $\ce{C2H4}$ has a carbon-carbon double bond: one $\sigma$ and one $\pi$
- $\ce{HCN}$ has a carbon-nitrogen triple bond: one $\sigma$ and two $\pi$
- $\ce{N2}$ has one $\sigma$ and two $\pi$ bonds

Hybridisation helps describe the orbitals used for bonding:

- $sp^3$ hybridisation gives four electron-pair directions, as in $\ce{CH4}$
- $sp^2$ hybridisation gives three electron-pair directions, as in $\ce{C2H4}$
- $sp$ hybridisation gives two electron-pair directions, as in $\ce{HCN}$

## Bond Energy, Bond Length and Reactivity

Bond energy is the energy required to break one mole of a particular covalent bond in the gaseous state.

Bond length is the internuclear distance of two covalently bonded atoms.

In general, a shorter bond is stronger and has a larger bond energy. A longer, weaker bond is usually easier to break, so molecules containing it may be more reactive at that bond.

This comparison is most useful when other conditions are similar. Always connect the reactivity explanation to the bond that is being broken.

## Shapes of Molecules: VSEPR Theory

VSEPR stands for valence shell electron pair repulsion. Electron pairs around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible.

Repulsion strength is usually:

$$
\text{lone pair-lone pair} > \text{lone pair-bond pair} > \text{bond pair-bond pair}
$$

Required shapes and bond angles:

| Species | Electron pairs around central atom | Shape | Bond angle |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $\ce{BF3}$ | 3 bonding pairs | trigonal planar | $120^\circ$ |
| $\ce{CO2}$ | 2 bonding regions | linear | $180^\circ$ |
| $\ce{CH4}$ | 4 bonding pairs | tetrahedral | $109.5^\circ$ |
| $\ce{NH3}$ | 3 bonding pairs, 1 lone pair | pyramidal | $107^\circ$ |
| $\ce{H2O}$ | 2 bonding pairs, 2 lone pairs | non-linear | $104.5^\circ$ |
| $\ce{SF6}$ | 6 bonding pairs | octahedral | $90^\circ$ |
| $\ce{PF5}$ | 5 bonding pairs | trigonal bipyramidal | $120^\circ$ and $90^\circ$ |

For analogous molecules and ions, count electron regions around the central atom first. Multiple bonds count as one region for shape.

## Bond Polarity and Molecular Dipoles

A bond is polar if the bonded atoms have different electronegativities. The more electronegative atom is $\delta-$ and the less electronegative atom is $\delta+$.

A molecule is polar only if its bond dipoles do not cancel. Shape matters.

For example, each $\ce{C=O}$ bond in $\ce{CO2}$ is polar, but $\ce{CO2}$ is linear, so the dipoles cancel and the molecule is non-polar. In $\ce{H2O}$, the molecule is non-linear, so the dipoles do not cancel and the molecule is polar.

## Intermolecular Forces

Van der Waals' forces is used in the syllabus as a generic term for intermolecular forces between molecular entities other than those due to bond formation.

Types required:

- **instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces**, also called London dispersion forces
- **permanent dipole-permanent dipole forces**
- **hydrogen bonding**, treated as a special case of permanent dipole-permanent dipole forces

Instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces occur in all molecular substances. They increase with number of electrons and surface contact.

Permanent dipole-permanent dipole forces occur between polar molecules.

Hydrogen bonding is limited here to molecules containing $\ce{N-H}$ and $\ce{O-H}$ groups, including ammonia and water as simple examples. It occurs when hydrogen bonded to a highly electronegative atom is attracted to a lone pair on another electronegative atom.

Hydrogen bonding explains anomalous properties of water:

- water has relatively high melting and boiling points because hydrogen bonds require extra energy to overcome
- water has relatively high surface tension because molecules at the surface are strongly attracted to each other
- ice is less dense than liquid water because hydrogen bonding holds water molecules in an open structure in ice

In general, ionic, covalent and metallic bonding are stronger than intermolecular forces. When explaining melting or boiling of simple molecular substances, the covalent bonds within molecules are usually not broken; intermolecular forces between molecules are overcome.

## Dot-and-Cross Diagrams

Dot-and-cross diagrams show outer-shell electrons. Use one symbol, usually dots, for electrons from one atom, and another symbol, usually crosses, for electrons from another atom.

They can show:

- ionic bonding, with electron transfer and brackets around ions
- covalent bonding, with shared pairs between atoms
- coordinate bonding, with both electrons in a shared pair coming from one atom
- expanded octets and odd-electron species where required

For ionic diagrams, show charges on ions. For covalent diagrams, show lone pairs as well as bonding pairs when they matter for shape or coordinate bonding.

## Working Routine

- For shape, count the electron regions around the central atom, note any lone pairs, then apply VSEPR.
- For polarity, check each bond's polarity, then check whether the molecular shape cancels the dipoles.
- For intermolecular forces, identify the functional groups and rank hydrogen bonding above permanent-dipole and London forces.
- In dot-and-cross diagrams, include lone pairs whenever they affect shape or dative bonding.
## Common Mistakes

- Calling methane polar; the tetrahedral shape cancels the tiny C-H bond dipoles.
- Forgetting that London dispersion forces exist in all molecules, including polar ones.
- Treating hydrogen bonding as separate from permanent dipole forces; it is a special case of them.
- Counting a double bond as two regions in VSEPR; it counts as one.
- Omitting lone pairs when a dot-and-cross diagram determines shape.
