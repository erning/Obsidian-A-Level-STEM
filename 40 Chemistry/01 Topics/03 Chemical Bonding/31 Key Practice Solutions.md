# Chemical Bonding - Key Practice Solutions

## Solutions

1. Electronegativity is the power of an atom to attract electrons to itself. Across a period, nuclear charge increases and atomic radius decreases, while shielding does not increase greatly because electrons are added to the same shell. Bonding electrons are attracted more strongly, so electronegativity increases.

2. Magnesium has low electronegativity and oxygen has high electronegativity. The difference is large, so electron transfer forms $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ and $\ce{O^{2-}}$, giving ionic bonding. In $\ce{HCl}$, hydrogen and chlorine share a pair of electrons, but chlorine is more electronegative, so the bond is polar covalent.

3. Ionic bonding is the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. In $\ce{CaF2}$, calcium forms $\ce{Ca^{2+}}$ and fluorine forms $\ce{F-}$. One $\ce{Ca^{2+}}$ ion is balanced by two $\ce{F-}$ ions, and the ions are held by electrostatic attraction in a giant ionic lattice.

4. Metallic bonding is the electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions and delocalised electrons. Metals conduct electricity because delocalised electrons are mobile charged particles.

5. Covalent bonding is electrostatic attraction between the nuclei of two atoms and a shared pair of electrons. Coordinate bonding is covalent bonding in which both electrons in the shared pair are donated by the same atom.

6. Nitrogen in $\ce{NH3}$ has a lone pair. $\ce{H+}$ has no electrons. Nitrogen donates both electrons in its lone pair to $\ce{H+}$, forming a coordinate bond:

   $$
   \ce{NH3 + H+ -> NH4+}
   $$

7. $\ce{H2}$ has one $\sigma$ bond and no $\pi$ bonds.

   $\ce{C2H6}$ has seven $\sigma$ bonds and no $\pi$ bonds: six $\ce{C-H}$ bonds and one $\ce{C-C}$ bond.

   $\ce{C2H4}$ has five $\sigma$ bonds and one $\pi$ bond: four $\ce{C-H}$ bonds and one carbon-carbon double bond.

   $\ce{N2}$ has one $\sigma$ bond and two $\pi$ bonds.

8. $\ce{BF3}$: trigonal planar, $120^\circ$.

   $\ce{CH4}$: tetrahedral, $109.5^\circ$.

   $\ce{NH3}$: pyramidal, $107^\circ$.

   $\ce{H2O}$: non-linear, $104.5^\circ$.

   $\ce{PF5}$: trigonal bipyramidal, $120^\circ$ and $90^\circ$.

9. $\ce{CH4}$ has four bonding pairs around carbon. $\ce{NH3}$ has three bonding pairs and one lone pair around nitrogen. A lone pair repels bonding pairs more strongly than bonding pairs repel each other, so the bond angle is compressed from $109.5^\circ$ to about $107^\circ$.

10. Each $\ce{C=O}$ bond is polar because oxygen is more electronegative than carbon. $\ce{CO2}$ is linear, so the two equal bond dipoles act in opposite directions and cancel. The molecule has no overall dipole.

11. Instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces arise from temporary uneven electron distribution in one molecule inducing a dipole in a neighbouring molecule. They increase with more electrons and greater surface contact.

12. Water molecules form hydrogen bonds with each other. Molecules at the surface are strongly attracted to neighbouring water molecules, so the surface resists being stretched or broken, giving relatively high surface tension.

13. In ice, hydrogen bonding holds water molecules in an open structure. This open structure occupies more volume for the same mass than liquid water, so ice is less dense.

14. During melting of a simple molecular substance, intermolecular forces between molecules are overcome. The covalent bonds within the molecules are not broken.
