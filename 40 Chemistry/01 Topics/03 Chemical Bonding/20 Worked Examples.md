# Chemical Bonding - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Electronegativity and Bond Type

Explain why $\ce{NaCl}$ is ionic but $\ce{HCl}$ is covalent and polar.

**Working**

Sodium is a metal with low electronegativity. Chlorine is a non-metal with high electronegativity. The electronegativity difference is large, so electron transfer forms $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$. Ionic bonding is the electrostatic attraction between these oppositely charged ions.

Hydrogen and chlorine are both non-metals, so they share a pair of electrons. Chlorine is more electronegative, so the shared pair is attracted more strongly to chlorine. The bond is polar covalent.

**Answer:** $\ce{NaCl}$ is ionic because it contains oppositely charged ions; $\ce{HCl}$ is covalent because electrons are shared, and polar because chlorine attracts the shared electrons more strongly.

## Example 2: Ionic Bonding in Magnesium Oxide

Describe the bonding in $\ce{MgO}$.

**Working**

Magnesium loses two electrons:

$$
\ce{Mg -> Mg^{2+} + 2e-}
$$

Oxygen gains two electrons:

$$
\ce{O + 2e- -> O^{2-}}
$$

The ions have opposite charges and attract each other strongly.

**Answer:** $\ce{MgO}$ contains $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ and $\ce{O^{2-}}$ ions held in a giant ionic lattice by electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.

## Example 3: Coordinate Bonding in the Ammonium Ion

Explain how $\ce{NH4+}$ forms from $\ce{NH3}$ and $\ce{H+}$.

**Working**

Nitrogen in $\ce{NH3}$ has one lone pair. The hydrogen ion, $\ce{H+}$, has no electrons. Nitrogen donates both electrons in its lone pair to $\ce{H+}$ to form a coordinate bond.

$$
\ce{NH3 + H+ -> NH4+}
$$

**Answer:** $\ce{NH4+}$ forms when nitrogen donates a lone pair to $\ce{H+}$; the new $\ce{N-H}$ bond is coordinate when formed.

**Check:** After formation, all four $\ce{N-H}$ bonds in $\ce{NH4+}$ are equivalent.

## Example 4: Sigma and Pi Bonds in Ethene

Describe the bonding in $\ce{C2H4}$ using $\sigma$ and $\pi$ bonds.

**Working**

Ethene has a carbon-carbon double bond. A double bond contains one $\sigma$ bond and one $\pi$ bond. Each carbon also forms two $\ce{C-H}$ single bonds, and each single bond is a $\sigma$ bond.

Total $\sigma$ bonds:

$$
1\ \ce{C-C}\ \sigma + 4\ \ce{C-H}\ \sigma = 5\ \sigma\ \text{bonds}
$$

Total $\pi$ bonds:

$$
1
$$

Each carbon is $sp^2$ hybridised.

**Answer:** $\ce{C2H4}$ has five $\sigma$ bonds and one $\pi$ bond; the carbon atoms are $sp^2$ hybridised.

## Example 5: Predicting Shape and Bond Angle

Predict the shape and bond angle of $\ce{NH4+}$.

**Working**

Nitrogen has four bonding pairs around it in $\ce{NH4+}$ and no lone pairs. Four electron pairs arrange themselves tetrahedrally.

**Answer:** tetrahedral, with bond angle $109.5^\circ$.

## Example 6: Polarity of $\ce{CO2}$ and $\ce{H2O}$

Explain why $\ce{CO2}$ is non-polar but $\ce{H2O}$ is polar.

**Working**

The $\ce{C=O}$ bonds in $\ce{CO2}$ are polar, but the molecule is linear. The two equal bond dipoles act in opposite directions and cancel.

The $\ce{O-H}$ bonds in water are polar. Water is non-linear because oxygen has two bonding pairs and two lone pairs. The bond dipoles do not cancel.

**Answer:** $\ce{CO2}$ is non-polar because its bond dipoles cancel in a linear molecule; $\ce{H2O}$ is polar because its non-linear shape prevents cancellation.

## Example 7: Explaining Boiling Point Difference

Explain why water has a much higher boiling point than hydrogen sulfide, $\ce{H2S}$.

**Working**

Water molecules contain $\ce{O-H}$ bonds and can form hydrogen bonds between molecules. Hydrogen bonding is stronger than the intermolecular forces between $\ce{H2S}$ molecules.

More energy is needed to overcome the intermolecular forces in water.

**Answer:** water has a higher boiling point because hydrogen bonding between water molecules is stronger than the intermolecular forces in $\ce{H2S}$.

## Example 8: Bond Length, Bond Energy and Reactivity

The bond energy of $\ce{Cl-Cl}$ is lower than that of $\ce{H-Cl}$. Which bond is easier to break, and what does this suggest?

**Working**

Bond energy is the energy required to break one mole of a bond in the gaseous state. A lower bond energy means less energy is required for bond breaking.

**Answer:** the $\ce{Cl-Cl}$ bond is easier to break. Reactions that require breaking $\ce{Cl-Cl}$ may occur more readily than reactions requiring the stronger $\ce{H-Cl}$ bond to break, if other factors are comparable.
