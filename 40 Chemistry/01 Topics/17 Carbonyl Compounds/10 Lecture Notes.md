# 17 Carbonyl Compounds - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level subject content for Topic 17.1, aldehydes and ketones. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## The Carbonyl Group

Aldehydes and ketones both contain the carbonyl group, $\ce{C=O}$. The carbonyl bond is polar because oxygen is more electronegative than carbon:

$$
\ce{C^{\delta+}=O^{\delta-}}
$$

The carbonyl carbon is electron-deficient, so it can be attacked by nucleophiles.

An **aldehyde** has the carbonyl group at the end of a carbon chain:

$$
\ce{RCHO}
$$

A **ketone** has the carbonyl group within the chain:

$$
\ce{RCO R'}
$$

This structural difference explains several test results: aldehydes are more easily oxidised than ketones.

## Producing Aldehydes and Ketones

CAIE AS requires production by oxidation of alcohols.

Primary alcohols are oxidised with acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ and distillation to produce aldehydes:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2OH + [O] -> CH3CHO + H2O}
$$

Distillation removes the aldehyde before it is further oxidised.

Secondary alcohols are oxidised with acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ and distillation to produce ketones:

$$
\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH3 + [O] -> CH3COCH3 + H2O}
$$

Ketones are not usually further oxidised under these AS conditions.

## Reduction of Aldehydes and Ketones

Aldehydes and ketones are reduced by $\ce{NaBH4}$ or $\ce{LiAlH4}$ to produce alcohols.

Aldehydes give primary alcohols:

$$
\ce{CH3CHO + 2[H] -> CH3CH2OH}
$$

Ketones give secondary alcohols:

$$
\ce{CH3COCH3 + 2[H] -> CH3CH(OH)CH3}
$$

This reaction reverses the oxidation relationship from Topic 16.

## Nucleophilic Addition of Hydrogen Cyanide

Aldehydes and ketones react with $\ce{HCN}$, using $\ce{KCN}$ as catalyst and heat, to form hydroxynitriles.

Ethanal:

$$
\ce{CH3CHO + HCN -> CH3CH(OH)CN}
$$

Propanone:

$$
\ce{CH3COCH3 + HCN -> CH3C(OH)(CN)CH3}
$$

The reaction is nucleophilic addition.

### Mechanism

The cyanide ion, $\ce{CN-}$, is the nucleophile. It attacks the electron-deficient carbonyl carbon. The $\pi$ electrons in $\ce{C=O}$ move onto oxygen, forming an alkoxide ion. The alkoxide is then protonated by $\ce{HCN}$ to form the hydroxynitrile and regenerate $\ce{CN-}$.

For ethanal, the key steps can be represented as:

$$
\ce{CH3CHO + CN- -> CH3CH(O-)CN}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3CH(O-)CN + HCN -> CH3CH(OH)CN + CN-}
$$

In a curly-arrow mechanism, one arrow starts at the lone pair on $\ce{CN-}$ and points to the carbonyl carbon. Another starts at the $\pi$ bond of $\ce{C=O}$ and points to oxygen.

## Tests for Carbonyl Compounds

### 2,4-DNPH

2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine, often written as 2,4-DNPH reagent, detects aldehydes and ketones. A positive result is an orange precipitate. This test shows that a carbonyl compound is present, but it does not by itself distinguish aldehyde from ketone.

### Fehling's Reagent

Fehling's reagent is reduced by aldehydes. The blue solution forms a brick-red precipitate of copper(I) oxide. Ketones do not give this result under these simple test conditions.

### Tollens' Reagent

Tollens' reagent is reduced by aldehydes to form a silver mirror or grey silver precipitate. Ketones do not give this result under these simple test conditions.

The reason is that aldehydes are readily oxidised to carboxylic acids, while ketones resist oxidation under mild conditions.

## Tri-Iodomethane Reaction

Aldehydes or ketones containing the group $\ce{CH3CO-R}$ give a positive tri-iodomethane test with alkaline $\ce{I2(aq)}$. The observation is a yellow precipitate of $\ce{CHI3}$. The other organic product is a carboxylate ion, $\ce{RCO2-}$.

Ethanal, $\ce{CH3CHO}$, can be written as $\ce{CH3CO-H}$ and gives a positive test. Methyl ketones such as propanone also give a positive test.

## Working Routine

For a carbonyl compound, first decide whether the carbonyl is at the end of the chain or inside it. Then choose the evidence: 2,4-DNPH for carbonyl presence, Fehling's or Tollens' reagent for aldehyde versus ketone, and tri-iodomethane for the $\ce{CH3CO-R}$ group. For reactions, treat the carbonyl carbon as the electrophilic centre.

## Common Mistakes

- Using Tollens' or Fehling's reagent to tell two ketones apart; they distinguish an aldehyde from a ketone.
- Using Fehling's or Tollens' reagent before confirming a carbonyl with 2,4-DNPH.
- Writing NaBH4 as a reagent for a carboxylic acid; it reduces aldehydes and ketones.
- Missing that the tri-iodomethane test identifies the CH3CO- group.
- Omitting the curly arrow from CN- to the carbonyl carbon in the mechanism.
