# 17 Carbonyl Compounds - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Making an Aldehyde

How can propanal be prepared from propan-1-ol?

Propan-1-ol is a primary alcohol. Oxidise it with acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ and use distillation to remove the aldehyde before further oxidation.

$$
\ce{CH3CH2CH2OH + [O] -> CH3CH2CHO + H2O}
$$

Check: reflux would favour further oxidation to propanoic acid.

## Example 2: Making a Ketone

Give the product when propan-2-ol is oxidised with acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$.

Propan-2-ol is secondary, so it oxidises to a ketone:

$$
\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH3 + [O] -> CH3COCH3 + H2O}
$$

The product is propanone.

Check: secondary alcohols do not oxidise to aldehydes.

## Example 3: Reduction Product

Predict the product when butanone is reduced by $\ce{NaBH4}$.

Butanone is $\ce{CH3COCH2CH3}$. Reduction adds hydrogen across the carbonyl group and gives a secondary alcohol:

$$
\ce{CH3COCH2CH3 + 2[H] -> CH3CH(OH)CH2CH3}
$$

The product is butan-2-ol.

Check: reduction of a ketone gives a secondary alcohol.

## Example 4: HCN Addition to Ethanal

Give the product when ethanal reacts with $\ce{HCN}$ in the presence of $\ce{KCN}$ and heat.

The cyanide ion attacks the carbonyl carbon, then the oxygen is protonated:

$$
\ce{CH3CHO + HCN -> CH3CH(OH)CN}
$$

The product is 2-hydroxypropanenitrile.

Check: the nitrile carbon becomes part of the product chain.

## Example 5: Mechanism Arrows

In the nucleophilic addition of $\ce{CN-}$ to propanone, where do the first curly arrows start and end?

The first curly arrow starts at a lone pair on the carbon atom of $\ce{CN-}$ and points to the carbonyl carbon. The second curly arrow starts at the $\pi$ bond in $\ce{C=O}$ and points to oxygen.

This forms an alkoxide intermediate:

$$
\ce{CH3COCH3 + CN- -> CH3C(O-)(CN)CH3}
$$

Check: the nucleophile attacks carbon, not oxygen, because carbon is electron-deficient.

## Example 6: Identifying an Aldehyde

An unknown compound gives an orange precipitate with 2,4-DNPH and a silver mirror with Tollens' reagent. What can be concluded?

The 2,4-DNPH result shows the compound is an aldehyde or ketone. The Tollens' result shows it is an aldehyde because aldehydes reduce Tollens' reagent but ketones do not under these simple conditions.

Check: 2,4-DNPH alone is not enough to distinguish aldehyde from ketone.

## Example 7: Tri-Iodomethane Test

Which compound gives a yellow precipitate with alkaline $\ce{I2(aq)}$: ethanal, propanal or butanone?

The required group is $\ce{CH3CO-R}$. Ethanal is $\ce{CH3CO-H}$, so it is positive. Propanal is $\ce{CH3CH2CHO}$, so it is not positive. Butanone is $\ce{CH3COCH2CH3}$, so it is positive.

Check: this test identifies a methyl carbonyl group, with ethanal as the aldehyde exception included by the same pattern.
