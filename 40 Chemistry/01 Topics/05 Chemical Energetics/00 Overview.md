# 05 Chemical Energetics

## Core Idea

Chemical energetics tracks energy transfer during reactions. AS work focuses on enthalpy change, reaction pathway diagrams, bond energy calculations, experimental calorimetry and Hess's law. These tools let you compare reactions and calculate energy changes that cannot be measured directly.

## What to Learn

- Describe exothermic and endothermic reactions using the sign of $\Delta H$.
- Construct and interpret reaction pathway diagrams using activation energy and enthalpy change.
- Define standard conditions as 298 K and 101 kPa, shown by $^\ominus$.
- Use standard enthalpy changes of reaction, formation, combustion and neutralisation.
- Explain energy transfer in terms of bond breaking and bond making.
- Use bond energies, including average bond energies, to estimate $\Delta H_r$.
- Calculate enthalpy changes from calorimetry data using $q = mc\Delta T$ and $\Delta H = -mc\Delta T/n$.
- Apply Hess's law to construct simple energy cycles and calculate enthalpy changes.

## How to Study

Start with signs and diagrams. If products are lower in enthalpy than reactants, the reaction is exothermic and $\Delta H$ is negative. If products are higher, the reaction is endothermic and $\Delta H$ is positive.

For calculations, write the chemical equation first and identify what one mole of reaction means. In calorimetry, the mass in $q = mc\Delta T$ is usually the mass of the solution being warmed or cooled, while $n$ is the amount of the limiting reactant or product specified by the enthalpy definition.

For Hess cycles, do not memorise one diagram. Use the principle that enthalpy change is independent of route, then reverse signs and multiply enthalpy changes when equations are reversed or scaled.

## Practice Directions

- Calculate $\Delta H$ from temperature-change data.
- Estimate $\Delta H_r$ using bond energy data.
- Build Hess cycles using enthalpy changes of formation or combustion.
- Interpret energy profiles with and without a catalyst when the diagram overlaps with kinetics.
- Explain why bond energy answers are estimates unless exact bond enthalpies for the actual species are used.

## Common Traps

- Reversing a chemical equation but forgetting to reverse the sign of $\Delta H$.
- Using the mass of a reactant instead of the mass of the solution in simple calorimetry.
- Treating average bond energies as exact values for a particular molecule.
- Omitting the sign of $\Delta H$ after a calculation.
- Confusing activation energy with enthalpy change.
