# Chemical Energetics - Key Practice Solutions

1. An exothermic reaction releases energy to the surroundings and has $\Delta H < 0$. An endothermic reaction absorbs energy from the surroundings and has $\Delta H > 0$.

2. Enthalpy change:

$$
\Delta H = 135 - 80 = +55\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

Activation energy:

$$
E_A = 190 - 80 = 110\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

The reaction is endothermic because the products are at higher enthalpy than the reactants.

3. The standard conditions are 298 K and 101 kPa. The symbol $^\ominus$ shows that the enthalpy change refers to standard conditions, with substances in their standard states.

4. Formation of $\ce{MgO(s)}$:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + 1/2O2(g) -> MgO(s)}
$$

Combustion of ethanol:

$$
\ce{C2H5OH(l) + 3O2(g) -> 2CO2(g) + 3H2O(l)}
$$

The combustion equation is written for one mole of ethanol.

5. Heat gained by solution:

$$
q = mc\Delta T = 50.0 \times 4.18 \times 18.2 = 3803.8\ \mathrm{J}
$$

Amount of $\ce{HCl}$:

$$
n = \frac{50.0}{1000} \times 1.00 = 0.0500\ \mathrm{mol}
$$

Temperature rises, so the reaction is exothermic:

$$
\Delta H = -\frac{3803.8}{0.0500} = -76076\ \mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}
$$

$$
\Delta H = -76.1\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

6. Bonds broken: one $\ce{C-H}$ and one $\ce{Cl-Cl}$.

$$
412 + 243 = 655\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

Bonds made: one $\ce{C-Cl}$ and one $\ce{H-Cl}$.

$$
338 + 431 = 769\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

$$
\Delta H_r = 655 - 769 = -114\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

7. The formation enthalpy of $\ce{O2(g)}$ is zero.

$$
\Delta H_r^\ominus = \sum \Delta H_f^\ominus(\text{products}) - \sum \Delta H_f^\ominus(\text{reactants})
$$

$$
\Delta H_r^\ominus = (-394) - [(-111) + 0] = -283\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

8. Using combustion data:

$$
\Delta H_r^\ominus = \sum \Delta H_c^\ominus(\text{reactants}) - \sum \Delta H_c^\ominus(\text{products})
$$

Reactants:

$$
(-394) + 2(-286) = -966\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

Products:

$$
-891\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

$$
\Delta H_r^\ominus = -966 - (-891) = -75\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}
$$

9. Many bond energy values are average bond energies taken over several compounds. The actual bond enthalpy in a particular molecule depends on its chemical environment, so a bond energy calculation is often an estimate. It may also assume gaseous molecules while experimental enthalpy values may involve liquids or aqueous solutions.
