# Chemical Energetics - Review Checklist

## Recall

- Exothermic reactions have $\Delta H < 0$; endothermic reactions have $\Delta H > 0$.
- Standard conditions in this syllabus are 298 K and 101 kPa, shown by $^\ominus$.
- Activation energy, $E_A$, is the energy barrier from reactants to the transition state.
- $\Delta H_f^\ominus$ forms one mole of compound from elements in their standard states.
- $\Delta H_c^\ominus$ burns one mole of substance completely in oxygen.
- $\Delta H_{neut}^\ominus$ forms one mole of water by acid-alkali neutralisation.
- Hess's law says enthalpy change is independent of route.

## Skills

- Draw and label exothermic and endothermic reaction pathway diagrams.
- Use $q = mc\Delta T$ and $\Delta H = -mc\Delta T/n$ with correct units.
- Decide the correct amount $n$ for a calorimetry calculation.
- Estimate $\Delta H_r$ from bond energies.
- Construct Hess cycles using formation or combustion enthalpies.
- Reverse signs and multiply enthalpy changes when equations are reversed or scaled.

## Common Errors To Avoid

- Forgetting that a temperature rise usually means an exothermic reaction and negative $\Delta H$.
- Using the mass of one reactant instead of the mass of the solution in calorimetry.
- Omitting coefficients when summing formation or combustion enthalpies.
- Confusing average bond energy with an exact bond enthalpy.
- Calling a catalyst a cause of changed $\Delta H$.

## Ready To Move On When

- You can explain the sign of $\Delta H$ from a temperature change or pathway diagram.
- You can calculate a calorimetry enthalpy change and justify the negative sign.
- You can choose between bond energy, formation enthalpy and combustion enthalpy methods.
- You can spot whether a Hess cycle answer has the sign reversed.
