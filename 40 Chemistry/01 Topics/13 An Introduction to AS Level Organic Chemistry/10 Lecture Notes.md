# 13 An Introduction to AS Level Organic Chemistry - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level subject content for Topic 13: 13.1 formulas, functional groups and naming; 13.2 characteristic organic reactions; 13.3 shapes of organic molecules, $\sigma$ and $\pi$ bonds; and 13.4 structural isomerism and stereoisomerism. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## The Language of Organic Molecules

Organic chemistry studies carbon compounds. Carbon forms strong covalent bonds to carbon and to other non-metals, so a small number of atoms can produce many structures. In AS organic chemistry, the first skill is to recognise the same molecule when it is written in different ways.

A **hydrocarbon** is a compound made up of carbon and hydrogen atoms only. Alkanes are simple hydrocarbons with no functional group. Other organic families contain a **functional group**, the atom or group of atoms that dictates many of the physical and chemical properties of the compound.

CAIE expects you to interpret and use these formula types:

| Formula type | What it shows | Example for ethanol |
|---|---|---|
| Molecular formula | Number of each atom only | $\ce{C2H6O}$ |
| Empirical formula | Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms | $\ce{C2H6O}$ |
| General formula | Pattern for a homologous series | alcohols, $\ce{C_nH_{2n+1}OH}$ for simple monohydric alcohols |
| Structural formula | Connectivity in a compact line | $\ce{CH3CH2OH}$ |
| Displayed formula | Every atom and bond | useful for mechanisms and isomerism |
| Skeletal formula | Carbon skeleton as lines, with most C and H atoms omitted | useful for larger molecules |

In skeletal formulae, each line end or vertex is a carbon atom unless another atom is written. Hydrogens attached to carbon are not shown but are assumed to complete carbon's valency of four. Heteroatoms such as $\ce{O}$, $\ce{N}$, $\ce{Cl}$ and their attached hydrogens are shown.

## Functional Groups in the AS Introduction

The syllabus table includes these families.

| Homologous series | Functional group | Typical structural pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Alkene | carbon-carbon double bond | $\ce{C=C}$ |
| Halogenoalkane | halogen | $\ce{R-X}$ |
| Alcohol | hydroxyl | $\ce{R-OH}$ |
| Aldehyde | carbonyl at chain end | $\ce{RCHO}$ |
| Ketone | carbonyl inside chain | $\ce{RCO R'}$ |
| Carboxylic acid | carboxyl | $\ce{RCOOH}$ |
| Ester | ester | $\ce{RCOO R'}$ |
| Primary amine | amine | $\ce{RNH2}$ |
| Nitrile | nitrile | $\ce{RCN}$ |

$\ce{R}$ and $\ce{R'}$ represent alkyl groups, or in some contexts hydrogen atoms. $\ce{X}$ represents a halogen atom.

A **homologous series** is a family of organic compounds with the same functional group and similar chemical properties, in which adjacent members differ by $\ce{CH2}$. Members of a homologous series usually show a gradual change in physical properties as chain length increases.

## Naming Simple Aliphatic Compounds

CAIE AS requires systematic nomenclature for simple aliphatic molecules with the functional groups in the table, up to six carbon atoms. Esters can have six carbon atoms in each carbon chain, and esters and nitriles are straight-chain only at AS.

Use this routine.

1. Find the longest carbon chain containing the main functional group.
2. Choose the stem: meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-, hex-.
3. Add the ending or prefix for the main family: -ane, -ene, -ol, -al, -one, -oic acid, -oate, -amine, -nitrile, or halo-.
4. Number the chain so the main functional group has the lowest possible locant.
5. Add side chains or halogens as prefixes in alphabetical order.

Examples:

- $\ce{CH3CH2CH2OH}$ is propan-1-ol.
- $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH3}$ is propan-2-ol.
- $\ce{CH3CH2CHO}$ is propanal.
- $\ce{CH3COCH3}$ is propanone.
- $\ce{CH3CH2COOCH3}$ is methyl propanoate. The alkyl group attached to oxygen is named first.

## Reaction Vocabulary

A compound is **saturated** if it has only single carbon-carbon bonds. It is **unsaturated** if it contains a multiple carbon-carbon bond such as $\ce{C=C}$.

Bond fission describes how a covalent bond breaks.

- **Homolytic fission** gives one electron to each atom and forms free radicals.
- **Heterolytic fission** gives both bonding electrons to one atom and forms ions.
- A **free radical** has an unpaired electron.

In free-radical reactions, **initiation** forms radicals, **propagation** consumes and regenerates radicals, and **termination** removes radicals by combining them.

Polar reaction mechanisms use electron-rich and electron-poor species.

- A **nucleophile** donates an electron pair to an electron-deficient atom.
- An **electrophile** accepts an electron pair from an electron-rich region.
- **Nucleophilic** and **electrophilic** describe the species or step doing that action.

Important reaction types:

- **Addition**: atoms or groups add across a multiple bond.
- **Substitution**: one atom or group is replaced by another.
- **Elimination**: atoms or groups are removed to form a multiple bond.
- **Hydrolysis**: reaction with water or aqueous reagent breaks a bond.
- **Condensation**: two molecules join with elimination of a small molecule.
- **Oxidation** and **reduction**: in organic equations, $[\ce{O}]$ can represent one atom of oxygen from an oxidising agent, and $[\ce{H}]$ can represent one atom of hydrogen from a reducing agent.

## Curly Arrows and Mechanisms

In AS mechanisms, curly arrows show movement of electron pairs. A curly arrow should start at a bond or a lone pair, not at a positive charge or at the middle of an atom label. The arrow head shows where the electron pair goes.

The AS mechanism names introduced here are:

- free-radical substitution
- electrophilic addition
- nucleophilic substitution
- nucleophilic addition

When checking a mechanism, ask:

- Which bond or lone pair supplies the electrons?
- Which atom is electron-deficient?
- Are charges conserved across the step?
- Does the organic product match the reaction type?

## Shape, Hybridisation, Sigma Bonds and Pi Bonds

Organic molecules may be straight-chained, branched or cyclic. Their local shapes come from orbital overlap.

| Hybridisation | Shape around atom | Bond angle | Bonding idea |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\mathrm{sp^3}$ | tetrahedral | about $109.5^\circ$ | four $\sigma$ bonds |
| $\mathrm{sp^2}$ | trigonal planar | about $120^\circ$ | three $\sigma$ bonds and one unhybridised p orbital for $\pi$ bonding |
| $\mathrm{sp}$ | linear | $180^\circ$ | two $\sigma$ bonds and two unhybridised p orbitals |

A **sigma bond**, $\sigma$, is formed by direct overlap of orbitals along the line between nuclei. A **pi bond**, $\pi$, is formed by sideways overlap of adjacent p orbitals above and below the sigma-bond framework. A carbon-carbon double bond contains one $\sigma$ bond and one $\pi$ bond. The atoms directly attached to a $\ce{C=C}$ unit are planar because the p orbitals must stay aligned.

## Isomerism

Isomers have the same molecular formula but different arrangements of atoms.

**Structural isomerism** means the atoms are connected differently.

- **Chain isomerism**: different carbon skeletons.
- **Positional isomerism**: same functional group in different positions.
- **Functional group isomerism**: different functional groups.

**Stereoisomerism** means the atoms are connected in the same order but arranged differently in space.

**Geometrical isomerism** in alkenes occurs because the $\pi$ bond restricts rotation around $\ce{C=C}$. Each carbon of the double bond must have two different groups attached. CAIE accepts cis/trans language; E/Z is acceptable but not required.

**Optical isomerism** arises when a molecule has a chiral centre, usually a carbon atom attached to four different groups. The two optical isomers are called enantiomers. AS candidates should be able to identify chiral centres in structural formulae, including cyclic compounds, and should know that molecules can contain more than one chiral centre. Meso compounds and terms such as diastereoisomer are not required.

## Working Routine

For any new organic molecule, first identify the formula type, then the functional group, then the family name, then possible isomerism. For any reaction, identify the functional group that changes, the reagent and conditions, the reaction type, and the mechanism class if one is specified in the syllabus.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating R and R' as necessarily different; they may be the same.
- Naming an ester with the acid part first; the alkyl group from the alcohol is named first.
- Calling a carbon chiral without checking that its four attached groups are all different.
- Confusing homolytic with heterolytic fission when assigning radicals and ions.
- Forgetting that cis/trans isomerism needs each double-bond carbon to bear two different groups.
