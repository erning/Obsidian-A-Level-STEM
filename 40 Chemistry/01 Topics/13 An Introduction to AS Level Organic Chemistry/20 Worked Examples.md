# 13 An Introduction to AS Level Organic Chemistry - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Formula Types

A compound has structural formula $\ce{CH3CH2CH2CH3}$. Give its molecular formula and empirical formula.

There are four carbon atoms. Count hydrogens: $\ce{CH3}$ gives 3, each $\ce{CH2}$ gives 2, and the final $\ce{CH3}$ gives 3. Total hydrogen atoms = $3 + 2 + 2 + 3 = 10$.

Molecular formula: $\ce{C4H10}$.

The ratio $4:10$ simplifies to $2:5$, so the empirical formula is $\ce{C2H5}$.

Check: empirical formulae show ratios, not real molecule sizes.

## Example 2: Naming an Alcohol

Name $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH2CH3}$.

The longest chain has four carbon atoms, so the stem is but-. The functional group is $\ce{-OH}$, so the ending is -ol. Number the chain from the end nearer $\ce{-OH}$; the hydroxyl group is on carbon 2.

The name is butan-2-ol.

Check: butan-3-ol would describe the same structure but uses the higher locant, so it is not preferred.

## Example 3: Naming an Ester

Name $\ce{CH3CH2COOCH3}$.

For an ester, name the alkyl group attached to oxygen first. $\ce{-OCH3}$ is methyl. The acyl part contains three carbons including the carbonyl carbon, so it comes from propanoic acid and becomes propanoate.

The name is methyl propanoate.

Check: do not call it propyl methanoate; the carbonyl side decides the -oate part.

## Example 4: Classifying a Reaction

Propene reacts with hydrogen bromide to form mainly 2-bromopropane:

$$
\ce{CH3CH=CH2 + HBr -> CH3CHBrCH3}
$$

The $\ce{C=C}$ double bond is converted into a single bond, and $\ce{H}$ and $\ce{Br}$ add across it. This is an addition reaction. Since the alkene first attacks an electron-deficient hydrogen in $\ce{HBr}$, the mechanism class is electrophilic addition.

Check: no atom or group has been replaced, so it is not substitution.

## Example 5: Structural Isomers

Deduce structural isomers with molecular formula $\ce{C4H10}$.

For alkanes, only chain isomerism is possible here. A straight chain gives butane, $\ce{CH3CH2CH2CH3}$. A branched chain gives 2-methylpropane, $\ce{(CH3)3CH}$.

There are two structural isomers.

Check: moving a methyl group to carbon 1 would just redraw butane, not make a new isomer.

## Example 6: Geometrical Isomerism

Explain why but-2-ene can show cis/trans isomerism but but-1-ene cannot.

But-2-ene is $\ce{CH3CH=CHCH3}$. Each carbon of the double bond is attached to two different groups: $\ce{H}$ and $\ce{CH3}$. Restricted rotation around the $\pi$ bond means two arrangements are possible: the $\ce{CH3}$ groups on the same side, or on opposite sides.

But-1-ene is $\ce{CH2=CHCH2CH3}$. The first double-bond carbon has two identical hydrogen atoms, so cis/trans isomerism is not possible.

Check: geometrical isomerism depends on the groups on each double-bond carbon, not just the presence of $\ce{C=C}$.

## Example 7: Chiral Centre

Identify the chiral centre in butan-2-ol.

The structure is $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH2CH3}$. Carbon 2 is attached to four different groups: $\ce{H}$, $\ce{OH}$, $\ce{CH3}$ and $\ce{CH2CH3}$. Therefore carbon 2 is a chiral centre, and butan-2-ol has two enantiomers.

Check: carbon 1 is attached to three hydrogen atoms, so it cannot be chiral.
