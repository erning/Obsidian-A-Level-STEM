# Nitrogen and Sulfur - Key Practice Solutions

## Solutions

1. Nitrogen gas exists as $\ce{N2}$ molecules with a very strong $\ce{N#N}$ triple bond. A large amount of energy is required to break this bond. The $\ce{N2}$ molecule is also non-polar, so it is not easily attacked by polar reagents or ions.

2. A Bronsted-Lowry base is a proton acceptor. Ammonia accepts a proton from water:

$$
\ce{NH3(aq) + H2O(l) <=> NH4+(aq) + OH-(aq)}
$$

The $\ce{OH-}$ ions make the solution alkaline.

3. Ammonia accepts a proton using the lone pair on nitrogen:

$$
\ce{NH3 + H+ -> NH4+}
$$

The new bond can be described as coordinate at the moment of formation because both bonding electrons come from nitrogen. In $\ce{NH4+}$, all four $\ce{N-H}$ bonds are equivalent and the ion is tetrahedral.

4. The equation is:

$$
\ce{NH3(g) + HCl(g) -> NH4Cl(s)}
$$

The observation is white fumes of solid ammonium chloride.

5. Ionic equation:

$$
\ce{NH4+(aq) + OH-(aq) -> NH3(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

Full equation:

$$
\ce{NH4Cl(s) + NaOH(aq) -> NH3(g) + NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

Ammonia turns damp red litmus paper blue.

6. Natural occurrences include lightning and soil or bacterial processes. Man-made occurrences include internal combustion engines and power stations or other high-temperature combustion processes.

7. In high-temperature combustion:

$$
\ce{N2(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO(g)}
$$

Then:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)}
$$

8. A catalytic converter reaction is:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + 2CO(g) -> N2(g) + 2CO2(g)}
$$

Nitrogen is reduced from +2 in $\ce{NO}$ to 0 in $\ce{N2}$. Carbon is oxidised from +2 in $\ce{CO}$ to +4 in $\ce{CO2}$.

9. Engines release $\ce{NO}$, $\ce{NO2}$ and unburned hydrocarbons. In sunlight, reactive organic species form from hydrocarbons and react with $\ce{NO2}$ to produce peroxyacetyl nitrate, PAN. PAN is a component of photochemical smog.

10. A useful overall equation is:

$$
\ce{4NO2(g) + O2(g) + 2H2O(l) -> 4HNO3(aq)}
$$

This shows direct formation of nitric acid, which lowers rainwater pH.

11. Sulfur dioxide can dissolve in water:

$$
\ce{SO2(g) + H2O(l) <=> H2SO3(aq)}
$$

It can be oxidised to sulfur trioxide:

$$
\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) -> 2SO3(g)}
$$

Sulfur trioxide forms sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{SO3(g) + H2O(l) -> H2SO4(aq)}
$$

12. Nitrogen dioxide oxidises sulfur dioxide:

$$
\ce{SO2(g) + NO2(g) -> SO3(g) + NO(g)}
$$

Nitrogen monoxide is then oxidised back to nitrogen dioxide:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)}
$$

The regenerated $\ce{NO2}$ can oxidise more $\ce{SO2}$, so $\ce{NO}$ and $\ce{NO2}$ act catalytically. The $\ce{SO3}$ formed reacts with water:

$$
\ce{SO3(g) + H2O(l) -> H2SO4(aq)}
$$
