# Nitrogen and Sulfur - Lecture Notes

## Learning Scope

This note follows CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level section 12. It covers the low reactivity of nitrogen, ammonia as a Bronsted-Lowry base, ammonium ion formation, displacement of ammonia from ammonium salts, oxides of nitrogen, catalytic removal from engine exhaust gases, photochemical smog and acid rain involving nitrogen oxides and sulfur dioxide. No Chemistry coursebook alignment is claimed.

## Nitrogen Molecules and Low Reactivity

Nitrogen gas exists as diatomic $\ce{N2}$ molecules. The two nitrogen atoms are joined by a strong triple bond:

$$
\ce{N#N}
$$

Nitrogen is unreactive under ordinary conditions for two linked reasons:

- The $\ce{N#N}$ triple bond is very strong, so a large amount of energy is needed to break it.
- The $\ce{N2}$ molecule is non-polar, so it is not easily attacked by polar reagents or ions.

This does not mean nitrogen never reacts. At high temperatures, such as in lightning or engines, enough energy is available for nitrogen and oxygen to react.

## Oxidation Numbers in This Topic

Oxidation numbers are a useful way to follow environmental redox chemistry.

| Species | Oxidation number of key element |
|---|---:|
| $\ce{N2}$ | 0 |
| $\ce{NH3}$ | -3 for nitrogen |
| $\ce{NH4+}$ | -3 for nitrogen |
| $\ce{NO}$ | +2 for nitrogen |
| $\ce{NO2}$ | +4 for nitrogen |
| $\ce{HNO3}$ | +5 for nitrogen |
| $\ce{SO2}$ | +4 for sulfur |
| $\ce{SO3}$ | +6 for sulfur |
| $\ce{H2SO4}$ | +6 for sulfur |

When $\ce{NO}$ becomes $\ce{NO2}$, nitrogen is oxidised from +2 to +4. When $\ce{SO2}$ becomes $\ce{SO3}$, sulfur is oxidised from +4 to +6.

## Ammonia as a Bronsted-Lowry Base

A Bronsted-Lowry base is a proton acceptor. Ammonia, $\ce{NH3}$, has a lone pair on nitrogen and can accept a proton:

$$
\ce{NH3(aq) + H+(aq) -> NH4+(aq)}
$$

In water, ammonia behaves as a weak base:

$$
\ce{NH3(aq) + H2O(l) <=> NH4+(aq) + OH-(aq)}
$$

The hydroxide ions make the solution alkaline. Because the reaction is reversible, ammonia is a weak base rather than a strong base.

## Formation and Structure of the Ammonium Ion

The ammonium ion, $\ce{NH4+}$, forms when ammonia accepts a proton:

$$
\ce{NH3 + H+ -> NH4+}
$$

The nitrogen atom in $\ce{NH3}$ uses its lone pair to form a bond to $\ce{H+}$. This can be described as coordinate bonding at the moment of formation because both electrons in the new bond come from nitrogen. In the ammonium ion, the four $\ce{N-H}$ bonds are equivalent and the ion has a tetrahedral shape.

Ammonia gas and hydrogen chloride gas form white fumes of ammonium chloride:

$$
\ce{NH3(g) + HCl(g) -> NH4Cl(s)}
$$

This is an acid-base reaction because $\ce{HCl}$ donates a proton and $\ce{NH3}$ accepts it.

## Displacement of Ammonia from Ammonium Salts

Ammonium salts react with strong bases to release ammonia. The base removes a proton from the ammonium ion:

$$
\ce{NH4+(aq) + OH-(aq) -> NH3(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

Example with ammonium chloride:

$$
\ce{NH4Cl(s) + NaOH(aq) -> NH3(g) + NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

Example with ammonium sulfate:

$$
\ce{(NH4)2SO4(s) + 2NaOH(aq) -> 2NH3(g) + Na2SO4(aq) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

Ammonia is recognised by its sharp smell, by turning damp red litmus paper blue, and by forming white fumes with hydrogen chloride.

## Natural Occurrences of Oxides of Nitrogen

Oxides of nitrogen include $\ce{NO}$ and $\ce{NO2}$, often written as $\ce{NOx}$ in environmental contexts.

Natural sources include:

- Lightning, where high temperature allows nitrogen and oxygen in air to react.
- Soil and bacterial processes that produce nitrogen oxides.

In lightning, nitrogen monoxide can form:

$$
\ce{N2(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO(g)}
$$

Nitrogen monoxide is then oxidised in air:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)}
$$

## Man-Made Occurrences of Oxides of Nitrogen

Man-made nitrogen oxides are mainly formed in high-temperature combustion, especially internal combustion engines. Air contains nitrogen and oxygen. At the high temperatures inside an engine, they react to form $\ce{NO}$:

$$
\ce{N2(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO(g)}
$$

Then $\ce{NO}$ is oxidised to $\ce{NO2}$ in air:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)}
$$

Power stations and other high-temperature combustion processes can also produce $\ce{NOx}$.

## Catalytic Removal from Exhaust Gases

Catalytic converters reduce nitrogen oxides in vehicle exhaust gases. The catalyst surface allows reactions between nitrogen oxides and reducing gases such as carbon monoxide or unburned hydrocarbons.

Useful equations include:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + 2CO(g) -> N2(g) + 2CO2(g)}
$$

$$
\ce{2NO2(g) + 4CO(g) -> N2(g) + 4CO2(g)}
$$

In these reactions, nitrogen is reduced from positive oxidation numbers in $\ce{NO}$ or $\ce{NO2}$ to 0 in $\ce{N2}$. Carbon monoxide is oxidised to carbon dioxide.

The purpose is to convert harmful nitrogen oxides into nitrogen, which is already the major gas in air.

## Photochemical Smog and PAN

Photochemical smog forms when nitrogen oxides and unburned hydrocarbons react in sunlight. The syllabus requires the idea that atmospheric $\ce{NO}$ and $\ce{NO2}$ can react with unburned hydrocarbons to form peroxyacetyl nitrate, PAN, which is a component of photochemical smog.

A simplified route is:

1. Engines release $\ce{NOx}$ and unburned hydrocarbons.
2. Sunlight helps produce reactive radicals from these pollutants.
3. Organic radicals react with $\ce{NO2}$ to form PAN.

PAN irritates eyes and lungs and is associated with urban photochemical smog. The exact radical mechanism is not required for the AS syllabus outcome, but the reacting species and product should be named.

## Nitrogen Oxides and Acid Rain: Direct Role

Nitrogen dioxide can react with water and oxygen in the atmosphere to form nitric acid. A useful overall equation is:

$$
\ce{4NO2(g) + O2(g) + 2H2O(l) -> 4HNO3(aq)}
$$

Another common representation is:

$$
\ce{2NO2(g) + H2O(l) -> HNO2(aq) + HNO3(aq)}
$$

The acids formed dissolve in rainwater and lower its pH. This is the direct contribution of nitrogen oxides to acid rain.

## Sulfur Dioxide and Acid Rain

Sulfur dioxide, $\ce{SO2}$, enters the atmosphere from sources such as burning sulfur-containing fossil fuels, smelting sulfide ores and volcanic emissions.

It dissolves in water to form sulfurous acid:

$$
\ce{SO2(g) + H2O(l) <=> H2SO3(aq)}
$$

It can also be oxidised to sulfur trioxide:

$$
\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) -> 2SO3(g)}
$$

Sulfur trioxide reacts with water to form sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{SO3(g) + H2O(l) -> H2SO4(aq)}
$$

Sulfuric acid is a major contributor to acid rain.

## Nitrogen Oxides as Catalysts in Sulfur Dioxide Oxidation

The syllabus specifically includes the catalytic role of $\ce{NO}$ and $\ce{NO2}$ in the oxidation of atmospheric sulfur dioxide.

Nitrogen dioxide can oxidise sulfur dioxide:

$$
\ce{SO2(g) + NO2(g) -> SO3(g) + NO(g)}
$$

Nitrogen monoxide is then oxidised back to nitrogen dioxide:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)}
$$

Because $\ce{NO2}$ is used and then regenerated, nitrogen oxides can act catalytically. The net effect is oxidation of $\ce{SO2}$ to $\ce{SO3}$, which then forms sulfuric acid in water.

## Environmental Effects of Acid Rain

Acid rain can:

- Lower the pH of lakes and rivers, harming aquatic life.
- Damage leaves and reduce plant growth.
- Leach nutrients from soils.
- React with carbonate rocks and building materials.

For example, calcium carbonate reacts with acid:

$$
\ce{CaCO3(s) + 2H+(aq) -> Ca^{2+}(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)}
$$

In exam answers, focus on the chemical species causing the effect, not only the environmental description.

## Working Routine

- Explain the low reactivity of nitrogen using both the strong triple bond and the lack of polarity.
- For acid rain, name the species (SO2, SO3, NO2) and show the oxidation or catalytic step.
- For the catalytic role of nitrogen oxides, show NO2 being used and then regenerated.
## Common Mistakes

- Explaining nitrogen's low reactivity by bond strength alone; the lack of polarity also matters.
- Forgetting that NO and NO2 catalyse the oxidation of sulfur dioxide to sulfur trioxide.
- Describing the catalytic converter as absorbing NOx; it reduces NOx to N2 and oxidises CO and hydrocarbons.
- Omitting the role of PAN in photochemical smog.
- Confusing the direct acid-rain role of NO2 with its catalytic role.
