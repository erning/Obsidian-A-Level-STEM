# Nitrogen and Sulfur - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Explain Nitrogen's Low Reactivity

**Question.** Nitrogen is abundant in air but is relatively unreactive at room temperature. Explain why.

**Working.**

Nitrogen exists as $\ce{N2}$ molecules. The two nitrogen atoms are joined by a very strong triple bond, $\ce{N#N}$. A large amount of energy is needed to break this bond before nitrogen can react.

The $\ce{N2}$ molecule is also non-polar, so it is not easily attacked by polar reagents or ions.

**Check.** A complete answer needs both triple bond strength and lack of polarity.

## Example 2: Ammonia as a Bronsted-Lowry Base

**Question.** Use the Bronsted-Lowry theory to explain why ammonia is a base in water.

**Working.**

A Bronsted-Lowry base accepts a proton. Ammonia accepts a proton from water:

$$
\ce{NH3(aq) + H2O(l) <=> NH4+(aq) + OH-(aq)}
$$

Ammonia forms ammonium ions, and hydroxide ions are produced. The solution is alkaline.

**Check.** Do not describe ammonia as a base only because it "contains nitrogen". The key is proton acceptance.

## Example 3: Ammonium Ion Formation

**Question.** Ammonia gas reacts with hydrogen chloride gas to form white fumes. Write an equation and explain the bonding change.

**Working.**

The equation is:

$$
\ce{NH3(g) + HCl(g) -> NH4Cl(s)}
$$

Hydrogen chloride donates a proton to ammonia. Nitrogen in ammonia uses its lone pair to form a bond to $\ce{H+}$, producing $\ce{NH4+}$. At formation, this can be described as a coordinate bond. In $\ce{NH4+}$, the four $\ce{N-H}$ bonds are equivalent and arranged tetrahedrally.

**Check.** The white fumes are solid ammonium chloride.

## Example 4: Displacing Ammonia from an Ammonium Salt

**Question.** Ammonium sulfate is warmed with sodium hydroxide. Write an equation and state how the gas is identified.

**Working.**

The equation is:

$$
\ce{(NH4)2SO4(s) + 2NaOH(aq) -> 2NH3(g) + Na2SO4(aq) + 2H2O(l)}
$$

The gas is ammonia. It turns damp red litmus paper blue and forms white fumes with hydrogen chloride.

**Check.** There are two ammonium ions in $\ce{(NH4)2SO4}$, so two ammonia molecules form.

## Example 5: Catalytic Removal of Nitrogen Oxides

**Question.** Explain how a catalytic converter can remove nitrogen monoxide from engine exhaust gases. Include an equation and oxidation number changes.

**Working.**

Nitrogen monoxide reacts with carbon monoxide on the catalyst surface:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + 2CO(g) -> N2(g) + 2CO2(g)}
$$

Nitrogen changes from +2 in $\ce{NO}$ to 0 in $\ce{N2}$, so it is reduced. Carbon changes from +2 in $\ce{CO}$ to +4 in $\ce{CO2}$, so carbon monoxide is oxidised.

**Check.** The harmful nitrogen oxide is converted into nitrogen, not into ammonia.

## Example 6: Nitrogen Oxides, Sulfur Dioxide and Acid Rain

**Question.** Explain how nitrogen dioxide can contribute to acid rain directly and by catalysing the oxidation of sulfur dioxide.

**Working.**

Directly, nitrogen dioxide can form nitric acid:

$$
\ce{4NO2(g) + O2(g) + 2H2O(l) -> 4HNO3(aq)}
$$

It can also oxidise sulfur dioxide to sulfur trioxide:

$$
\ce{SO2(g) + NO2(g) -> SO3(g) + NO(g)}
$$

Nitrogen monoxide is oxidised back to nitrogen dioxide:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)}
$$

The sulfur trioxide reacts with water to form sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{SO3(g) + H2O(l) -> H2SO4(aq)}
$$

Because $\ce{NO2}$ is regenerated through $\ce{NO}$, nitrogen oxides can act catalytically in the oxidation of $\ce{SO2}$.

**Check.** Name both acids: nitric acid and sulfuric acid.
