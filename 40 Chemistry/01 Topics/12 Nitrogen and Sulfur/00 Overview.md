# 12 Nitrogen and Sulfur

## Core Idea

Nitrogen and sulfur chemistry connects bonding, Bronsted-Lowry acid-base behaviour, oxidation numbers and environmental chemistry. Nitrogen is unreactive because $\ce{N2}$ has a strong non-polar triple bond, ammonia acts as a base by accepting protons, and nitrogen oxides link engine chemistry to catalytic converters, photochemical smog and acid rain.

## What to Learn

- Explain the low reactivity of nitrogen using triple bond strength and lack of polarity.
- Describe ammonia as a Bronsted-Lowry base and explain ammonium ion formation.
- Describe displacement of ammonia from ammonium salts by acid-base reaction.
- State and explain natural and man-made occurrences of oxides of nitrogen.
- Describe catalytic removal of nitrogen oxides from exhaust gases of internal combustion engines.
- Explain how nitrogen oxides and unburned hydrocarbons form PAN in photochemical smog.
- Describe the role of $\ce{NO}$ and $\ce{NO2}$ in acid rain, both directly and through oxidation of atmospheric sulfur dioxide.

## How to Study

- Track proton transfer for ammonia and ammonium compounds.
- Track oxidation numbers for $\ce{NO}$, $\ce{NO2}$, $\ce{SO2}$ and $\ce{SO3}$.
- Keep environmental contexts chemical: name the species, source, reaction role and product.
- Use equations for catalytic converter and acid rain explanations whenever possible.

## Common Traps

- Saying nitrogen is unreactive without mentioning both the strong triple bond and lack of polarity.
- Treating ammonia and ammonium ion as the same species.
- Describing environmental effects without identifying the relevant oxide or reaction role.
- Forgetting that $\ce{NO}$ and $\ce{NO2}$ can act catalytically in the oxidation of $\ce{SO2}$.
