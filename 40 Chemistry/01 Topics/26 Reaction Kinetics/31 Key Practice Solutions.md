# 26 Reaction Kinetics - Key Practice Solutions

## 1. Definitions

- A rate equation shows how the rate depends on reactant concentrations, for example $\text{rate}=k[\ce{A}]^m[\ce{B}]^n$.
- The order of reaction with respect to a reactant is the power of that reactant concentration in the rate equation.
- The overall order is the sum of the individual orders.
- The rate constant, $k$, is the proportionality constant in the rate equation at a stated temperature.
- Half-life is the time taken for the concentration of a reactant to fall to half its value.
- The rate-determining step is the slowest step in a reaction mechanism.
- An intermediate is formed in one step and used up in a later step, so it does not appear in the overall equation.

## 2. Initial Rates

Compare experiments 1 and 2. $[\ce{X}]$ doubles while $[\ce{Y}]$ is constant. Rate doubles, so the order with respect to $\ce{X}$ is 1.

Compare experiments 1 and 3. $[\ce{Y}]$ doubles while $[\ce{X}]$ is constant. Rate increases by a factor of 4, so the order with respect to $\ce{Y}$ is 2.

Therefore:

$$
\text{rate} = k[\ce{X}][\ce{Y}]^2
$$

The overall order is 3.

## 3. Rate Constant

Using experiment 1:

$$
1.60 \times 10^{-3} = k(0.100)(0.200)^2
$$

$$
1.60 \times 10^{-3} = k(0.00400)
$$

$$
k = 0.400
$$

For an overall third-order reaction:

$$
k = 0.400\ \text{dm}^6\text{ mol}^{-2}\text{ s}^{-1}
$$

## 5. Graph Interpretation

(a) Zero order: horizontal line because rate is independent of concentration.

(b) First order: straight line through the origin because rate is directly proportional to concentration.

(c) Second order: upward curve because rate is proportional to the square of concentration.

## 6. Mechanism And Rate Equation

(a) Add the two steps:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + H2(g) -> N2O(g) + H2O(g)}
$$

$$
\ce{N2O(g) + H2(g) -> N2(g) + H2O(g)}
$$

Cancel $\ce{N2O}$ to get:

$$
\ce{2NO(g) + 2H2(g) -> N2(g) + 2H2O(g)}
$$

This matches the overall equation.

(b) The slow step contains two $\ce{NO}$ particles and one $\ce{H2}$ particle, so the predicted rate equation is:

$$
\text{rate} = k[\ce{NO}]^2[\ce{H2}]
$$

(c) $\ce{N2O}$ is an intermediate because it is formed in the first step and used up in the second step.

## 7. Temperature Effect

Increasing temperature increases the rate constant $k$. A larger fraction of particles has energy equal to or greater than the activation energy, so there are more successful collisions per unit time. If concentrations are unchanged, the increased $k$ increases the rate. The reaction order is not changed by temperature.

## 8. Catalysts

(a) In the Haber process:

$$
\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}
$$

iron is a heterogeneous catalyst because it is a solid while the reactants are gases. Reactants adsorb onto the iron surface, bonds are weakened, the reaction occurs on the surface, and ammonia desorbs. The catalyst provides an alternative route with lower activation energy and is regenerated.

(b) $\ce{Fe^{2+}}/\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ is homogeneous because the catalyst ions and reactant ions are all in aqueous solution. The iron ions are used in one step and regenerated in another. For example:

$$
\ce{S2O8^{2-}(aq) + 2Fe^{2+}(aq) -> 2SO4^{2-}(aq) + 2Fe^{3+}(aq)}
$$

$$
\ce{2Fe^{3+}(aq) + 2I-(aq) -> 2Fe^{2+}(aq) + I2(aq)}
$$

Adding the steps cancels the iron ions and gives:

$$
\ce{S2O8^{2-}(aq) + 2I-(aq) -> 2SO4^{2-}(aq) + I2(aq)}
$$
