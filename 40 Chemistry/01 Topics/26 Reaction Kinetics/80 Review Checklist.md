# 26 Reaction Kinetics - Review Checklist

## Recall

- Define rate equation, order of reaction, overall order, rate constant, half-life, rate-determining step, intermediate, and catalyst.
- State that orders are found from experimental data, not from the overall equation.
- Recall that first-order half-life is independent of concentration.
- State $k = 0.693/t_{1/2}$ for a first-order reaction.
- Distinguish homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysts.

## Perform

- Deduce orders from initial-rate tables.
- Construct a rate equation from orders.
- Calculate an initial rate from the gradient of a concentration-time graph.
- Calculate $k$ and derive its units from the rate equation.
- Use half-life data to identify first-order behaviour and calculate $k$.
- Add mechanism steps to check that they give the overall equation.
- Deduce a rate equation from a given rate-determining step.
- Identify intermediates and catalysts in a mechanism.
- Explain heterogeneous catalysis using adsorption, bond weakening, reaction, and desorption.

## Explain

- Zero order means concentration changes do not affect rate.
- First order means rate is directly proportional to concentration.
- Second order means doubling concentration quadruples the rate.
- Increasing temperature increases $k$ because more particles exceed the activation energy.
- Catalysts increase rate by providing a lower activation energy route and are regenerated overall.

## Avoid

- Do not infer orders from stoichiometric coefficients unless the step is explicitly an elementary slow step.
- Do not compare initial-rate experiments where multiple concentrations change unless you account for all changes.
- Do not give $k$ without units.
- Do not assume all half-lives are constant.
- Do not call an intermediate a catalyst unless it is regenerated.
- Do not say catalysts change the equilibrium position.

## Ready To Move On

You are ready when you can turn a table of rate data into a rate equation, calculate and unit-check $k$, recognise first-order half-life patterns, and test whether a proposed mechanism is consistent with the observed rate equation.
