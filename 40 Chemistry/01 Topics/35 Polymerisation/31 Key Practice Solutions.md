# 35 Polymerisation - Key Practice Solutions

1. Addition polymerisation joins alkene monomers by opening the $\ce{C=C}$ bond and no small molecule is lost. Condensation polymerisation joins difunctional monomers and eliminates a small molecule such as $\ce{H2O}$ or $\ce{HCl}$.

2. Ethane-1,2-diol and propanedioic acid form a polyester:

$$
\ce{[-OCH2CH2OOCCH2CO-]_{n}}
$$

3. $\ce{HOCH2CH2COOH}$ self-condenses to form:

$$
\ce{[-OCH2CH2CO-]_{n}}
$$

4. The diamine and dicarboxylic acid form a polyamide:

$$
\ce{[-NH(CH2)4NHCO(CH2)4CO-]_{n}}
$$

5. $\ce{H2NCH2COOH}$ self-condenses to form a polyamide:

$$
\ce{[-NHCH2CO-]_{n}}
$$

6. Cut at the amide links. The monomers are $\ce{H2NCH2CH2NH2}$ and $\ce{HOOCC6H4COOH}$.

7. Propene undergoes addition polymerisation. Ethane-1,2-diol with ethanedioic acid undergoes condensation polymerisation to form a polyester. 6-aminohexanoic acid undergoes condensation polymerisation to form a polyamide.

8. Poly(alkenes) have chemically inert carbon-carbon backbones and lack hydrolysable links, so they can be difficult to biodegrade.

9. Polyesters contain ester links and polyamides contain amide links. These links can be broken by acidic or alkaline hydrolysis, cutting the polymer chain.

10. Light can break bonds or create reactive sites in some polymers, leading to chain breakdown and further degradation.
