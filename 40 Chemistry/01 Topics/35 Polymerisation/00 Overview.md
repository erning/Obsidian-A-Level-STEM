# 35 Polymerisation

## Core Idea

A Level polymerisation extends AS addition polymerisation to condensation polymers and degradable polymers. The topic is about recognising monomers, repeat units, polymerisation type, and the chemical features that make some polymers easier to break down.

## Source Alignment

- Primary syllabus source: CAIE Chemistry 9701, A Level subject content.
- 35 Polymerisation
- 35.1 Condensation polymerisation
- 35.2 Predicting the type of polymerisation
- 35.3 Degradable polymers

## What to Learn

- Describe formation of polyesters from diols, dicarboxylic acids, dioyl chlorides, or hydroxycarboxylic acids.
- Describe formation of polyamides from diamines, dicarboxylic acids, dioyl chlorides, aminocarboxylic acids, or amino acids.
- Deduce repeat units and monomers for condensation polymers.
- Predict whether a monomer or pair of monomers gives addition or condensation polymerisation.
- Recognise why poly(alkenes), polyesters, and polyamides differ in degradability.

## How to Study

- Identify the functional groups before deciding the polymerisation type.
- Mark the atoms lost as a small molecule in condensation polymerisation.
- Practise cutting repeat units at ester or amide links to recover monomers.
- Relate biodegradability to hydrolysable bonds, not to the word "polymer" alone.

## Practice Directions

- Draw repeat units from given difunctional monomers.
- Identify monomers from a section of condensation polymer.
- Classify polymerisation type from monomer structures.
- Explain why some polymers are biodegradable by hydrolysis and poly(alkenes) are not.

## Common Traps

- Forgetting that condensation polymerisation eliminates a small molecule.
- Drawing repeat units with the wrong boundary or missing the continuation bonds.
- Assuming every polymer is biodegradable.
- Confusing polyamide links with peptide links without recognising both are amide links.
