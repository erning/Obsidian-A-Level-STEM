# 35 Polymerisation - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 A Level subject content for Topic 35: condensation polymerisation, predicting the type of polymerisation, and degradable polymers. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## AS Link

AS polymerisation focused on addition polymerisation of alkenes. The monomer double bond opens and no small molecule is eliminated. A Level adds condensation polymerisation, where monomers have two functional groups and join with elimination of a small molecule such as water or hydrogen chloride. The key skill is to identify the functional groups before deciding the polymerisation type.

## Addition or Condensation?

Use the structure of the monomer or monomers.

- Addition polymerisation usually uses alkene monomers with a $\ce{C=C}$ bond. The repeat unit contains the same atoms as the monomer and no small molecule is lost.
- Condensation polymerisation uses difunctional monomers such as diols, dicarboxylic acids, dioyl chlorides, diamines, hydroxycarboxylic acids, aminocarboxylic acids and amino acids. A small molecule is eliminated each time a link forms.

The common A Level condensation links are ester links and amide links.

## Polyesters

Polyesters contain ester links, $\ce{-COO-}$.

They can form from a diol and a dicarboxylic acid:

$$
\ce{n HO-R-OH + n HOOC-R'-COOH -> [-O-R-OOC-R'-CO-]_{n} + 2n H2O}
$$

They can also form from a diol and a dioyl chloride:

$$
\ce{n HO-R-OH + n ClOC-R'-COCl -> [-O-R-OOC-R'-CO-]_{n} + 2n HCl}
$$

Hydroxycarboxylic acids can self-condense because each monomer contains both $\ce{-OH}$ and $\ce{-COOH}$:

$$
\ce{n HO-R-COOH -> [-O-R-CO-]_{n} + n H2O}
$$

When drawing repeat units, include continuation bonds through the oxygen and carbonyl carbon.

## Polyamides

Polyamides contain amide links, $\ce{-CONH-}$. Proteins are natural polyamides because peptide bonds are amide links.

Polyamides can form from a diamine and a dicarboxylic acid:

$$
\ce{n H2N-R-NH2 + n HOOC-R'-COOH -> [-NH-R-NHCO-R'-CO-]_{n} + 2n H2O}
$$

They can also form from a diamine and a dioyl chloride:

$$
\ce{n H2N-R-NH2 + n ClOC-R'-COCl -> [-NH-R-NHCO-R'-CO-]_{n} + 2n HCl}
$$

Aminocarboxylic acids and amino acids can self-condense because each molecule contains both an amine group and a carboxylic acid group:

$$
\ce{n H2N-R-COOH -> [-NH-R-CO-]_{n} + n H2O}
$$

## Deducing Repeat Units

To draw a repeat unit from monomers:

1. Identify the two functional groups that react.
2. Remove $\ce{H}$ from $\ce{-OH}$ or $\ce{-NH2}$ and remove $\ce{OH}$ or $\ce{Cl}$ from the acid derivative as appropriate.
3. Join the fragments through an ester or amide link.
4. Put brackets around the smallest repeating section.
5. Add continuation bonds through the atoms that connect to neighbouring repeat units.

## Finding Monomers from a Polymer Section

To identify monomers, cut the polymer at the ester or amide links.

For an ester link, cut $\ce{-COO-}$ and restore $\ce{-COOH}$ to the carbonyl side and $\ce{-OH}$ to the oxygen side. If the original monomer was a dioyl chloride, the acid part could also be represented as an acyl chloride derivative.

For an amide link, cut $\ce{-CONH-}$ and restore $\ce{-COOH}$ to the carbonyl side and $\ce{-NH2}$ to the nitrogen side.

## Degradable Polymers

Poly(alkenes) have strong carbon-carbon backbones and are chemically inert, so they can be difficult to biodegrade.

Some polymers can be degraded by the action of light. This breaks bonds or creates reactive sites that allow further breakdown.

Polyesters and polyamides are biodegradable by acidic and alkaline hydrolysis because their ester and amide links can be broken. Hydrolysis cuts the chain at these functional groups. This is why the chemical link in the backbone matters more than the word "polymer" itself.

## Working Routine

- Decide addition or condensation from the functional groups: alkenes give addition; diols, diacids and diamines give condensation.
- Draw repeat units by cutting out the small molecule (water or HCl) and adding continuation bonds.
- For biodegradability, look for ester or amide links in the backbone that can be hydrolysed.
## Common Mistakes

- Calling any polymer with two monomers an addition polymer.
- Forgetting that condensation polymerisation eliminates a small molecule.
- Drawing repeat units without continuation bonds.
- Cutting a repeat unit at the wrong place and producing impossible monomers.
- Assuming all polymers biodegrade at similar rates.
