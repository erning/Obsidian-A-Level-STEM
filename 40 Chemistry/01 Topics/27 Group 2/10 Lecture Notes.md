# 27 Group 2 - Lecture Notes

## Source Route and Scope

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 section 27.1, "Similarities and trends in the properties of the Group 2 metals, magnesium to barium, and their compounds". The local source checked is the 2025-2027 syllabus, page 44. No local Chemistry coursebook has been verified, so the wording and scope are based on the syllabus only.

The A Level extension is not about listing more reactions for Group 2. It asks you to explain two kinds of trend:

- thermal stability of the nitrates and carbonates
- solubility and enthalpy change of solution, $\Delta H^\circ_\mathrm{sol}$, of the hydroxides and sulfates

The required explanations use ionic radius, polarisation, lattice energy, and enthalpy change of hydration.

## The Group 2 Ions in This Topic

The metals considered are magnesium to barium:

$$
\ce{Mg,\ Ca,\ Sr,\ Ba}
$$

Their common ions in these compounds are all $\ce{M^{2+}}$. The charge is the same down the group, but the ionic radius increases:

$$
\ce{Mg^{2+}} < \ce{Ca^{2+}} < \ce{Sr^{2+}} < \ce{Ba^{2+}}
$$

As radius increases, charge density decreases. A small $\ce{M^{2+}}$ ion such as $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ has a high charge density and strongly attracts electron density near it. A larger $\ce{M^{2+}}$ ion such as $\ce{Ba^{2+}}$ has a lower charge density and a weaker polarising effect.

That one change in cation radius controls much of the topic, but it acts in different ways in thermal decomposition and in dissolving ionic solids.

## Thermal Stability of Carbonates and Nitrates

Group 2 carbonates decompose on heating to form the metal oxide and carbon dioxide:

$$
\ce{MCO3(s) -> MO(s) + CO2(g)}
$$

Group 2 nitrates decompose on heating to form the metal oxide, nitrogen dioxide, and oxygen:

$$
\ce{2M(NO3)2(s) -> 2MO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)}
$$

The trend is:

$$
\text{thermal stability increases from } \ce{Mg} \text{ to } \ce{Ba}
$$

So $\ce{MgCO3}$ decomposes more easily than $\ce{BaCO3}$, and $\ce{Mg(NO3)2}$ decomposes more easily than $\ce{Ba(NO3)2}$.

### Observation and Explanation

| Trend point | Observation | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Down Group 2, cation radius increases. | A higher temperature is needed to decompose the carbonate or nitrate. | The larger $\ce{M^{2+}}$ ion has lower charge density and less polarising power. |
| Smaller cations polarise more strongly. | $\ce{MgCO3}$ and $\ce{Mg(NO3)2}$ are less thermally stable. | $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ distorts the electron cloud of the large anion more strongly. |
| Larger cations polarise less strongly. | $\ce{BaCO3}$ and $\ce{Ba(NO3)2}$ are more thermally stable. | $\ce{Ba^{2+}}$ distorts the anion less, so the anion is less destabilised. |

The important phrase is "polarisation of the large anion". The carbonate ion, $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$, and nitrate ion, $\ce{NO3-}$, are large anions with electron clouds that can be distorted by a neighbouring cation. Strong polarisation weakens and destabilises the anion, so decomposition needs less heat.

Down the group:

1. $\ce{M^{2+}}$ radius increases.
2. Charge density decreases.
3. Polarising power decreases.
4. The carbonate or nitrate ion is less distorted.
5. Thermal stability increases.

## Why "Ionic Radius" Is Not Enough by Itself

An answer that says "the ions get bigger, so stability increases" is incomplete. The radius matters because it changes charge density and polarising power.

A full explanation should connect the ideas:

$$
\text{larger } \ce{M^{2+}} \rightarrow \text{lower charge density} \rightarrow \text{less polarisation of } \ce{CO3^{2-}} \text{ or } \ce{NO3-} \rightarrow \text{greater thermal stability}
$$

For a smaller cation, reverse the logic:

$$
\text{smaller } \ce{M^{2+}} \rightarrow \text{higher charge density} \rightarrow \text{more polarisation} \rightarrow \text{lower thermal stability}
$$

## Solubility and Enthalpy Change of Solution

When an ionic solid dissolves, two energy changes are compared.

First, the ionic lattice must be separated. This requires energy if expressed as lattice dissociation enthalpy:

$$
\ce{MX(s) -> M^{2+}(g) + X^{2-}(g)}
$$

or, for hydroxides:

$$
\ce{M(OH)2(s) -> M^{2+}(g) + 2OH-(g)}
$$

Second, the gaseous ions become hydrated by water. This releases energy:

$$
\ce{M^{2+}(g) -> M^{2+}(aq)}
$$

$$
\ce{X^{2-}(g) -> X^{2-}(aq)}
$$

The overall enthalpy change of solution is the balance:

$$
\Delta H^\circ_\mathrm{sol} =
\text{lattice dissociation enthalpy} +
\text{enthalpy changes of hydration}
$$

If the hydration energy released is large enough compared with the energy needed to separate the lattice, dissolving is more favourable. If the lattice is hard to separate and hydration does not compensate enough, dissolving is less favourable.

CAIE often asks this qualitatively, so focus on relative magnitudes rather than exact numbers.

## Group 2 Hydroxides

For the hydroxides:

$$
\ce{M(OH)2(s) <=> M^{2+}(aq) + 2OH-(aq)}
$$

The solubility trend is:

$$
\ce{Mg(OH)2} \text{ is least soluble, and } \ce{Ba(OH)2} \text{ is most soluble.}
$$

So Group 2 hydroxides become more soluble down the group.

The explanation uses the two competing energy changes:

- Down the group, $\ce{M^{2+}}$ gets larger, so its hydration enthalpy becomes less exothermic.
- Down the group, the lattice energy also decreases in magnitude because the ions attract each other less strongly.
- For Group 2 hydroxides, the decrease in lattice energy is more important than the decrease in hydration enthalpy.
- Therefore $\Delta H^\circ_\mathrm{sol}$ becomes more favourable down the group, and solubility increases.

In words: as the lattice becomes easier to break apart, the hydroxide becomes easier to dissolve.

## Group 2 Sulfates

For the sulfates:

$$
\ce{MSO4(s) <=> M^{2+}(aq) + SO4^{2-}(aq)}
$$

The solubility trend is:

$$
\ce{MgSO4} \text{ is most soluble, and } \ce{BaSO4} \text{ is least soluble.}
$$

So Group 2 sulfates become less soluble down the group.

The explanation is different from the hydroxides:

- Down the group, $\ce{M^{2+}}$ gets larger, so its hydration enthalpy becomes less exothermic.
- The sulfate ion, $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$, is large, so changing the Group 2 cation has a smaller effect on lattice energy than it does for hydroxides.
- For Group 2 sulfates, the decrease in hydration enthalpy is more important than the decrease in lattice energy.
- Therefore $\Delta H^\circ_\mathrm{sol}$ becomes less favourable down the group, and solubility decreases.

In words: hydration becomes too weak to compensate enough for separating the lattice.

## Comparing Hydroxides and Sulfates

| Compound type | Solubility down Group 2 | Energy explanation |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{M(OH)2}$ | Increases | The decrease in lattice energy is more important than the decrease in hydration enthalpy. |
| $\ce{MSO4}$ | Decreases | The decrease in hydration enthalpy is more important than the decrease in lattice energy. |

This is a common comparison question. Do not use the sulfate explanation for hydroxides, or the hydroxide explanation for sulfates.

## Data Prompts

If a question gives decomposition temperatures, the more thermally stable compound is the one with the higher decomposition temperature. Your explanation should still use polarisation.

If a question gives enthalpy changes or solubility data, identify the trend first. Then explain it using the relative magnitudes of lattice energy and hydration enthalpy. A data statement such as "solubility decreases from $\ce{MgSO4}$ to $\ce{BaSO4}$" is not the explanation by itself.

## Working Routine

For thermal stability:

1. State the trend down Group 2.
2. State that $\ce{M^{2+}}$ radius increases and charge density decreases.
3. Link this to less polarisation of $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$ or $\ce{NO3-}$.
4. Conclude that the anion is less destabilised and the compound is more thermally stable.

For solubility:

1. State the solubility trend for the particular compound type.
2. State that both lattice energy magnitude and hydration enthalpy magnitude decrease down the group.
3. Say which decrease is more important.
4. Link the balance to $\Delta H^\circ_\mathrm{sol}$ and solubility.

## Common Mistakes

- Saying "larger ions are more stable" without mentioning lower charge density and less polarisation.
- Forgetting that the carbonate and nitrate trends have the same direction.
- Mixing up hydroxide and sulfate solubility trends.
- Treating lattice energy and hydration enthalpy as if they always change solubility in the same direction.
- Using exact numerical language when the question asks for a qualitative explanation.

## Quick Self-Checks

- Can you write the decomposition equations for $\ce{MCO3}$ and $\ce{M(NO3)2}$?
- Can you explain why $\ce{BaCO3}$ is more thermally stable than $\ce{MgCO3}$?
- Can you state the opposite solubility trends for $\ce{M(OH)2}$ and $\ce{MSO4}$?

- Can you keep observation, trend, and explanation separate in a written answer?
