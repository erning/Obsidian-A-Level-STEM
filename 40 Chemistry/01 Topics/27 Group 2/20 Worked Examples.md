# 27 Group 2 - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Ranking Carbonate Thermal Stability

**Question.** Arrange $\ce{MgCO3}$, $\ce{CaCO3}$, $\ce{SrCO3}$, and $\ce{BaCO3}$ in order of increasing thermal stability. Explain the trend.

**Working.**

Group 2 thermal stability of carbonates increases down the group. Therefore the order of increasing stability is:

$$
\ce{MgCO3} < \ce{CaCO3} < \ce{SrCO3} < \ce{BaCO3}
$$

The cations all have charge $2+$, but ionic radius increases from $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ to $\ce{Ba^{2+}}$. As radius increases, charge density decreases, so the cation has less polarising power. The carbonate ion, $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$, is a large anion. It is polarised more strongly by smaller cations such as $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$, which makes the carbonate less stable to heating.

**Answer.** $\ce{MgCO3}$ is least thermally stable and $\ce{BaCO3}$ is most thermally stable because larger Group 2 cations polarise $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$ less strongly.

**Check.** The explanation includes radius, charge density, polarisation, and the large anion.

## Example 2: Explaining Nitrate Decomposition

**Question.** A student says that $\ce{Ba(NO3)2}$ is more thermally stable than $\ce{Mg(NO3)2}$ because barium is lower in Group 2. Improve this explanation.

**Working.**

The statement gives the trend but not the reason. A better answer must connect position in the group to ionic radius and polarisation.

Down Group 2, the ionic radius of $\ce{M^{2+}}$ increases. Therefore $\ce{Ba^{2+}}$ has lower charge density than $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$. It polarises the large nitrate ion, $\ce{NO3-}$, less strongly. The nitrate ion is less distorted and less destabilised, so more energy is required for decomposition.

The decomposition equation is:

$$
\ce{2M(NO3)2(s) -> 2MO(s) + 4NO2(g) + O2(g)}
$$

**Answer.** $\ce{Ba(NO3)2}$ is more thermally stable because $\ce{Ba^{2+}}$ has a larger ionic radius and lower charge density than $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$, so it polarises $\ce{NO3-}$ less strongly.

**Check.** "Lower in the group" is not used as the explanation by itself.

## Example 3: Interpreting Decomposition Data

**Question.** A data table gives the following qualitative observations.

| Compound | Heating observation |
|---|---|
| $\ce{MgCO3}$ | Decomposes with moderate heating |
| $\ce{CaCO3}$ | Requires stronger heating |
| $\ce{BaCO3}$ | Requires very strong heating |

Use the data to identify the trend and explain it.

**Working.**

The compound that needs stronger heating is more thermally stable. The data show that stability increases:

$$
\ce{MgCO3} < \ce{CaCO3} < \ce{BaCO3}
$$

The reason is not that barium compounds are always less reactive. It is specifically about polarisation of $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$.

From $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ to $\ce{Ba^{2+}}$, cation radius increases and charge density decreases. The larger cation polarises the carbonate ion less strongly, so the carbonate ion is less destabilised. Thermal decomposition therefore becomes more difficult.

**Answer.** Thermal stability increases from $\ce{MgCO3}$ to $\ce{BaCO3}$ because polarisation of $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$ decreases down Group 2.

**Check.** The trend is drawn from observations, then explained at particle level.

## Example 4: Hydroxide Solubility

**Question.** Explain why $\ce{Ba(OH)2}$ is more soluble in water than $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$.

**Working.**

The dissolving process is:

$$
\ce{M(OH)2(s) <=> M^{2+}(aq) + 2OH-(aq)}
$$

Down Group 2, $\ce{M^{2+}}$ becomes larger. This makes the hydration enthalpy of the metal ion less exothermic, which would make dissolving less favourable. However, the lattice energy also decreases in magnitude, so the lattice becomes easier to separate.

For Group 2 hydroxides, the decrease in lattice energy is more important than the decrease in hydration enthalpy. Therefore $\Delta H^\circ_\mathrm{sol}$ becomes more favourable down the group.

**Answer.** $\ce{Ba(OH)2}$ is more soluble because the lower lattice energy of the larger-ion lattice more than compensates for the less exothermic hydration enthalpy.

**Check.** The answer compares both energy terms, rather than mentioning only one.

## Example 5: Sulfate Solubility

**Question.** Explain why $\ce{BaSO4}$ is much less soluble than $\ce{MgSO4}$.

**Working.**

The dissolving process is:

$$
\ce{MSO4(s) <=> M^{2+}(aq) + SO4^{2-}(aq)}
$$

Down Group 2, the metal ion becomes larger. The hydration enthalpy of $\ce{M^{2+}}$ becomes less exothermic because the ion has lower charge density and attracts water molecules less strongly.

The lattice energy also decreases in magnitude, but for sulfates this decrease is not enough to offset the decrease in hydration enthalpy. The sulfate ion, $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$, is large, so the lattice energy does not become favourable enough to keep solubility high.

**Answer.** $\ce{BaSO4}$ is less soluble because, down Group 2, hydration enthalpy becomes much less exothermic and this effect is more important than the decrease in lattice energy.

**Check.** This is the opposite solubility trend from the hydroxides.

## Example 6: Choosing the Correct Explanation from a Data Prompt

**Question.** A table shows that solubility increases down Group 2 for hydroxides but decreases down Group 2 for sulfates. Write one sentence explaining why the same cation trend gives different solubility trends.

**Working.**

The same cation change occurs in both series: $\ce{M^{2+}}$ radius increases. That makes hydration enthalpy less exothermic and lattice energy smaller in magnitude. The difference is which change dominates.

For hydroxides, the decrease in lattice energy dominates. For sulfates, the decrease in hydration enthalpy dominates.

**Answer.** The trends differ because both lattice energy and hydration enthalpy decrease in magnitude down Group 2, but lattice energy decrease dominates for $\ce{M(OH)2}$ whereas hydration enthalpy decrease dominates for $\ce{MSO4}$.

**Check.** The sentence names both compound types and both energy terms.
