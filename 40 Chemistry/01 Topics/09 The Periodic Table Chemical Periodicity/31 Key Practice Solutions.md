# The Periodic Table: Chemical Periodicity - Key Practice Solutions

## Solutions

1. Atomic radius decreases from $\ce{Na}$ to $\ce{Cl}$. Proton number increases across the period, while added electrons enter the same principal shell. Shielding increases only slightly, so the effective attraction between the nucleus and outer electrons increases. The electron cloud is pulled closer to the nucleus.

2. $\ce{Na+}$, $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ and $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ each have 10 electrons, so they are isoelectronic. Proton number increases from 11 to 13, so the same number of electrons is attracted more strongly. Radius therefore decreases:

$$
\ce{Na+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}}
$$

3. $\ce{Na}$, $\ce{Mg}$ and $\ce{Al}$ are metals. Their melting points increase because metallic bonding becomes stronger as cation charge and number of delocalised electrons increase. $\ce{Si}$ has a giant covalent structure, so many strong covalent bonds must be broken and its melting point is very high. $\ce{P}$, $\ce{S}$, $\ce{Cl}$ and $\ce{Ar}$ are simple molecular or atomic substances, so melting only overcomes weak instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces.

4. $\ce{Na}$, $\ce{Mg}$ and $\ce{Al}$ conduct well because they contain mobile delocalised electrons. $\ce{Al}$ is usually the best conductor of the three because each atom contributes more delocalised electrons. $\ce{Si}$ is a semiconductor. $\ce{P}$, $\ce{S}$, $\ce{Cl}$ and $\ce{Ar}$ do not conduct because they have no mobile ions or delocalised electrons.

5. The equations are:

$$
\ce{4Na(s) + O2(g) -> 2Na2O(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{2Mg(s) + O2(g) -> 2MgO(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{4Al(s) + 3O2(g) -> 2Al2O3(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{4P(s) + 5O2(g) -> P4O10(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{S(s) + O2(g) -> SO2(g)}
$$

6. The equations are:

$$
\ce{2Na(s) + Cl2(g) -> 2NaCl(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + Cl2(g) -> MgCl2(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{2Al(s) + 3Cl2(g) -> 2AlCl3(s)}
$$

$$
\ce{Si(s) + 2Cl2(g) -> SiCl4(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{2P(s) + 5Cl2(g) -> 2PCl5(s)}
$$

7. Sodium reacts vigorously with cold water:

$$
\ce{2Na(s) + 2H2O(l) -> 2NaOH(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

The solution is strongly alkaline. Magnesium reacts slowly with cold water:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + 2H2O(l) -> Mg(OH)2(s) + H2(g)}
$$

The mixture is weakly alkaline because $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$ is sparingly soluble. Magnesium reacts more readily with steam:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + H2O(g) -> MgO(s) + H2(g)}
$$

8. The oxidation numbers are: $\ce{Na}$ in $\ce{Na2O}$ is +1; $\ce{Mg}$ in $\ce{MgO}$ is +2; $\ce{Al}$ in $\ce{Al2O3}$ is +3; $\ce{P}$ in $\ce{P4O10}$ is +5; $\ce{S}$ in $\ce{SO2}$ is +4; $\ce{S}$ in $\ce{SO3}$ is +6; $\ce{Si}$ in $\ce{SiCl4}$ is +4; $\ce{P}$ in $\ce{PCl5}$ is +5.

9. $\ce{Na2O}$ and $\ce{MgO}$ are basic oxides. Example:

$$
\ce{MgO(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MgCl2(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

$\ce{Al2O3}$ is amphoteric. Examples:

$$
\ce{Al2O3(s) + 6HCl(aq) -> 2AlCl3(aq) + 3H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{Al2O3(s) + 2NaOH(aq) + 3H2O(l) -> 2NaAl(OH)4(aq)}
$$

$\ce{P4O10}$, $\ce{SO2}$ and $\ce{SO3}$ are acidic oxides. Example:

$$
\ce{SO3(g) + 2NaOH(aq) -> Na2SO4(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

10. $\ce{NaCl}$ dissolves without significant hydrolysis, giving a solution about pH 7. $\ce{MgCl2}$ gives a slightly acidic solution. $\ce{AlCl3}$ hydrolyses and gives an acidic solution; a simplified equation is:

$$
\ce{AlCl3(s) + 3H2O(l) -> Al(OH)3(s) + 3HCl(aq)}
$$

$\ce{SiCl4}$ hydrolyses vigorously:

$$
\ce{SiCl4(l) + 2H2O(l) -> SiO2(s) + 4HCl(aq)}
$$

$\ce{PCl5}$ hydrolyses:

$$
\ce{PCl5(s) + 4H2O(l) -> H3PO4(aq) + 5HCl(aq)}
$$

The $\ce{SiCl4}$ and $\ce{PCl5}$ solutions are strongly acidic because $\ce{HCl}$ is formed.

11. The oxide is likely aluminium oxide, $\ce{Al2O3}$, because it is amphoteric:

$$
\ce{Al2O3(s) + 6HCl(aq) -> 2AlCl3(aq) + 3H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{Al2O3(s) + 2NaOH(aq) + 3H2O(l) -> 2NaAl(OH)4(aq)}
$$

12. A covalent chloride that fumes in moist air and gives a strongly acidic solution suggests a non-metal or near non-metal chloride towards the right of Period 3. In the syllabus examples, $\ce{SiCl4}$ and $\ce{PCl5}$ hydrolyse to give acidic products. This behaviour contrasts with ionic chlorides such as $\ce{NaCl}$, which do not hydrolyse significantly.
