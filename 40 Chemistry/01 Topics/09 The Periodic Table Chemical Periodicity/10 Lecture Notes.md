# The Periodic Table: Chemical Periodicity - Lecture Notes

## Learning Scope

This note follows CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level section 9. It covers periodic trends in Period 3, reactions of selected Period 3 elements, acid-base behaviour of named oxides and hydroxides, reactions of named chlorides with water, and the use of periodicity to predict unknown properties. No Chemistry coursebook alignment is claimed.

The Period 3 sequence is:

$$
\ce{Na, Mg, Al, Si, P, S, Cl, Ar}
$$

## What Periodicity Means

Periodicity is the repeating pattern in properties caused by repeating patterns in electronic structure. Across Period 3, each successive element has one more proton and one more electron. The outer electrons are added to the same principal shell, so shielding changes only slightly while nuclear charge increases. This makes many atomic properties change systematically from left to right.

The important AS habit is to connect a property to its cause:

| Property | Main cause to discuss |
|---|---|
| Atomic radius | Nuclear charge, shielding and attraction for outer electrons |
| Ionic radius | Electron loss or gain, number of occupied shells, nuclear attraction in an isoelectronic series |
| Melting point | Structure and bonding |
| Electrical conductivity | Availability of mobile charged particles |
| Oxide and chloride behaviour | Bonding, electronegativity and acid-base character |

## Atomic Radius

Atomic radius decreases across Period 3 from sodium to chlorine.

The number of protons increases across the period. The added electrons enter the same outer shell, so the increase in shielding is small. The effective nuclear attraction for the outer electrons increases, pulling the electron cloud closer to the nucleus.

Argon follows the same general decrease in atomic size, although noble gas radii are measured differently from covalent radii. In explanations, focus on the increasing nuclear charge with similar shielding.

## Ionic Radius

Ionic radius is not a single smooth trend across all Period 3 ions because the common ions do not all have the same charge or the same number of electrons.

For cations:

$$
\ce{Na+ > Mg^{2+} > Al^{3+}}
$$

These ions are isoelectronic: each has 10 electrons. The number of protons increases from $\ce{Na+}$ to $\ce{Al^{3+}}$, so the same electron cloud is pulled more strongly and the radius decreases.

For common anions:

$$
\ce{P^{3-} > S^{2-} > Cl-}
$$

These ions are also isoelectronic: each has 18 electrons. The proton number increases from phosphorus to chlorine, so the radius decreases.

Cations are much smaller than their parent atoms because the outer shell is removed. Anions are larger than their parent atoms because added electrons increase electron-electron repulsion in the outer shell.

## Melting Point Across Period 3

Melting points show a strong structure and bonding pattern:

| Elements | Structure | Melting point explanation |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{Na, Mg, Al}$ | Giant metallic | Metallic bonding becomes stronger from $\ce{Na}$ to $\ce{Al}$ because cation charge increases and there are more delocalised electrons |
| $\ce{Si}$ | Giant covalent | Very high melting point because many strong covalent bonds must be broken |
| $\ce{P, S, Cl, Ar}$ | Simple molecular or atomic | Low melting points because only weak instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces are overcome |

Silicon has the highest melting point in Period 3 because it forms a giant covalent structure. Sulfur has a higher melting point than phosphorus and chlorine because $\ce{S8}$ molecules have more electrons and a larger surface area than $\ce{P4}$ or $\ce{Cl2}$, so the instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces are stronger.

## Electrical Conductivity Across Period 3

Sodium, magnesium and aluminium conduct electricity because they have metallic bonding with mobile delocalised electrons. Conductivity generally increases from sodium to aluminium because there are more delocalised electrons per atom and stronger attraction between metal cations and the electron sea.

Silicon is a semiconductor. Its giant covalent structure does not provide many mobile charged particles at room temperature, but its conductivity can increase under suitable conditions.

Phosphorus, sulfur, chlorine and argon do not conduct electricity because their particles do not contain mobile ions or delocalised electrons.

## Reactions of Period 3 Elements with Oxygen

The syllabus specifies these products:

| Element | Product | Example equation |
|---|---|---|
| Sodium | Sodium oxide | $\ce{4Na(s) + O2(g) -> 2Na2O(s)}$ |
| Magnesium | Magnesium oxide | $\ce{2Mg(s) + O2(g) -> 2MgO(s)}$ |
| Aluminium | Aluminium oxide | $\ce{4Al(s) + 3O2(g) -> 2Al2O3(s)}$ |
| Phosphorus | Phosphorus(V) oxide | $\ce{4P(s) + 5O2(g) -> P4O10(s)}$ |
| Sulfur | Sulfur dioxide | $\ce{S(s) + O2(g) -> SO2(g)}$ |

The metallic oxides on the left are mainly ionic. The non-metal oxides on the right are covalent. Aluminium oxide is important because it is amphoteric.

## Reactions of Period 3 Elements with Chlorine

The syllabus specifies these products:

| Element | Product | Example equation |
|---|---|---|
| Sodium | Sodium chloride | $\ce{2Na(s) + Cl2(g) -> 2NaCl(s)}$ |
| Magnesium | Magnesium chloride | $\ce{Mg(s) + Cl2(g) -> MgCl2(s)}$ |
| Aluminium | Aluminium chloride | $\ce{2Al(s) + 3Cl2(g) -> 2AlCl3(s)}$ |
| Silicon | Silicon tetrachloride | $\ce{Si(s) + 2Cl2(g) -> SiCl4(l)}$ |
| Phosphorus | Phosphorus(V) chloride | $\ce{2P(s) + 5Cl2(g) -> 2PCl5(s)}$ |

Across the period, the chlorides move from mainly ionic solids such as $\ce{NaCl}$ and $\ce{MgCl2}$ towards covalent chlorides such as $\ce{SiCl4}$ and $\ce{PCl5}$.

## Reactions of Sodium and Magnesium with Water

Sodium reacts vigorously with cold water:

$$
\ce{2Na(s) + 2H2O(l) -> 2NaOH(aq) + H2(g)}
$$

The solution is strongly alkaline because $\ce{NaOH}$ is produced.

Magnesium reacts very slowly with cold water because a sparingly soluble layer of magnesium hydroxide forms:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + 2H2O(l) -> Mg(OH)2(s) + H2(g)}
$$

Magnesium reacts more readily with steam:

$$
\ce{Mg(s) + H2O(g) -> MgO(s) + H2(g)}
$$

## Oxidation Numbers in Named Oxides and Chlorides

Oxidation numbers reflect the number of outer shell electrons used or transferred in forming compounds.

| Compound | Oxidation number of Period 3 element |
|---|---:|
| $\ce{Na2O}$ | +1 |
| $\ce{MgO}$ | +2 |
| $\ce{Al2O3}$ | +3 |
| $\ce{P4O10}$ | +5 |
| $\ce{SO2}$ | +4 |
| $\ce{SO3}$ | +6 |
| $\ce{NaCl}$ | +1 |
| $\ce{MgCl2}$ | +2 |
| $\ce{AlCl3}$ | +3 |
| $\ce{SiCl4}$ | +4 |
| $\ce{PCl5}$ | +5 |

For oxides, oxygen is usually -2. For chlorides, chlorine is usually -1. Use the total oxidation number of a neutral compound as zero.

## Oxides with Water

| Oxide | Reaction with water | Likely pH |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{Na2O}$ | $\ce{Na2O(s) + H2O(l) -> 2NaOH(aq)}$ | Strongly alkaline |
| $\ce{MgO}$ | $\ce{MgO(s) + H2O(l) -> Mg(OH)2(s)}$ | Weakly alkaline |
| $\ce{Al2O3}$ | No simple reaction with water | About neutral |
| $\ce{SiO2}$ | No reaction with water | About neutral |
| $\ce{P4O10}$ | $\ce{P4O10(s) + 6H2O(l) -> 4H3PO4(aq)}$ | Acidic |
| $\ce{SO2}$ | $\ce{SO2(g) + H2O(l) <=> H2SO3(aq)}$ | Acidic |
| $\ce{SO3}$ | $\ce{SO3(g) + H2O(l) -> H2SO4(aq)}$ | Strongly acidic |

The trend is from basic oxides on the left, through amphoteric or unreactive oxides in the middle, to acidic oxides on the right. This follows the change from metallic elements to non-metallic elements and from ionic to covalent bonding.

## Acid-Base Behaviour of Oxides and Hydroxides

Basic oxides react with acids:

$$
\ce{Na2O(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> 2NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{MgO(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MgCl2(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

Acidic oxides react with bases:

$$
\ce{SO2(g) + 2NaOH(aq) -> Na2SO3(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{SO3(g) + 2NaOH(aq) -> Na2SO4(aq) + H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{P4O10(s) + 12NaOH(aq) -> 4Na3PO4(aq) + 6H2O(l)}
$$

Aluminium oxide and aluminium hydroxide are amphoteric. They react with both acids and bases:

$$
\ce{Al2O3(s) + 6HCl(aq) -> 2AlCl3(aq) + 3H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{Al2O3(s) + 2NaOH(aq) + 3H2O(l) -> 2NaAl(OH)4(aq)}
$$

$$
\ce{Al(OH)3(s) + 3HCl(aq) -> AlCl3(aq) + 3H2O(l)}
$$

$$
\ce{Al(OH)3(s) + NaOH(aq) -> NaAl(OH)4(aq)}
$$

The hydroxides also show the trend:

- $\ce{NaOH}$ is a soluble strong base.
- $\ce{Mg(OH)2}$ is a sparingly soluble base.
- $\ce{Al(OH)3}$ is amphoteric.

## Chlorides with Water

The named chlorides show a change from neutral ionic chlorides to acidic hydrolysis products.

| Chloride | Behaviour with water | Likely pH |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{NaCl}$ | Dissolves without significant hydrolysis | About 7 |
| $\ce{MgCl2}$ | Dissolves to give a slightly acidic solution | Slightly below 7 |
| $\ce{AlCl3}$ | Hydrolyses to give acidic solution | Acidic |
| $\ce{SiCl4}$ | Vigorous hydrolysis, steamy fumes of $\ce{HCl}$ | Strongly acidic |
| $\ce{PCl5}$ | Hydrolyses to phosphoric acid and $\ce{HCl}$ | Strongly acidic |

Useful equations include:

$$
\ce{SiCl4(l) + 2H2O(l) -> SiO2(s) + 4HCl(aq)}
$$

$$
\ce{PCl5(s) + 4H2O(l) -> H3PO4(aq) + 5HCl(aq)}
$$

A simple representation for aluminium chloride hydrolysis is:

$$
\ce{AlCl3(s) + 3H2O(l) -> Al(OH)3(s) + 3HCl(aq)}
$$

The more covalent the chloride, the more strongly it tends to hydrolyse in water. The products make the solution acidic because hydrogen chloride or acidic hydrated ions are formed.

## Bonding, Electronegativity and Chemical Behaviour

Across Period 3, electronegativity increases. Compounds formed by metals on the left with oxygen or chlorine are more ionic. Compounds formed by non-metals on the right are more covalent.

This explains the chemical pattern:

- Ionic metal oxides contain $\ce{O^{2-}}$ and behave as bases.
- Covalent non-metal oxides form acids in water or react with bases.
- Aluminium oxide and aluminium hydroxide are amphoteric because aluminium compounds sit between strongly metallic and non-metallic behaviour.
- Ionic chlorides such as $\ce{NaCl}$ do not hydrolyse significantly.
- Covalent chlorides such as $\ce{SiCl4}$ and $\ce{PCl5}$ hydrolyse to give acidic solutions.

## Using Periodicity to Predict Unknowns

When given data for an unknown element, ask:

1. Is the substance metallic, giant covalent, simple molecular or monatomic?
2. Does it conduct electricity as a solid?
3. Is its oxide basic, amphoteric or acidic?
4. Does its chloride hydrolyse in water?
5. Do the oxidation numbers fit a group number or valence shell pattern?

For example, an element whose oxide is amphoteric and whose chloride gives an acidic solution is likely to be near the metal to non-metal boundary. In Period 3, that points strongly towards aluminium.
