# The Periodic Table: Chemical Periodicity - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Explain the Melting Point Pattern

**Question.** Explain why the melting point rises from sodium to aluminium, reaches a maximum at silicon, and then falls sharply at phosphorus.

**Working.**

Sodium, magnesium and aluminium have giant metallic structures. From $\ce{Na}$ to $\ce{Al}$, the metal cation charge increases and each atom contributes more delocalised electrons. The attraction between the positive ions and delocalised electrons becomes stronger, so more energy is needed to melt the solid.

Silicon has a giant covalent structure. Melting silicon requires many strong covalent bonds to be broken, so its melting point is much higher than those of the metals.

Phosphorus exists as simple $\ce{P4}$ molecules. Melting phosphorus only overcomes weak instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces between molecules, not covalent bonds inside the molecules. Therefore the melting point falls sharply after silicon.

**Check.** Always name the structure before explaining the melting point.

## Example 2: Compare Atomic Radius and Ionic Radius

**Question.** Explain why atomic radius decreases from $\ce{Na}$ to $\ce{Cl}$, but $\ce{Cl-}$ is much larger than $\ce{Na+}$.

**Working.**

Across the period, proton number increases while added electrons enter the same main shell. Shielding increases only slightly, so the attraction between the nucleus and outer electrons increases. Atomic radius therefore decreases.

$\ce{Na+}$ has lost the third shell and has 10 electrons. $\ce{Cl-}$ has gained an electron and has 18 electrons, so it still has the third shell occupied. $\ce{Cl-}$ is therefore much larger than $\ce{Na+}$.

**Check.** Do not compare these two ions as if they were in one isoelectronic series. They have different numbers of electrons and occupied shells.

## Example 3: Write Reactions with Oxygen and Chlorine

**Question.** Write balanced equations for the reactions of aluminium with oxygen and phosphorus with chlorine to form the syllabus-specified products.

**Working.**

Aluminium forms aluminium oxide, $\ce{Al2O3}$:

$$
\ce{4Al(s) + 3O2(g) -> 2Al2O3(s)}
$$

Phosphorus forms phosphorus(V) chloride, $\ce{PCl5}$:

$$
\ce{2P(s) + 5Cl2(g) -> 2PCl5(s)}
$$

**Check.** The products must match the specified Period 3 products, not just any possible oxide or chloride.

## Example 4: Classify Oxides and Predict pH

**Question.** Three Period 3 oxides are added separately to water: $\ce{Na2O}$, $\ce{Al2O3}$ and $\ce{SO3}$. Predict the reaction or behaviour and the likely pH.

**Working.**

$\ce{Na2O}$ is a basic ionic oxide. It reacts with water to form sodium hydroxide:

$$
\ce{Na2O(s) + H2O(l) -> 2NaOH(aq)}
$$

The solution is strongly alkaline.

$\ce{Al2O3}$ is amphoteric but does not react simply with water. The mixture is about neutral.

$\ce{SO3}$ is an acidic covalent oxide. It reacts with water to form sulfuric acid:

$$
\ce{SO3(g) + H2O(l) -> H2SO4(aq)}
$$

The solution is strongly acidic.

**Check.** Amphoteric does not mean "reacts with water as both acid and base". It means reacts with acids and bases.

## Example 5: Show Amphoteric Behaviour

**Question.** Use equations to show that aluminium hydroxide is amphoteric.

**Working.**

Aluminium hydroxide reacts with acids:

$$
\ce{Al(OH)3(s) + 3HCl(aq) -> AlCl3(aq) + 3H2O(l)}
$$

It also reacts with aqueous sodium hydroxide:

$$
\ce{Al(OH)3(s) + NaOH(aq) -> NaAl(OH)4(aq)}
$$

Because it reacts with both acid and base, $\ce{Al(OH)3}$ is amphoteric.

**Check.** The base named in the syllabus for amphoteric reactions is sodium hydroxide.

## Example 6: Identify a Period 3 Chloride

**Question.** A colourless liquid chloride reacts vigorously with water to give a white solid and an acidic solution. Suggest the chloride and write an equation.

**Working.**

A colourless liquid Period 3 chloride that hydrolyses vigorously is silicon tetrachloride, $\ce{SiCl4}$. It reacts with water to form silicon dioxide and hydrochloric acid:

$$
\ce{SiCl4(l) + 2H2O(l) -> SiO2(s) + 4HCl(aq)}
$$

The acidic solution is caused by $\ce{HCl}$.

**Check.** $\ce{NaCl}$ and $\ce{MgCl2}$ are ionic solids and do not fit "colourless liquid chloride".
