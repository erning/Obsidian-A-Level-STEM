# Electrochemistry - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Oxidation Numbers In Ions

**Question.** Calculate the oxidation number of nitrogen in $\ce{NO3-}$ and chromium in $\ce{Cr2O7^{2-}}$.

**Working.**

For $\ce{NO3-}$, oxygen is usually -2.

$$
x + 3(-2) = -1
$$

$$
x = +5
$$

Nitrogen is +5.

For $\ce{Cr2O7^{2-}}$, oxygen is usually -2.

$$
2x + 7(-2) = -2
$$

$$
2x - 14 = -2
$$

$$
x = +6
$$

Chromium is +6.

**Check.** The oxidation numbers inside a polyatomic ion do not all equal the ion charge. Their sum equals the ion charge.

## Example 2: Identifying Oxidation And Reduction

**Question.** For the reaction:

$$
\ce{2Fe^{2+} + Cl2 -> 2Fe^{3+} + 2Cl-}
$$

identify the species oxidised, the species reduced, the oxidising agent and the reducing agent.

**Working.**

Iron changes from +2 in $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ to +3 in $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$. Its oxidation number increases, so $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ is oxidised.

Chlorine changes from 0 in $\ce{Cl2}$ to -1 in $\ce{Cl-}$. Its oxidation number decreases, so $\ce{Cl2}$ is reduced.

The oxidising agent is the species that is reduced, so $\ce{Cl2}$ is the oxidising agent.

The reducing agent is the species that is oxidised, so $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ is the reducing agent.

**Check.** The agent names are opposite to what happens to the other species, but the agent itself gives the safest clue.

## Example 3: Disproportionation

**Question.** Show that this reaction is disproportionation:

$$
\ce{Cl2 + H2O -> HCl + HClO}
$$

**Working.**

Chlorine in $\ce{Cl2}$ has oxidation number 0.

In $\ce{HCl}$, hydrogen is +1, so chlorine is -1.

In $\ce{HClO}$, hydrogen is +1 and oxygen is -2:

$$
(+1) + x + (-2) = 0
$$

$$
x = +1
$$

Chlorine has changed from 0 to -1 and from 0 to +1. The same element has been reduced and oxidised.

**Answer.** This is disproportionation.

**Check.** Disproportionation requires the same element in the same reactant to move in both directions of oxidation number.

## Example 4: Balancing A Redox Equation

**Question.** Balance the equation for the reaction between acidified manganate(VII) ions and iron(II) ions:

$$
\ce{MnO4- + Fe^{2+} + H+ -> Mn^{2+} + Fe^{3+} + H2O}
$$

**Working.**

Iron changes from +2 to +3, so one $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ loses one electron:

$$
\ce{Fe^{2+} -> Fe^{3+} + e-}
$$

Manganese changes from +7 in $\ce{MnO4-}$ to +2 in $\ce{Mn^{2+}}$, so it gains five electrons:

$$
\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}
$$

Multiply the iron half-equation by 5 and add:

$$
\ce{5Fe^{2+} + MnO4- + 8H+ -> 5Fe^{3+} + Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}
$$

**Check.** Atoms balance, and total charge is +17 on both sides.

## Example 5: Roman Numerals In Names

**Question.** Give the formula of iron(III) oxide and name $\ce{Cu2O}$ using a Roman numeral.

**Working.**

Iron(III) means $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$. Oxide is $\ce{O^{2-}}$. The smallest whole-number ratio that balances charge is two iron ions to three oxide ions:

$$
\ce{Fe2O3}
$$

For $\ce{Cu2O}$, oxygen is -2. The compound is neutral, so the two copper atoms must total +2. Each copper is +1.

**Answer.** Iron(III) oxide is $\ce{Fe2O3}$. $\ce{Cu2O}$ is copper(I) oxide.

**Check.** The Roman numeral gives oxidation number, not the number of atoms.
