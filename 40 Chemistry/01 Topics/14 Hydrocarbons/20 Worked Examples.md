# 14 Hydrocarbons - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Combustion Equation

Write a balanced equation for the complete combustion of propane.

Start with $\ce{C3H8 + O2 -> CO2 + H2O}$. Balance carbon: three $\ce{CO2}$. Balance hydrogen: four $\ce{H2O}$. Oxygen atoms on the right = $3 \times 2 + 4 = 10$, so use five $\ce{O2}$.

$$
\ce{C3H8 + 5O2 -> 3CO2 + 4H2O}
$$

Check: complete combustion gives only $\ce{CO2}$ and $\ce{H2O}$.

## Example 2: Free-Radical Substitution Steps

Show propagation steps for chlorination of ethane.

Initiation first forms chlorine radicals:

$$
\ce{Cl2 ->[uv] 2Cl^.}
$$

Propagation step 1 removes hydrogen from ethane:

$$
\ce{Cl^. + CH3CH3 -> HCl + CH3CH2^.}
$$

Propagation step 2 forms chloroethane and regenerates chlorine radical:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2^. + Cl2 -> CH3CH2Cl + Cl^.}
$$

Check: a propagation sequence must regenerate a radical so the chain can continue.

## Example 3: Cracking

A molecule $\ce{C12H26}$ cracks to form hexane and one alkene. Deduce the alkene.

Write:

$$
\ce{C12H26 -> C6H14 + C_xH_y}
$$

Carbon balance gives $x = 6$. Hydrogen balance gives $y = 26 - 14 = 12$.

The alkene is $\ce{C6H12}$.

$$
\ce{C12H26 -> C6H14 + C6H12}
$$

Check: $\ce{C6H12}$ matches the general formula $\ce{C_nH_{2n}}$ for one non-cyclic alkene.

## Example 4: Major Product of Addition

Predict the major product when propene reacts with $\ce{HBr}$ at room temperature.

The double bond can be protonated in two ways. The major route forms the secondary carbocation $\ce{CH3CH+CH3}$, which is more stable than the primary carbocation $\ce{CH3CH2CH2+}$. Bromide then attacks the carbocation.

Major product:

$$
\ce{CH3CH=CH2 + HBr -> CH3CHBrCH3}
$$

The product is 2-bromopropane.

Check: this is Markovnikov addition explained by carbocation stability, not by memorising which atom goes where.

## Example 5: Testing for a Double Bond

An unknown hydrocarbon decolourises aqueous bromine at room temperature. What does this suggest?

Aqueous bromine is orange/brown. Decolourisation without ultraviolet light suggests addition of bromine across a $\ce{C=C}$ bond. Therefore the hydrocarbon is likely to be an alkene.

Check: alkanes need ultraviolet light for radical substitution with halogens, so rapid decolourisation in the dark points to alkene addition.

## Example 6: Oxidative Cleavage

Predict the organic product when but-2-ene is heated with hot concentrated acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$.

But-2-ene is symmetrical:

$$
\ce{CH3CH=CHCH3}
$$

Each double-bond carbon has one hydrogen, so each side is oxidised to a carboxylic acid. Cleavage gives two molecules of ethanoic acid:

$$
\ce{CH3CH=CHCH3 ->[hot\ acidified\ KMnO4] 2CH3COOH}
$$

Check: cold dilute acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ would form a diol instead; the hot concentrated reagent breaks the double bond.

## Example 7: Addition Polymerisation

Give the repeat unit of poly(propene).

The monomer is $\ce{CH2=CHCH3}$. Open the double bond and keep the carbon skeleton:

$$
\ce{n CH2=CHCH3 -> [-CH2-CH(CH3)-]_{n}}
$$

The repeat unit is $\ce{-CH2-CH(CH3)-}$.

Check: addition polymerisation does not eliminate a small molecule.
