# 14 Hydrocarbons - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level subject content for Topic 14: 14.1 alkanes and 14.2 alkenes. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## Hydrocarbons at AS

A hydrocarbon contains carbon and hydrogen only. AS hydrocarbons focus on two families.

- **Alkanes** are saturated hydrocarbons. A straight-chain alkane has general formula $\ce{C_nH_{2n+2}}$ and contains only carbon-carbon single bonds.
- **Alkenes** are unsaturated hydrocarbons. A non-cyclic alkene with one double bond has general formula $\ce{C_nH_{2n}}$ and contains a $\ce{C=C}$ functional group.

The contrast matters. Alkanes have strong, almost non-polar $\ce{C-H}$ and $\ce{C-C}$ bonds, so they are generally unreactive towards polar reagents. Alkenes have a $\pi$ bond in $\ce{C=C}$, which is electron-rich and more easily attacked by electrophiles.

## Producing Alkanes

Alkanes can be produced by hydrogenating alkenes:

$$
\ce{CH2=CH2 + H2 -> CH3CH3}
$$

Reagents and conditions: $\ce{H2(g)}$, Pt or Ni catalyst, heat.

Alkanes can also be obtained from cracking longer-chain alkanes. Cracking uses heat with $\ce{Al2O3}$ and produces smaller alkanes and alkenes. It is useful because heavier crude oil fractions are converted into lower-$M_r$ hydrocarbons that are more useful as fuels and chemical feedstocks.

Example:

$$
\ce{C10H22 -> C6H14 + C4H8}
$$

## Reactions of Alkanes

### Combustion

Complete combustion produces carbon dioxide and water:

$$
\ce{CH4 + 2O2 -> CO2 + 2H2O}
$$

Incomplete combustion occurs when oxygen is limited and can produce carbon monoxide, carbon and water:

$$
\ce{2CH4 + 3O2 -> 2CO + 4H2O}
$$

Combustion in internal combustion engines has environmental consequences. Carbon monoxide is toxic. Oxides of nitrogen form at high temperature from nitrogen and oxygen in air. Unburnt hydrocarbons contribute to pollution. Catalytic converters help remove these pollutants by oxidising carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons and reducing oxides of nitrogen.

### Free-Radical Substitution

Alkanes react with $\ce{Cl2}$ or $\ce{Br2}$ in ultraviolet light. A hydrogen atom is substituted by a halogen atom. For ethane:

$$
\ce{CH3CH3 + Cl2 -> CH3CH2Cl + HCl}
$$

The mechanism has three stages.

Initiation forms radicals by homolytic fission:

$$
\ce{Cl2 ->[uv] 2Cl^.}
$$

Propagation consumes one radical and forms another:

$$
\ce{Cl^. + CH3CH3 -> HCl + CH3CH2^.}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3CH2^. + Cl2 -> CH3CH2Cl + Cl^.}
$$

Termination removes radicals:

$$
\ce{Cl^. + Cl^. -> Cl2}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3CH2^. + Cl^. -> CH3CH2Cl}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3CH2^. + CH3CH2^. -> CH3CH2CH2CH3}
$$

Free-radical substitution can continue to give further substituted products, so mixtures are common.

## Producing Alkenes

Alkenes can be produced in three AS routes.

1. Elimination of $\ce{HX}$ from a halogenoalkane using ethanolic $\ce{NaOH}$ and heat:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br + NaOH -> CH2=CH2 + NaBr + H2O}
$$

2. Dehydration of an alcohol using a heated catalyst such as $\ce{Al2O3}$ or a concentrated acid such as concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2OH -> CH2=CH2 + H2O}
$$

3. Cracking of a longer-chain alkane.

## Reactions of Alkenes

### Electrophilic Addition

The $\pi$ bond in an alkene is electron-rich. It can attack an electrophile, so alkenes undergo electrophilic addition.

Hydrogenation:

$$
\ce{CH2=CH2 + H2 -> CH3CH3}
$$

Conditions: $\ce{H2(g)}$, Pt or Ni catalyst, heat.

Hydration with steam:

$$
\ce{CH2=CH2 + H2O -> CH3CH2OH}
$$

Conditions: $\ce{H2O(g)}$ and $\ce{H3PO4}$ catalyst.

Addition of hydrogen halides at room temperature:

$$
\ce{CH3CH=CH2 + HBr -> CH3CHBrCH3}
$$

Addition of halogens:

$$
\ce{CH2=CH2 + Br2 -> CH2BrCH2Br}
$$

Aqueous bromine tests for $\ce{C=C}$: orange/brown bromine is decolourised when it adds across the double bond.

### Electrophilic Addition Mechanisms

For bromine and ethene, the electron-rich $\ce{C=C}$ polarises $\ce{Br2}$. A curly arrow from the double bond to the nearer bromine starts the reaction, and a curly arrow from the $\ce{Br-Br}$ bond to the farther bromine forms $\ce{Br-}$. The bromide ion then attacks the carbocation to form $\ce{CH2BrCH2Br}$.

For hydrogen bromide and propene, the first step can form two possible carbocations. The major route forms the more stable secondary carbocation:

$$
\ce{CH3CH=CH2 + H+ -> CH3CH+CH3}
$$

Then bromide attacks:

$$
\ce{CH3CH+CH3 + Br- -> CH3CHBrCH3}
$$

Alkyl groups have a positive inductive effect: they push electron density towards the carbocation and stabilise it. Carbocation stability increases from primary to secondary to tertiary. This explains Markovnikov addition at AS level: the major product comes from the more stable carbocation intermediate.

### Oxidation of Alkenes

Cold dilute acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ oxidises an alkene to a diol:

$$
\ce{CH2=CH2 + [O] + H2O -> HOCH2CH2OH}
$$

Hot concentrated acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ breaks the carbon-carbon double bond. The products reveal where the double bond was in a larger molecule.

- A double-bond carbon with at least one hydrogen is oxidised to a carboxylic acid.
- A terminal $\ce{=CH2}$ carbon is oxidised fully to $\ce{CO2}$.
- A double-bond carbon with no hydrogen becomes a ketone.

### Addition Polymerisation

Alkenes can form addition polymers. The double bonds open and monomers join with no small molecule eliminated:

$$
\ce{n CH2=CH2 -> [-CH2-CH2-]_{n}}
$$

Propene forms poly(propene):

$$
\ce{n CH2=CHCH3 -> [-CH2-CH(CH3)-]_{n}}
$$

## Working Routine

For alkanes, think strong non-polar bonds, combustion, cracking and free-radical substitution. For alkenes, think electron-rich double bond, electrophilic addition, oxidation and polymerisation.

## Common Mistakes

- Showing only termination in a free-radical mechanism; initiation and propagation are required.
- Applying an alkene addition mechanism to an alkane.
- Predicting the wrong major product from HBr and propene; the secondary carbocation dominates (Markovnikov).
- Forgetting that hot concentrated acidified KMnO4 cleaves the carbon-carbon double bond.
- Saying alkanes are unreactive only because they are non-polar; the strong C-H and C-C bonds also matter.
