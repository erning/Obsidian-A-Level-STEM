# 37 Analytical Techniques - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Calculating an Rf Value

In a TLC experiment, a spot moves 3.2 cm from the baseline and the solvent front moves 8.0 cm. Calculate the $R_f$ value.

$$
R_f=\frac{3.2}{8.0}=0.40
$$

Check: $R_f$ has no units and should be between 0 and 1.

## Example 2: Explaining Different Rf Values

Two compounds are run on an aluminium oxide TLC plate with the same solvent. Compound A has $R_f=0.20$ and compound B has $R_f=0.75$. Explain the difference.

Compound A moves less far. It either interacts more strongly with the stationary phase or is less soluble in the mobile phase than compound B. Compound B moves further, so it has greater relative solubility in the mobile phase or weaker interaction with the stationary phase.

Check: both stationary-phase interaction and mobile-phase solubility should be considered.

## Example 3: Gas / Liquid Chromatography Composition

A chromatogram has three peaks with areas 12, 18 and 30 arbitrary units. Calculate the percentage composition.

Total area:

$$
12+18+30=60
$$

Percentages:

$$
\frac{12}{60}\times 100=20\%
$$

$$
\frac{18}{60}\times 100=30\%
$$

$$
\frac{30}{60}\times 100=50\%
$$

Check: percentages add to 100%.

## Example 4: Carbon-13 NMR Environments

Predict the number of carbon-13 NMR peaks for propanone, $\ce{CH3COCH3}$.

The two methyl carbons are equivalent by symmetry. The carbonyl carbon is a different environment. Therefore there are two carbon environments and two carbon-13 NMR peaks.

Check: carbon-13 peak count is about environments, not total carbon atoms.

## Example 5: Proton NMR Splitting in an Ethyl Group

Predict the splitting pattern and integration ratio for the ethyl group in $\ce{CH3CH2Br}$.

The $\ce{CH3}$ protons are adjacent to two equivalent $\ce{CH2}$ protons, so they appear as a triplet by the $n+1$ rule.

The $\ce{CH2}$ protons are adjacent to three equivalent $\ce{CH3}$ protons, so they appear as a quartet.

Integration ratio:

$$
\ce{CH3:CH2}=3:2
$$

Check: splitting uses neighbouring equivalent protons, while integration uses the number of protons in each environment.

## Example 6: D2O Exchange

A proton NMR spectrum has a broad signal that disappears after shaking with $\ce{D2O}$. What does this suggest?

The disappearing signal is due to an exchangeable proton, usually $\ce{O-H}$ or $\ce{N-H}$. The proton exchanges with deuterium from $\ce{D2O}$, so its proton NMR signal disappears or greatly reduces.

Check: $\ce{C-H}$ protons do not normally disappear by this test.

## Example 7: Combining Proton NMR Evidence

A compound has proton NMR signals with relative peak areas 3:2 and splitting patterns triplet and quartet. What fragment is suggested?

A 3:2 integration ratio with a triplet and quartet suggests an ethyl group, $\ce{-CH2CH3}$. The $\ce{CH3}$ group is split by two neighbouring $\ce{CH2}$ protons into a triplet. The $\ce{CH2}$ group is split by three neighbouring $\ce{CH3}$ protons into a quartet.

Check: the fragment is evidence, not necessarily the whole molecule.
