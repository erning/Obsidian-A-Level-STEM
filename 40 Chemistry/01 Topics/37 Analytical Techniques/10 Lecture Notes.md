# 37 Analytical Techniques - Lecture Notes

## AS Link

## Thin-Layer Chromatography

Thin-layer chromatography, TLC, separates substances on a plate.

- The **stationary phase** is a solid such as aluminium oxide on a solid support.
- The **mobile phase** is a polar or non-polar solvent.
- The **baseline** is the starting line where the sample is placed.
- The **solvent front** is the furthest point reached by the solvent.
- The **$R_f$ value** is:

$$
R_f=\frac{\text{distance moved by spot}}{\text{distance moved by solvent front}}
$$

$R_f$ values are interpreted under fixed conditions. A substance with stronger interaction with the stationary phase moves less and has a lower $R_f$. A substance with greater relative solubility in the mobile phase moves further and has a higher $R_f$.

TLC can test purity: a pure sample usually gives one spot in a given solvent system, while a mixture gives more than one spot. A known standard can help identify a component by matching $R_f$ values.

## Gas / Liquid Chromatography

In gas / liquid chromatography, the stationary phase is a high boiling point non-polar liquid on a solid support. The mobile phase is an unreactive gas.

Different components leave the column at different **retention times**. A component that interacts more strongly with the stationary phase is retained longer and has a larger retention time.

The percentage composition of a mixture can be interpreted from the chromatogram using peak areas:

$$
\text{percentage of component}=\frac{\text{area of its peak}}{\text{total peak area}}\times 100
$$

Retention time can help identify a substance by comparison with standards. Peak area helps estimate composition.

## Carbon-13 NMR Spectroscopy

Carbon-13 NMR gives information about different carbon environments. In a simple molecule, each set of chemically equivalent carbon atoms gives one peak.

Use symmetry before counting. For example, ethane has one carbon environment because the two carbon atoms are equivalent. Propanone, $\ce{CH3COCH3}$, has two carbon environments: the two methyl carbons are equivalent, and the carbonyl carbon is different.

Carbon-13 NMR usually does not use peak area to count numbers of carbon atoms at this level. The main information is the number of different carbon environments and approximate chemical shift.

## Proton NMR Spectroscopy

Proton NMR gives several kinds of evidence.

**Chemical shift** shows the environment of the proton. Protons near electronegative atoms or unsaturated groups usually appear further downfield.

**Relative peak area**, or integration, shows the relative number of protons in each environment.

**Splitting pattern** shows the number of equivalent protons on adjacent carbon atoms. The $n+1$ rule is used for simple cases:

- 0 neighbouring equivalent protons gives a singlet.
- 1 gives a doublet.
- 2 gives a triplet.
- 3 gives a quartet.
- More complex cases may appear as a multiplet.

For example, an ethyl group, $\ce{-CH2CH3}$, often gives a triplet for $\ce{-CH3}$ and a quartet for $\ce{-CH2}$, with integration ratio 3:2.

## TMS, Deuterated Solvents and D2O Exchange

Tetramethylsilane, TMS, is used as the standard for chemical shift measurements. It gives a reference signal at $\delta=0$ and is chemically inert in typical NMR samples.

Deuterated solvents such as $\ce{CDCl3}$ are used because ordinary hydrogen-containing solvents would produce large proton NMR signals that obscure the sample.

Protons attached to oxygen or nitrogen can be identified by proton exchange using $\ce{D2O}$. After shaking with $\ce{D2O}$, $\ce{O-H}$ and $\ce{N-H}$ proton signals disappear or greatly reduce because those protons exchange with deuterium.

## Combining Evidence

Good structure deduction uses all data.

1. Use chromatography to judge purity or mixture composition.
2. Use carbon-13 NMR to count carbon environments and symmetry.
3. Use proton NMR chemical shifts to identify proton environments.
4. Use integration to set ratios of protons.
5. Use splitting to identify neighbouring protons.
6. Use $\ce{D2O}$ exchange to identify $\ce{O-H}$ or $\ce{N-H}$ protons.

If two structures fit one piece of evidence, keep both possibilities until another piece distinguishes them.

## Working Routine

- Use chromatography to judge purity or mixture composition before spectroscopy.
- Count carbon environments from the carbon-13 NMR spectrum, allowing for symmetry.
- In proton NMR, combine chemical shift, integration, the n+1 splitting and D2O exchange.
- Keep two candidate structures until a further clue separates them.
## Common Mistakes

- Confusing $R_f$ value with retention time.
- Comparing $R_f$ values from different solvent systems as if they were universal.
- Using carbon-13 NMR peak heights as if they gave carbon ratios.
- Applying the $n+1$ rule without checking adjacent equivalent protons.
- Forgetting that $\ce{O-H}$ and $\ce{N-H}$ protons can exchange with $\ce{D2O}$.
