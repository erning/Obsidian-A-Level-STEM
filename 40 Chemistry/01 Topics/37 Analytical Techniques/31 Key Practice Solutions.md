# 37 Analytical Techniques - Key Practice Solutions

1. The stationary phase is the solid phase, for example aluminium oxide on a support. The mobile phase is a polar or non-polar solvent. The baseline is the starting line for the sample. The solvent front is the furthest distance reached by the solvent. $R_f$ is distance moved by spot divided by distance moved by solvent front.

2. Calculation:

$$
R_f=\frac{4.5}{9.0}=0.50
$$

3. A compound with stronger interaction with the stationary phase moves less and has a lower $R_f$. A compound with greater relative solubility in the mobile phase moves further and has a higher $R_f$.

4. In gas / liquid chromatography, the stationary phase is a high boiling point non-polar liquid on a solid support. The mobile phase is an unreactive gas. Retention time is the time taken for a component to pass through the column and be detected.

5. Total peak area is $25+15+10=50$. Percentages are $50\%$, $30\%$ and $20\%$.

6. Stronger interaction with the stationary phase means the compound spends more time retained on the column, so it takes longer to leave and has a longer retention time.

7. Ethane has 1 carbon-13 peak. Propanone has 2 peaks. Ethanol has 2 peaks.

8. Equivalent carbon atoms give the same carbon-13 NMR signal. Therefore the number of peaks equals the number of carbon environments, not the total number of carbon atoms.

9. Ethanol, $\ce{CH3CH2OH}$, has proton environments $\ce{CH3}$, $\ce{CH2}$ and $\ce{OH}$ with integration ratio 3:2:1.

10. In an ethyl group, $\ce{CH3}$ is next to two equivalent protons, so it is a triplet. $\ce{CH2}$ is next to three equivalent protons, so it is a quartet.

11. TMS is used as the standard for chemical shift measurements. It gives a reference signal at $\delta=0$ and is chemically inert under typical conditions.

12. Deuterated solvents are used because ordinary hydrogen-containing solvents would give large proton NMR signals that obscure the sample.

13. After adding $\ce{D2O}$, exchangeable $\ce{O-H}$ and $\ce{N-H}$ protons are replaced by deuterium, so their proton NMR signals disappear or greatly reduce.

14. TLC can check purity or compare with standards. Gas / liquid chromatography can estimate mixture composition from peak areas. NMR can confirm carbon and proton environments and help identify the product structure.
