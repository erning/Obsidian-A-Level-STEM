# 31 Halogen Compounds - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Making Chlorobenzene

State the reagents and catalyst for converting benzene into chlorobenzene, and write the equation.

Use chlorine with aluminium chloride catalyst.

$$
\ce{C6H6 + Cl2 ->[AlCl3] C6H5Cl + HCl}
$$

Check: this is electrophilic substitution in an arene, so a Lewis acid catalyst is needed.

## Example 2: Halogenoarene or Halogenoalkane?

Classify $\ce{C6H5CH2Cl}$ and explain your answer.

The chlorine is attached to the side-chain carbon, not directly to the aromatic ring. Therefore the compound is not a halogenoarene. It is a halogenoalkane side-chain attached to a phenyl group.

Check: a halogenoarene requires the halogen to be directly bonded to an aromatic ring carbon.

## Example 3: Products from Methylbenzene Ring Chlorination

Predict the main ring positions when methylbenzene reacts with $\ce{Cl2}$ in the presence of $\ce{AlCl3}$.

The methyl group directs electrophilic substitution mainly to the 2- and 4-positions. The main products are therefore the 2- and 4-chlorinated methylbenzene isomers, described in the syllabus wording as 2-chloromethylbenzene and 4-chloromethylbenzene.

Check: the catalyst shows this is ring substitution, not side-chain free-radical substitution.

## Example 4: Reactivity Comparison

Explain why chlorobenzene is less reactive than chloroethane towards nucleophilic substitution.

In chloroethane, the $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond is polar and a nucleophile can attack the carbon attached to chlorine.

In chlorobenzene, chlorine is attached directly to the aromatic ring. Interaction between the chlorine p orbital and the ring $\pi$ system gives the aryl $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond partial double-bond character, so it is stronger. Backside attack is also difficult at an aromatic carbon, and formation of a phenyl cation is unfavourable.

Check: the explanation compares bonding and mechanism possibilities, not just names.

## Example 5: Conditions Decide the Product

Methylbenzene reacts with chlorine in ultraviolet light. Is the product a halogenoarene?

No. Ultraviolet light favours free-radical substitution in the side-chain. The product $\ce{C6H5CH2Cl}$ has chlorine on the side-chain, not directly on the ring, so it is not a halogenoarene.

$$
\ce{C6H5CH3 + Cl2 ->[uv] C6H5CH2Cl + HCl}
$$

Check: ring chlorination would need $\ce{AlCl3}$.
