# 31 Halogen Compounds - Key Practice Solutions

1. A halogenoarene has a halogen atom bonded directly to an aromatic ring. Example: chlorobenzene, $\ce{C6H5Cl}$.

2. Use $\ce{Br2}$ with $\ce{AlBr3}$ catalyst:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + Br2 ->[AlBr3] C6H5Br + HBr}
$$

3. Use $\ce{Cl2}$ with $\ce{AlCl3}$ catalyst:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + Cl2 ->[AlCl3] C6H5Cl + HCl}
$$

4. The methyl group directs substitution mainly to the 2- and 4-positions.

5. The product is $\ce{C6H5CH2Cl}$. It is not a halogenoarene because chlorine is attached to the side-chain carbon, not directly to the aromatic ring.

6. Chloroethane has a polar aliphatic $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond that can be attacked by nucleophiles. In chlorobenzene, interaction with the aromatic $\pi$ system gives the aryl $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond partial double-bond character, making it stronger and less reactive.

7. Backside attack at the aromatic carbon is difficult because of the ring structure and the bonding environment of the aryl carbon.

8. An $S_N1$ pathway would require formation of a phenyl cation, which is highly unfavourable.

9. The topic compares halogenoarenes with AS halogenoalkanes and uses AS ideas about nucleophilic substitution and free-radical side-chain halogenation.

10. Halogen compounds are useful intermediates. In synthesis, deciding whether a halogen is on an alkyl chain or directly on an aromatic ring determines which reactions are realistic.
