# 31 Halogen Compounds - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 A Level subject content for Topic 31.1 Halogen compounds. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## AS Link

AS halogen compounds focused on halogenoalkanes. A halogenoalkane such as chloroethane has a polar carbon-halogen bond and can undergo nucleophilic substitution or elimination. A Level adds halogenoarenes, where the halogen is attached directly to an aromatic ring. The same element is present, but the bonding environment is different, so the reactivity changes.

## What Is a Halogenoarene?

A halogenoarene, also called an aryl halide, has a halogen atom bonded directly to an aromatic ring. Chlorobenzene, $\ce{C6H5Cl}$, is the simplest example.

This is different from a compound such as $\ce{C6H5CH2Cl}$. In $\ce{C6H5CH2Cl}$, the chlorine is on the side-chain, so the carbon-chlorine bond is an alkyl $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond rather than an aryl $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond.

## Producing Halogenoarenes

Halogenoarenes are produced by electrophilic substitution of an arene using chlorine or bromine in the presence of a Lewis acid catalyst.

For benzene to chlorobenzene:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + Cl2 ->[AlCl3] C6H5Cl + HCl}
$$

For benzene to bromobenzene:

$$
\ce{C6H6 + Br2 ->[AlBr3] C6H5Br + HBr}
$$

The catalyst polarises the halogen molecule and helps form the electrophile needed for aromatic substitution.

Methylbenzene reacts similarly on the ring. The methyl group directs substitution mainly to the 2- and 4-positions, so chlorination of the ring gives mainly 2-chloromethylbenzene and 4-chloromethylbenzene in the CAIE wording: chlorine is on the ring, and the methyl group remains attached to the ring.

## Chloroethane Compared with Chlorobenzene

Chloroethane and chlorobenzene both contain a $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond, but they do not react in the same way.

In chloroethane, the carbon attached to chlorine is an aliphatic carbon. The $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond is polar, and nucleophiles can attack the electron-deficient carbon. Chloroethane therefore undergoes nucleophilic substitution under suitable conditions.

In chlorobenzene, the chlorine is bonded directly to an aromatic carbon. The p orbital on chlorine can interact with the delocalised $\pi$ system of the ring, giving the aryl $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond partial double-bond character. The bond is stronger and harder to break. The aromatic ring also prevents the usual backside attack required for a simple $S_N2$ pathway, and forming a phenyl cation for an $S_N1$ pathway is highly unfavourable.

The result is that chlorobenzene is much less reactive than chloroethane in nucleophilic substitution.

## Ring Halogen or Side-Chain Halogen?

This topic depends on the distinction from Topic 30.

- Ring halogenation uses $\ce{Cl2}$ or $\ce{Br2}$ with $\ce{AlCl3}$ or $\ce{AlBr3}$ and gives a halogenoarene.
- Side-chain halogenation of methylbenzene uses ultraviolet light and gives a halogenoalkane side-chain, such as $\ce{C6H5CH2Cl}$.

The products look similar if written too quickly, so always identify whether the halogen is directly attached to the ring.

## Common Mistakes

- Calling $\ce{C6H5CH2Cl}$ a halogenoarene. It is an aryl-substituted halogenoalkane because chlorine is on the side-chain.
- Applying AS $S_N1$ or $S_N2$ mechanisms directly to chlorobenzene.
- Explaining low reactivity only by saying "benzene is stable" without discussing the aryl $\ce{C-Cl}$ bond.
- Forgetting the Lewis acid catalyst for producing halogenoarenes.
