# 16 Hydroxy Compounds - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level subject content for Topic 16.1, alcohols. No coursebook alignment is claimed.

## Alcohols and the Hydroxyl Group

Alcohols contain the hydroxyl group, $\ce{-OH}$, bonded to a saturated carbon atom. They are named with the suffix -ol, for example ethanol, $\ce{CH3CH2OH}$, and propan-2-ol, $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH3}$.

The $\ce{O-H}$ bond is polar, so alcohols can form hydrogen bonds. Their chemistry at AS is controlled by two main ideas:

- the $\ce{C-O}$ and $\ce{O-H}$ bonds can react in substitution, sodium reaction and esterification
- the carbon bearing $\ce{-OH}$ can be oxidised or dehydrated, depending on the structure and conditions

## Classifying Alcohols

Classification depends on how many carbon atoms are attached to the carbon bearing the hydroxyl group.

| Class | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | $\ce{-OH}$ carbon attached to one carbon, or none in methanol | $\ce{CH3CH2OH}$ |
| Secondary | $\ce{-OH}$ carbon attached to two carbons | $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH3}$ |
| Tertiary | $\ce{-OH}$ carbon attached to three carbons | $\ce{(CH3)3COH}$ |

In molecules with more than one alcohol group, classify each $\ce{-OH}$-bearing carbon separately.

## Producing Alcohols

CAIE AS requires these routes.

### Hydration of Alkenes

Alkenes react with steam using $\ce{H3PO4}$ catalyst:

$$
\ce{CH2=CH2 + H2O -> CH3CH2OH}
$$

Conditions: $\ce{H2O(g)}$ and $\ce{H3PO4}$ catalyst.

### Oxidation of Alkenes to Diols

Cold dilute acidified potassium manganate(VII), $\ce{KMnO4}$, adds two hydroxyl groups across $\ce{C=C}$:

$$
\ce{CH2=CH2 + [O] + H2O -> HOCH2CH2OH}
$$

### Hydrolysis of Halogenoalkanes

Halogenoalkanes react with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ and heat:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2Br + OH- -> CH3CH2OH + Br-}
$$

### Reduction of Carbonyl Compounds

Aldehydes and ketones are reduced by $\ce{NaBH4}$ or $\ce{LiAlH4}$:

$$
\ce{CH3CHO + 2[H] -> CH3CH2OH}
$$

$$
\ce{CH3COCH3 + 2[H] -> CH3CH(OH)CH3}
$$

Aldehydes give primary alcohols. Ketones give secondary alcohols.

### Reduction of Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids are reduced by $\ce{LiAlH4}$ to primary alcohols:

$$
\ce{CH3COOH + 4[H] -> CH3CH2OH + H2O}
$$

### Hydrolysis of Esters

Esters hydrolyse with dilute acid or dilute alkali and heat to give alcohols as one product:

$$
\ce{CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O -> CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH}
$$

In alkaline hydrolysis, a carboxylate salt forms first.

## Reactions of Alcohols

### Combustion

Alcohols burn in oxygen to form carbon dioxide and water:

$$
\ce{C2H5OH + 3O2 -> 2CO2 + 3H2O}
$$

### Substitution to Halogenoalkanes

Alcohols can be converted to halogenoalkanes. Reagent options include $\ce{HX(g)}$, $\ce{KCl}$ with concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$ or concentrated $\ce{H3PO4}$, $\ce{PCl3}$ and heat, $\ce{PCl5}$, or $\ce{SOCl2}$.

Example:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2OH + HBr -> CH3CH2Br + H2O}
$$

### Reaction with Sodium

Alcohols react with sodium to form a sodium alkoxide and hydrogen gas:

$$
\ce{2CH3CH2OH + 2Na -> 2CH3CH2ONa + H2}
$$

This shows alcohols can act as very weak acids.

### Oxidation

Acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ or acidified $\ce{KMnO4}$ oxidises primary and secondary alcohols. With acidified dichromate(VI), the orange solution turns green when oxidation occurs.

Primary alcohols first oxidise to aldehydes. To collect the aldehyde, use distillation so it leaves the oxidising mixture before further oxidation:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2OH + [O] -> CH3CHO + H2O}
$$

Under reflux, the aldehyde remains in the mixture and is further oxidised to a carboxylic acid:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2OH + 2[O] -> CH3COOH + H2O}
$$

Secondary alcohols oxidise to ketones:

$$
\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH3 + [O] -> CH3COCH3 + H2O}
$$

Tertiary alcohols are not oxidised under these AS conditions because the carbon bearing $\ce{-OH}$ has no hydrogen atom to remove.

### Dehydration

Alcohols can be dehydrated to alkenes using a heated catalyst such as $\ce{Al2O3}$ or a concentrated acid:

$$
\ce{CH3CH2OH -> CH2=CH2 + H2O}
$$

### Esterification

Alcohols react with carboxylic acids using concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$ as catalyst to form esters:

$$
\ce{CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH <=> CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O}
$$

This example forms ethyl ethanoate.

## Tri-Iodomethane Reaction

Alcohols containing the group $\ce{CH3CH(OH)-R}$ give a positive tri-iodomethane test with alkaline $\ce{I2(aq)}$. The observation is a yellow precipitate of tri-iodomethane, $\ce{CHI3}$. The other organic product is a carboxylate ion, $\ce{RCO2-}$.

For ethanol, the group is $\ce{CH3CH(OH)-H}$, so ethanol gives a positive test. For propan-2-ol, $\ce{R}$ is $\ce{CH3}$, so it also gives a positive test.

## Acidity Compared with Water

Alcohols are less acidic than water. Sodium reacts with both water and alcohols, but alcohols do not react significantly with aqueous sodium hydroxide. Alkyl groups tend to donate electron density and make the alkoxide ion, $\ce{RO-}$, slightly less stable than hydroxide, $\ce{OH-}$.

## Study Routine

For alcohol reactions, identify the alcohol class first. Then choose conditions: distillation or reflux for primary alcohol oxidation, aqueous or ethanolic conditions for links to halogenoalkanes, and concentrated acid or heated catalyst for dehydration.
