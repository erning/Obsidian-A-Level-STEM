# 20 Polymerisation - Lecture Notes

## Source Route

These notes follow the CAIE Chemistry 9701 AS Level subject content for topic 20, **Polymerisation**:

- 20.1 Addition polymerisation

The AS focus is addition polymerisation of alkene monomers, including poly(ethene) and poly(chloroethene), PVC. Condensation polymerisation is not part of this AS topic.

## Learning Scope

By the end of this topic you should be able to:

- describe addition polymerisation as exemplified by poly(ethene) and poly(chloroethene), PVC;
- deduce the repeat unit of an addition polymer from a given monomer;
- identify the monomer or monomers present in a given section of an addition polymer molecule;
- recognise disposal difficulties of poly(alkene)s, including non-biodegradability and harmful combustion products.

## What Addition Polymerisation Does

Addition polymerisation joins many alkene monomers together. The carbon-carbon double bond opens, and the monomers link into a long chain. No small molecule is lost.

For ethene:

$$
\ce{n CH2=CH2 -> [-CH2-CH2-]}_n
$$

Key idea: the double bond in the monomer becomes a single-bonded carbon chain in the polymer.

## Monomer, Repeat Unit, and Polymer

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| monomer | small molecule that joins to make a polymer |
| repeat unit | smallest section that repeats in the polymer chain |
| polymer | long-chain molecule made from many repeat units |

For an alkene monomer $\ce{CH2=CHR}$, the repeat unit is:

$$
\ce{[-CH2-CH(R)-]}_n
$$

The group $\ce{R}$ stays attached to the same carbon; only the double bond changes into single bonds along the chain.

## Example: Poly(ethene)

Ethene is $\ce{CH2=CH2}$. During addition polymerisation, the double bond opens:

$$
\ce{n CH2=CH2 -> [-CH2-CH2-]}_n
$$

The repeat unit is $\ce{-CH2-CH2-}$.

The polymer name is poly(ethene). The name inside the brackets is the monomer name.

## Example: Poly(chloroethene), PVC

Chloroethene is $\ce{CH2=CHCl}$. Its common polymer is poly(chloroethene), often called PVC.

$$
\ce{n CH2=CHCl -> [-CH2-CHCl-]}_n
$$

The chlorine atom remains as a side atom on alternate chain carbons in the repeat unit. The double bond does not remain in the polymer chain.

## Drawing Repeat Units

Use this routine:

1. Find the $\ce{C=C}$ bond in the monomer.
2. Change $\ce{C=C}$ into $\ce{C-C}$.
3. Keep all atoms or groups attached to the two alkene carbons.
4. Put brackets around the repeat unit.
5. Show open continuation bonds through the bracket edges.
6. Add $n$ outside the brackets.

For propene, $\ce{CH2=CHCH3}$:

$$
\ce{n CH2=CHCH3 -> [-CH2-CH(CH3)-]}_n
$$

The methyl group is a side group. It is not placed in the main chain unless the displayed structure requires a different orientation.

## Finding Monomers from Polymer Sections

To work backwards:

1. Identify two adjacent carbon atoms that form one repeat unit.
2. Remove the continuation bonds.
3. Re-form the double bond between the two backbone carbons.
4. Keep side groups attached to their original carbons.

For a repeat unit $\ce{-CH2-CH(CH3)-}$, the monomer is:

$$
\ce{CH2=CHCH3}
$$

For a repeat unit $\ce{-CH2-CHCl-}$, the monomer is:

$$
\ce{CH2=CHCl}
$$

If a given polymer section has more than one kind of repeat pattern, the question may be asking for more than one monomer. In that case, restore a double bond across each pair of backbone carbons that came from an alkene unit.

## Disposal Difficulties

The syllabus names two required disposal issues for poly(alkene)s:

- **non-biodegradability**: many poly(alkene)s are chemically inert, so microorganisms do not break them down quickly;
- **harmful combustion products**: burning polymers can produce harmful gases, especially if combustion is incomplete or if the polymer contains atoms such as chlorine.

For example, poly(chloroethene), PVC, contains chlorine. Burning PVC can produce acidic and harmful chlorine-containing gases such as hydrogen chloride.

Good answers should connect the disposal problem to chemical structure. Poly(alkene)s have strong covalent bonds and usually lack easily hydrolysed functional groups, so they persist in the environment.

## Working Routine

- For a repeat unit, change C=C into C-C, keep all side groups, then add brackets, continuation bonds and n.
- To recover the monomer, cut one repeat unit and restore the double bond.
- For disposal, link non-biodegradability to the strong C-C backbone and the absence of hydrolysable groups.
## Common Mistakes

- Leaving the $\ce{C=C}$ double bond in the addition polymer repeat unit.
- Forgetting the brackets, $n$, or continuation bonds.
- Moving a side group to the wrong carbon when drawing the repeat unit.
- Naming the polymer from the repeat unit incorrectly; poly(ethene) comes from ethene, not from "polyethylene monomer".
- Confusing addition polymerisation with condensation polymerisation.
- Giving vague disposal answers without mentioning non-biodegradability or harmful combustion products.
