# 20 Polymerisation - Worked Examples

## Example 1: Poly(ethene)

**Question.** Write an equation for the addition polymerisation of ethene and state the repeat unit.

**Working.** Ethene contains a $\ce{C=C}$ bond. Addition polymerisation opens the double bond and links the monomers together.

$$
\ce{n CH2=CH2 -> [-CH2-CH2-]}_n
$$

**Answer.** The repeat unit is $\ce{-CH2-CH2-}$.

**Check.** No atoms are lost, and the double bond is no longer present in the repeat unit.

## Example 2: Poly(chloroethene), PVC

**Question.** Deduce the repeat unit of poly(chloroethene), PVC, from chloroethene, $\ce{CH2=CHCl}$.

**Working.** Open the double bond and keep the chlorine attached to the same carbon.

$$
\ce{n CH2=CHCl -> [-CH2-CHCl-]}_n
$$

**Answer.** The repeat unit is $\ce{-CH2-CHCl-}$.

**Check.** The chlorine atom is retained as a side atom. It is not eliminated.

## Example 3: Repeat Unit from Propene

**Question.** Draw the repeat unit formed from propene, $\ce{CH2=CHCH3}$.

**Working.** The double bond is between the first two carbon atoms. Replace it with a single bond in the chain and keep $\ce{CH3}$ as a side group.

$$
\ce{n CH2=CHCH3 -> [-CH2-CH(CH3)-]}_n
$$

**Answer.** $\ce{[-CH2-CH(CH3)-]}_n$.

**Check.** The repeat unit has three carbon atoms, matching the propene monomer.

## Example 4: Finding a Monomer from a Repeat Unit

**Question.** A polymer has repeat unit $\ce{-CH2-CH(CN)-}$. Identify the monomer.

**Working.** Restore a double bond between the two backbone carbons and keep $\ce{CN}$ attached to the second carbon.

$$
\ce{-CH2-CH(CN)- -> CH2=CHCN}
$$

**Answer.** $\ce{CH2=CHCN}$.

**Check.** Addition polymerisation changes only the double bond; the side group remains attached.

## Example 5: Disposal Explanation

**Question.** Explain two difficulties associated with disposal of poly(alkene)s.

**Working.** The syllabus names non-biodegradability and harmful combustion products. A complete answer should link the issue to the polymer.

**Answer.** Many poly(alkene)s are non-biodegradable because their strong carbon-carbon chains and lack of easily hydrolysed groups make them resistant to breakdown by microorganisms. Burning them can produce harmful combustion products; for example, incomplete combustion can produce carbon monoxide, and chlorine-containing polymers such as PVC can produce acidic chlorine-containing gases.

**Check.** The answer gives two named disposal problems and explains why they matter.

## Example 6: Addition or Condensation?

**Question.** A polymer forms from many molecules of $\ce{CH2=CH2}$ and no small molecule is eliminated. What type of polymerisation is this?

**Working.** The monomer is an alkene, and the double bond opens. No small molecule is lost.

**Answer.** Addition polymerisation.

**Check.** Condensation polymerisation would involve elimination of a small molecule such as water or hydrogen chloride; that is not happening here.
