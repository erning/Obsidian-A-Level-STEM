# 24 Electrochemistry - Key Practice Solutions

## 1. Electrolysis Product Prediction

(a) Molten $\ce{NaCl}$ contains $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$.

Cathode:

$$
\ce{Na+ + e- -> Na}
$$

Anode:

$$
\ce{2Cl- -> Cl2 + 2e-}
$$

(b) In aqueous $\ce{CuSO4}$ with inert electrodes, $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ is reduced at the cathode and water or hydroxide ions are oxidised at the anode.

Cathode:

$$
\ce{Cu^{2+} + 2e- -> Cu}
$$

Anode:

$$
\ce{4OH- -> O2 + 2H2O + 4e-}
$$

(c) Concentrated aqueous $\ce{NaCl}$ usually gives hydrogen at the cathode and chlorine at the anode.

Cathode:

$$
\ce{2H2O + 2e- -> H2 + 2OH-}
$$

Anode:

$$
\ce{2Cl- -> Cl2 + 2e-}
$$

Q = It = 1.50 \times (20.0 \times 60) = 1800\ \text{C}

## 3. Gas Volume From Electrolysis

$$
Q = 3.00 \times (15.0 \times 60) = 2700\ \text{C}
$$

$$
n(e^-) = \frac{2700}{96500} = 0.0280\ \text{mol}
$$

From:

$$
\ce{2H+ + 2e- -> H2}
$$

$$
n(\ce{H2}) = \frac{0.0280}{2} = 0.0140\ \text{mol}
$$

$$
V(\ce{H2}) = 0.0140 \times 24.0 = 0.336\ \text{dm}^3
$$

## 4. Avogadro Constant By Electrolysis

For copper deposition:

$$
\ce{Cu^{2+} + 2e- -> Cu}
$$

Charge:

$$
Q = 0.600 \times 1608 = 964.8\ \text{C}
$$

Moles of copper:

$$
n(\ce{Cu}) = \frac{0.3175}{63.5} = 5.00 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{mol}
$$

Moles of electrons:

$$
n(e^-) = 2(5.00 \times 10^{-3}) = 1.00 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{mol}
$$

$$
F = \frac{964.8}{1.00 \times 10^{-2}} = 9.65 \times 10^4\ \text{C mol}^{-1}
$$

$$
L = \frac{F}{e}
= \frac{9.65 \times 10^4}{1.60 \times 10^{-19}}
= 6.03 \times 10^{23}\ \text{mol}^{-1}
$$

## 5. Standard Hydrogen Electrode

The standard hydrogen electrode uses the half-equation:

$$
\ce{2H+(aq) + 2e- <=> H2(g)}
$$

It contains a platinum electrode, hydrogen gas at standard pressure, and $\ce{H+}$ at $1\ \text{mol dm}^{-3}$, usually at $298\ \text{K}$. Platinum is used because it conducts and provides a surface for electron transfer but is chemically inert. Its potential is assigned $0.00\ \text{V}$ by convention, so other standard electrode potentials can be measured relative to it.

## 6. Standard Cell Potential

Silver has the more positive reduction potential, so $\ce{Ag+}$ is reduced and copper is oxidised.

Reduction:

$$
\ce{Ag+ + e- -> Ag}
$$

Oxidation:

$$
\ce{Cu -> Cu^{2+} + 2e-}
$$

Overall:

$$
\ce{Cu(s) + 2Ag+(aq) -> Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2Ag(s)}
$$

$$
E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = +0.80 - (+0.34) = +0.46\ \text{V}
$$

Electrons flow from the copper electrode to the silver electrode in the external circuit.

## 7. Redox Prediction

If $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ oxidises $\ce{Sn^{2+}}$, then $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ is reduced and $\ce{Sn^{2+}}$ is oxidised.

$$
E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = +0.77 - (+0.15) = +0.62\ \text{V}
$$

The positive value means the reaction is feasible under standard conditions.

Overall equation:

$$
\ce{2Fe^{3+}(aq) + Sn^{2+}(aq) -> 2Fe^{2+}(aq) + Sn^{4+}(aq)}
$$

## 8. Concentration And Gibbs Free Energy

(a) For:

$$
\ce{Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e- <=> Cu(s)}
$$

the solid copper is omitted from the concentration expression:

$$
E = E^\circ + \frac{0.059}{2}\log[\ce{Cu^{2+}}]

$$
E = 0.34 + 0.0295\log(0.010)
$$

$$
E = 0.34 + 0.0295(-2.00) = 0.281\ \text{V}
$$

$$
\Delta G^\circ = -nE^\circ_{\text{cell}}F
