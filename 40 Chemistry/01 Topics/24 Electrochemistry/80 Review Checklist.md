# 24 Electrochemistry - Review Checklist

## Recall

- Cathode means reduction; anode means oxidation.
- $Q=It$ and $F=Le$.
- One mole of electrons carries one Faraday of charge.
- Standard electrode potentials are standard reduction potentials measured relative to the standard hydrogen electrode.
- $E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cathode}} - E^\circ_{\text{anode}}$.
- $\Delta G^\circ = -nE^\circ_{\text{cell}}F$.

## Perform

- Predict electrolysis products from molten or aqueous state, redox position, and concentration.
- Calculate mass or gas volume liberated during electrolysis from current and time.
- Describe how the standard hydrogen electrode and other half-cells are set up.
- Combine two standard electrode potentials to calculate $E^\circ_{\text{cell}}$.
- Deduce electrode polarity and electron flow in a simple cell.
- Construct full redox equations from half-equations.
- Use the Nernst equation to calculate how concentration changes an electrode potential.

## Explain

- A more positive $E^\circ$ means a stronger tendency for reduction.
- A positive $E^\circ_{\text{cell}}$ means a reaction is feasible under standard conditions.
- Changing ion concentration shifts electrode potential because it changes the relative tendency of the half-cell to accept electrons.
- A positive cell potential corresponds to a negative Gibbs free energy change.

## Avoid

- Do not assign anode and cathode from charge alone; use oxidation and reduction.
- Do not forget water in aqueous electrolysis.
- Do not use minutes directly in $Q=It$.
- Do not multiply $E^\circ$ values when balancing electrons.
- Do not reverse a redox prediction without recalculating the cell potential.
- Do not use the Nernst equation without matching the oxidised and reduced species to the written half-equation.

## Ready To Move On

You are ready when you can predict electrolysis products, calculate products from charge, set up a standard cell from two half-equations, determine the feasible redox direction from $E^\circ$ values, and explain how concentration or Gibbs free energy changes the prediction.
