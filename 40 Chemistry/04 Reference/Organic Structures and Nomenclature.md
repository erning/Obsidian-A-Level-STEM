# Organic Structures and Nomenclature

This note is a quick guide to representation and naming conventions for Chemistry 9701 organic work. The local syllabus was checked for expected conventions for representing organic structures; this is not a full organic chemistry lesson.

## Structural Formulae

A structural formula should give enough detail to identify the structure unambiguously.

- Propan-1-ol: $\ce{CH3CH2CH2OH}$, not just $\ce{C3H8O}$.
- But-2-ene: $\ce{CH3CH=CHCH3}$, not just $\ce{C4H8}$.
- Put the functional group where it belongs: $\ce{CH3COOH}$ is not the same structural information as a molecular formula.

## Displayed and Skeletal Formulae

- A displayed formula shows the relative placing of atoms and the number of bonds between them.
- A skeletal formula removes carbon atoms and hydrogen atoms attached to carbon from the carbon skeleton, leaving the carbon-carbon framework and functional groups.
- Skeletal and partial-skeletal formulae are acceptable when the structure is unambiguous.
- The aromatic ring convention may be used where the syllabus expects aromatic structures.

## Naming Checklist

- Find the longest relevant carbon chain or the parent structure.
- Identify the principal functional group.
- Number the chain to give the functional group or substituents the lowest sensible locants.
- Use prefixes for substituents and suffixes for the main functional group.
- Use `di-`, `tri-`, and locants when groups repeat.
- Include stereochemical or positional detail when the structure is otherwise ambiguous.

## Mechanism Conventions

When drawing organic mechanisms, show:

- charges and partial charges where they explain reactivity;
- lone pairs when they are the source of an electron-pair movement;
- curly arrows from the electron pair or bond being moved;
- products and by-products with charges balanced.

## Common Weaknesses

- Giving a molecular formula when a structural formula is required.
- Omitting the position of a functional group or double bond.
- Drawing a skeletal formula that hides a heteroatom or functional group.
- Starting a curly arrow from a positive charge instead of from an electron pair.
