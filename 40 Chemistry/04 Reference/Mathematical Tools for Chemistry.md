# Mathematical Tools for Chemistry

This reference collects the calculation tools that repeatedly appear in Chemistry 9701. The local syllabus mathematical requirements were checked for scope.

## Core Formulae

$$
\text{density} = \frac{\text{mass}}{\text{volume}}
$$

$$
\text{concentration} = \frac{\text{amount}}{\text{volume}}
$$

$$
n = \frac{m}{M_r}
$$

Useful gas-volume form:

$$
n = \frac{V}{V_m}
$$

Use consistent units before substitution.

## Units and Conversions

- Convert $\mathrm{cm^3}$ to $\mathrm{dm^3}$ by dividing by $1000$.
- Convert $\mathrm{dm^3}$ to $\mathrm{m^3}$ by dividing by $1000$.
- Convert $\mathrm{mg}$ to $\mathrm{g}$ by dividing by $1000$.
- Convert $\mathrm{J}$ to $\mathrm{kJ}$ by dividing by $1000$.
- Convert temperature in degrees Celsius to kelvin using $T / \mathrm{K} = t / ^\circ\mathrm{C} + 273$ unless the question specifies a more precise conversion.

## Significant Figures

- Round final answers to an appropriate number of significant figures.
- A calculated answer is usually given to the same number of significant figures as, or one more than, the least precise value used.
- Keep extra digits during working to avoid rounding error.
- Do not make an experimental result look more precise than the apparatus allows.

## Percentages and Uncertainty

$$
\text{percentage error} = \frac{\text{absolute error}}{\text{measured value}} \times 100
$$

For a change calculated from two readings, include the uncertainty in both readings when appropriate.

Example for a temperature change:

$$
\text{percentage error} = \frac{2 \times 0.5}{14.0} \times 100
$$

## Graph Tools

- Test direct proportionality by checking whether a graph passes through the origin or whether the ratio is constant.
- Use $y = mx + c$ when a relationship can be linearised.
- Interpret the gradient $m$ and intercept $c$ in chemical terms.
- Use a tangent gradient as a rate of change.
- Use area under a curve only where the chemical meaning is defined.

## Logarithms

- Use $\log x$ for base 10 logarithms.
- Use $\ln x$ for natural logarithms.
- pH calculations use logarithms, so check whether the value is a concentration, a hydrogen ion concentration, or a transformed expression.
