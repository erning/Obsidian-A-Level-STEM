# Kinematics Key Practice Solutions

These solutions correspond to [Key Practice Problems](30%20Key%20Practice%20Problems.md).

Take $g=9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}$ where needed.

## A. Motion Language and Graphs

1. Distance is the total path length:

   $$
   420+160=580\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   Take east as positive. The displacement is

   $$
   420-160=260\ \mathrm{m}
   $$

   east.

   Average speed:

   $$
   \frac{580}{145}=4.00\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   Average velocity:

   $$
   \frac{260}{145}=1.79\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}
   $$

   east.

   The speed is greater than the magnitude of the velocity because part of the
   motion is back towards the starting point.

2. Convert the speeds:

   $$
   36\ \mathrm{km\ h^{-1}}=10\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}},
   $$

   $$
   90\ \mathrm{km\ h^{-1}}=25\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   The average acceleration is

   $$
   a=\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}
   =\frac{25-10}{12}
   =1.25\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

3. Velocity is the gradient of the displacement-time graph.

   First section:

   $$
   v=\frac{9.0-0}{3.0-0}
   =3.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   Second section:

   $$
   v=\frac{9.0-9.0}{5.0-3.0}
   =0.
   $$

   Third section:

   $$
   v=\frac{-3.0-9.0}{9.0-5.0}
   =-3.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   The object is stationary from $3.0$ to $5.0\ \mathrm{s}$. It moves in the
   negative direction from $5.0$ to $9.0\ \mathrm{s}$.

4. Acceleration is the gradient of the velocity-time graph.

   First section:

   $$
   a=\frac{10.0-2.0}{4.0}
   =2.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   Final section:

   $$
   a=\frac{-2.0-10.0}{12.0-8.0}
   =-3.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   Displacement is signed area.

   From $0$ to $4.0\ \mathrm{s}$:

   $$
   \frac{1}{2}(2.0+10.0)(4.0)=24\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   From $4.0$ to $8.0\ \mathrm{s}$:

   $$
   10.0(4.0)=40\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   From $8.0$ to $12.0\ \mathrm{s}$:

   $$
   \frac{1}{2}(10.0+(-2.0))(4.0)=16\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   Total displacement:

   $$
   24+40+16=80\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   For distance travelled, count the area below the time axis as positive. In
   the final section, the velocity reaches zero after

   $$
   \frac{10.0}{3.0}=3.33\ \mathrm{s}
   $$

   after $t=8.0\ \mathrm{s}$. The positive part has area

   $$
   \frac{1}{2}(3.33)(10.0)=16.7\ \mathrm{m},
   $$

   and the negative part has magnitude

   $$
   \frac{1}{2}(0.67)(2.0)=0.67\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   Total distance:

   $$
   24+40+16.7+0.67=81\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

5. Instantaneous acceleration is the tangent gradient:

   $$
   a=\frac{16.0-4.0}{8.0-2.0}
   =2.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   The constant-acceleration equations cannot automatically be used over the
   whole curve because a curved velocity-time graph has changing gradient, so
   acceleration is changing. The tangent gives acceleration at one instant, not
   necessarily the acceleration for the whole interval.

## B. Uniform Acceleration in a Straight Line

6. Use

   $$
   v=u+at.
   $$

   With $u=0$, $a=3.2\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}$, and $t=4.0\ \mathrm{s}$,

   $$
   v=0+(3.2)(4.0)=12.8\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   Displacement:

   $$
   s=ut+\frac{1}{2}at^2
   =0+\frac{1}{2}(3.2)(4.0)^2
   =25.6\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

7. Use

   $$
   v^2=u^2+2as.
   $$

   Rearranging,

   $$
   a=\frac{v^2-u^2}{2s}
   =\frac{28^2-12^2}{2(400)}
   =0.800\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   Then

   $$
   t=\frac{v-u}{a}
   =\frac{28-12}{0.800}
   =20.0\ \mathrm{s}.
   $$

8. Acceleration:

   $$
   a=\frac{v-u}{t}
   =\frac{12-30}{9.0}
   =-2.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   Displacement:

   $$
   s=\frac{(u+v)}{2}t
   =\frac{30+12}{2}(9.0)
   =189\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   The negative acceleration shows that the car is slowing while still moving
   in the positive direction.

9. At the stopping point, $v=0$:

   $$
   0=5.0+(-0.80)t.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   t=\frac{5.0}{0.80}=6.25\ \mathrm{s}.
   $$

   Use

   $$
   v^2=u^2+2as.
   $$

   Then

   $$
   0^2=5.0^2+2(-0.80)s.
   $$

   So

   $$
   s=\frac{25}{1.60}=15.6\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

10. From the definition of acceleration for constant acceleration,

    $$
    a=\frac{v-u}{t}.
    $$

    Rearranging gives

    $$
    v=u+at.
    $$

    On a velocity-time graph with constant acceleration, the displacement is
    the area under the straight line. This area is a rectangle plus a triangle:

    $$
    s=ut+\frac{1}{2}(v-u)t.
    $$

    Since

    $$
    v-u=at,
    $$

    substitute into the area expression:

    $$
    s=ut+\frac{1}{2}(at)t
    =ut+\frac{1}{2}at^2.
    $$

## C. Free Fall and Determining $g$

11. For a dropped object, take downward as positive:

    $$
    u=0,\qquad a=9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}},\qquad s=65\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Use

    $$
    s=\frac{1}{2}gt^2.
    $$

    So

    $$
    t=\sqrt{\frac{2s}{g}}
    =\sqrt{\frac{2(65)}{9.81}}
    =3.64\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

    Impact speed:

    $$
    v=gt=(9.81)(3.64)=35.7\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

12. Take upward as positive. Then

    $$
    u=18\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}},
    \qquad
    a=-9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    At the maximum height, $v=0$:

    $$
    0^2=18^2+2(-9.81)s.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    s=\frac{18^2}{2(9.81)}
    =16.5\ \mathrm{m}
    $$

    above the balcony.

    To find the time to hit the ground, use $s=-12\ \mathrm{m}$:

    $$
    -12=18t-\frac{1}{2}(9.81)t^2.
    $$

    Rearranging:

    $$
    4.905t^2-18t-12=0.
    $$

    The positive root is

    $$
    t=4.25\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

    The impact velocity is

    $$
    v=u+at=18-9.81(4.25)=-23.7\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    So the ball hits the ground at

    $$
    23.7\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}
    $$

    downwards.

13. Use

    $$
    g=\frac{2h}{t^2}.
    $$

    The central value is

    $$
    g=\frac{2(0.800)}{0.405^2}
    =9.75\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    The percentage uncertainty in $h$ is

    $$
    \frac{0.005}{0.800}\times 100\%=0.625\%.
    $$

    The percentage uncertainty in $t$ is

    $$
    \frac{0.005}{0.405}\times 100\%=1.23\%.
    $$

    Since $t$ is squared, the time contribution is

    $$
    2(1.23\%)=2.47\%.
    $$

    Total percentage uncertainty:

    $$
    0.625\%+2.47\%=3.10\%.
    $$

    Absolute uncertainty:

    $$
    0.0310(9.75)=0.30\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    So

    $$
    g=(9.8\pm0.3)\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

14. A suitable experiment uses a steel ball held by an electromagnet above a
    trapdoor connected to an electronic timer. Measure the distance $h$ from
    the bottom of the ball to the trapdoor. When the current to the electromagnet
    is switched off, the timer starts. When the ball reaches the trapdoor, the
    timer stops, giving the fall time $t$.

    Repeat for several values of $h$, and repeat timings at each height to find
    a mean time. Calculate $t^2$ for each height and plot $h$ on the vertical
    axis against $t^2$ on the horizontal axis.

    Since

    $$
    h=\frac{1}{2}gt^2,
    $$

    the gradient is

    $$
    \frac{g}{2}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    g=2\times \text{gradient}.
    $$

    Residual magnetism in the electromagnet could delay release, making the
    measured time too large. This would make the calculated or graphical value
    of $g$ too small.

## D. Projectile Motion

15. For vertical motion, take downward as positive:

    $$
    s_y=1.25\ \mathrm{m},
    \qquad
    u_y=0,
    \qquad
    a_y=9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    Use

    $$
    s_y=\frac{1}{2}gt^2.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    t=\sqrt{\frac{2(1.25)}{9.81}}
    =0.505\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

    Horizontal range:

    $$
    x=u_x t=(3.2)(0.505)
    =1.62\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

16. Resolve the initial velocity:

    $$
    u_x=20\cos40^\circ=15.3\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}},
    $$

    $$
    u_y=20\sin40^\circ=12.9\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    For landing at the same height,

    $$
    t=\frac{2u_y}{g}
    =\frac{2(12.9)}{9.81}
    =2.62\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

    Range:

    $$
    x=u_x t=(15.3)(2.62)
    =40.2\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Maximum height:

    $$
    h=\frac{u_y^2}{2g}
    =\frac{12.9^2}{2(9.81)}
    =8.42\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

17. Time of fall depends only on the vertical motion:

    $$
    t=\sqrt{\frac{2(30)}{9.81}}
    =2.47\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

    Horizontal speed:

    $$
    u_x=\frac{42}{2.47}
    =17.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Just before impact, the vertical component is

    $$
    v_y=gt=(9.81)(2.47)=24.3\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}
    $$

    downwards.

    The angle below the horizontal is

    $$
    \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{24.3}{17.0}\right)
    =55^\circ.
    $$

18. Resolve the initial velocity:

    $$
    u_x=15\cos30^\circ=13.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}},
    $$

    $$
    u_y=15\sin30^\circ=7.5\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    At the highest point,

    $$
    v_y=0.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    0=7.5-9.81t,
    $$

    so

    $$
    t=0.765\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

    The horizontal velocity is unchanged, so the velocity vector at the highest
    point is

    $$
    13.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}
    $$

    horizontally in the original direction.

## E. Mixed Review

19. Acceleration from $0$ to $6.0\ \mathrm{s}$:

    $$
    a=\frac{16-4.0}{6.0}
    =2.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    From $6.0$ to $10.0\ \mathrm{s}$:

    $$
    a=0.
    $$

    From $10.0$ to $14.0\ \mathrm{s}$:

    $$
    a=\frac{0-16}{4.0}
    =-4.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    Displacement is area under the graph.

    First section:

    $$
    \frac{1}{2}(4.0+16)(6.0)=60\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Second section:

    $$
    (16)(4.0)=64\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Third section:

    $$
    \frac{1}{2}(16+0)(4.0)=32\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Total displacement:

    $$
    60+64+32=156\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

20. Car A starts from rest, so

    $$
    s_A=\frac{1}{2}at^2.
    $$

    After $8.0\ \mathrm{s}$,

    $$
    s_A=\frac{1}{2}(2.0)(8.0)^2=64\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Car B moves at constant velocity:

    $$
    s_B=vt=(15)(8.0)=120\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    For car A to catch car B,

    $$
    \frac{1}{2}(2.0)t^2=15t.
    $$

    So

    $$
    t^2=15t.
    $$

    Ignoring $t=0$, the catch-up time is

    $$
    t=15\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

    The displacement then is

    $$
    s=(15)(15)=225\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

21. The equation

    $$
    s=ut+\frac{1}{2}at^2
    $$

    assumes constant acceleration. A curved velocity-time graph has changing
    gradient, so acceleration is not constant over the whole interval.

    The graph is still useful. The instantaneous acceleration at a chosen time
    is the gradient of the tangent to the velocity-time graph. The displacement
    over a time interval is the area under the velocity-time graph, estimated by
    counting squares or splitting the area into suitable shapes.

22. Both balls have the same vertical motion:

    $$
    s_y=18\ \mathrm{m},
    \qquad
    u_y=0,
    \qquad
    a_y=9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    The horizontal velocity of the projectile does not affect the time to fall
    when air resistance is ignored. For both balls,

    $$
    t=\sqrt{\frac{2(18)}{9.81}}
    =1.92\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

    They hit the ground at the same time. The projected ball lands farther away
    because it also has constant horizontal velocity.
