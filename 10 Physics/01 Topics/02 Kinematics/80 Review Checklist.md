# Kinematics Review Checklist

Use this after working through [[10 Physics/01 Topics/02 Kinematics/10 Lecture Notes|Lecture Notes]], [[10 Physics/01 Topics/02 Kinematics/20 Worked Examples|Worked Examples]], and [[10 Physics/01 Topics/02 Kinematics/30 Key Practice Problems|Key Practice Problems]].

## Core Ideas

- [ ] I can define distance, displacement, speed, velocity, and acceleration.
- [ ] I can distinguish average speed from instantaneous speed.
- [ ] I can distinguish speed from velocity using direction and sign.
- [ ] I can explain why acceleration is a vector quantity.
- [ ] I can state when the constant-acceleration equations are valid.
- [ ] I can explain free fall as motion with acceleration $g$ downwards when air resistance is negligible.
- [ ] I can describe projectile motion as horizontal constant velocity plus vertical constant acceleration.

## Graph Skills

- [ ] I can find velocity from the gradient of a displacement-time graph.
- [ ] I can find acceleration from the gradient of a velocity-time graph.
- [ ] I can find displacement from signed area under a velocity-time graph.
- [ ] I can distinguish displacement from distance when a velocity-time graph crosses the time axis.
- [ ] I can use a tangent to estimate instantaneous acceleration on a curved velocity-time graph.
- [ ] I can explain why a curved velocity-time graph means non-uniform acceleration.

## Equation Skills

- [ ] I can choose a sign convention before substituting values.
- [ ] I can choose the correct equation from $s$, $u$, $v$, $a$, and $t$.
- [ ] I can derive $v=u+at$ from the definition of acceleration.
- [ ] I can derive $s=ut+\frac{1}{2}at^2$ from the area under a velocity-time graph.
- [ ] I can solve braking problems without losing the negative sign of acceleration.
- [ ] I can solve free-fall problems using $a=+g$ or $a=-g$ consistently.
- [ ] I can resolve a projectile's initial velocity into horizontal and vertical components.
- [ ] I can use the same time in the horizontal and vertical parts of a projectile problem.

## Experimental Skills

- [ ] I can describe a falling-object experiment to determine $g$.
- [ ] I can explain why plotting $h$ against $t^2$ gives a gradient equal to $g/2$.
- [ ] I can identify timing, height, air resistance, and release-delay uncertainties in a $g$ experiment.
- [ ] I can explain how residual magnetism can make the measured value of $g$ too small.

## Common Errors to Avoid

- [ ] Using distance when the equation requires displacement.
- [ ] Giving average speed when the question asks for average velocity.
- [ ] Reading area under a displacement-time graph.
- [ ] Treating a negative acceleration as always meaning "slowing down".
- [ ] Using $g=9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}$ with the wrong sign.
- [ ] Applying constant-acceleration equations to a whole interval when acceleration changes.
- [ ] Forgetting that horizontal acceleration is zero for projectile motion without air resistance.
- [ ] Giving a projectile range before checking whether it lands at the launch height.

## Ready to Connect

You are ready to connect this topic to [[10 Physics/01 Topics/03 Dynamics/00 Overview|Dynamics]] when you can:

- describe a motion fully before asking what force caused it;
- connect acceleration direction with the later idea of resultant force direction;
- interpret velocity-time graph gradients as physical accelerations;
- keep signs and directions explicit in one-dimensional motion.

You are ready to connect it to [[20 Mathematics/02 Mechanics/02 Kinematics and Newtonian Motion/00 Overview|Kinematics and Newtonian Motion]] when you can:

- move fluently between graph, equation, and vector-component descriptions;
- solve multi-step constant-acceleration problems without changing sign convention;
- treat projectile motion as two linked one-dimensional motions sharing the same time.
