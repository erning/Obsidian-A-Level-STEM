# Gravitational Fields Key Practice Solutions

Use these solutions to check the concept selected before checking the
arithmetic. A force question, a field-strength question, and a potential
question can use similar symbols but different meanings.

## A. Field Strength, Field Lines, and Newton's Law

1. A gravitational field is a region where a mass experiences a gravitational
   force. Gravitational field strength is the force per unit mass on a small
   test mass:

   $$
   g=\frac{F}{m}.
   $$

   Its unit is $\mathrm{N\ kg^{-1}}$, equivalent to
   $\mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}$.

2. Use $F=mg$:

   $$
   F=(2.0)(3.7)=7.4\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   The force is in the direction of the gravitational field.

3. Use Newton's law of gravitation:

   $$
   F=\frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2}.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   F=
   \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(6.0 \times 10^{24})(1000)}
   {(7.0 \times 10^6)^2}
   =
   8.2 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

4. For a point outside a uniform spherical mass, the mass may be treated as if
   it were concentrated at the centre of the sphere. The separation in
   Newton's law is therefore the centre-to-centre distance, or orbital radius,
   not the height above the surface.

5. Use

   $$
   g=\frac{GM_{\mathrm{E}}}{R_{\mathrm{E}}^2}.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   g=
   \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}
   {(6.37 \times 10^6)^2}
   =
   9.81\ \mathrm{N\ kg^{-1}}.
   $$

## B. Radial Fields, Potential, and Potential Energy

6. The gravitational force on a small test mass $m$ due to a point mass $M$ is

   $$
   F=\frac{GMm}{r^2}.
   $$

   Since field strength is force per unit mass,

   $$
   g=\frac{F}{m}
   =
   \frac{GM}{r^2}.
   $$

7. Field strength follows an inverse-square relationship:

   $$
   g \propto \frac{1}{r^2}.
   $$

   At $2R$,

   $$
   g=\frac{9.8}{2^2}=2.45\ \mathrm{N\ kg^{-1}}.
   $$

   At $3R$,

   $$
   g=\frac{9.8}{3^2}=1.09\ \mathrm{N\ kg^{-1}}.
   $$

8. One quarter of the surface value means

   $$
   \frac{GM}{r^2}
   =
   \frac{1}{4}\frac{GM}{R^2}.
   $$

   Therefore $r=2R$. Height above the surface is

   $$
   h=r-R=R.
   $$

9. Field strength is inverse-square:

   $$
   g_{2r}=\frac{g}{4}.
   $$

   Potential is inverse-distance and negative:

   $$
   \phi_{2r}=\frac{\phi}{2}.
   $$

   Since $\phi$ is negative, this is less negative and closer to zero.

10. Use

    $$
    \phi=-\frac{GM}{r}.
    $$

    At the Moon's surface,

    $$
    \phi
    =
    -\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(7.35 \times 10^{22})}
    {1.74 \times 10^6}
    =
    -2.82 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{J\ kg^{-1}}.
    $$

11. Use

    $$
    E_p=-\frac{GM_{\mathrm{E}}m}{r}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    E_p
    =
    -\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})(250)}
    {2.0 \times 10^7}
    =
    -5.0 \times 10^9\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

12. Use

    $$
    \Delta E_p
    =
    GM_{\mathrm{E}}m
    \left(
    \frac{1}{r_1}-\frac{1}{r_2}
    \right).
    $$

    So

    $$
    \Delta E_p
    =
    (6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})(1000)
    \left(
    \frac{1}{7.0 \times 10^6}
    -
    \frac{1}{1.40 \times 10^7}
    \right)
    =
    2.8 \times 10^{10}\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    The increase is positive because the satellite is moved farther from the
    Earth.

## C. Circular Orbits and Geostationary Satellites

13. For a circular orbit, gravity supplies the centripetal force:

    $$
    \frac{GMm}{r^2}=\frac{mv^2}{r}.
    $$

    Cancel $m$ and multiply by $r$:

    $$
    \frac{GM}{r}=v^2.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    v=\sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}.
    $$

14. Orbital speed:

    $$
    v=
    \sqrt{
    \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}
    {7.0 \times 10^6}
    }
    =
    7.5 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Period:

    $$
    T=\frac{2\pi r}{v}
    =
    \frac{2\pi(7.0 \times 10^6)}
    {7.54 \times 10^3}
    =
    5.8 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

15. For a circular orbit,

    $$
    T^2=\frac{4\pi^2r^3}{GM_{\mathrm{E}}}.
    $$

    With $T=24.0 \times 3600=86400\ \mathrm{s}$,

    $$
    r=
    \left(
    \frac{GM_{\mathrm{E}}T^2}{4\pi^2}
    \right)^{1/3}
    =
    4.22 \times 10^7\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Height above the surface:

    $$
    h=r-R_{\mathrm{E}}
    =
    4.22 \times 10^7 - 6.37 \times 10^6
    =
    3.58 \times 10^7\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

16. A geostationary satellite must have period $24\ \mathrm{h}$, orbit from
    west to east, orbit directly above the Equator, and have an orbital plane
    through the Earth's centre. It then stays above the same point on the
    Earth's surface.

17. Astronauts in low Earth orbit are still in the Earth's gravitational field.
    Gravity supplies the centripetal acceleration for the orbit. They appear
    weightless because they and their spacecraft are in continuous free fall
    together, so there is no normal contact force supporting them.

## D. Interpretation and Approximation

18. Gravitational field strength outside a point mass has magnitude

    $$
    g=\frac{GM}{r^2}.
    $$

    Its graph is positive if magnitude is plotted, decreases rapidly with
    distance, and approaches zero as $r$ becomes very large.

    Gravitational potential is

    $$
    \phi=-\frac{GM}{r}.
    $$

    It is negative, becomes less negative as $r$ increases, and approaches
    zero from below at very large distance.

19. Near the Earth's surface, small height changes are tiny compared with the
    Earth's radius, so $g$ changes very little and can be treated as constant.
    Then $\Delta E_p=mg\Delta h$ is a good approximation. Over very large
    height changes, the field is radial and $g$ changes significantly, so the
    point-mass potential-energy expression must be used.

20. At the surface,

    $$
    E_{p1}=-\frac{GM_{\mathrm{E}}m}{R_{\mathrm{E}}}.
    $$

    At $2R_{\mathrm{E}}$,

    $$
    E_{p2}=-\frac{GM_{\mathrm{E}}m}{2R_{\mathrm{E}}}.
    $$

    The increase is

    $$
    \Delta E_p
    =
    E_{p2}-E_{p1}
    =
    \frac{GM_{\mathrm{E}}m}{2R_{\mathrm{E}}}.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    \Delta E_p
    =
    \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})(500)}
    {2(6.37 \times 10^6)}
    =
    1.56 \times 10^{10}\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$
