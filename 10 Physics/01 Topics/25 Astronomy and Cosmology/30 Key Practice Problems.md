# Astronomy and Cosmology Key Practice Problems

Work these without looking at the solutions first. Label each quantity as an
intrinsic property of the source or an observed quantity at Earth before using
an equation.

Use these constants unless stated otherwise:

$$
c=3.00 \times 10^8\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}},\quad
b=2.9 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m\ K},\quad
\sigma=5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \mathrm{W\ m^{-2}\ K^{-4}}.
$$

## A. Luminosity, Flux, and Standard Candles

1. Define luminosity and radiant flux intensity, and state their units.

2. A star has luminosity $2.0 \times 10^{28}\ \mathrm{W}$. The radiant flux
   intensity measured at Earth is
   $5.0 \times 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{W\ m^{-2}}$. Find its distance.

3. A star is moved to twice its original distance from Earth, with luminosity
   unchanged. State the factor by which the radiant flux intensity changes.

4. Define standard candle and explain how a standard candle can be used to
   estimate the distance to a galaxy.

5. A standard candle has luminosity $1.0 \times 10^{32}\ \mathrm{W}$ and
   observed radiant flux intensity
   $8.0 \times 10^{-14}\ \mathrm{W\ m^{-2}}$. Find its distance.

## B. Stellar Radii

6. State Wien's displacement law and explain what it says about hotter stars.

7. A star has peak wavelength $500\ \mathrm{nm}$. Estimate its surface
   temperature.

8. A second star has peak wavelength $350\ \mathrm{nm}$. Estimate its surface
   temperature and compare it with the star in question 7.

9. A star has radius $7.0 \times 10^8\ \mathrm{m}$ and surface temperature
   $5800\ \mathrm{K}$. Estimate its luminosity.

10. A star has luminosity $1.0 \times 10^{28}\ \mathrm{W}$ and surface
    temperature $1.0 \times 10^4\ \mathrm{K}$. Estimate its radius.

11. Explain how a cool star can have greater luminosity than a hotter star.

12. If a star's radius is unchanged and its surface temperature is doubled,
    by what factor does its luminosity change?

## C. Redshift, Hubble's Law, and the Big Bang

13. Explain what is meant by redshift of spectral lines from a distant galaxy.

14. A spectral line has laboratory wavelength $500\ \mathrm{nm}$ and observed
    wavelength $505\ \mathrm{nm}$. Estimate the recession speed of the galaxy.

15. A spectral line has laboratory frequency
    $6.00 \times 10^{14}\ \mathrm{Hz}$ and is observed from a distant galaxy at
    $5.94 \times 10^{14}\ \mathrm{Hz}$. Estimate the recession speed.

16. Use $H_0=2.3 \times 10^{-18}\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$ to find the recession speed
    of a galaxy at distance $1.0 \times 10^{25}\ \mathrm{m}$.

17. A galaxy has recession speed $6.9 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$. Use
    $H_0=2.3 \times 10^{-18}\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$ to estimate its distance.

18. Use $H_0=2.3 \times 10^{-18}\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$ to estimate the Hubble time
    in seconds and years. Take
    $1\ \mathrm{year}=3.16 \times 10^7\ \mathrm{s}$.

19. Explain why redshift leads to the idea that the Universe is expanding.

20. Explain why Hubble's law does not mean that Earth is at the centre of the
    Universe.

21. Explain how Hubble's law leads to the Big Bang theory.

22. A student says that redshift means every spectral line from a galaxy is
    literally red. Correct this statement.

23. Explain how standard candles and Hubble's law can work together in
    estimating large astronomical distances.

24. State why SI units must be used consistently in Hubble's law calculations
    in this syllabus.
