# Astronomy and Cosmology Key Practice Solutions

Use these solutions to check the setup, powers of ten, units, and physical
interpretation for [[10 Physics/01 Topics/25 Astronomy and Cosmology/30 Key Practice Problems|Key Practice Problems]].

## A. Luminosity, Flux, and Standard Candles

1. Luminosity is the total power of radiation emitted by a star. Its unit is
   the watt. Radiant flux intensity is the radiant power received per unit area
   at the observation point. Its unit is $\mathrm{W\ m^{-2}}$.

2. Use

   $$
   F=\frac{L}{4\pi d^2}.
   $$

   Rearranging,

   $$
   d=\sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi F}}.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   d=
   \sqrt{
   \frac{2.0 \times 10^{28}}
   {4\pi(5.0 \times 10^{-12})}
   }
   =1.8 \times 10^{19}\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

3. Radiant flux intensity follows $F\propto 1/d^2$. Doubling the distance
   reduces $F$ by a factor of $4$.

4. A standard candle is an astronomical object of known luminosity. Once its
   luminosity is known, the observed radiant flux intensity can be used in
   $F=L/(4\pi d^2)$ to calculate its distance. If it is in a distant galaxy,
   this estimates the distance to that galaxy.

5. Use

   $$
   d=\sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi F}}.
   $$

   Then

   $$
   d=
   \sqrt{
   \frac{1.0 \times 10^{32}}
   {4\pi(8.0 \times 10^{-14})}
   }
   =1.0 \times 10^{22}\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

## B. Stellar Radii

6. Wien's displacement law is

   $$
   \lambda_{\max}T=b.
   $$

   A hotter star has a smaller peak wavelength, so its black-body spectrum
   peaks at a shorter wavelength.

7. Convert $500\ \mathrm{nm}=5.00 \times 10^{-7}\ \mathrm{m}$. Then

   $$
   T=\frac{b}{\lambda_{\max}}
   =
   \frac{2.9 \times 10^{-3}}{5.00 \times 10^{-7}}
   =5.8 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{K}.
   $$

8. Convert $350\ \mathrm{nm}=3.50 \times 10^{-7}\ \mathrm{m}$. Then

   $$
   T=\frac{2.9 \times 10^{-3}}{3.50 \times 10^{-7}}
   =8.3 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{K}.
   $$

   This star is hotter because its peak wavelength is shorter.

9. Use

   $$
   L=4\pi\sigma r^2T^4.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   L=
   4\pi(5.67 \times 10^{-8})(7.0 \times 10^8)^2(5800)^4
   =4.0 \times 10^{26}\ \mathrm{W}.
   $$

10. Rearranging the Stefan-Boltzmann law,

    $$
    r=\sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi\sigma T^4}}.
    $$

    Substitute:

    $$
    r=
    \sqrt{
    \frac{1.0 \times 10^{28}}
    {4\pi(5.67 \times 10^{-8})(1.0 \times 10^4)^4}
    }
    =1.2 \times 10^9\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

11. Luminosity depends on both temperature and surface area. A cool star can be
    very large, so its large surface area can make its total power output
    greater than that of a smaller, hotter star.

12. Since $L\propto T^4$ when radius is unchanged, doubling $T$ increases
    luminosity by

    $$
    2^4=16.
    $$

## C. Redshift, Hubble's Law, and the Big Bang

13. Redshift means that spectral lines from a distant galaxy are observed at
    longer wavelengths than their known laboratory values.

14. The fractional wavelength change is

    $$
    \frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda}
    =
    \frac{505-500}{500}
    =0.010.
    $$

    Using

    $$
    \frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda}\approx\frac{v}{c},
    $$

    gives

    $$
    v=(0.010)(3.00 \times 10^8)
    =3.0 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

15. The frequency has decreased. Using the magnitude of the fractional change,

    $$
    \left|\frac{\Delta f}{f}\right|
    =
    \frac{6.00 \times 10^{14}-5.94 \times 10^{14}}
    {6.00 \times 10^{14}}
    =0.010.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    v=0.010c=3.0 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

16. Hubble's law gives

    $$
    v=H_0d
    =(2.3 \times 10^{-18})(1.0 \times 10^{25})
    =2.3 \times 10^7\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

17. Rearranging Hubble's law,

    $$
    d=\frac{v}{H_0}
    =
    \frac{6.9 \times 10^6}{2.3 \times 10^{-18}}
    =3.0 \times 10^{24}\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

18. The Hubble time is approximately

    $$
    t\sim\frac{1}{H_0}
    =
    \frac{1}{2.3 \times 10^{-18}}
    =4.35 \times 10^{17}\ \mathrm{s}.
    $$

    In years,

    $$
    t=
    \frac{4.35 \times 10^{17}}{3.16 \times 10^7}
    =1.38 \times 10^{10}\ \mathrm{years}.
    $$

19. Redshift shows that light from distant galaxies has been stretched to
    longer wavelengths. This is interpreted as galaxies receding as space
    expands.

20. Hubble's law is interpreted as expansion of space. Observers in other
    galaxies would also see distant galaxies receding, so Earth is not a
    special centre.

21. If the Universe is expanding now, then in the past galaxies were closer
    together. Tracing the expansion backwards leads to the idea that the
    Universe evolved from a much hotter, denser early state: the Big Bang
    model.

22. Redshift means wavelengths have increased. It does not mean that every
    spectral line becomes visibly red; lines can shift while remaining outside
    or inside different parts of the spectrum.

23. Standard candles give distances to galaxies from known luminosity and
    measured flux. Those distances can be combined with redshift measurements
    to test or use Hubble's law on large scales.

24. In this syllabus, Hubble's law is used with SI units. If $H_0$ is in
    $\mathrm{s^{-1}}$, distance must be in metres and speed in
    $\mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$.
