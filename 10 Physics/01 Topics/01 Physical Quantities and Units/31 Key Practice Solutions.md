# Physical Quantities and Units Key Practice Solutions

These solutions correspond to [[10 Physics/01 Topics/01 Physical Quantities and Units/30 Key Practice Problems|Key Practice Problems]].

## A. Quantities, SI Units, and Prefixes

1. A physical quantity needs a numerical magnitude and a unit. The number
   $18$ alone is incomplete because it does not say what has been measured.

   Two possible complete physical quantities are:

   $$
   18\ \mathrm{m}
   $$

   for a length, and

   $$
   18\ \mathrm{s}
   $$

   for a time.

   These have the same numerical magnitude but different physical meanings.

2. One day contains

   $$
   24\times 60=1440\ \mathrm{min}.
   $$

   At $70$ beats per minute, the number of beats is

   $$
   70\times 1440=100800.
   $$

   To one significant figure, this is

   $$
   1\times 10^5\ \text{heartbeats}.
   $$

   The estimate is reasonable because a day has roughly $10^3$ minutes and the
   pulse rate is roughly $10^2\ \text{min}^{-1}$.

3. Convert each prefix carefully.

   a. Since $\mathrm{k}=10^3$,

      $$
      4.8\ \mathrm{km}=4.8\times 10^3\ \mathrm{m}.
      $$

   b. Since $\mu=10^{-6}$,

      $$
      75\ \mu\mathrm{s}=75\times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{s}
      =7.5\times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{s}.
      $$

   c. Since $\mathrm{G}=10^9$,

      $$
      0.032\ \mathrm{G}\Omega
      =0.032\times 10^9\ \Omega
      =3.2\times 10^7\ \Omega.
      $$

   d. Since $1\ \mathrm{cm^3}=10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m^3}$,

      $$
      6.0\ \mathrm{cm^3}=6.0\times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m^3}.
      $$

4. Use defining equations.

   a. From $F=ma$,

      $$
      \mathrm{N}=\mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-2}}.
      $$

   b. From $p=F/A$,

      $$
      \mathrm{Pa}=\mathrm{N\ m^{-2}}
      =\mathrm{kg\ m^{-1}\ s^{-2}}.
      $$

   c. From $Q=It$,

      $$
      \mathrm{C}=\mathrm{A\ s}.
      $$

   d. From $V=E/Q$, with
      $\mathrm{J}=\mathrm{kg\ m^2\ s^{-2}}$ and
      $\mathrm{C}=\mathrm{A\ s}$,

      $$
      \mathrm{V}
      =\mathrm{kg\ m^2\ A^{-1}\ s^{-3}}.
      $$

5. Convert both the mass and the volume:

   $$
   1\ \mathrm{g}=10^{-3}\ \mathrm{kg},
   $$

   and

   $$
   1\ \mathrm{cm^3}=10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m^3}.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   1\ \mathrm{g\ cm^{-3}}
   =10^3\ \mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}.
   $$

   So

   $$
   0.92\ \mathrm{g\ cm^{-3}}
   =920\ \mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}.
   $$

6. For part a:

   $$
   v^2 \quad \text{has unit} \quad
   (\mathrm{m\ s^{-1}})^2
   =\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   Also

   $$
   u^2
   =\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-2}},
   $$

   and

   $$
   as=(\mathrm{m\ s^{-2}})(\mathrm{m})
   =\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   Therefore $v^2=u^2+2as$ is homogeneous.

   For part b, $u$ has unit $\mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$, but

   $$
   as=\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   These units are not the same, so $v=u+as$ is not homogeneous.

## B. Errors, Accuracy, Precision, and Uncertainty

7. The mean is

   $$
   \bar{L}
   =\frac{81.2+81.4+81.3+81.1}{4}
   =81.25\ \mathrm{cm}.
   $$

   The range is

   $$
   81.4-81.1=0.3\ \mathrm{cm}.
   $$

   Half the range is

   $$
   0.15\ \mathrm{cm}.
   $$

   A sensible result is

   $$
   L=(81.3\pm0.2)\ \mathrm{cm}.
   $$

   The uncertainty is rounded to a sensible precision, and the value is rounded
   to the same decimal place.

8. The resistance is

   $$
   R=\frac{V}{I}
   =\frac{4.82}{0.620}
   =7.77\ \Omega.
   $$

   The percentage uncertainty in $V$ is

   $$
   \frac{0.01}{4.82}\times 100\%=0.21\%.
   $$

   The percentage uncertainty in $I$ is

   $$
   \frac{0.005}{0.620}\times 100\%=0.81\%.
   $$

   For division, add percentage uncertainties:

   $$
   0.21\%+0.81\%=1.02\%.
   $$

   The absolute uncertainty in $R$ is

   $$
   0.0102(7.77)=0.079\ \Omega.
   $$

   A suitable final answer is

   $$
   R=(7.8\pm0.1)\ \Omega.
   $$

9. The micrometer has a positive zero error, so it reads too high by
   $0.03\ \mathrm{mm}$. Subtract the zero error:

   $$
   d=0.56\ \mathrm{mm}-0.03\ \mathrm{mm}
   =0.53\ \mathrm{mm}.
   $$

   The zero error is a systematic error. Repeating the measurement does not
   remove it; the instrument reading must be corrected or the instrument must
   be re-zeroed.

10. The perimeter is

    $$
    P=2L+2W
    =2(8.0)+2(5.0)
    =26.0\ \mathrm{cm}.
    $$

    For addition and subtraction, add absolute uncertainties. Multiplying a
    measurement by $2$ doubles its uncertainty, so

    $$
    \Delta P=2(0.1)+2(0.1)=0.4\ \mathrm{cm}.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    P=(26.0\pm0.4)\ \mathrm{cm}.
    $$

    The area is

    $$
    A=LW=(8.0)(5.0)=40.0\ \mathrm{cm^2}.
    $$

    The percentage uncertainty in $L$ is

    $$
    \frac{0.1}{8.0}\times 100\%=1.25\%.
    $$

    The percentage uncertainty in $W$ is

    $$
    \frac{0.1}{5.0}\times 100\%=2.0\%.
    $$

    For multiplication, add percentage uncertainties:

    $$
    1.25\%+2.0\%=3.25\%.
    $$

    The absolute uncertainty in area is

    $$
    0.0325(40.0)=1.3\ \mathrm{cm^2}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    A=(40.0\pm1.3)\ \mathrm{cm^2}.
    $$

11. Closely grouped readings show good precision because repeated readings are
    similar. The balance reading $0.40\ \mathrm{g}$ when empty is a zero error,
    which is systematic. It makes the readings inaccurate unless the zero error
    is corrected.

    The student can have high precision but poor accuracy: the repeated values
    may agree with each other but still be shifted away from the true value.

## C. Scalars and Vectors

12. Scalars have magnitude only. Vectors have magnitude and direction.

    | Scalar | Vector |
    |---|---|
    | distance | displacement |
    | speed | velocity |
    | mass | force |
    | energy | acceleration |
    | temperature | electric field strength |

13. Distance is the total path length:

    $$
    120\ \mathrm{m}+50\ \mathrm{m}=170\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Displacement includes direction. Taking north as positive,

    $$
    120\ \mathrm{m}-50\ \mathrm{m}=70\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    The resultant displacement is

    $$
    70\ \mathrm{m}\ \text{north}.
    $$

14. The east and north displacements are perpendicular, so

    $$
    R=\sqrt{60^2+80^2}=100\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    The angle north of east is

    $$
    \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{80}{60}\right)
    =53^\circ.
    $$

    The displacement is

    $$
    100\ \mathrm{m}\ \text{at}\ 53^\circ\ \text{north of east}.
    $$

15. Resolve the force into components:

    $$
    F_x=35\cos40^\circ=26.8\ \mathrm{N},
    $$

    $$
    F_y=35\sin40^\circ=22.5\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    So the components are approximately

    $$
    27\ \mathrm{N}\ \text{horizontally}
    $$

    and

    $$
    23\ \mathrm{N}\ \text{vertically upward}.
    $$

16. Take east as positive $x$ and north as positive $y$.

    The first force has components

    $$
    (12,\ 0)\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    The second force has components

    $$
    (18\cos120^\circ,\ 18\sin120^\circ)
    =(-9.0,\ 15.6)\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    The resultant components are

    $$
    R_x=12-9.0=3.0\ \mathrm{N},
    $$

    $$
    R_y=15.6\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    The magnitude is

    $$
    R=\sqrt{3.0^2+15.6^2}
    =15.9\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    The direction is

    $$
    \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{15.6}{3.0}\right)
    =79^\circ
    $$

    north of east.

17. The northward and eastward velocities are perpendicular:

    $$
    v=\sqrt{3.0^2+1.2^2}
    =3.2\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    The angle east of north is

    $$
    \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1.2}{3.0}\right)
    =22^\circ.
    $$

    The boat's velocity relative to the bank is

    $$
    3.2\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}\ \text{at}\ 22^\circ\ \text{east of north}.
    $$

18. Write the vectors as components:

    $$
    \mathbf{A}=(9.0,\ 0)\ \mathrm{N},
    $$

    $$
    \mathbf{B}=(0,\ 5.0)\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    Then

    $$
    \mathbf{A}-\mathbf{B}
    =(9.0,\ -5.0)\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    The magnitude is

    $$
    \sqrt{9.0^2+5.0^2}=10.3\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    The direction below east is

    $$
    \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{5.0}{9.0}\right)
    =29^\circ.
    $$

    So

    $$
    \mathbf{A}-\mathbf{B}
    =10.3\ \mathrm{N}\ \text{at}\ 29^\circ\ \text{south of east}.
    $$

## D. Mixed Review

19. The displacement along the track is

    $$
    \Delta x=48.9\ \mathrm{cm}-12.4\ \mathrm{cm}
    =36.5\ \mathrm{cm}
    =0.365\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    For subtraction, add absolute uncertainties:

    $$
    \Delta(\Delta x)=0.1\ \mathrm{cm}+0.1\ \mathrm{cm}
    =0.2\ \mathrm{cm}
    =0.002\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    The speed is

    $$
    v=\frac{0.365}{0.120}
    =3.04\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    The percentage uncertainty in $\Delta x$ is

    $$
    \frac{0.002}{0.365}\times 100\%=0.55\%.
    $$

    The percentage uncertainty in $t$ is

    $$
    \frac{0.002}{0.120}\times 100\%=1.67\%.
    $$

    For division, add percentage uncertainties:

    $$
    0.55\%+1.67\%=2.22\%.
    $$

    The absolute uncertainty is

    $$
    0.0222(3.04)=0.067\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    A suitable answer is

    $$
    v=(3.04\pm0.07)\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

20. The central density is

    $$
    \rho=\frac{m}{a^3}
    =\frac{125}{2.50^3}
    =8.00\ \mathrm{g\ cm^{-3}}.
    $$

    Since

    $$
    1\ \mathrm{g\ cm^{-3}}=10^3\ \mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}},
    $$

    the density is

    $$
    8.00\times 10^3\ \mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}.
    $$

    The percentage uncertainty in mass is

    $$
    \frac{1}{125}\times 100\%=0.80\%.
    $$

    The percentage uncertainty in side length is

    $$
    \frac{0.02}{2.50}\times 100\%=0.80\%.
    $$

    Since volume depends on $a^3$, the percentage uncertainty from the side
    length is

    $$
    3(0.80\%)=2.40\%.
    $$

    Total percentage uncertainty:

    $$
    0.80\%+2.40\%=3.20\%.
    $$

    Absolute uncertainty:

    $$
    0.0320(8.00\times 10^3)
    =2.56\times 10^2\ \mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}.
    $$

    A sensible final answer is

    $$
    \rho=(8.0\pm0.3)\times 10^3\ \mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}.
    $$

21. The unit of $k$ is

    $$
    \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}
    =(\mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-2}})\mathrm{m^{-1}}
    =\mathrm{kg\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    \frac{m}{k}
    \quad \text{has unit} \quad
    \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{kg\ s^{-2}}}
    =\mathrm{s^2}.
    $$

    So

    $$
    \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}
    $$

    has unit $\mathrm{s}$. The factor $2\pi$ is dimensionless, so the
    right-hand side has the same unit as period $T$. The equation is
    homogeneous.

22. Rearrange

    $$
    F=Kv^2
    $$

    to get

    $$
    K=\frac{F}{v^2}.
    $$

    The unit of $F$ is

    $$
    \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    The unit of $v^2$ is

    $$
    (\mathrm{m\ s^{-1}})^2
    =\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    K
    =\frac{\mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-2}}}{\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-2}}}
    =\mathrm{kg\ m^{-1}}.
    $$
