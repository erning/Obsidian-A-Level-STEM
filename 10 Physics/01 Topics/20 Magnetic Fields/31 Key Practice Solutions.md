# Magnetic Fields Key Practice Solutions

Use these solutions to check the setup, direction reasoning, units, and final
answers for [Key Practice Problems](30%20Key%20Practice%20Problems.md).

## A. Field Patterns and Conductor Forces

1. A magnetic field is a field of force produced by moving charges or by
   permanent magnets. Field lines show the direction of the force on a north
   pole at each point. Closer spacing represents a stronger field.

2. Around a long straight wire, field lines are concentric circles centred on
   the wire. Around a flat circular coil, the field is similar to a short bar
   magnet, with field passing through the centre of the coil. Inside a long
   solenoid, the field is nearly uniform and parallel to the axis. Use the
   right-hand grip rule for current directions.

3. A ferrous core increases the magnetic field inside the solenoid. The core
   becomes magnetised and concentrates the magnetic field.

4. Use

   $$
   F=BIL\sin\theta.
   $$

   The wire is at right angles to the field, so

   $$
   F=(0.28)(4.0)(0.15)=0.168\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

5. With $\theta=35^\circ$,

   $$
   F=(0.28)(4.0)(0.15)\sin 35^\circ
   =9.6 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

6. At right angles,

   $$
   B=\frac{F}{IL}
   =\frac{0.048}{(2.0)(0.12)}
   =0.20\ \mathrm{T}.
   $$

7. If the wire is parallel to the field, $\theta=0^\circ$, so
   $\sin\theta=0$ and $F=BIL\sin\theta=0$. The current has no component
   perpendicular to the magnetic field.

## B. Moving Charges, Hall Voltage, and Selection

8. For a positive ion moving to the right in a field into the page, Fleming's
   left-hand rule gives a force upwards. A negative ion would experience a
   force downwards.

9. Use

   $$
   F=BQv\sin\theta.
   $$

   For perpendicular motion,

   $$
   F=(0.18)(1.60 \times 10^{-19})(2.5 \times 10^6)
   =7.2 \times 10^{-14}\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

10. With $\theta=40^\circ$,

    $$
    F=(0.18)(1.60 \times 10^{-19})(2.5 \times 10^6)\sin 40^\circ
    =4.6 \times 10^{-14}\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

11. The magnetic force is perpendicular to the velocity. It therefore changes
    the direction of the velocity but does no work on the particle, so the
    speed and kinetic energy remain constant.

12. Use

    $$
    V_H=\frac{BI}{ntq}.
    $$

    Convert $30\ \mathrm{mA}=3.0 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{A}$ and
    $0.40\ \mathrm{mm}=4.0 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{m}$:

    $$
    V_H=
    \frac{(0.40)(3.0 \times 10^{-2})}
    {(6.0 \times 10^{22})(4.0 \times 10^{-4})(1.60 \times 10^{-19})}
    =3.1 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{V}.
    $$

    So the Hall voltage is about $3.1\ \mathrm{mV}$.

13. The reading is maximum when the magnetic field is perpendicular to the
    active face of the Hall probe. It falls when the probe is rotated away
    from this orientation because the perpendicular component of the field is
    smaller.

14. The magnetic force provides centripetal force:

    $$
    BQv=\frac{mv^2}{r}.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    r=\frac{mv}{BQ}
    =
    \frac{(1.67 \times 10^{-27})(2.4 \times 10^6)}
    {(0.16)(1.60 \times 10^{-19})}
    =0.157\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

15. Since $r=\frac{mv}{BQ}$, the proton has a much larger radius because its
    mass is much larger than the electron's mass. Their paths curve in
    opposite directions because their charges have opposite signs.

16. For undeflected particles in crossed fields,

    $$
    QE=BQv.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    v=\frac{E}{B}
    =\frac{2.7 \times 10^4}{0.090}
    =3.0 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

## C. Currents, Flux, and Induction

17. Each wire produces a magnetic field. The other wire carries a current in
    that field and therefore experiences a force. Applying the right-hand grip
    rule and Fleming's left-hand rule shows that currents in the same direction
    attract, while currents in opposite directions repel. The two forces are
    equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

18. The field is perpendicular to the coil, so

    $$
    \Phi=BA=(0.45)(3.0 \times 10^{-3})
    =1.35 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{Wb}.
    $$

19. The angle is between the field and the normal to the area, so

    $$
    \Phi=BA\cos\theta.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    \Phi=(0.45)(3.0 \times 10^{-3})\cos 60^\circ
    =6.75 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{Wb}.
    $$

20. The change in flux density is

    $$
    \Delta B=0.10-0.50=-0.40\ \mathrm{T}.
    $$

    The magnitude of the change in flux linkage is

    $$
    |\Delta(N\Phi)|
    =N A |\Delta B|
    =(240)(2.5 \times 10^{-3})(0.40)
    =0.24\ \mathrm{Wb\,turn}.
    $$

    Faraday's law gives

    $$
    |\mathcal{E}|=\frac{0.24}{0.80}=0.30\ \mathrm{V}.
    $$

    Since the flux is decreasing, Lenz's law says the induced current is in
    the direction that tries to maintain the original flux.

21. The induced e.m.f. is

    $$
    \mathcal{E}=BLv=(0.50)(0.20)(4.0)=0.40\ \mathrm{V}.
    $$

    The current is

    $$
    I=\frac{\mathcal{E}}{R}
    =\frac{0.40}{5.0}
    =0.080\ \mathrm{A}.
    $$

22. The induced e.m.f. can be increased by increasing the number of turns,
    increasing the area of the coil, using a stronger magnetic field, changing
    the flux more rapidly, or rotating/moving the coil faster.

23. As the north pole approaches, the magnetic flux linkage through the coil
    increases. By Lenz's law, the induced current acts to oppose this increase.
    The near end of the coil therefore behaves like a north pole, repelling
    the approaching north pole.

24. Lenz's law says that the induced e.m.f. is in a direction such that its
    effects oppose the change producing it. It does not say that the induced
    current always opposes the magnetic field itself. For example, if the
    external flux is decreasing, the induced current acts to maintain it.
