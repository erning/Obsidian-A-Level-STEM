# Superposition Key Practice Solutions

These solutions correspond to [[10 Physics/01 Topics/08 Superposition/30 Key Practice Problems|Key Practice Problems]].

## A. Superposition and Interference Conditions

1. Add the displacements algebraically:

   $$
   s=+3.0-1.2+0.5
   =+2.3\ \mathrm{mm}.
   $$

2. If the waves are in phase, the resultant amplitude is

   $$
   4.0+4.0=8.0\ \mathrm{mm}.
   $$

   Since intensity is proportional to amplitude squared,

   $$
   \frac{I_{\text{resultant}}}{I_{\text{one wave}}}
   =\left(\frac{8.0}{4.0}\right)^2
   =4.
   $$

   If the waves are exactly in antiphase and have equal amplitude, the
   resultant amplitude is $0$.

3. Express the path difference in wavelengths:

   $$
   \frac{2.4}{0.80}=3.
   $$

   The path difference is $3\lambda$, a whole number of wavelengths, so the
   interference is constructive.

   Phase difference:

   $$
   \phi=360^\circ(3)
   =1080^\circ.
   $$

   This is equivalent to $0^\circ$ modulo a complete cycle.

4. Express the path difference in wavelengths:

   $$
   \frac{0.90}{0.60}=1.5.
   $$

   The path difference is $1.5\lambda$, an odd number of half wavelengths, so
   the interference is destructive.

5. Coherent sources emit waves with a constant phase difference. For a stable
   two-source interference pattern, the sources should be coherent and have the
   same frequency. The waves must also overlap, and similar amplitudes help
   produce clear dark regions.

## B. Diffraction and Double-Slit Interference

6. Diffraction is the spreading of waves as they pass through a gap or around
   an edge. If the gap is much larger than the wavelength, the waves spread only
   slightly. If the gap width is about equal to the wavelength, the spreading is
   large and the wavefronts after the gap are strongly curved.

7. Use

   $$
   x=\frac{\lambda D}{a}.
   $$

   Convert:

   $$
   \lambda=630\times 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{m},
   \qquad
   a=0.40\times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   Then

   $$
   x=\frac{(630\times 10^{-9})(2.0)}
   {0.40\times 10^{-3}}
   =3.15\times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   The fringe spacing is $3.15\ \mathrm{mm}$.

8. From the first bright fringe to the eleventh bright fringe there are $10$
   spacings:

   $$
   x=\frac{24.0\ \mathrm{mm}}{10}
   =2.40\times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   Use

   $$
   \lambda=\frac{ax}{D}.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   \lambda=
   \frac{(0.25\times 10^{-3})(2.40\times 10^{-3})}{1.2}
   =5.0\times 10^{-7}\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   This is $500\ \mathrm{nm}$.

9. From

   $$
   x=\frac{\lambda D}{a},
   $$

   the fringe spacing:

   a. increases when wavelength is increased;
   b. increases when screen distance is increased;
   c. decreases when slit separation is increased.

10. Express the path difference in wavelengths:

    $$
    \frac{4.2}{2.8}=1.5.
    $$

    A path difference of $1.5\lambda$ is destructive, so the detector is at a
    minimum.

## C. Diffraction Gratings

11. Convert the grating line density:

    $$
    600\ \mathrm{mm^{-1}}=6.00\times 10^5\ \mathrm{m^{-1}}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    d=\frac{1}{6.00\times 10^5}
    =1.67\times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    For first order,

    $$
    d\sin\theta=\lambda.
    $$

    So

    $$
    \sin\theta
    =\frac{500\times 10^{-9}}{1.67\times 10^{-6}}
    =0.300.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    \theta=17.5^\circ.
    $$

12. The largest possible order satisfies

    $$
    n\lambda\le d.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    n\le \frac{d}{\lambda}
    =\frac{1.67\times 10^{-6}}{500\times 10^{-9}}
    =3.33.
    $$

    The largest possible order is $3$.

13. Use

    $$
    d\sin\theta=n\lambda.
    $$

    So

    $$
    \lambda=\frac{d\sin\theta}{n}
    =\frac{(2.00\times 10^{-6})\sin30.0^\circ}{2}
    =5.00\times 10^{-7}\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    This is $500\ \mathrm{nm}$.

14. In the zero order, $\theta=0$ for all wavelengths, so all visible
    wavelengths travel in the same direction and the maximum is white. For a
    non-zero order,

    $$
    d\sin\theta=n\lambda.
    $$

    A longer wavelength gives a larger value of $\sin\theta$, so red light is
    diffracted through a larger angle than violet light in the same order.

15. Line density is the reciprocal of spacing:

    $$
    N=\frac{1}{d}
    =\frac{1}{1.25\times 10^{-6}}
    =8.00\times 10^5\ \mathrm{m^{-1}}.
    $$

    Since $1\ \mathrm{m}=1000\ \mathrm{mm}$,

    $$
    N=800\ \mathrm{mm^{-1}}.
    $$

## D. Stationary Waves

16. Adjacent nodes are separated by $\lambda/2$, so

    $$
    \lambda=2(12\ \mathrm{cm})
    =24\ \mathrm{cm}
    =0.24\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Frequency:

    $$
    f=\frac{v}{\lambda}
    =\frac{48}{0.24}
    =200\ \mathrm{Hz}.
    $$

17. A string fixed at both ends with four loops has

    $$
    L=4\frac{\lambda}{2}=2\lambda.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    \lambda=\frac{L}{2}
    =\frac{1.2}{2}
    =0.60\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Wave speed:

    $$
    v=f\lambda=(180)(0.60)
    =108\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

18. From the first minimum to the fifth minimum there are $4$ spacings:

    $$
    \text{node spacing}=\frac{6.0\ \mathrm{cm}}{4}
    =1.5\ \mathrm{cm}.
    $$

    Adjacent minima are separated by $\lambda/2$, so

    $$
    \lambda=2(1.5\ \mathrm{cm})
    =3.0\ \mathrm{cm}
    =3.0\times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Frequency:

    $$
    f=\frac{c}{\lambda}
    =\frac{3.0\times 10^8}{3.0\times 10^{-2}}
    =1.0\times 10^{10}\ \mathrm{Hz}.
    $$

19. For the simplest pattern in a tube open at both ends, both ends are
    displacement antinodes and

    $$
    L=\frac{\lambda}{2}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    \lambda=2L=2(0.85)=1.70\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Frequency:

    $$
    f=\frac{v}{\lambda}
    =\frac{340}{1.70}
    =200\ \mathrm{Hz}.
    $$

20. A stationary wave is formed when two progressive waves of the same
    frequency, wavelength, and amplitude travel in opposite directions and
    superpose. The pattern has nodes, where the amplitude is always zero, and
    antinodes, where the amplitude is maximum. Points between the same pair of
    adjacent nodes oscillate in phase. Points in adjacent loops oscillate in
    antiphase.
