# Electric Fields Key Practice Solutions

Use these solutions to check signs as well as magnitudes. Electric field
direction is the direction of the force on a positive test charge.

## A. Field Strength and Uniform Fields

1. An electric field is a region where a charge experiences an electric force.
   Electric field strength is force per unit positive charge:

   $$
   E=\frac{F}{q}.
   $$

   Its unit is $\mathrm{N\ C^{-1}}$, equivalent to
   $\mathrm{V\ m^{-1}}$.

2. Magnitude:

   $$
   F=qE=(2.0 \times 10^{-6})(3.0 \times 10^4)
   =
   6.0 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   The positive charge is forced to the right, in the field direction. The
   negative charge has the same force magnitude but is forced to the left,
   opposite to the field direction.

3. A uniform electric field is shown by straight, parallel, equally spaced
   field lines. Around an isolated positive point charge, field lines are
   radial and point outwards. Closer lines mean a stronger field.

4. For parallel plates,

   $$
   E=\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta d}
   =
   \frac{500}{2.0 \times 10^{-2}}
   =
   2.5 \times 10^4\ \mathrm{V\ m^{-1}}.
   $$

5. Force:

   $$
   F=qE=(3.20 \times 10^{-19})(1.0 \times 10^4)
   =
   3.20 \times 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   Acceleration:

   $$
   a=\frac{F}{m}
   =
   \frac{3.20 \times 10^{-15}}{6.64 \times 10^{-27}}
   =
   4.8 \times 10^{11}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   The alpha particle is positive, so it accelerates in the field direction.

6. The force on an electron is opposite to the electric field direction. The
   velocity component perpendicular to the force remains constant, while the
   component in the force direction changes uniformly. The path is parabolic,
   like projectile motion.

## B. Point Charges and Radial Fields

7. Use Coulomb's law:

   $$
   F=
   \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9)(4.0 \times 10^{-6})(1.5 \times 10^{-6})}
   {(0.30)^2}
   =
   0.60\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   The charges are both positive, so the force is repulsive.

8. Coulomb force follows an inverse-square law:

   $$
   F\propto \frac{1}{r^2}.
   $$

   Doubling $r$ makes the force one quarter as large.

9. For a point outside a charged spherical conductor, the charge may be treated
   as if it were concentrated at the centre of the sphere. The separation in
   Coulomb's law is therefore the distance from this centre to the point.

10. Magnitude:

    $$
    E=
    \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9)(5.0 \times 10^{-9})}
    {(0.25)^2}
    =
    7.2 \times 10^2\ \mathrm{N\ C^{-1}}.
    $$

    The source charge is negative, so the field is directed towards the
    charge.

11. At the midpoint, the fields from the two equal positive charges have equal
    magnitudes and opposite directions. They cancel, so the resultant electric
    field is zero.

## C. Electric Potential and Potential Energy

12. Electric potential at a point is the work done per unit positive charge in
    bringing a small test charge from infinity to that point.

13. Use

    $$
    V=\frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0r}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    V=
    \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9)(6.0 \times 10^{-9})}
    {0.40}
    =
    135\ \mathrm{V}.
    $$

14. Potential is scalar, so add algebraically:

    $$
    V=
    \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9)(6.0 \times 10^{-9})}{0.40}
    +
    \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9)(-2.0 \times 10^{-9})}{0.40}.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    V=135-45=90\ \mathrm{V}.
    $$

15. Use $E_p=qV$:

    $$
    E_p=(-3.0 \times 10^{-6})(135)
    =
    -4.05 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

16. Use

    $$
    E_p=\frac{Qq}{4\pi\varepsilon_0r}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    E_p=
    \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9)(2.0 \times 10^{-6})(-3.0 \times 10^{-6})}
    {0.20}
    =
    -0.270\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    The value is negative because the charges are unlike.

## D. Potential Gradient, Equipotentials, and Interpretation

17. Magnitude:

    $$
    E=\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta d}
    =
    \frac{120-20}{5.0 \times 10^{-2}}
    =
    2.0 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{V\ m^{-1}}.
    $$

    The field points in the direction of decreasing potential, from the
    $120\ \mathrm{V}$ side towards the $20\ \mathrm{V}$ side.

18. Along an equipotential, $\Delta V=0$. Work done moving a charge is

    $$
    W=q\Delta V.
    $$

    Therefore $W=0$.

19. Similarity: both are fields of force, and radial fields from point sources
    obey inverse-square laws. Difference: gravitational force is always
    attractive because mass is positive, while electric forces can attract or
    repel because charge can be positive or negative.

20. $E=\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta d}$ applies directly to a uniform field, such as
    between parallel plates, or locally as a gradient. A point-charge field is
    radial and changes with distance, so over a large distance the field is not
    uniform. Use

    $$
    E=\frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0r^2}
    $$

    for the field at a distance $r$, or use the negative potential gradient at
    a point.
