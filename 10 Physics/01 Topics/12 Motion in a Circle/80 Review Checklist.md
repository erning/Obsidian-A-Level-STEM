# Motion in a Circle Review Checklist

Use this checklist after the practice problems. Any unchecked item is a cue to
return to the lecture notes, draw the circle again, and identify the inward
resultant before calculating.

## Ideas to Recall

- [ ] One radian is defined using arc length equal to radius.
- [ ] Angular displacement must be in radians when using circular motion
      formulae.
- [ ] Angular speed is the rate of change of angular displacement.
- [ ] For uniform circular motion, $\omega=\frac{2\pi}{T}=2\pi f$.
- [ ] Linear speed and angular speed are related by $v=r\omega$.
- [ ] Uniform circular motion has constant speed but changing velocity.
- [ ] Centripetal acceleration is directed towards the centre.
- [ ] Centripetal acceleration can be written as $a=r\omega^2$ or
      $a=\frac{v^2}{r}$.
- [ ] The inward resultant force can be written as $F=mr\omega^2$ or
      $F=\frac{mv^2}{r}$.
- [ ] Centripetal force is not an extra force; it is the resultant of real
      forces towards the centre.
- [ ] A force perpendicular to velocity changes direction of motion but does
      no work in uniform circular motion.

## Skills to Perform

- [ ] Convert between degrees, revolutions, and radians.
- [ ] Convert revolutions per minute into frequency and angular speed.
- [ ] Use $s=r\theta$ for arc length and angular displacement.
- [ ] Use $v=r\omega$ to compare points at different radii on the same
      rotating object.
- [ ] Choose $a=r\omega^2$ or $a=\frac{v^2}{r}$ from the data given.
- [ ] Calculate inward resultant force using $F=ma$ after finding centripetal
      acceleration.
- [ ] Name the real force supplying the inward resultant: tension, friction,
      gravity, normal contact force, lift, electric force, or magnetic force.
- [ ] Write the correct force balance at the top and bottom of a vertical
      circle.
- [ ] Explain why constant speed does not imply equilibrium.

## Common Errors to Avoid

- [ ] Using degrees directly in formulae that require radians.
- [ ] Forgetting that $1\ \mathrm{rev}=2\pi\ \mathrm{rad}$.
- [ ] Mixing up period and frequency.
- [ ] Drawing velocity towards the centre instead of tangentially.
- [ ] Drawing acceleration along the tangent instead of towards the centre.
- [ ] Adding "centripetal force" as a separate force in the free-body diagram.
- [ ] Saying the inward force increases speed. It changes direction of
      velocity.
- [ ] Forgetting that the inward direction is different at the top and bottom
      of a vertical circle.
- [ ] Leaving radius in centimetres or kilometres when SI units are needed.

## Ready to Move On When

- [ ] I can define the radian and use $s=r\theta$ without converting from
      memory alone.
- [ ] I can move between $T$, $f$, $\omega$, and $v$ for a rotating object.
- [ ] I can calculate centripetal acceleration from either $r,\omega$ or
      $v,r$.
- [ ] I can calculate the inward resultant force and name the real physical
      force that supplies it.
- [ ] I can explain which quantities are constant and which change in uniform
      circular motion.
- [ ] I can connect this topic to [[10 Physics/01 Topics/13 Gravitational Fields/00 Overview|Gravitational Fields]],
      [[10 Physics/01 Topics/17 Oscillations/00 Overview|Oscillations]], and
