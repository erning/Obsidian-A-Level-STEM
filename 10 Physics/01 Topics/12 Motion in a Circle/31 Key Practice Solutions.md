# Motion in a Circle Key Practice Solutions

Use these solutions to mark the setup as well as the answer. The central test
is whether the acceleration and resultant force are drawn towards the centre.

## A. Radians, Period, Frequency, and Angular Speed

1. One radian is the angle subtended at the centre of a circle by an arc equal
   in length to the radius. For a complete revolution, the arc length is the
   circumference $2\pi r$, so

   $$
   \theta=\frac{s}{r}=\frac{2\pi r}{r}=2\pi\ \mathrm{rad}.
   $$

2. Use

   $$
   \theta=\frac{s}{r}.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   \theta=\frac{1.8}{0.60}=3.0\ \mathrm{rad}.
   $$

3. Convert using $\theta_{\mathrm{rad}}=\theta_{\mathrm{deg}}\pi/180$:

   $$
   45^\circ=\frac{\pi}{4},
   \qquad
   120^\circ=\frac{2\pi}{3},
   \qquad
   315^\circ=\frac{7\pi}{4}.
   $$

4. The period is $T=0.25\ \mathrm{s}$. Hence

   $$
   f=\frac{1}{T}=\frac{1}{0.25}=4.0\ \mathrm{Hz}.
   $$

   Angular speed is

   $$
   \omega=2\pi f=2\pi(4.0)=25.1\ \mathrm{rad\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

5. Convert revolutions per minute to revolutions per second:

   $$
   f=\frac{1800}{60}=30\ \mathrm{Hz}.
   $$

   Then

   $$
   \omega=2\pi f=2\pi(30)=1.88 \times 10^2\ \mathrm{rad\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

6. Use $v=r\omega$.

   At $r=0.050\ \mathrm{m}$,

   $$
   v=(0.050)(8.0)=0.40\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   At $r=0.20\ \mathrm{m}$,

   $$
   v=(0.20)(8.0)=1.6\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   The outer point has the larger linear speed because it travels a longer arc
   in the same time.

## B. Centripetal Acceleration and Force

7. The centripetal acceleration is

   $$
   a=\frac{v^2}{r}
   =\frac{18^2}{60}
   =5.4\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   The inward resultant force is

   $$
   F=ma=(1200)(5.4)=6.48 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

8. Linear speed:

   $$
   v=r\omega=(0.25)(12)=3.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   Centripetal acceleration:

   $$
   a=r\omega^2=(0.25)(12)^2=36\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   Inward force:

   $$
   F=ma=(0.50)(36)=18\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

9. Angular speed:

   $$
   \omega=\frac{2\pi}{T}
   =\frac{2\pi}{3.0}
   =2.09\ \mathrm{rad\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   Linear speed:

   $$
   v=r\omega=(15)(2.09)=31.4\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   Acceleration:

   $$
   a=r\omega^2=(15)(2.09)^2=65.8\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

10. Use $a=v^2/r$, so

    $$
    r=\frac{v^2}{a}
    =\frac{6.0^2}{24}
    =1.5\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

11. A force perpendicular to velocity has no component along the direction of
    motion, so it does no work and does not change the speed. It changes the
    direction of the velocity. If the force is always towards a fixed centre,
    the path is circular and the acceleration is centripetal.

## C. Real Forces Supplying the Inward Resultant

12. The horizontal string tension provides the inward force:

    $$
    T=\frac{mv^2}{r}
    =\frac{(0.30)(5.0)^2}{0.75}
    =10\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

13. At the maximum safe speed, friction is the limiting inward force:

    $$
    \frac{mv^2}{r}=0.50mg.
    $$

    The mass cancels, so

    $$
    v=\sqrt{0.50gr}
    =\sqrt{(0.50)(9.81)(45)}
    =14.9\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

14. Use the given relation:

    $$
    \tan\theta=\frac{v^2}{rg}.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    v=\sqrt{rg\tan\theta}
    =\sqrt{(0.80)(9.81)\tan 30^\circ}
    =2.13\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

15. The required inward resultant is

    $$
    \frac{mv^2}{r}
    =\frac{(0.25)(3.0)^2}{0.45}
    =5.0\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    At the top, inward is downward:

    $$
    T_{\text{top}}+mg=\frac{mv^2}{r}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    T_{\text{top}}
    =5.0-(0.25)(9.81)
    =2.5\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    At the bottom, inward is upward:

    $$
    T_{\text{bottom}}-mg=\frac{mv^2}{r}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    T_{\text{bottom}}
    =5.0+(0.25)(9.81)
    =7.5\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

## D. Interpretation and Connected Contexts

16. The speed is

    $$
    v=\frac{2\pi r}{T}
    =\frac{2\pi(7.0 \times 10^6)}{5.9 \times 10^3}
    =7.45 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    The centripetal acceleration is

    $$
    a=\frac{v^2}{r}
    =\frac{(7.45 \times 10^3)^2}{7.0 \times 10^6}
    =7.9\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    Gravity provides the inward resultant force.

17. Use

    $$
    F=\frac{mv^2}{r}.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    r=\frac{mv^2}{F}
    =\frac{(9.1 \times 10^{-31})(2.0 \times 10^6)^2}{1.6 \times 10^{-14}}
    =2.3 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

18. Both riders have the same angular speed. Use $v=r\omega$:

    $$
    v_A=(1.5)(1.2)=1.8\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}},
    $$

    $$
    v_B=(3.0)(1.2)=3.6\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Use $a=r\omega^2$:

    $$
    a_A=(1.5)(1.2)^2=2.16\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}},
    $$

    $$
    a_B=(3.0)(1.2)^2=4.32\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    Rider B has twice the radius, so both linear speed and centripetal
    acceleration are twice as large.

19. Constant speed does not mean zero acceleration. Velocity changes because
    its direction changes, so acceleration is directed towards the centre.
    The centripetal force is not outward. The inward resultant force provides
    the centripetal acceleration. In an inertial frame, there is no real
    outward force acting on the object.

20. The frequency is

    $$
    f=\frac{45}{60}=0.75\ \mathrm{Hz}.
    $$

    The angular speed is

    $$
    \omega=2\pi f=2\pi(0.75)=4.71\ \mathrm{rad\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    The radius is $3.0\ \mathrm{m}$, so

    $$
    v=r\omega=(3.0)(4.71)=14.1\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    The centripetal acceleration is

    $$
    a=r\omega^2=(3.0)(4.71)^2=66.6\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
    $$

    The inward resultant force on the rider is

    $$
    F=ma=(70)(66.6)=4.7 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$
