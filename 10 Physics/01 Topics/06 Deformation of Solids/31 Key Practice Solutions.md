# Deformation of Solids Key Practice Solutions

These solutions correspond to [Key Practice Problems](30%20Key%20Practice%20Problems.md).

## A. Hooke's Law and Force-Extension Graphs

1. Convert the extension:

   $$
   x=4.0\ \mathrm{cm}=0.040\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   Using $F=kx$,

   $$
   k=\frac{F}{x}
   =\frac{8.0}{0.040}
   =200\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
   $$

2. Rearrange $F=kx$:

   $$
   x=\frac{F}{k}
   =\frac{12}{320}
   =3.75\times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   The extension is $0.0375\ \mathrm{m}$, or $3.75\ \mathrm{cm}$.

3. Convert the extension:

   $$
   x=5.0\ \mathrm{mm}=5.0\times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   Spring constant:

   $$
   k=\frac{15}{5.0\times 10^{-3}}
   =3.0\times 10^3\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
   $$

   Work done is area under the graph. In the straight-line region:

   $$
   E_p=\frac{1}{2}Fx
   =\frac{1}{2}(15)(5.0\times 10^{-3})
   =3.75\times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

4. The limit of proportionality is the point beyond which extension is no
   longer directly proportional to load. The elastic limit is the greatest load
   or deformation for which the object returns to its original shape when the
   load is removed. They may be close, but they are not the same definition.

5. For spring A:

   $$
   k_A=\frac{6.0}{3.0\times 10^{-3}}
   =2.0\times 10^3\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
   $$

   For spring B:

   $$
   k_B=\frac{6.0}{5.0\times 10^{-3}}
   =1.2\times 10^3\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
   $$

   Spring A is stiffer because it has the larger spring constant.

## B. Stress, Strain, and Young Modulus

6. Cross-sectional area:

   $$
   A=\frac{\pi d^2}{4}
   =\frac{\pi(0.60\times 10^{-3})^2}{4}
   =2.83\times 10^{-7}\ \mathrm{m^2}.
   $$

   Stress:

   $$
   \sigma=\frac{F}{A}
   =\frac{45}{2.83\times 10^{-7}}
   =1.59\times 10^8\ \mathrm{Pa}.
   $$

7. Strain:

   $$
   \varepsilon=\frac{x}{L}
   =\frac{1.8\times 10^{-3}}{1.2}
   =1.5\times 10^{-3}.
   $$

   Strain has no unit.

8. Young modulus:

   $$
   E=\frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}
   =\frac{1.59\times 10^8}{1.5\times 10^{-3}}
   =1.06\times 10^{11}\ \mathrm{Pa}.
   $$

9. Use

   $$
   E=\frac{FL}{Ax}.
   $$

   Cross-sectional area:

   $$
   A=\frac{\pi(0.50\times 10^{-3})^2}{4}
   =1.96\times 10^{-7}\ \mathrm{m^2}.
   $$

   Rearranging:

   $$
   x=\frac{FL}{AE}.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   x=\frac{(30)(2.0)}
   {(1.96\times 10^{-7})(2.1\times 10^{11})}
   =1.46\times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

   The extension is $1.46\ \mathrm{mm}$.

10. Young modulus:

    $$
    E=\frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}
    =\frac{3.0\times 10^8}{2.0\times 10^{-3}}
    =1.5\times 10^{11}\ \mathrm{Pa}.
    $$

    In the linear region, $\sigma=E\varepsilon$, so at
    $\varepsilon=8.0\times 10^{-4}$,

    $$
    \sigma=(1.5\times 10^{11})(8.0\times 10^{-4})
    =1.2\times 10^8\ \mathrm{Pa}.
    $$

11. Convert the stress:

    $$
    6.0\ \mathrm{MPa}=6.0\times 10^6\ \mathrm{Pa}.
    $$

    For material P:

    $$
    \varepsilon_P=\frac{\sigma}{E_P}
    =\frac{6.0\times 10^6}{200\times 10^9}
    =3.0\times 10^{-5}.
    $$

    For material Q:

    $$
    \varepsilon_Q=\frac{6.0\times 10^6}{3.0\times 10^9}
    =2.0\times 10^{-3}.
    $$

    Material Q extends more for the same original length because it has the
    larger strain.

## C. Elastic Potential Energy

12. For a spring obeying Hooke's law,

    $$
    E_p=\frac{1}{2}kx^2.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    E_p=\frac{1}{2}(250)(0.080)^2
    =0.80\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

13. Estimate using the area of a triangle under a straight-line graph:

    $$
    E_p\approx \frac{1}{2}Fx
    =\frac{1}{2}(12)(0.18)
    =1.08\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    This is only an estimate because a rubber band may not have a straight
    force-extension graph, so the average force may not be exactly half the
    final force.

14. Work done is the area under the force-extension graph.

    First section, a triangle:

    $$
    W_1=\frac{1}{2}(5.0\times 10^{-3})(20)
    =5.0\times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Second section, a trapezium:

    $$
    W_2=\frac{1}{2}(20+35)(4.0\times 10^{-3})
    =1.10\times 10^{-1}\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Total:

    $$
    W=0.050+0.110
    =0.160\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

15. Use

    $$
    E_p=\frac{1}{2}kx^2.
    $$

    Rearranging:

    $$
    k=\frac{2E_p}{x^2}
    =\frac{2(0.36)}{(12\times 10^{-3})^2}
    =5.0\times 10^3\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
    $$

    Force at this compression:

    $$
    F=kx=(5.0\times 10^3)(12\times 10^{-3})
    =60\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

## D. Experiment and Interpretation

16. Use a long, thin metal wire clamped at one end and loaded at the other end.
    Measure the original length $L$ from the clamp to a marker. Measure the
    diameter $d$ at several positions using a micrometer and calculate
    $A=\pi d^2/4$. Add loads in steps; for each load calculate
    $F=mg$ and measure the extension $x$ using a travelling microscope,
    vernier scale, or other fine scale. Plot $F$ against $x$ for the initial
    straight-line region. The gradient is $F/x$, so

    $$
    E=\frac{L}{A}\times \text{gradient}.
    $$

    Remove the load gradually to check that there has been no permanent
    deformation.

17. A long wire gives a larger extension for the same strain, making the
    extension easier to measure. A thin wire gives a larger stress and larger
    extension for a given load, also improving measurement. Diameter is measured
    several times because the cross-sectional area depends on $d^2$, so a small
    error in diameter causes a significant error in stress and Young modulus.

18. If the unloading curve does not return to zero extension, the wire has a
    permanent extension and has undergone plastic deformation. The area between
    the loading and unloading curves represents energy transferred to internal
    energy, for example as the material warms or its internal structure changes.

19. Use

    $$
    E=\frac{FL}{Ax}.
    $$

    Rearranging:

    $$
    F=\frac{EAx}{L}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    F=\frac{(2.0\times 10^{11})(2.0\times 10^{-7})(1.5\times 10^{-3})}{1.5}
    =40\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    Elastic potential energy:

    $$
    E_p=\frac{1}{2}Fx
    =\frac{1}{2}(40)(1.5\times 10^{-3})
    =3.0\times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

20. Young modulus from the graph:

    $$
    E=\frac{1.8\times 10^8}{1.0\times 10^{-3}}
    =1.8\times 10^{11}\ \mathrm{Pa}.
    $$

    Force at stress $1.2\times 10^8\ \mathrm{Pa}$:

    $$
    F=\sigma A
    =(1.2\times 10^8)(3.0\times 10^{-7})
    =36\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    Strain:

    $$
    \varepsilon=\frac{\sigma}{E}
    =\frac{1.2\times 10^8}{1.8\times 10^{11}}
    =6.67\times 10^{-4}.
    $$

    Extension:

    $$
    x=\varepsilon L
    =(6.67\times 10^{-4})(2.0)
    =1.33\times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    Elastic potential energy:

    $$
    E_p=\frac{1}{2}Fx
    =\frac{1}{2}(36)(1.33\times 10^{-3})
    =2.4\times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$
