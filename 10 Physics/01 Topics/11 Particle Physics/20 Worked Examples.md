# Particle Physics Worked Examples

These examples model the standard CAIE route through the nuclear atom,
radioactive decay equations, particle classification, quark charge, and beta
decay at the quark level.

## Source Route

- Syllabus: CAIE Physics 9702 section 11, Particle physics.
- Main subtopics: atoms, nuclei and radiation; fundamental particles.
- Coursebook route: alpha-particle scattering, radioactive radiation,
  radioactive decay equations, antiparticles, neutrinos, leptons, hadrons,
  and quarks.

## Example 1: Interpreting Alpha-Particle Scattering

**Prompt.** In Rutherford's alpha-particle scattering experiment, most
$\alpha$-particles pass straight through gold foil, some are deflected through
small angles, and a very small fraction are deflected through angles greater
than $90^\circ$. What conclusions follow?

**Solution.**

Most $\alpha$-particles pass through because most of the atom is empty space.
If positive charge and mass filled the atom, most particles would be affected.

Small deflections show that some $\alpha$-particles pass near a region of
positive charge and are repelled. Large deflections show that this positive
charge is concentrated into a very small volume.

Back-scattering is especially important. A massive, positively charged
$\alpha$-particle can be turned through a large angle only if it passes very
close to a small, dense, positively charged nucleus.

The conclusions are:

- the atom contains a tiny nucleus
- the nucleus is positively charged
- most of the mass of the atom is concentrated in the nucleus
- most of the atom is empty space

**What this example trains.** Do not merely name Rutherford. Link each
observation to one conclusion about the nuclear atom.

## Example 2: Reading Nuclide Notation

**Prompt.** For a neutral atom of ${}^{238}_{92}\mathrm{U}$, find the number
of protons, neutrons, and electrons. State whether ${}^{235}_{92}\mathrm{U}$
is an isotope of it.

**Solution.**

In the notation ${}^{A}_{Z}X$, $A$ is the nucleon number and $Z$ is the proton
number.

For ${}^{238}_{92}\mathrm{U}$,

$$
Z=92,
\qquad
A=238.
$$

So the number of protons is

$$
92.
$$

The number of neutrons is

$$
N=A-Z=238-92=146.
$$

For a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons, so
there are $92$ electrons.

${}^{235}_{92}\mathrm{U}$ is an isotope of ${}^{238}_{92}\mathrm{U}$ because
both nuclei have the same proton number, $92$, but different nucleon numbers.
The ${}^{235}_{92}\mathrm{U}$ nucleus has

$$
235-92=143
$$

neutrons.

**What this example trains.** Proton number identifies the element; nucleon
number is not the same thing as neutron number.

## Example 3: Balancing Radioactive Decay Equations

**Prompt.** Complete these decay equations.

$$
{}^{226}_{88}\mathrm{Ra}
\to
{}^{A}_{Z}\mathrm{Rn}
+ {}^{4}_{2}\alpha
$$

$$
{}^{14}_{6}\mathrm{C}
\to
{}^{A}_{Z}\mathrm{N}
+ {}^{0}_{-1}\beta^-
+ \bar{\nu}_e
$$

$$
{}^{22}_{11}\mathrm{Na}
\to
{}^{A}_{Z}\mathrm{Ne}
+ {}^{0}_{+1}\beta^+
+ \nu_e
$$

**Solution.**

In each nuclear equation, nucleon number and charge are conserved.

For alpha decay,

$$
226=A+4
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
A=222,
$$

and

$$
88=Z+2
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
Z=86.
$$

So

$$
{}^{226}_{88}\mathrm{Ra}
\to
{}^{222}_{86}\mathrm{Rn}
+ {}^{4}_{2}\alpha.
$$

For beta minus decay,

$$
14=A+0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
A=14,
$$

and

$$
6=Z+(-1)
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
Z=7.
$$

So

$$
{}^{14}_{6}\mathrm{C}
\to
{}^{14}_{7}\mathrm{N}
+ {}^{0}_{-1}\beta^-
+ \bar{\nu}_e.
$$

For beta plus decay,

$$
22=A+0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
A=22,
$$

and

$$
11=Z+(+1)
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
Z=10.
$$

So

$$
{}^{22}_{11}\mathrm{Na}
\to
{}^{22}_{10}\mathrm{Ne}
+ {}^{0}_{+1}\beta^+
+ \nu_e.
$$

**What this example trains.** Treat the top numbers and bottom numbers as two
separate conservation checks.

## Example 4: Comparing Alpha, Beta, and Gamma Radiation

**Prompt.** Describe the composition, mass, and charge of $\alpha$,
$\beta^-$, $\beta^+$, and $\gamma$ radiation. Also state how $A$ and $Z$
change in each emission.

**Solution.**

| Radiation | Composition | Relative mass | Charge | Nuclear change |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| $\alpha$ | helium nucleus, $2p+2n$ | about $4$ | $+2e$ | $A$ decreases by $4$, $Z$ decreases by $2$ |
| $\beta^-$ | electron | about $\frac{1}{1840}$ | $-e$ | $A$ unchanged, $Z$ increases by $1$ |
| $\beta^+$ | positron | about $\frac{1}{1840}$ | $+e$ | $A$ unchanged, $Z$ decreases by $1$ |
| $\gamma$ | photon | $0$ | $0$ | $A$ and $Z$ unchanged |

The equation for gamma emission is written as

$$
{}^{A}_{Z}X^*
\to
{}^{A}_{Z}X
+ \gamma,
$$

where $X^*$ is an excited nucleus.

**What this example trains.** Composition and nuclear change must agree. For
example, $\beta^-$ emission raises $Z$ because a neutron changes into a
proton.

## Example 5: Neutrinos and Beta-Particle Energies

**Prompt.** Explain why $\alpha$-particles from one alpha decay have discrete
energies, while $\beta$-particles from one beta decay have a continuous range
of energies. State the neutrino or antineutrino emitted in each beta decay.

**Solution.**

In a particular alpha decay, the daughter nucleus and the $\alpha$-particle
are the only massive products. The available energy is shared in a fixed way,
so the emitted $\alpha$-particles have discrete energies.

In beta decay, an additional particle is emitted. In beta minus decay,

$$
n \to p+\beta^-+\bar{\nu}_e.
$$

An electron antineutrino, $\bar{\nu}_e$, is emitted.

In beta plus decay,

$$
p \to n+\beta^+ + \nu_e.
$$

An electron neutrino, $\nu_e$, is emitted.

The neutrino or antineutrino can carry away different amounts of energy. This
leaves the beta particle with a continuous range of possible kinetic energies.

**What this example trains.** The neutrino is not a decoration in the equation;
it explains the observed beta energy spectrum.

## Example 6: Classifying Particles

**Prompt.** Classify an electron, electron neutrino, proton, neutron, quark,
and positive pion in this syllabus language.

**Solution.**

| Particle | Classification | Fundamental? | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| electron | lepton | yes | leptons are fundamental in this topic |
| electron neutrino | lepton | yes | neutrinos are leptons |
| proton | hadron, baryon | no | made from three quarks, $uud$ |
| neutron | hadron, baryon | no | made from three quarks, $udd$ |
| quark | quark | yes | quarks are fundamental particles |
| positive pion | hadron, meson | no | a meson is one quark and one antiquark |

Leptons are not affected by the strong nuclear force. Hadrons are affected by
the strong nuclear force and are made from quarks.

**What this example trains.** First decide lepton or hadron. Only then split
hadrons into baryons or mesons.

## Example 7: Using Quark Charges

**Prompt.** Use quark charges to check the charges of the proton, neutron,
and positive pion. Use

$$
u=+\frac{2}{3}e,
\qquad
d=-\frac{1}{3}e,
\qquad
\bar{d}=+\frac{1}{3}e.
$$

**Solution.**

A proton has quark composition $uud$:

$$
+\frac{2}{3}e+\frac{2}{3}e-\frac{1}{3}e=+e.
$$

A neutron has quark composition $udd$:

$$
+\frac{2}{3}e-\frac{1}{3}e-\frac{1}{3}e=0.
$$

A positive pion can be represented as $\pi^+=u\bar{d}$:

$$
+\frac{2}{3}e+\frac{1}{3}e=+e.
$$

The antiquark has the opposite charge to the corresponding quark, so
$\bar{d}$ has charge $+\frac{1}{3}e$.

**What this example trains.** A hadron's charge should be checked from its
quark or antiquark content.

## Example 8: Beta Decay at the Quark Level

**Prompt.** Describe beta minus decay and beta plus decay at the quark level,
and check charge in each quark change.

**Solution.**

In beta minus decay, a neutron changes into a proton:

$$
n \to p+\beta^-+\bar{\nu}_e.
$$

The quark composition changes from $udd$ to $uud$, so one down quark changes
into an up quark:

$$
d \to u+\beta^-+\bar{\nu}_e.
$$

Charge check:

$$
-\frac{1}{3}e
=
+\frac{2}{3}e-e+0.
$$

In beta plus decay, a proton changes into a neutron:

$$
p \to n+\beta^+ + \nu_e.
$$

The quark composition changes from $uud$ to $udd$, so one up quark changes
into a down quark:

$$
u \to d+\beta^+ + \nu_e.
$$

Charge check:

$$
+\frac{2}{3}e
=
-\frac{1}{3}e+e+0.
$$

**What this example trains.** The nuclear change and quark change are the same
story at different levels.
