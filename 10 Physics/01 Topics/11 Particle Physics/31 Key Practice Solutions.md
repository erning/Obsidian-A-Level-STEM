# Particle Physics Key Practice Solutions

Use these solutions to mark the reasoning, not just the final statement.
Particle physics errors usually come from mixing levels: atom, nucleus,
nucleon, quark, lepton, and photon.

## A. Nuclear Atom and Nuclide Notation

1. Most $\alpha$-particles pass straight through, so most of the atom is empty
   space. Small deflections show that some $\alpha$-particles pass near a
   positive charge and are repelled. Very large deflections show that the
   positive charge and most of the mass are concentrated in a tiny nucleus.

2. The atom has a small central nucleus containing protons and neutrons.
   Protons are positive, neutrons are neutral, and electrons occupy the space
   around the nucleus. The nucleus is positively charged and much smaller than
   the atom, so most of the atom is empty space.

3. For ${}^{27}_{13}\mathrm{Al}$,

   $$
   A=27,
   \qquad
   Z=13.
   $$

   There are $13$ protons. The number of neutrons is

   $$
   N=A-Z=27-13=14.
   $$

   A neutral atom has $13$ electrons.

4. ${}^{35}_{17}\mathrm{Cl}$ and ${}^{37}_{17}\mathrm{Cl}$ are isotopes. They
   have the same proton number, $17$, so they are the same element. They have
   different nucleon numbers, so they have different numbers of neutrons.
   ${}^{37}_{18}\mathrm{Ar}$ is not an isotope of chlorine because its proton
   number is different.

5. The mass estimate is

   $$
   12\ \mathrm{u}
   =
   12(1.66 \times 10^{-27})
   =
   1.99 \times 10^{-26}\ \mathrm{kg}.
   $$

   This is an estimate because the question asks us to use $12\ \mathrm{u}$
   for the atom.

## B. Radiation and Nuclear Decay

6. The comparison is:

   | Radiation | Composition | Relative mass | Charge |
   | --- | --- | ---: | ---: |
   | $\alpha$ | helium nucleus, $2p+2n$ | about $4$ | $+2e$ |
   | $\beta^-$ | electron | about $\frac{1}{1840}$ | $-e$ |
   | $\beta^+$ | positron | about $\frac{1}{1840}$ | $+e$ |
   | $\gamma$ | photon | $0$ | $0$ |

7. Conserve nucleon number and charge:

   $$
   241=A+4
   \quad\Rightarrow\quad
   A=237,
   $$

   $$
   95=Z+2
   \quad\Rightarrow\quad
   Z=93.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   {}^{241}_{95}\mathrm{Am}
   \to
   {}^{237}_{93}\mathrm{Np}
   + {}^{4}_{2}\alpha.
   $$

8. In beta minus decay, nucleon number is unchanged and proton number
   increases by $1$:

   $$
   A=32,
   \qquad
   Z=16.
   $$

   Beta minus decay emits an electron antineutrino:

   $$
   {}^{32}_{15}\mathrm{P}
   \to
   {}^{32}_{16}\mathrm{S}
   + {}^{0}_{-1}\beta^-
   + \bar{\nu}_e.
   $$

   Charge check:

   $$
   15=16-1.
   $$

9. In beta plus decay, nucleon number is unchanged and proton number decreases
   by $1$:

   $$
   A=11,
   \qquad
   Z=5.
   $$

   Beta plus decay emits an electron neutrino:

   $$
   {}^{11}_{6}\mathrm{C}
   \to
   {}^{11}_{5}\mathrm{B}
   + {}^{0}_{+1}\beta^+
   + \nu_e.
   $$

   Charge check:

   $$
   6=5+1.
   $$

10. Gamma emission is

    $$
    {}^{A}_{Z}X^*
    \to
    {}^{A}_{Z}X
    + \gamma.
    $$

    The nucleus loses energy, but $A$ and $Z$ do not change.

11. In a particular alpha decay, the daughter nucleus and the
    $\alpha$-particle are the only massive products, so the available energy
    is shared in a fixed way. The $\alpha$-particles therefore have discrete
    energies. In beta decay, the neutrino or antineutrino can carry away
    different amounts of energy, so the emitted beta particle has a continuous
    range of kinetic energies.

## C. Fundamental Particles and Quarks

12. The quark charges are:

    | Quark | Charge |
    | --- | ---: |
    | up, $u$ | $+\frac{2}{3}e$ |
    | down, $d$ | $-\frac{1}{3}e$ |
    | strange, $s$ | $-\frac{1}{3}e$ |
    | charm, $c$ | $+\frac{2}{3}e$ |
    | top, $t$ | $+\frac{2}{3}e$ |
    | bottom, $b$ | $-\frac{1}{3}e$ |

    The corresponding antiquark has the opposite charge.

13. Electron and electron neutrino are leptons. A proton and a neutron are
    hadrons and baryons. An up quark is a quark and is fundamental. An
    anti-down quark is an antiquark. A positive pion is a hadron and a meson
    because it contains one quark and one antiquark.

14. A proton is $uud$, so its charge is

    $$
    +\frac{2}{3}e+\frac{2}{3}e-\frac{1}{3}e
    =
    +e.
    $$

15. A neutron is $udd$, so its charge is

    $$
    +\frac{2}{3}e-\frac{1}{3}e-\frac{1}{3}e
    =
    0.
    $$

16. For $u\bar{d}$,

    $$
    +\frac{2}{3}e+\frac{1}{3}e
    =
    +e.
    $$

    It is a meson, not a baryon, because it contains one quark and one
    antiquark.

17. Protons and neutrons are made from quarks, so they are not fundamental
    particles. Electrons and neutrinos are leptons, and leptons are treated as
    fundamental in this topic. Quarks are also treated as fundamental
    particles.

## D. Beta Decay at the Quark Level

18. Beta minus decay changes a neutron into a proton:

    $$
    n \to p+\beta^-+\bar{\nu}_e.
    $$

    At the quark level,

    $$
    d \to u+\beta^-+\bar{\nu}_e.
    $$

    The nucleon changes from $udd$ to $uud$.

19. Beta plus decay changes a proton into a neutron:

    $$
    p \to n+\beta^+ + \nu_e.
    $$

    At the quark level,

    $$
    u \to d+\beta^+ + \nu_e.
    $$

    The nucleon changes from $uud$ to $udd$.

20. The complete quark-level equation is

    $$
    d \to u+\beta^-+\bar{\nu}_e.
    $$

    Charge on the left is

    $$
    -\frac{1}{3}e.
    $$

    Charge on the right is

    $$
    +\frac{2}{3}e-e+0
    =
    -\frac{1}{3}e.
    $$

    The emitted lepton is $\beta^-$, and the emitted antilepton is
    $\bar{\nu}_e$.
