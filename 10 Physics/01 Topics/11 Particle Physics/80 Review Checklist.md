# Particle Physics Review Checklist

Use this checklist after the practice problems. If an item feels vague, return
to the worked examples and rebuild the bookkeeping from first principles.

## Ideas to Recall

- [ ] Alpha-particle scattering supports a small, dense, positively charged
      nucleus and mostly empty atomic space.
- [ ] The nuclear atom contains protons and neutrons in the nucleus, with
      electrons outside the nucleus.
- [ ] Nucleon number $A$ is the total number of protons and neutrons; proton
      number $Z$ identifies the element.
- [ ] Isotopes have the same proton number but different numbers of neutrons.
- [ ] In nuclear processes, nucleon number and charge are conserved.
- [ ] $\alpha$, $\beta^-$, $\beta^+$, and $\gamma$ radiation have different
      compositions, masses, charges, and effects on $A$ and $Z$.
- [ ] An antiparticle has the same mass but opposite charge to its particle;
      the positron is the antiparticle of the electron.
- [ ] Beta minus decay emits an electron antineutrino, and beta plus decay
      emits an electron neutrino.
- [ ] Alpha particles from one alpha decay have discrete energies, while beta
      particles have a continuous range because a neutrino or antineutrino is
      emitted.
- [ ] Quarks are fundamental particles with six flavours: up, down, strange,
      charm, top, and bottom.
- [ ] Protons and neutrons are hadrons and baryons, not fundamental particles.
- [ ] Electrons and neutrinos are leptons and are fundamental in this topic.
- [ ] A baryon contains three quarks; a meson contains one quark and one
      antiquark.

## Skills to Perform

- [ ] Infer atomic structure from alpha-particle scattering observations.
- [ ] Read ${}^{A}_{Z}X$ notation and find proton, neutron, and electron
      numbers for a neutral atom.
- [ ] Identify isotopes from proton number and neutron number.
- [ ] Balance alpha and beta decay equations using top-number and
      bottom-number checks.
- [ ] Include $\bar{\nu}_e$ in beta minus decay and $\nu_e$ in beta plus decay.
- [ ] Use the unified atomic mass unit, $\mathrm{u}$, for simple mass
      estimates.
- [ ] Classify particles as leptons, hadrons, baryons, mesons, quarks, or
      antiquarks.
- [ ] Recall quark charges and assign the opposite charge to each antiquark.
- [ ] Check proton, neutron, and meson charge from quark content.
- [ ] Describe beta minus and beta plus decay at the quark level.

## Common Errors to Avoid

- [ ] Treating nucleon number $A$ as proton number $Z$.
- [ ] Saying isotopes have different numbers of protons. They do not.
- [ ] Forgetting that a neutral atom has the same number of electrons as
      protons.
- [ ] Treating $\gamma$ radiation as a charged particle rather than a photon.
- [ ] Omitting $\bar{\nu}_e$ from beta minus decay or $\nu_e$ from beta plus
      decay.
- [ ] Giving an antiquark the same charge as the corresponding quark.
- [ ] Calling protons or neutrons fundamental particles.
- [ ] Classifying a meson as three quarks instead of one quark and one
      antiquark.
- [ ] Describing beta decay only at the nuclear level when the question asks
      for the quark-level change.

## Ready to Move On When

- [ ] I can explain the scattering evidence for the nuclear atom without
      memorised phrases.
- [ ] I can balance an alpha, beta minus, beta plus, or gamma equation and
      justify the missing particle.
- [ ] I can move between nuclide notation, isotope language, and particle
      counts.
- [ ] I can classify simple particles and explain whether each is fundamental.
- [ ] I can use quark charges to check the charge of protons, neutrons, and
      simple mesons.
- [ ] I can explain beta minus and beta plus decay at both nuclear and quark
      levels.
- [ ] I can connect this topic to [Quantum Physics](../22%20Quantum%20Physics/00%20Overview.md)
      and [Nuclear Physics](../23%20Nuclear%20Physics/00%20Overview.md).
