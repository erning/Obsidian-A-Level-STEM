# Electricity Review Checklist

Use this after the worked examples and practice problems. Any unchecked item is
a cue to return to the lecture notes and redo one relevant calculation or graph
interpretation.

## Core Ideas to Recall

- [ ] Electric current is a flow of charge carriers.
- [ ] Current is rate of flow of charge: $I=Q/t$.
- [ ] Charge is quantised in integer multiples of $e$.
- [ ] For a current-carrying conductor, $I=Anvq$.
- [ ] Conventional current in a metal is opposite to electron drift.
- [ ] Potential difference is energy transferred per unit charge:
  $V=W/Q$.
- [ ] Electrical power can be found from $P=VI$, $P=I^2R$, or
  $P=V^2/R$ when the quantities match.
- [ ] Resistance is defined as $R=V/I$.
- [ ] Ohm's law says current is directly proportional to p.d. under constant
  physical conditions.
- [ ] For an $I$ against $V$ graph, the gradient is $1/R$ for an ohmic
  conductor.
- [ ] A filament lamp is non-ohmic because its temperature rises as current
  increases.
- [ ] A silicon diode conducts strongly only in forward bias above about
  $0.6\ \mathrm{V}$.
- [ ] Resistivity is defined by $R=\rho L/A$ and has unit
  $\Omega\ \mathrm{m}$.
- [ ] An LDR's resistance decreases as light intensity increases.
- [ ] An NTC thermistor's resistance decreases as temperature increases.

## Skills to Perform

- [ ] Convert minutes to seconds before using $Q=It$.
- [ ] Convert charge into number of elementary charges.
- [ ] Use $I=Anvq$ and explain why drift velocity can be small.
- [ ] Calculate energy transferred from $W=VQ$ or $W=Pt$.
- [ ] Choose the correct electrical power equation.
- [ ] Calculate resistance from a p.d. and current pair.
- [ ] Test whether readings are consistent with Ohm's law.
- [ ] Interpret metallic conductor, filament lamp, and diode $I$-$V$ graphs.
- [ ] Calculate resistivity, resistance, length, area, or diameter from
  $R=\rho L/A$.
- [ ] Explain resistance changes in metals, thermistors, and LDRs using charge
  carriers and collisions.

## Common Errors to Avoid

- [ ] Saying current is used up in a component.
- [ ] Treating potential difference as the same quantity as current.
- [ ] Forgetting that electrons in a metal drift opposite to conventional
  current.
- [ ] Leaving time in minutes when calculating charge or energy.
- [ ] Treating $V=IR$ itself as Ohm's law.
- [ ] Reading resistance directly from the gradient of an $I$ against $V$ graph.
- [ ] Assuming every component has constant resistance.
- [ ] Forgetting that a filament lamp's resistance increases as it heats.
- [ ] Using wire diameter instead of cross-sectional area in resistivity.
- [ ] Writing the unit of resistivity as $\Omega\ \mathrm{m^{-1}}$.

## Quick Self-Checks

- [ ] I can define current, p.d., resistance, and resistivity in words.
- [ ] I can state the meaning of each symbol in $I=Anvq$.
- [ ] I can explain why a lamp lights quickly even though electron drift is
  slow.
- [ ] I can distinguish a straight ohmic $I$-$V$ graph from a filament lamp and
  diode graph.
- [ ] I can predict how an LDR or NTC thermistor responds to changing light or
  temperature.
