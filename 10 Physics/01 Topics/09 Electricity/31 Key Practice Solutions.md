# Electricity Key Practice Solutions

These solutions correspond to [[10 Physics/01 Topics/09 Electricity/30 Key Practice Problems|Key Practice Problems]].

Use $e=1.60\times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C}$ where needed.

## A. Charge, Current, and Charge Carriers

1. Convert the time:

   $$
   t=5.0\ \mathrm{min}=300\ \mathrm{s}.
   $$

   Use

   $$
   Q=It.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   Q=(0.20)(300)=60\ \mathrm{C}.
   $$

2. Convert the time:

   $$
   t=2.0\ \mathrm{min}=120\ \mathrm{s}.
   $$

   Current:

   $$
   I=\frac{Q}{t}
   =\frac{180}{120}
   =1.5\ \mathrm{A}.
   $$

3. Current is charge per second. The number of electrons per second is

   $$
   N=\frac{0.50}{1.60\times 10^{-19}}
   =3.1\times 10^{18}\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}.
   $$

4. For $-6.40\times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C}$,

   $$
   \frac{-6.40\times 10^{-19}}{1.60\times 10^{-19}}
   =-4.
   $$

   This is possible: it is $-4e$.

   For $+7.00\times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C}$,

   $$
   \frac{7.00\times 10^{-19}}{1.60\times 10^{-19}}
   =4.375.
   $$

   This is not an integer, so it is not possible for an isolated ion.

5. Convert area:

   $$
   A=0.75\ \mathrm{mm^2}=0.75\times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m^2}.
   $$

   Use

   $$
   I=Anvq.
   $$

   So

   $$
   v=\frac{I}{Anq}
   =\frac{2.0}
   {(0.75\times 10^{-6})(8.0\times 10^{28})(1.60\times 10^{-19})}
   =2.1\times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

## B. Potential Difference, Power, and Resistance

6. Potential difference is energy per unit charge:

   $$
   V=\frac{W}{Q}.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   W=VQ=(9.0)(25)
   =225\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

7. Rearrange

   $$
   V=\frac{W}{Q}.
   $$

   Thus

   $$
   V=\frac{360}{120}
   =3.0\ \mathrm{V}.
   $$

8. Power:

   $$
   P=VI=(12)(0.80)=9.6\ \mathrm{W}.
   $$

   Time:

   $$
   t=5.0\ \mathrm{min}=300\ \mathrm{s}.
   $$

   Energy:

   $$
   W=Pt=(9.6)(300)
   =2.88\times 10^3\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

9. Potential difference:

   $$
   V=IR=(0.40)(15)=6.0\ \mathrm{V}.
   $$

   Power:

   $$
   P=I^2R=(0.40)^2(15)
   =2.4\ \mathrm{W}.
   $$

10. Current:

    $$
    I=\frac{V}{R}
    =\frac{12}{24}
    =0.50\ \mathrm{A}.
    $$

    Power:

    $$
    P=\frac{V^2}{R}
    =\frac{12^2}{24}
    =6.0\ \mathrm{W}.
    $$

## C. Ohm's Law and Component Characteristics

11. For an $I$ against $V$ graph,

    $$
    \text{gradient}=\frac{I}{V}=\frac{1}{R}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    R=\frac{1}{0.20}
    =5.0\ \Omega.
    $$

12. At $3.0\ \mathrm{V}$ and $0.10\ \mathrm{A}$,

    $$
    R=\frac{3.0}{0.10}=30\ \Omega.
    $$

    At $9.0\ \mathrm{V}$ and $0.30\ \mathrm{A}$,

    $$
    R=\frac{9.0}{0.30}=30\ \Omega.
    $$

    The resistance is the same for these readings, so they are consistent with
    Ohm's law, provided physical conditions such as temperature are constant.

13. At $2.0\ \mathrm{V}$,

    $$
    R=\frac{2.0}{0.20}=10\ \Omega.
    $$

    At $6.0\ \mathrm{V}$,

    $$
    R=\frac{6.0}{0.40}=15\ \Omega.
    $$

    The resistance increases as current and p.d. increase. This is typical of a
    filament lamp, because the filament heats up and electron collisions with
    vibrating ions become more frequent.

14. A silicon diode has a highly non-linear $I$-$V$ characteristic. In reverse
    bias, only a tiny current flows. In forward bias, the current is very small
    until the p.d. is about $0.6\ \mathrm{V}$; above this threshold, the current
    rises rapidly. The graph is not symmetrical about the origin.

15. For a metallic conductor, resistance increases as temperature increases. A
    filament lamp also increases in resistance as current increases because the
    filament gets hotter. An NTC thermistor decreases in resistance as
    temperature increases. An LDR decreases in resistance as light intensity
    increases.

## D. Resistivity

16. Use

    $$
    \rho=\frac{RA}{L}.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    \rho=\frac{(4.0)(1.5\times 10^{-7})}{2.0}
    =3.0\times 10^{-7}\ \Omega\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

17. Use

    $$
    R=\frac{\rho L}{A}.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    R=\frac{(1.7\times 10^{-8})(10)}{2.0\times 10^{-6}}
    =8.5\times 10^{-2}\ \Omega.
    $$

18. Resistance is proportional to $L/A$. Doubling the length multiplies
    resistance by $2$. Halving the diameter makes the cross-sectional area one
    quarter as large, which multiplies resistance by $4$. Overall,

    $$
    2\times 4=8.
    $$

    The resistance becomes $8$ times larger.

19. Rearrange

    $$
    R=\frac{\rho L}{A}
    $$

    to give

    $$
    A=\frac{\rho L}{R}.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    A=\frac{(1.69\times 10^{-8})(5.0)}{0.50}
    =1.69\times 10^{-7}\ \mathrm{m^2}.
    $$

    Use

    $$
    A=\frac{\pi d^2}{4}.
    $$

    Then

    $$
    d=\sqrt{\frac{4A}{\pi}}
    =\sqrt{\frac{4(1.69\times 10^{-7})}{\pi}}
    =4.64\times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    This is $0.464\ \mathrm{mm}$.

20. In a metal, increasing temperature makes lattice ions vibrate more strongly.
    Electrons collide with the ions more frequently, so resistance increases.
    In an NTC thermistor, which is a semiconductor, increasing temperature
    releases many more charge carriers. This increase in charge carriers is the
    dominant effect, so resistance decreases.
