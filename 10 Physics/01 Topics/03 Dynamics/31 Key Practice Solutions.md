# Dynamics Key Practice Solutions

These solutions correspond to [Key Practice Problems](30%20Key%20Practice%20Problems.md).

Take $g=9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}$ where needed.

## A. Forces and Newton's Laws

1. Take forwards as positive. The resultant force is

   $$
   F_{\text{resultant}}=2600-850=1750\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   Using $F=ma$,

   $$
   a=\frac{1750}{1250}=1.40\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   The acceleration is forwards.

2. The horizontal component of the pull is

   $$
   F_x=50\cos25^\circ=45.3\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   The horizontal resultant force is

   $$
   45.3-14=31.3\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   a=\frac{31.3}{8.0}=3.9\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

3. On Earth,

   $$
   W=mg=(65)(9.81)=638\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   On the Moon,

   $$
   W=(65)(1.6)=104\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   The astronaut's mass remains $65\ \mathrm{kg}$. The astronaut's inertia is
   unchanged because inertia depends on mass, not on weight.

4. The forces acting on the book are:

   - weight, acting downwards;
   - normal contact force from the table, acting upwards.

   These two forces both act on the book, so they are not a Newton's third-law
   pair. A third-law pair acts on two different objects. The third-law partner
   of the book's weight is the gravitational force exerted by the book on the
   Earth. The third-law partner of the table's contact force on the book is the
   contact force exerted by the book on the table.

5. Acceleration:

   $$
   a=\frac{F}{m}
   =\frac{6.0}{0.40}
   =15\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

   Change in velocity:

   $$
   \Delta v=a\Delta t
   =(15)(0.30)
   =4.5\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

## B. Resistive Forces and Terminal Velocity

6. At release, the ball's speed is zero, so drag is very small. Weight acts
   downwards, so the resultant force and acceleration are downwards, close to
   $g$ if upthrust is negligible.

   As the ball speeds up, drag increases upwards. Weight is nearly constant, so
   the resultant downward force decreases. Therefore the downward acceleration
   decreases, although the ball is still speeding up.

   At terminal velocity, drag balances weight. The resultant force is zero, so
   acceleration is zero. The ball continues moving at constant velocity.

7. Top speed occurs when the driving force equals the drag force:

   $$
   900=0.36v^2.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   v=\sqrt{\frac{900}{0.36}}
   =50\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   At $v=30\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$,

   $$
   D=0.36(30)^2=324\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   The resultant force is

   $$
   900-324=576\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   Thus

   $$
   a=\frac{576}{1000}
   =0.576\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}.
   $$

## C. Momentum, Impulse, and Newton's Second Law

8. Momentum is $p=mv$.

   a. The ball has momentum

      $$
      p=(0.20)(18)=3.6\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}
      $$

      east.

   b. The car has momentum

      $$
      p=(1500)(22)=3.3\times 10^4\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}
      $$

      west.

   c. The pellet has momentum

      $$
      p=(7.5\times 10^{-3})(240)
      =1.8\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
      $$

9. Take motion towards the wall as positive. Initial momentum:

   $$
   p_i=(0.060)(20)=1.20\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   After rebound, the velocity is negative:

   $$
   p_f=(0.060)(-15)=-0.90\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   Change in momentum:

   $$
   \Delta p=p_f-p_i=-0.90-1.20=-2.10\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

   Average force:

   $$
   F=\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}
   =\frac{-2.10}{0.010}
   =-210\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   The force is away from the wall.

10. Change in momentum:

    $$
    \Delta p=m(v-u)
    =(900)(24-5.0)
    =1.71\times 10^4\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Average resultant force:

    $$
    F=\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}
    =\frac{1.71\times 10^4}{12}
    =1.43\times 10^3\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

11. The change in momentum has magnitude

    $$
    \Delta p=(70)(15)=1050\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    If the stopping time is $0.020\ \mathrm{s}$,

    $$
    F=\frac{1050}{0.020}
    =5.25\times 10^4\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    If the stopping time is $0.15\ \mathrm{s}$,

    $$
    F=\frac{1050}{0.15}
    =7.0\times 10^3\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    The airbag reduces the average force because the same momentum change
    happens over a longer time.

12. Momentum of one pellet:

    $$
    p=(0.012)(600)=7.2\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    For $8$ pellets each second, the rate of change of momentum of the pellets
    is

    $$
    8(7.2)=57.6\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    The average recoil force on the machine is $57.6\ \mathrm{N}$ in the
    opposite direction to the pellets' motion.

## D. Collisions and Explosions

13. Conservation of momentum gives

    $$
    (0.80)(3.0)+(1.60)(0)=(0.80+1.60)v.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    v=1.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Initial kinetic energy:

    $$
    E_i=\frac{1}{2}(0.80)(3.0)^2=3.6\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Final kinetic energy:

    $$
    E_f=\frac{1}{2}(2.40)(1.0)^2=1.2\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Kinetic energy lost:

    $$
    3.6-1.2=2.4\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

14. Take the first trolley's initial direction as positive. Momentum before:

    $$
    (2.0)(4.0)+(3.0)(-1.0)=5.0\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Since the trolleys stick together,

    $$
    (2.0+3.0)v=5.0.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    v=1.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}
    $$

    in the positive direction.

    Initial kinetic energy:

    $$
    E_i=\frac{1}{2}(2.0)(4.0)^2+\frac{1}{2}(3.0)(1.0)^2
    =17.5\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Final kinetic energy:

    $$
    E_f=\frac{1}{2}(5.0)(1.0)^2=2.5\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Kinetic energy lost:

    $$
    17.5-2.5=15.0\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

15. Momentum conservation:

    $$
    (1.0)(3.0)=(1.0)v_1+(2.0)v_2.
    $$

    So

    $$
    3.0=v_1+2v_2.
    $$

    For a perfectly elastic one-dimensional collision,

    $$
    u_1-u_2=v_2-v_1.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    3.0=v_2-v_1,
    $$

    so

    $$
    v_2=v_1+3.0.
    $$

    Substitute into momentum conservation:

    $$
    3.0=v_1+2(v_1+3.0).
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    v_1=-1.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}},
    \qquad
    v_2=2.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

16. Let the mass of each ball be $m$ and the final speed of each ball be $v$.
    Momentum perpendicular to the original direction cancels because the balls
    leave symmetrically.

    In the original direction:

    $$
    m(0.60)=2mv\cos45^\circ.
    $$

    So

    $$
    v=\frac{0.60}{2\cos45^\circ}
    =0.424\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Initial kinetic energy:

    $$
    E_i=\frac{1}{2}m(0.60)^2=0.180m.
    $$

    Final kinetic energy:

    $$
    E_f=2\left(\frac{1}{2}mv^2\right)=mv^2.
    $$

    Since

    $$
    v^2=(0.424)^2=0.180,
    $$

    the final kinetic energy is $0.180m$, equal to the initial kinetic energy
    to the precision of the calculation. Kinetic energy is conserved.

17. Initial components:

    $$
    p_x=5.0,\qquad p_y=0.
    $$

    Known final momentum:

    $$
    p_{1x}=3.0\cos30^\circ=2.60,
    $$

    $$
    p_{1y}=3.0\sin30^\circ=1.50.
    $$

    Other object's components:

    $$
    p_{2x}=5.0-2.60=2.40,
    $$

    $$
    p_{2y}=0-1.50=-1.50.
    $$

    Magnitude:

    $$
    p_2=\sqrt{2.40^2+1.50^2}
    =2.83\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Direction:

    $$
    \theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1.50}{2.40}\right)
    =32^\circ.
    $$

    The momentum is

    $$
    2.83\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}
    $$

    at $32^\circ$ south of east.

18. Initial momentum is zero. Take east as positive:

    $$
    0=(0.12)(40)+(0.18)v.
    $$

    So

    $$
    v=-26.7\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    The second fragment moves at $26.7\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$ west.

    Momentum is conserved because the two momenta are equal and opposite.
    Kinetic energy can increase because internal energy stored in the object is
    transferred to kinetic energy during the explosion.

## E. Mixed Review

19. For the ball alone, momentum is not conserved because the wall exerts an
    external force on the ball. Taking motion towards the wall as positive,

    $$
    p_i=(0.50)(8.0)=4.0\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}},
    $$

    $$
    p_f=(0.50)(-6.0)=-3.0\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    The change in momentum of the ball is

    $$
    -3.0-4.0=-7.0\ \mathrm{kg\ m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    For the wider ball-wall-Earth system, momentum is conserved. The wall and
    Earth receive an equal and opposite impulse. Their speed change is normally
    too small to notice because their mass is very large.

20. Take right as positive. If skater A moves left at
    $1.4\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$, then

    $$
    v_A=-1.4\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Initial total momentum is zero, so final total momentum is zero:

    $$
    (50)(-1.4)+(70)v_B=0.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    70v_B=70,
    $$

    and

    $$
    v_B=1.0\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Skater B moves right. Momentum is conserved for the two-skater system
    because the push forces are internal and the external horizontal force is
    negligible on frictionless ice.
