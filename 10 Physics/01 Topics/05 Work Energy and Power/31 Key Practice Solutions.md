# Work, Energy and Power Key Practice Solutions

These solutions correspond to [Key Practice Problems](30%20Key%20Practice%20Problems.md).

Take $g=9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}$ where needed.

## A. Work Done

1. Work done by a constant force in the direction of displacement is

   $$
   W=Fs.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   W=(75)(3.2)=240\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

2. Only the horizontal component of the rope force does work:

   $$
   W=Fs\cos\theta.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   W=(150)(6.0)\cos25^\circ
   =816\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

3. Friction acts opposite the displacement, so the work done by friction is
   negative:

   $$
   W=-(40)(8.0)=-320\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

   The negative sign shows that friction transfers mechanical energy away from
   the block.

4. In circular motion, the instantaneous displacement is tangential to the
   circle, while tension is radial. These directions are at $90^\circ$, so

   $$
   W=Fs\cos90^\circ=0.
   $$

   The tension changes the direction of the velocity, but it does not change
   the stone's kinetic energy when the speed is constant.

5. Work done is the area under the force-displacement graph.

   Triangle from $0$ to $0.40\ \mathrm{m}$:

   $$
   W_1=\frac{1}{2}(0.40)(60)=12\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

   Rectangle from $0.40\ \mathrm{m}$ to $1.20\ \mathrm{m}$:

   $$
   W_2=(0.80)(60)=48\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

   Total:

   $$
   W=12+48=60\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

## B. Gravitational Potential Energy and Kinetic Energy

6. Increase in gravitational potential energy:

   $$
   \Delta E_p=mg\Delta h
   =(12)(9.81)(2.5)
   =294\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

7. Kinetic energy:

   $$
   E_k=\frac{1}{2}mv^2
   =\frac{1}{2}(0.25)(18)^2
   =40.5\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

8. The increase in kinetic energy is

   $$
   \Delta E_k
   =\frac{1}{2}m(v^2-u^2).
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   \Delta E_k
   =\frac{1}{2}(1000)(20^2-12^2)
   =1.28\times 10^5\ \mathrm{J}.
   $$

9. With negligible air resistance,

   $$
   mgh=\frac{1}{2}mv^2.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   v=\sqrt{2gh}
   =\sqrt{2(9.81)(15)}
   =17.2\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
   $$

10. At the maximum height, the speed is zero, so the initial kinetic energy has
    become gravitational potential energy:

    $$
    \frac{1}{2}mv^2=mgh.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    h=\frac{v^2}{2g}
    =\frac{14^2}{2(9.81)}
    =9.99\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    To an appropriate number of significant figures, the maximum height is
    about $10\ \mathrm{m}$.

## C. Conservation of Energy and Losses

11. Loss of gravitational potential energy:

    $$
    \Delta E_p=(0.60)(9.81)(1.8)
    =10.6\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Gain in kinetic energy:

    $$
    E_k=\frac{1}{2}(0.60)(5.0)^2
    =7.5\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Energy transferred to internal energy and sound:

    $$
    10.6-7.5=3.1\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Percentage efficiency:

    $$
    \frac{7.5}{10.6}\times 100\%
    =70.8\%.
    $$

12. Loss of gravitational potential energy becomes gain in kinetic energy:

    $$
    mgh=\frac{1}{2}mv^2.
    $$

    So

    $$
    v=\sqrt{2gh}
    =\sqrt{2(9.81)(0.45)}
    =2.97\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

13. The vertical drop is

    $$
    25-10=15\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

    With no energy losses,

    $$
    mg(15)=\frac{1}{2}mv^2.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    v=\sqrt{2(9.81)(15)}
    =17.2\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    The mass cancels because all gravitational potential energy lost becomes
    kinetic energy gained.

14. The initial kinetic energy is

    $$
    E_k=\frac{1}{2}(1200)(22)^2
    =2.904\times 10^5\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    The braking force does negative work. Using magnitudes,

    $$
    Fs=E_k.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    s=\frac{2.904\times 10^5}{4800}
    =60.5\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

15. Useful work done against resistance:

    $$
    W=Fs=(800)(500)
    =4.0\times 10^5\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Efficiency:

    $$
    \text{efficiency}
    =\frac{4.0\times 10^5}{8.0\times 10^5}
    =0.50=50\%.
    $$

## D. Power and Mixed Review

16. Useful work done against gravity:

    $$
    W=mg\Delta h
    =(500)(9.81)(18)
    =8.829\times 10^4\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Useful output power:

    $$
    P=\frac{W}{t}
    =\frac{8.829\times 10^4}{12}
    =7.36\times 10^3\ \mathrm{W}.
    $$

    This is $7.36\ \mathrm{kW}$.

17. For steady motion,

    $$
    P=Fv.
    $$

    Rearranging,

    $$
    F=\frac{P}{v}.
    $$

    Convert $20\ \mathrm{kW}$ to $2.0\times 10^4\ \mathrm{W}$:

    $$
    F=\frac{2.0\times 10^4}{16}
    =1250\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

18. Useful work against gravity:

    $$
    W=F\Delta h=(600)(4.0)=2400\ \mathrm{J}.
    $$

    Useful output power:

    $$
    P_{\text{useful}}=\frac{2400}{6.0}
    =400\ \mathrm{W}.
    $$

    If the body is $25\%$ efficient,

    $$
    0.25=\frac{P_{\text{useful}}}{P_{\text{input}}}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    P_{\text{input}}=\frac{400}{0.25}
    =1600\ \mathrm{W}.
    $$

19. Power is work done per unit time:

    $$
    P=\frac{W}{t}.
    $$

    For a constant force acting in the direction of motion,

    $$
    W=Fs.
    $$

    Substitute into the definition of power:

    $$
    P=\frac{Fs}{t}.
    $$

    Since

    $$
    v=\frac{s}{t},
    $$

    it follows that

    $$
    P=Fv.
    $$

20. Energy method:

    $$
    mgh=\frac{1}{2}mv^2.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    v=\sqrt{2gh}
    =\sqrt{2(9.81)(5.0)}
    =9.90\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    Force and kinematics method:

    The resultant force is the weight, so $a=g$ downward. From

    $$
    v^2=u^2+2as,
    $$

    with $u=0$, $a=9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}$, and $s=5.0\ \mathrm{m}$,

    $$
    v=\sqrt{2(9.81)(5.0)}
    =9.90\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}.
    $$

    The methods agree because the work done by the weight equals the gain in
    kinetic energy when air resistance is negligible.
