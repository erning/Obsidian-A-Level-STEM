# Forces, Density and Pressure Key Practice Solutions

These solutions correspond to [Key Practice Problems](30%20Key%20Practice%20Problems.md).

Take $g=9.81\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-2}}$ where needed.

## A. Moments, Couples, and Equilibrium

1. Moment is

   $$
   M=Fd_\perp.
   $$

   For a perpendicular force,

   $$
   M=(45)(0.28)=12.6\ \mathrm{N\ m}.
   $$

   For a force at $40^\circ$ to the lever,

   $$
   d_\perp=0.28\sin40^\circ.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   M=(45)(0.28\sin40^\circ)
   =8.1\ \mathrm{N\ m}.
   $$

2. For equilibrium, clockwise moment equals anticlockwise moment:

   $$
   350(1.2)=420x.
   $$

   Thus

   $$
   x=\frac{350(1.2)}{420}=1.0\ \mathrm{m}.
   $$

3. Let $R_L$ and $R_R$ be the left and right support forces. The weight of the
   uniform beam acts at its centre, $1.2\ \mathrm{m}$ from the left support.

   Take moments about the left support:

   $$
   R_R(2.4)=60(1.2)+90(1.8).
   $$

   So

   $$
   R_R=\frac{72+162}{2.4}
   =97.5\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   Vertical force equilibrium gives

   $$
   R_L+R_R=60+90=150.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   R_L=150-97.5=52.5\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

4. Torque of a couple is

   $$
   \tau=Fd.
   $$

   Therefore

   $$
   \tau=(25)(0.18)=4.5\ \mathrm{N\ m}.
   $$

5. Let the left and right tensions be $T_L$ and $T_R$.

   Horizontal equilibrium:

   $$
   T_L\cos45^\circ=T_R\cos30^\circ.
   $$

   So

   $$
   T_R=\frac{\cos45^\circ}{\cos30^\circ}T_L=0.816T_L.
   $$

   Vertical equilibrium:

   $$
   T_L\sin45^\circ+T_R\sin30^\circ=40.
   $$

   Substitute:

   $$
   T_L(0.707)+(0.816T_L)(0.500)=40.
   $$

   Thus

   $$
   1.115T_L=40,
   $$

   so

   $$
   T_L=35.9\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

   Then

   $$
   T_R=29.3\ \mathrm{N}.
   $$

6. Suspend the lamina freely from one point and hang a plumb line from the same
   point. When the lamina is at rest, draw the vertical line marked by the
   plumb line. Repeat from a second suspension point. The centre of gravity lies
   at the intersection of the two lines because, in equilibrium, the weight acts
   vertically below the suspension point.

## B. Density and Pressure

7. The volume is

   $$
   V=(4.0)(3.0)(2.0)=24.0\ \mathrm{cm^3}.
   $$

   Convert:

   $$
   24.0\ \mathrm{cm^3}=24.0\times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m^3}
   =2.40\times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{m^3}.
   $$

   Density:

   $$
   \rho=\frac{0.840}{2.40\times 10^{-5}}
   =3.50\times 10^4\ \mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}.
   $$

8. Since

   $$
   1\ \mathrm{g\ cm^{-3}}=1000\ \mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}},
   $$

   then

   $$
   1.25\ \mathrm{g\ cm^{-3}}
   =1250\ \mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}.
   $$

9. Pressure is

   $$
   p=\frac{F}{A}.
   $$

   So

   $$
   p=\frac{180}{0.015}
   =1.2\times 10^4\ \mathrm{Pa}.
   $$

10. Pressure on the small piston:

    $$
    p=\frac{80}{4.0\times 10^{-4}}
    =2.0\times 10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}.
    $$

    The same pressure acts on the large piston, so

    $$
    F=pA=(2.0\times 10^5)(0.020)
    =4.0\times 10^3\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

## C. Hydrostatic Pressure and Upthrust

11. Use

    $$
    \Delta p=\rho g\Delta h.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    \Delta p=(1000)(9.81)(2.5)
    =2.45\times 10^4\ \mathrm{Pa}.
    $$

12. For oil,

    $$
    \Delta p=(850)(9.81)(6.0)
    =5.0\times 10^4\ \mathrm{Pa}.
    $$

13. Pressure difference:

    $$
    \Delta p=\rho g\Delta h
    =(1000)(9.81)(0.12)
    =1.18\times 10^3\ \mathrm{Pa}.
    $$

    The upthrust is this pressure difference multiplied by the area:

    $$
    F=(1.18\times 10^3)(0.030)
    =35.3\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    Check using $F=\rho gV$. The volume is

    $$
    V=(0.030)(0.12)=3.60\times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m^3}.
    $$

    So

    $$
    F=(1000)(9.81)(3.60\times 10^{-3})
    =35.3\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

14. Upthrust:

    $$
    F_{\text{upthrust}}=\rho gV
    =(1000)(9.81)(2.0\times 10^{-4})
    =1.96\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    Weight:

    $$
    W=mg=(1.8)(9.81)=17.7\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

    Apparent weight:

    $$
    17.7-1.96=15.7\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

15. For a floating object,

    $$
    \rho_{\text{water}}gV_s=\rho_{\text{wood}}gV.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    \frac{V_s}{V}
    =\frac{600}{1000}
    =0.600.
    $$

    So $60.0\%$ of the volume is submerged.

    Since the block floats, upthrust equals weight:

    $$
    F_{\text{upthrust}}=\rho_{\text{wood}}gV
    =(600)(9.81)(0.020)
    =118\ \mathrm{N}.
    $$

16. The extra mass is

    $$
    12(1000)=1.20\times 10^4\ \mathrm{kg}.
    $$

    The extra volume of water displaced is

    $$
    V=\frac{m}{\rho}
    =\frac{1.20\times 10^4}{1000}
    =12.0\ \mathrm{m^3}.
    $$

    If the water-line area is $600\ \mathrm{m^2}$, the extra depth is

    $$
    h=\frac{V}{A}
    =\frac{12.0}{600}
    =0.020\ \mathrm{m}.
    $$

## D. Mixed Review

17. Consider a vertical column of fluid with cross-sectional area $A$ and height
    $\Delta h$.

    Its volume is

    $$
    V=A\Delta h.
    $$

    Its mass is

    $$
    m=\rho A\Delta h.
    $$

    Its weight is

    $$
    W=mg=\rho gA\Delta h.
    $$

    Pressure difference is force per unit area:

    $$
    \Delta p=\frac{W}{A}
    =\frac{\rho gA\Delta h}{A}
    =\rho g\Delta h.
    $$

18. The forces are equal and opposite, so their vector sum is zero. However,
    they do not have the same line of action. They form a couple.

    The torque is

    $$
    \tau=Fd=(10)(0.50)=5.0\ \mathrm{N\ m}.
    $$

    Therefore the rod has zero resultant force but a non-zero torque, so it is
    not in rotational equilibrium.

19. The object is less dense than the oil, so it floats. For floating,

    $$
    \frac{V_s}{V}
    =\frac{\rho_{\text{object}}}{\rho_{\text{oil}}}
    =\frac{750}{900}
    =0.833.
    $$

    About $83\%$ of its volume is submerged.

20. The moment is

    $$
    M=Fd\sin\theta.
    $$

    Thus

    $$
    M=(60)(0.32)\sin70^\circ
    =18.0\ \mathrm{N\ m}.
    $$
