# Measurement and Data Presentation Key Practice Solutions

Use these solutions to check practical judgement, uncertainty handling, graph
work, and final presentation for [[10 Physics/02 Experimental Thinking/Measurement and Data Presentation/30 Key Practice Problems|Key Practice Problems]].

## A. Measurement, Tables, and Significant Figures

1. A micrometer screw gauge is suitable because it can read small diameters,
   typically to $0.01\ \mathrm{mm}$. A metre rule has millimetre divisions, so
   a diameter of about $0.40\ \mathrm{mm}$ is smaller than one division and
   cannot be measured reliably.

2. A suitable uncertainty is about one count of the last digit:

   $$
   V=(2.34\pm0.01)\ \mathrm{V}.
   $$

3. Human reaction time is much larger than $0.01\ \mathrm{s}$. When a
   stop-watch is started and stopped by hand, a more realistic uncertainty is
   often about $0.1$ to $0.2\ \mathrm{s}$ unless timing many oscillations.

4. The mean is

   $$
   \bar{T}=\frac{1.83+1.87+1.85+1.84+1.86}{5}
   =1.85\ \mathrm{s}.
   $$

   The range is $0.04\ \mathrm{s}$, so the half-range uncertainty is

   $$
   \Delta T=0.02\ \mathrm{s}.
   $$

   Hence

   $$
   T=(1.85\pm0.02)\ \mathrm{s}.
   $$

5. Percentage uncertainty is

   $$
   \frac{0.05}{2.40}\times 100\%=2.1\%.
   $$

6. Raw readings taken with the same instrument should be recorded to the same
   precision. Either the first value should be written as $4.30\ \mathrm{cm}$
   if the instrument justifies hundredths of a centimetre, or the second value
   should be rounded to match the millimetre-rule precision.

7. A calculated resistance should usually be quoted to $2$ or $3$ significant
   figures, because the least precise measured input has $2$ significant
   figures.

8. Suitable headings are:

   | $I/\mathrm{mA}$ | $V/\mathrm{V}$ | $R/\Omega$ |
   |---:|---:|---:|

   Each heading includes a quantity and a unit.

## B. Uncertainty Calculations

9. The distance is

   $$
   d=84.0-12.0=72.0\ \mathrm{cm}.
   $$

   For a subtraction, absolute uncertainties add:

   $$
   \Delta d=0.5+0.5=1.0\ \mathrm{cm}.
   $$

   So

   $$
   d=(72.0\pm1.0)\ \mathrm{cm}.
   $$

10. The resistance is

    $$
    R=\frac{2.40}{0.520}=4.62\ \Omega.
    $$

    Percentage uncertainties add for a quotient:

    $$
    \frac{0.05}{2.40}\times 100\%=2.08\%,
    $$

    $$
    \frac{0.005}{0.520}\times 100\%=0.96\%.
    $$

    Total percentage uncertainty:

    $$
    2.08\%+0.96\%=3.04\%.
    $$

    Absolute uncertainty:

    $$
    \Delta R=0.0304(4.62)=0.14\ \Omega.
    $$

    A sensible final value is

    $$
    R=(4.6\pm0.1)\ \Omega.
    $$

11. For $\rho=m/V$, add percentage uncertainties:

    $$
    \frac{0.1}{42.0}\times 100\%=0.24\%,
    $$

    $$
    \frac{0.5}{15.0}\times 100\%=3.3\%.
    $$

    So the percentage uncertainty in density is about

    $$
    3.6\%.
    $$

12. The value is

    $$
    T^2=(1.85)^2=3.42\ \mathrm{s^2}.
    $$

    The percentage uncertainty in $T$ is

    $$
    \frac{0.02}{1.85}\times 100\%=1.1\%.
    $$

    For $T^2$, double it:

    $$
    2.2\%.
    $$

13. The force dominates, because its percentage uncertainty $4.0\%$ is larger
    than the length percentage uncertainty $1.5\%$. For $F/l$, both
    percentages add, but the larger contribution is from $F$.

14. Quote the uncertainty to one significant figure and match the value to the
    same decimal place:

    $$
    (3.5\pm0.2)\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
    $$

## C. Graphs and Linearisation

15. Three features include: both axes labelled with quantity and unit; sensible
    scales such as $1$, $2$, or $5$ units per large square; data points using at
    least half the grid in both directions; regular axis numbering; and a clear
    best-fit line or curve.

16. A line of best fit represents the trend in data with random scatter.
    Joining points implies the model must pass through every measured value,
    which is usually not justified.

17. The gradient is

    $$
    m=\frac{3.35-0.95}{0.80-0.20}
    =\frac{2.40}{0.60}
    =4.0\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
    $$

18. Use $V=mI+c$:

    $$
    1.10=(2.50)(0.30)+c.
    $$

    Hence

    $$
    c=0.35\ \mathrm{V}.
    $$

19. Squaring the equation gives

    $$
    T^2=\frac{4\pi^2}{k}m.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    \text{gradient}=\frac{4\pi^2}{k},
    $$

    so

    $$
    k=\frac{4\pi^2}{1.60}
    =24.7\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
    $$

20. The gradient uncertainty is

    $$
    |2.40-2.15|=0.25\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
    $$

    A sensible quoted value is

    $$
    (2.4\pm0.3)\ \mathrm{N\ m^{-1}}.
    $$

21. Take logarithms:

    $$
    \lg y=\lg a+n\lg x.
    $$

    Plot $\lg y$ against $\lg x$. The gradient is $n$ and the intercept is
    $\lg a$, so $a=10^{\text{intercept}}$.

22. Take natural logarithms:

    $$
    \ln y=\ln a+kx.
    $$

    Plot $\ln y$ against $x$. The gradient is $k$ and the intercept is
    $\ln a$, so $a=e^{\text{intercept}}$.

23. Taking natural logarithms gives

    $$
    \ln V=\ln V_0-\frac{t}{RC}.
    $$

    Plot $\ln(V/\mathrm{V})$ against $t$. The gradient is

    $$
    -\frac{1}{RC}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    RC=-\frac{1}{\text{gradient}}.
    $$

24. The point should be plotted, circled or labelled as anomalous, and then
    excluded from the best-fit line only if it clearly does not fit the trend.
    It should not be silently ignored.
